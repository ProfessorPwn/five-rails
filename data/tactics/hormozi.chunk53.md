## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Value Equation (Deal Variables)**
Every deal has four axes: price, speed, risk, and ease. Map all 80+ variables across those four before sitting down. When you have five variables and they have two, you can move on ease or speed without touching price — and still close. Use this to stay in reciprocity while barely moving on the number that matters.

**BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)**
Before any negotiation, manufacture a real alternative so you can walk away. Your psychological power at the table is directly proportional to how little you need this deal. Get multiple bids, competing offers, or a status quo you're genuinely happy with — then negotiate from that base, not from desperation.

**Anchoring + Incremental Signaling**
The first number sets the entire frame. Anchor high on opening, anchor low on counters. The size of your counter-move signals how much room is left. If they move $3M and you move $250K, you're telling them: I'm nearly done moving. Stack non-price sweeteners (cash, speed, terms) when you nudge the number up.

**MESO (Multiple Equivalent Simultaneous Offers)**
Present two or three structurally different deals — all of which work for you — and let them pick. This embeds reciprocity, signals flexibility, and reveals their hidden priorities. Once you see which components they favor, you know what's cheap to give and what to guard. Counter-tactic: ask for their versions, then propose "option D" that cherrypicks the best pieces.

**Two-Tier Affiliate Structure**
Design affiliate programs with a pinnacle prize (unscalable, exclusive, desirable) and a floor threshold (achievable by the everyday person). The top tier drives competitive intensity; the floor tier captures long-tail volume. Prize value must benefit the affiliate's *audience*, not just the affiliate — otherwise they feel like they're burning goodwill hard-closing their list.

**The SEEUA Business Quality Filter**
Five criteria for evaluating any opportunity vehicle: **S**ticky (revenue retention), **E**xpensive (high gross margin), **E**xpansion (growing market tailwind), **A**ir (low capex + low operational complexity), **U**nique (defensible moat). Even one of these makes a business better than average. None means you're grinding uphill forever.

**Retention Cliff Map**
Churn is highest in month one (20%+), spikes again at month three (~10%), then drops sharply after month six (~2%/month). The only real goal is getting customers to month six. Obsess over first-30-day experience, engineer a bridge to month three, and the long tail takes care of itself.

**Reframe as Investment, Not Cost**
Never let the other side frame your price as overhead. Immediately convert it: "For a $5,000 investment, you recover $15,000 in maintenance costs." When you're buying, flip it — frame their ask as overhead, drag, or risk. Whoever controls the frame controls the perceived value.

**Frustration Tolerance as a Learnable Skill**
The number of times you can be rejected and re-attempt without quitting is not fixed personality — it's trainable. The unlock is translating negative repetition into a reinforcing event: reframe the shock not as punishment but as protecting something you care about. Every rep builds the skill of sitting in the unknown with confidence that it's solvable.

**Proficiency > Passion (The Duty Frame)**
Passion is too narrow to sustain a business — it covers maybe 10% of what running that business actually requires. The sustainable fuel is pride in excellent execution regardless of subject matter. Ask: "Am I passionate about being *excellent* at this?" If yes, the subject matter is almost irrelevant.

**Moat Stacking**
Moats stack: brand + patent + capital barrier + network effect = nearly unassailable. Start with brand (convert any commodity with a name and story). Layer in capital investment (raises the barrier to entry). Add network effects only where they actually exist. Trade secrets and patents require defense but they compound your pricing power.

**Affiliate Collateral Machine**
Affiliates will promote consistently only if you remove all friction. Provide: (1) custom tracking link with real-time leaderboard attribution, (2) pre-written plug-and-play ads, emails, posts, and text messages, (3) behind-the-scenes data on what's converting. Then send leaderboard updates daily during the campaign. Your top performers get proactive outreach from your team.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** You made an offer and they accepted fast, making you wonder if you left money on the table. | **Script:** "Hey — just so you know, if you'd come in at [higher number], I wouldn't have done it." (Puts them at ease. Kills buyer's remorse. Signals deal integrity even if you're bluffing.)

**Situation:** Vendor or contractor damaged or botched something. | **Script:** "What would it cost *you* to fix or replace this?" — let them name the number — then: "That sounds like a pretty good place to start for a discount."

**Situation:** Opening anchor on price as seller. | **Script:** Put out a number that gets a gasp. Say nothing. Let silence sit. The gasp tells you it landed; the silence forces them to counter rather than you walking it back.

**Situation:** Presenting multiple offers simultaneously. | **Script:** "I've put together three versions — all of which work for me. Which one fits your situation best?" Then listen for which components they react to.

**Situation:** They present you with multiple offers. | **Script:** "I like [piece from offer A], and [piece from offer B], and [piece from offer C]. Why don't we build an Option D that takes the best of all three?"

**Situation:** Framing your fee as a vendor. | **Script:** "This isn't a cost line — it's an investment. Based on [specific data], you're looking at [X return] within [timeframe]. We're really just moving money from one pocket to another, and you keep the asset."

**Situation:** Negotiating as an employee asking for more compensation. | **Script:** "I'd like to frame this as an investment, not overhead. If we structure this as a percentage of what I bring in, I'm not a cost center — I'm a revenue generator."

**Situation:** You've moved and they've barely budged; you want to signal you're close to done. | **Script:** Counter with a tiny increment (e.g., they move $3M, you move $250K) and simultaneously add a non-price sweetener. Say: "I'll move to [number], and I'll also [cash/speed/risk concession]." Signal: I'm nearly at my ceiling.

**Situation:** They won't reveal their priorities. | **Script:** Offer three structurally different deals with different price/term combos and watch which one they engage with. Their counter reveals what they actually value.

**Situation:** Recruiting affiliates who are protective of their audience. | **Script:** "You're not promoting my thing — you're giving your audience access to something valuable. The registration link *is* the gift to them."

**Situation:** Someone quits or pivots because they're "not passionate." | **Script:** "The lesson isn't to find the right passion. It's to learn to be consistent after something gets boring — because that's where everyone falls off. Not when it gets hard. When it gets boring."

**Situation:** Pushing through a competitive objection. | **Script:** "The people who know me know I don't stop. That's not a threat — it's just a fact. We can keep going until someone physically locks the door. So we might as well figure out how to make this work."

**Situation:** Counter-anchoring after they've anchored high. | **Script:** Name a dramatically lower number immediately and say: "I know that's a big gap — let me show you why that's actually the right starting point." Then bring data.

**Situation:** Trying to get someone past early subscription churn. | **Script:** Don't sell the outcome — sell the first 30 days. "I just want to make sure the next 30 days are the best possible experience. After that, you'll know."

**Situation:** Closing an affiliate on promoting your offer. | **Script:** "Here's everything written and ready — ads, emails, posts, text messages. You literally copy, paste, and tweak two lines. The leaderboard updates daily so you'll see every opt-in attributed to you in real time."

**Situation:** Justifying a premium price for a home improvement against sticker shock. | **Script:** "Every comparable house that sold at [price] had [feature]. The ones without it sold for [lower]. You're not spending $100K — you're capturing $200K in equity, and you get to enjoy it the whole time."

**Situation:** Someone asks your advice and you want to give them the hard truth about passion. | **Script:** "Pick a path and commit to it. The person you want to become is at the end of it — and everything between you now and that version of you is the pain you haven't learned to tolerate yet."

**Situation:** Entering a competitive market with no track record. | **Script:** "Of course I'm not good yet. I just started. The only question is how many times I'm willing to do this badly before I do it well."

**Situation:** Vendor asks for your budget before you've anchored. | **Script:** "I'd rather see what you think fair looks like first — then we can figure out if there's a deal here."

**Situation:** Partner deal where they're fixated on headline price. | **Script:** "Let's table the number for a second. What are the terms that would make this actually work for you? Because I've got flexibility in ways that might matter more than the price."

## DECISION RULES

**When you need the deal, create an alternative first.** Negotiating from desperation is playing poker on bluffs alone. Get a real BATNA before you sit down — competing offers, a status quo you're genuinely happy with, or a walk-away position you've already priced out.

**When anchoring, go higher than feels comfortable.** You can always come down. You cannot come up. Get the gasp. The gasp shifts all subsequent numbers in the negotiation.

**When you counter, move in small increments and add non-price value simultaneously.** Big counter-moves signal that you have more room. Small moves signal you're near your floor. Sweeten with terms (speed, cash, risk reduction) rather than price.

**When evaluating a business to start or buy, check retention first.** If there's no revenue retention, everything else is irrelevant — you're permanently in sales mode. Fix retention before you fix anything else.

**When building an affiliate program, design the top prize to be unscalable.** Exclusivity drives competition. Scale the *floor* incentive (10+ opt-ins), not the ceiling. Both tiers must serve the affiliate's audience, not just the affiliate.

**When a customer is new, obsess over days 1–30.** Month-one churn is 20%+. That's the single highest-leverage intervention in any subscription business. Everything else is secondary until first-30-day experience is locked.

**When someone gives you multiple simultaneous offers, don't pick one — build Option D.** Extract the best term from each and propose a hybrid. You get to cherry-pick what they revealed as negotiable.

**When framing price, always convert to ROI before you state the number.** Never say "this costs X." Say "for X, you get Y return." Whoever frames first, frames the negotiation.

**When your business has weak moats, invest in brand before anything else.** Brand is the fastest way to de-commoditize any service or product. You can brand anything. It immediately justifies a price premium.

**When entering a market, look for tailwinds first.** If the market is shrinking 6%/year, your execution has to overcome the headwind before it grows you. If it's growing 20%/year, showing up and doing average work still produces growth.

**When you have multiple deal variables, never play all your cards at once.** Identify 5–10 levers (price, speed, cash vs. financed, risk allocation, etc.) and deploy them in sequence as concessions so you stay in reciprocity without actually moving much on price.

**When someone quits because of boredom (not difficulty), flag it.** That's the real dropout point. The skill gap isn't competence — it's frustration tolerance. The prescription is more repetitions, not a new path.

**When evaluating whether to pursue high-capex investment, check if it creates a moat.** Capital expenditure that raises the barrier to entry for competitors is worth it. Capital that doesn't create exclusivity is just overhead.

**When gross margins are below ~50%, find the de-commoditization lever immediately.** Low margins trap you in volume dependency and slow your reinvestment cycle. Brand, specialization, or service bundling are the primary tools.

**When an affiliate isn't promoting, check friction before assuming motivation.** Remove every manual step. If they still don't move, the offer isn't compelling enough. Solve friction first, then offer quality.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**"Follow your passion" as a business strategy.** It's only true in the vague. The moment you operationalize it — sourcing, logistics, customer complaints, payroll, accounting — the passion is gone and you're stuck. It's an excuse to avoid tolerating hardness.

**Negotiating when you need the deal.** If you have no real alternative and you need this deal, all tactics become bluffing. You cannot out-tactic a structural power imbalance. Build your BATNA before you sit down.

**Accepting the other party's frame without resistance.** If they call your offer a "cost," you've already lost ground. The moment someone sets a frame (cost, overhead, risk), counter-frame immediately or you're negotiating inside their story.

**Showing all your concession cards at once.** Bundling all your flexibility into an opening offer signals you have nothing left. Reveal concessions sequentially — each one should feel like a hard-won extraction to the other side.

**Building a business with no retention mechanism.** Zero churn compounding beats 3x marketing spend every time. If your business model requires constantly replacing every customer you lose, you're on a treadmill, not a compounding machine.

**Expecting affiliates to build their own campaigns.** If you hand someone a promotional opportunity and don't give them plug-and-play collateral, they won't do it. The ask is too large. Remove all friction or the program dies.

**Conflating reward with reinforcement.** People do what they're reinforced for, not just what they're rewarded for. A repetitive, unrewarded task can still be reinforcing if it's connected to something they care about protecting. Don't assume boredom equals lack of reinforcement — diagnose the actual loop.

**Jumping to a new path when the current one gets boring (not hard).** Hard is a signal to adapt. Boring is a signal you're close to mastery. Quitting when it gets boring is the single most common failure mode in entrepreneurship.

**Confusing logo retention with revenue retention.** You can lose 20% of customers and still grow revenue if your remaining customers expand spend. Never manage to headcount alone — manage to net revenue retention.

**Picking a market because you're passionate about the product, not the economics.** Markets that are shrinking, have low gross margins, and no retention are hard regardless of your passion level. Evaluate the vehicle first, then determine if you can make it yours.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Triplet escalation:** Hormozi builds arguments in threes, escalating specificity each time. "Interests get you started. Proficiency keeps you going. Pain tolerance helps you finish." Pattern: introduce concept → deepen it → land the hardest version. Use this to close any argument.

**Self-story as proof, not brag:** He narrates his own failures and pivots in detail before making the prescriptive claim. "I did the whole thing — gyms, turnarounds, 6,000 locations, sold it all to PE — and what do I have today? A gym with no members." Earn the right to the lesson by showing you paid the price for it.

**"And here's the thing..."** transition: Used to pivot from observation to mechanism. Sets up the non-obvious insight after the obvious statement. Don't skip it — it signals to the listener that the real point is coming.

**Numbered frameworks with named components:** Everything gets a name and a number. "Five advantages." "Three contexts." "Two types of retention." This compresses complex ideas into scannable structures and makes them quotable. If you can't name the framework, you haven't finished thinking about it.

**Contrast pair structure:** Sets up two extremes to locate the real answer in the middle or force a choice. "Company A sells 100 customers and loses 100. Company B sells 100 and loses zero." One is obviously better — but the mechanism behind *why* is the actual lesson.

**Earned permission statements:** Before delivering an uncomfortable truth, he explicitly acknowledges the discomfort and his right to say it. "I only say this as somebody who went all the way with it." This disarms defensiveness before the hard claim lands.

**Specificity as credibility:** Generic advice is dismissed. Specific numbers, real scenarios, and named people signal lived experience. "$15.25M counter after they moved to $17M." "5,000 ads this year." Replace every vague claim with the most specific version you can back up — it's not bragging, it's proof.
