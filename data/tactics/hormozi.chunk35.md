## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. Supply vs. Demand Constraint Triage**
Every business is either supply-constrained (customers but not enough capacity) or demand-constrained (capacity but not enough customers) — never both at the same time. Ask: "If I doubled my ad budget tomorrow, would I double sales or create a mess I can't handle?" If it creates a mess, you're supply-constrained. Fix the real constraint before spending a dollar on the other side.

**2. The Constraint Accordion**
Constraints alternate — solve your demand problem well enough and you create a supply problem; solve supply and demand becomes the bottleneck. Businesses stay stuck for years because founders keep solving the old constraint after it's already shifted. Audit which constraint is active right now, not which one hurt last year.

**3. The Reward Loop (Email Behavior Engine)**
People repeat behaviors they were rewarded for, not behaviors they were triggered to do. Every email interaction is a step in a reward chain: open → reward, read → reward, click → reward, reply → reward. Design every element of the email sequence to deliver a positive return on attention at each micro-step, or you erode the loop permanently.

**4. The Promo Tab Avoidance Stack**
Email deliverability is a composite score built from: plain-text format, 1–2 links max, no money language in body, asking for replies, and consistent engagement rates over time. There's no silver bullet — it's 100 golden BBs. Align with the platform's objective (best-quality inbox) instead of gaming it, and you get rewarded with primary inbox placement.

**5. The Principal-Agent Problem in Sales**
Identify who actually makes the decision vs. who writes the check — they are often different people. The owner says yes, but the operator (foreman, building manager) kills the deal because it creates work for them with no upside. Rescripting the sales process to elevate the status and serve the actual decision-maker can unlock years of stalled growth.

**6. The PS Statement System**
Beyond the subject line, the PS is the most-read part of any email. Use it as either a secondary CTA, a meme/delight moment, or a forward-looking hook ("big announcement coming — stay subscribed"). Never waste it. It is real estate, not decoration.

**7. Segmentation as a 791% Lever**
HubSpot data: segmented email lists produce a 791% higher ROI. The quality of the match between content and recipient matters more than the quality of the email itself. Segment by revenue tier, business stage, or customer type. Sending beginner content to advanced buyers destroys credibility; sending advanced content to beginners destroys relevance.

**8. Supply Problem Pricing Lever**
For supply-constrained businesses, raising prices aggressively (50–100%, not 5%) is the fastest fix. If 30% of clients leave after a price double, you make more money while working less. The freed capacity then funds the next level of growth — whether that's better systems, better staff, or a more favorable delivery ratio.

**9. Contextual CTA Architecture**
Every CTA should be earned by and connected to the content that precedes it. Don't paste the same CTA across emails or videos — the bridge from content to offer should feel inevitable. If the email is about testimonials, the CTA is about converting testimonials. Mismatched CTAs leak conversion.

**10. The Average Cart Value Trap**
Businesses that are "fully booked and barely profitable" often have a sales skills problem, not a demand or supply problem. The technician is already in the house; he just isn't offering the second service. Doubling upsell rate doubles revenue with near-zero incremental cost. Teach two- to three-line scripts, role-play daily, track the metric.

**11. Behavioral Email Formatting (Looks Like a Real Email)**
The ideal email approximates a personal message: mostly text, no images, one or two links, no money language, short sentences. The further your email looks from a Vegas-strip promotion, the better your deliverability and the higher the trust signal to the reader. Format is a trust signal.

**12. Lead Magnet Resell at Optin**
The moment someone opts in for a lead magnet is the highest-motivation moment you will ever have with that person. Use it to sell them on *staying subscribed*, not just on delivering the asset. Reveal surprise bonuses before delivering the promised item. Ask for a reply to deliver the asset — this trains reply behavior and boosts domain authority simultaneously.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Someone hasn't emailed their list in months and is afraid to start | **Script:** "Start with 'Is this on?' — acknowledge the gap, own it with a self-deprecating line, then immediately deliver value. The first email's only job is to reward them for opening. Everything else can come later."

**Situation:** Prospect hesitates on a price increase | **Script:** "Add $3 and see what happens. Most people are about to add 25 cents. The market is telling you what they're willing to pay. You're the one getting in the way."

**Situation:** Client is fully booked but wants more customers | **Script:** "You don't have a marketing problem. You have a supply problem — and pricing is your fastest fix. If you doubled your prices and lost 30% of clients, do the math. You'd make more money with less work."

**Situation:** Diagnosing a business's core constraint | **Script:** "If you doubled your ad spend tomorrow, would you double sales or create a mess? That answer tells you everything. One answer means fix demand. The other means touch marketing and you'll make it worse."

**Situation:** Convincing a supply-constrained business not to run more ads | **Script:** "More marketing right now just means more angry customers you can't serve. That damages your reputation more than not following up with a lead."

**Situation:** Upsell scripting for home services / in-person delivery | **Script:** "Once you finish the primary job, ask them: 'While I'm here, would it make sense to take a look at [X]?' Two lines. Role-play it every morning. Track what percentage say yes. That number is your profit margin."

**Situation:** Asking for testimonials | **Script:** "Don't ask 'What was life like before?' Ask them for their worst moment. 'What was the single worst moment before you started working with us?' That's the hook. The details of that moment are the ad."

**Situation:** Writing the PS statement | **Script:** "In the spirit of PS statements — if you like tactics like these to grow your business, go to [URL] and opt in. I send this twice a week and it takes less than 60 seconds to read."

**Situation:** Convincing a prospect the sales team doesn't need to double | **Script:** "You're closing two out of 500 demos. Let's not double the sales team — let's get 500 demos turning into 100 deals. That's a 50x without hiring a single person."

**Situation:** Framing a price increase to avoid customer resistance | **Script:** "Don't say 'weekends cost more.' Say 'weekdays are 20% less.' People hate price increases. They love discounts. Same net effect on your P&L, completely different emotional reception."

**Situation:** Positioning email as high-ROI with low-effort barrier | **Script:** "I sat down and wrote 24 emails. It took half a day. I thought — I could have been doing this for a year off a half day's work. Start there. Write 12. You've handled the whole year. Everything else is bonus."

**Situation:** Explaining why unsubscribes aren't a threat | **Script:** "Every day, thousands of people unsubscribe from my Instagram. Thousands from YouTube. I still grow because more subscribe than leave. Unsubscribes aren't insults — they're people self-selecting. The ones who stay are your buyers."

**Situation:** Explaining dormant subscribers vs. unsubscribers | **Script:** "Making it hard to unsubscribe doesn't keep them engaged — it makes them dormant. And dormant is worse. They don't open, your domain score tanks, and the people who *want* your emails stop getting them."

**Situation:** Selling someone on the value of the email newsletter at optin | **Script:** "I want to reward you before I give you what I promised. Here's what you're also going to get that I didn't mention upfront: [surprise bonuses]. And by the way — reply 'yes' and I'll send over the chapter."

**Situation:** Redirecting a founder who's over-investing in product | **Script:** "No one's churning. Everyone loves the product. But it's not growing. Pull resources from the product and put them into getting more customers. The product will still be there. The market window might not."

**Situation:** Diagnosing a demand-side leak in the funnel | **Script:** "People are opting in. They're scheduling calls. They're showing up. But they're not buying. That's not a marketing problem — that's a sales process problem. Fix the leak before you put more water in."

**Situation:** Justifying a high premium price tier | **Script:** "Create a high-end version at 10x the base price. Another 10% of the market will happily pay it. That 10% at $10K adds $1,000 per sale to your average. Same content, same marketing, very different P&L."

**Situation:** Recommending plain-text email format | **Script:** "Think about the emails you exchange every day. They're mostly text, maybe a link, not a lot of images. That's what your marketing emails should look like. The Vegas Strip in your inbox is how you end up in the promo tab."

**Situation:** Teaching email timing optimization | **Script:** "B2C — Mondays and Tuesdays, 10am to noon or 1pm to 3pm. B2B — Wednesday, same windows. Don't send first thing in the morning — they're working through real business emails. Slide in when they come up for air."

**Situation:** Justifying preview text optimization | **Script:** "24% of people check the preview text before they open. It takes 10 seconds to set. If you don't set it, it defaults to whatever the first 150 characters of your email are — which is usually 'Hey John, I hope this finds you.' Don't waste it."

## DECISION RULES

1. **When you're supply-constrained, raise prices first.** It's the fastest fix, it's non-dilutive, and it creates time and cash to solve the real capacity problem.

2. **When you're demand-constrained, fix advertising and sales before adding headcount.** Doubling the team with a broken funnel doubles the cost, not the revenue.

3. **When a constraint shifts, switch what you're working on immediately.** Succeeding at the old constraint after it's resolved creates a new crisis.

4. **When in doubt on email format, make it look like a personal email.** Plain text, 1–2 links, no money language, short paragraphs.

5. **When writing email CTAs, make them contextual to that specific email.** A mismatched CTA leaks conversions regardless of how good the content is.

6. **When your email list is unengaged, send anyway — but accept the churn.** The first email after a long silence will pull forward all the dormant churn. That's fine. Take it.

7. **When diagnosing a stuck business, ask the supply/demand question before suggesting any solution.** Everything downstream depends on getting that answer right.

8. **When closing is the problem, don't hire more salespeople — rescript the existing ones first.** Two deals out of 500 demos is a scripting problem, not a volume problem.

9. **When you have high retention but no growth, you have a demand problem.** The product is not the issue. Go get more customers.

10. **When pricing feels scary, go further than you think.** Most people add 25 cents when they should add $3. The market will tell you if you've gone too far. Timidity just costs you.

11. **When sending email, always include a PS statement.** It's the second-most-read element after the subject line. Never leave it empty.

12. **When you can't increase prices, increase average cart value per job.** One upsell script deployed consistently outperforms most marketing investments.

13. **When building an email list, sell prospects on the ongoing relationship — not just the lead magnet.** If they only want the download, they'll unsubscribe the second it lands. Resell the subscription before you deliver the asset.

14. **When you have a beautiful homepage but low conversion, fix the landing page first.** Send paid traffic to a conversion-optimized page, not to a brand showcase.

15. **When someone is stuck at the same revenue for 2+ years, the constraint has likely shifted and they didn't.** Diagnose before prescribing.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Running more ads when you're supply-constrained.** This is the single most common mistake. More marketing into a constrained business creates chaos, delays, bad reviews, and rep damage. You are literally making it worse.

2. **Making unsubscribing hard.** It keeps dormant contacts on the list, tanks deliverability, and destroys domain authority. The vanity of a big list is bought at the cost of the people who actually want to read you.

3. **Sending the same CTA across every email or video regardless of context.** It signals to the reader that the CTA is an afterthought, not a relevant next step. Context mismatches kill conversion.

4. **Doubling the sales team before fixing the sales process.** If you're closing 0.4% of demos, doubling headcount doubles the cost of a broken system. Diagnose the conversion failure first.

5. **Building more product features when awareness is the constraint.** "If you build it, they will come" is the most dangerous myth in business. No one churning means the product is fine. Go get customers.

6. **Treating unsubscribes as failures.** Unsubscribes are self-selection. The people who stay are your buyers. The ones who leave weren't going to buy anyway. Trying to suppress unsubscribes suppresses list health.

7. **Adding money language to email copy.** It trains spam filters to route you to the promo tab. Remove dollar signs, pricing language, and revenue-heavy framing from the email body itself.

8. **Waiting until your email is "perfect" to start sending.** Every day you don't email is a day you're leaving 35–45x ROI on the table. The day you start is the day the return clock starts.

9. **Loading emails with multiple images and five-plus links.** This looks like promotion to both the algorithm and the reader. It triggers promo tab routing and reduces trust. Keep it simple.

10. **Improving operations or product when you have no customers to serve.** Widening the lanes on a highway doesn't help if there's no traffic. Increasing potential without increasing throughput is wasted capital.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. Problem → Implication → Solution → Proof → CTA**
Hormozi structures almost every tactical explanation the same way: state the problem clearly, make the implication visceral ("what happens if you don't"), offer the solution, anchor it with a stat or case study, then bridge to the next action. He rarely skips the implication step — that's where urgency lives.

**2. "Play it out" logic chains**
He favors walking through the exact scenario to its conclusion rather than asserting a point. "Let's say 30% of clients leave after you double prices. Do the math. You make more money while working with fewer people. That's the double benefit." The chain does the persuasion; he just walks you through it.

**3. Quantified specificity over vague claims**
Every assertion gets a number: 391% increase in sales from speed-to-lead, 791% ROI lift from segmentation, 24% open lift from preview text, 18% open lift from day-of-week. He treats specificity as credibility. Vague claims ("email works really well") are replaced with anchored numbers from named sources.

**4. Self-deprecating admission before teaching**
He routinely leads with his own failure or delay on the exact topic he's teaching. "I didn't email for the vast majority of my life because I hate money apparently." This disarms skepticism and signals that the lesson was earned, not theoretical.

**5. The rhetorical extreme**
When defending a counterintuitive point, he pushes it to its logical extreme to make the alternative absurd. "If you're afraid to email because people will unsubscribe, why do you have an email list to begin with? The logical extreme of that is: just never do anything." Forces the listener to abandon the weak objection.

**6. Stacked "and the secondary benefit is..." structure**
He layers benefits in sequence — first-order, second-order, third-order — to make the case feel overwhelming. "He makes more money. That's benefit one. He has more time — that's benefit two. That extra time lets him build capacity — that's benefit three." By the time he's done, the case feels airtight.

**7. Short declarative punches, then elaboration**
He establishes a point in one sentence, then unpacks it. "Email's ROI is 35 to 45x. That means for every dollar in, you get $45 back. Imagine if there were a stock that 50x'd in a week." The declarative lands the anchor; everything after is just making it stick.
