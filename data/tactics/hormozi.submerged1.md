## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. More → Better → New (Prioritization Order)**
Before trying something new, exhaust "more of what's working," then "better execution of what's working." Only escalate to "new" when you've hit a structural ceiling. Your working business is a statistically rare configuration of variables — every change risks breaking the stack. One new thing per year maximum; everything else must be a variation or volume play on what's already proven. Apply: when growth stalls, audit raw volume versus the leader in your lane before inventing anything new.

**2. The CLOSER Framework**
C = Clarify why they're here. L = Label them with the problem. O = Overview past pain. S = Sell the vacation (outcome, not process). E = Explain away concerns. R = Reinforce the decision. Use as the sequential spine of every sales call. Never skip C and L before pitching — without establishing the gap, there is nothing to sell against. Run it in sequence; back-load closes for after the first "no."

**3. The Pain Cycle**
"What have you tried before? How'd that work out for you?" — cycle through every attempt they've made. Deprivation creates motivation. Expand their felt deprivation before asking for money: what have they tried, how much spent, what has failure cost them daily in status/money/time? The deeper you anchor pain, the more urgency to act. Never present the offer until you've run at least two full loops.

**4. The Value Equation**
Value = (Dream Outcome × Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort/Sacrifice). The five friction categories: delay, effort, sacrifice, risk, inconsistency. The highest-leverage move is almost always *removing* something — not adding features. "Customers cause their own demise when you give them everything they ask for." Apply: your job in an offer is elimination, not accumulation.

**5. Uninformed Optimism → Informed Pessimism → Valley of Despair → Upside**
Every opportunity follows this arc. The valley is not evidence it's broken — it's the price of entry. Most people exit at despair and restart at optimism with something new, forever cycling without upside. Apply: when evaluating a new opportunity, decide if you can commit through the valley *before* entering. If not, don't start.

**6. The Blame-Power Equation**
Wherever you point your finger of blame is exactly where your power drains. Own everything up to this moment — not because it's fair, but because it's the only way to be in control. Apply every time you catch yourself explaining why something isn't working: "Does placing blame here increase or decrease the likelihood I hit my goal?"

**7. The Four Levers of a Subscription Business**
Traffic × Conversion × Price ÷ Churn = growth. Every initiative must ladder up to one of these four. If you can't name which lever it moves, don't do it. EPC (Earnings Per Click) is the single unifying metric: Price ÷ Churn = LTV → LTV × Conversion = EPC. The business that maximizes EPC wins.

**8. Supply/Demand Constraint Diagnosis**
Every business is either supply-constrained (hard to find people who can deliver) or demand-constrained (hard to find customers). Most owners fight the wrong constraint. Identify yours first — then ask: how do I gain leverage over this specific bottleneck at scale? If supply-constrained, your real business is talent attraction, not marketing.

**9. The Single Domino Strategy**
When you have five priorities, identify the one that makes the others irrelevant or easier, then throw everything at it. A good strategist asks: given the resources I have, what one thing makes all other problems disappear? Everything else gets deleted until the domino falls.

**10. Problems vs. Missed Opportunities**
Problems are finite — you can list them all. Missed opportunities are infinite. When you have an urge to start something new, ask: is this a missed opportunity or a problem? Nine times out of ten it's an opportunity. Opportunities feel exciting; problems feel like work. Solve problems first. Park opportunities on a "big money ideas" list so your brain lets go — revisit with fresh eyes when you have bandwidth.

**11. The Six Horsemen of Business Stagnation**
The six reasons businesses stop growing: (1) underpriced, (2) employees overcompensated relative to market, (3) serving too many avatars, (4) overextension before operators are in place, (5) running more than one business, (6) no data. Each has a rock-and-hard-place scenario. The short-term pain is always the correct choice.

**12. The Two-Question Decision Frame**
Before reacting to anything — a bad employee, an online critic, a conflict — ask: (1) What do I want to have happen? (2) What actually increases the odds of that? Your gut reaction is almost always antithetical to the answer. The heavyweight champion of responses is usually "do nothing." Run every interpersonal and business decision through both questions before acting.

**13. Work = Volume × Leverage**
Output isn't determined by hours — it's volume multiplied by leverage. Leverage compounds with skill: more volume → more skill → more leverage → more output per rep. In the beginning, you have near-zero leverage, so the only lever you control is volume. Do a thousand reps before you optimize. Output is the only scorecard that matters.

**14. The Ignorance Tax**
Your biggest monthly expense is the gap between what you're making and what you'd make if you knew what you should know. If you make $50K and could make $1M, you're paying $950K/year in ignorance tax. Reframes education from cost to the highest-ROI investment available. Compare any education purchase to the earning gap it closes — not to the sticker price.

**15. The Management Diamond**
Five reasons an employee doesn't perform: (1) didn't know you wanted it, (2) didn't know how, (3) didn't know when, (4) didn't know why, (5) blocked by an obstacle. Diagnose in that order before assuming motivation failure. Fix the constraint before replacing the person.

**16. Declarative vs. Procedural Knowledge**
Declarative = knowing *about* something (watching 10 sales videos). Procedural = knowing *how* to do something (making 100 cold calls). People mistake consuming content for building skill. You will learn more from your first 100 cold calls than from 100 hours of research. Treat all current "knowledge" as hypothesis until you've logged the reps.

**17. The TEAM Framework for Partnerships**
A partner is only worth bringing in if they provide at least one of four things you lack: **T**ime, **E**xpertise, **A**ssets (brand, audience, distribution), or **M**oney. The worst partnership is two people with the same expertise who are friends. Slice equity by risk exposure, not by headcount.

**18. Build Back to Front**
Most founders pour money into acquisition before fixing retention. The right order: fix delivery quality and back-end economics first, fix onboarding and churn second, scale front-end acquisition last. "I build businesses back to front. I try and solve how do we make this thing so unbelievably profitable that I can then outspend everybody else on acquisition."

**19. Activation Points Framework**
Find the single early behavior with the highest correlation to long-term retention. Look at your best customers, identify what they did in the first 30–90 days, then reverse-engineer that behavior into onboarding for every customer. Example: Orange Theory found customers who did 5 workouts in month one retained dramatically longer — so all onboarding drove toward that one activation point.

**20. The Deliberate Pairing Model (Branding)**
Branding is not a logo — it's the association that forms between your product and an outcome the customer experienced. Good branding means you deliberately engineer that pairing toward outcomes your ideal customer likes. Bad branding happens the same way — just accidentally. Measure it with three metrics: reach, influence (does it change behavior), and direction (toward you or away). Every discount, influencer partner, and promotional channel is a brand vote.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it" | **Script:** "Cool — what's your main concern?"

**Situation:** Prospect still stalling after redirect | **Script:** "What are you afraid of having happen?"

**Situation:** Isolating the real close condition | **Script:** "What would make this a yes? … Can I share what I usually hear when I ask that?"

**Situation:** Discount request on core services | **Script:** "We could do it for more." [Full stop. Let it land. Many buy immediately.]

**Situation:** Discount as barter | **Script:** "How about this — if you refer me three friends, I'll give you that discount right now. The terms change, but the deal is there."

**Situation:** Best Case / Worst Case close | **Script:** "Let's zoom out. Best case: you change your life, you finally hit this goal, you stick with it for good. Worst case: you do this basically for free and I give your money back. The only thing more guaranteed than worst case is that if you walk out that door right now, you're definitely not going to get where you want to go. So what do you want to do?"

**Situation:** 1-to-10 temperature check | **Script:** "On a scale from 1 to 10, where are we? … What would make it a 10? … And why not a 1?"

**Situation:** Prospect brings up cheap competitor | **Script:** "If ours was the same price as theirs, which would you choose? … Why? … Right — that's why we're more."

**Situation:** Spouse/partner objection | **Script:** "What would happen if they said no?" [If they'd do it anyway: "So what are we waiting for?"] [If not: "What do you think their main concern would be?"]

**Situation:** Opening a sales call with a warm lead | **Script:** "Hey — before we get into anything, help me understand: what made you actually take the time to respond/show up today? What's going on?"

**Situation:** Labeling the prospect's problem | **Script:** "So let me make sure I have this right — you're here [current state], you want to get to [desired state], and the thing standing in the way is [obstacle]. Does that sound about right?"

**Situation:** Entering the pain cycle | **Script:** "What have you tried before to solve this? Tell me everything. And how'd that go?"

**Situation:** Transitioning from pain to pitch | **Script:** "Based on everything you've told me, I think I might actually have something for you. Would you like to hear about it?"

**Situation:** Three-pillar pitch | **Script:** "There are really three things that matter here — [Pillar 1], [Pillar 2], and [Pillar 3]. Let me break each one down. [Pillar 1] — it's kind of like [analogy]..."

**Situation:** Zooming out when lost in details | **Script:** "Okay, let's zoom out — big picture: best case, we get you [outcome]. Worst case, you get [guarantee/refund]. Both options are risk-free. One guarantees you stay exactly where you are. The other gives you a shot. Which do you want?"

**Situation:** Prospect raises specific objection (travel, budget, time) | **Script:** "Oh, don't worry — I actually made something for exactly that. [Pull out pre-built bonus.] Would this take care of it for you?"

**Situation:** Pre-call reminder the night before | **Script:** "Hey [Name], really looking forward to our call tomorrow — I already pulled three things from your site I think we can immediately help with. Very excited to share them. See you at [time], hit me up if anything comes up."

**Situation:** Someone says they don't have time | **Script:** "Every person has 5 to 9 a.m. and 5 to 9 p.m. That's eight hours a day outside a full-time job. You have time. You just haven't decided this is worth it yet."

**Situation:** Selling education to someone price-sensitive | **Script:** "Write your income goal on a whiteboard. Now subtract what you make today. That difference is what you pay reality every single year for not knowing how to achieve it. The course costs less than one month of that bill."

**Situation:** Someone wants to start multiple businesses | **Script:** "Do you remember the guy who got rich dropshipping, day trading, buying crypto, and wholesaling all at the same time? Yeah. Me neither. Pick one."

**Situation:** Explaining why someone hasn't gotten results | **Script:** "It's not that ads don't work. It's that you don't yet know how to make them work. You're not making as much money as you want because you're not as good as you think you are. That's fixable."

**Situation:** Price increase email to existing customers | **Script:** (1) "Big news coming tomorrow — just letting you know. Brace yourself." (2) "I want to be straight: we're raising prices. Not because I'm a greedy bastard — but because I see we're no longer fulfilling the promises I originally made you, and to reinvest in [A, B, C], this change is necessary." (3) "Because you've been loyal, I'm giving you a discount equal to your current payment for the next six months. After that, you move to the new rate. That's my thank-you for sticking with me." (4) PS: "If this price change will materially affect your business, please just reach out. I'd love to get on the phone and figure something out."

**Situation:** Overcompensated employee conversation | **Script:** "I've got something serious I want to talk to you about. This business isn't profitable, and I can't give you the career growth I originally promised. Here's what I want you to know: there's a path where you still make what you're making today. It requires [three specific things]. I can outline that if you want. Or if you need time to explore other options, I'm giving you three to six months — whatever you need."

**Situation:** Employee not performing | **Script:** "Help me understand — did you not know I wanted this done? Did you not know how? Did you not know when I needed it? Or was there something blocking you I don't know about?"

**Situation:** BAMFAM — booking next meeting before hanging up | **Script:** "Before we wrap — let's put a time on the calendar right now for your onboarding call with [person]. What does your [day/time] look like?"

**Situation:** Helping someone reframe failure | **Script:** "Every time after the first time you start from scratch, you start with experience. You never actually start at zero again. The bet always nets positive."

**Situation:** Someone waiting for the right moment | **Script:** "The perfect moment doesn't come. It becomes the perfect moment when you look back on it, because today it just looks like now."

**Situation:** Someone burned out thinking about quitting | **Script:** "You're in the valley of despair — you've stopped being naively optimistic and you now see the real costs. This is where every person who ever succeeded sat, right before they broke through. The people who quit here never find out how close they were."

**Situation:** Closing the loop on acting now vs. later | **Script:** "If you genuinely believe you're going to do this sooner or later — why later? You're going to enjoy the benefits either way. Why delay them?"

**Situation:** Building a B2B brand via content | **Script:** Three buckets: (1) help your avatar achieve their goals for free via content, (2) show someone *like them* getting the aspirational outcome, (3) achieve the aspirational outcome yourself publicly. "What is the aspirational thing that, if I do it at the extreme, makes the average version feel low-risk to buy?"

## DECISION RULES

1. **When you're about to blame something or someone for your results:** Ask "Does placing blame here increase or decrease the likelihood I hit my goal?" If decrease — drop it. Blame transfers power to the thing blamed.

2. **When something is working:** Double down immediately. The moment of first success is when competitors will come. Go harder, not looser.

3. **When something is hard and not working:** Ask "Is this hard because it's genuinely broken, or hard because I haven't done enough volume yet?" If volume — do more. If structurally broken — do different.

4. **When a lead opts in:** Contact within 60 seconds. After 5 minutes, close rate drops 80%. Speed of response is your single highest-leverage variable.

5. **When you have multiple priorities:** Delete everything except the one that makes the others easier or irrelevant. Assigning all five is abdication, not strategy.

6. **When a department is broken:** Question every activity, delete first, then optimize, then compress timelines, then automate. Deletion is always step one — you can always add back.

7. **When churn exceeds 20% monthly:** Stop acquiring. Fix the retention problem first. Scaling a leaky bucket only accelerates the leak and compounds a negative reputation.

8. **When at full capacity (supply = demand):** Raise prices, then hire before you promote. Expand supply first, even at short-term profit cost, to preserve reputation as you grow.

9. **When afraid to act:** Write down the worst-case chain in excruciating detail — who you'll call, where you'll sleep, what you'll actually lose. It almost always ends survivable. Fear only exists in the fog.

10. **When your gut reaction is to counter-punch or retaliate:** Run the two-question frame first. "What do I want to have happen? What actually increases those odds?" Your instinct is almost always wrong. The correct answer is usually "do nothing."

11. **When adding a bonus to an offer:** Ask — does this bonus, by itself, justify the entire price? If no, cut it. If you have to explain why it's valuable, cut it.

12. **When choosing between two customer avatars:** Run (LTV ÷ CAC) × TAM for each. Pick the highest output. Stop running two front ends simultaneously.

13. **When someone gives you advice:** First ask: "Do I want their life?" If no — don't take the advice. Only accept input from people whose outcomes you'd trade for.

14. **When considering a new opportunity:** Identify where you are on the compounding curve in your current business. Year 4→5 of a known thing beats Year 0→1 of a new thing every time.

15. **When evaluating an educational purchase:** Cost of ignorance = (goal income) − (current income). If the program costs less than one year of that delta, it's almost certainly worth it.

16. **When you want to expand (new location, product, service line):** Ask: do I have a who to own the new thing while I run the current thing, or a who to run the current thing while I own the new thing? No who = no expansion. Period.

17. **When a prospect says yes:** Shut up and close. Don't add details. Don't keep selling. Ask for payment information immediately.

18. **When a metric declines:** Look at where it stopped growing, not where it started shrinking. Find the inflection point — usually you added someone bad, not lost someone good.

19. **When raising prices:** Test on new customers first. If close rate drops but revenue goes up, keep the new price. Only then bring it to existing customers with a loyalty discount bridge.

20. **When you want to improve any metric:** Measure it publicly and track it consistently. Measurement alone produces improvement before any process change.

21. **When hiring a partner:** Require at least one of: Time, Expertise, Assets, or Money (TEAM). Two people with the same expertise who are friends is the worst possible partnership.

22. **When content metrics show views down but revenue up:** You're targeting better. Narrowing audience increases conversion even as reach shrinks. Stay the course — don't widen back out to chase vanity metrics.

23. **When something takes longer than expected:** Remind yourself: it happens slower than you expect, then faster than you can imagine. The delay is the cost of entry.

24. **When an employee says something can't be done:** Replace "can't" with "what would it take?" Impossible is almost never true. Resources required and trade-offs are always true.

25. **When you feel compelled to start something new:** Ask first: can I just do more of what's already working? Then: can I do what's working better? Only if both answers are no do you start something new.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Delaying pain to "tomorrow," "Monday," or "next year."** The hallmark of poverty thinking: pleasure now, pain later. Watch for this in yourself and in prospects. If every action is "starting soon," nothing will start.

2. **Spreading across multiple businesses or income streams before any one works.** Seven income streams means you're split seven ways. You can only be CEO of one thing. Every additional lane is a vote against the lane you're actually in.

3. **Building a personal brand before building a track record.** Everyone can copy your promises, your logo, your services. Nobody can copy what you've actually done. Credibility before likability. Proof before pudding.

4. **Using "trauma," "demons," or "self-sabotage" as explanations instead of skill gaps.** These are stories, not diagnoses. They cast power to the past, which you can't change. The reframe: "Under these specific conditions, I behave in a way that's not conducive to my goals. What skill do I need?" That's solvable.

5. **Treating understanding as mastery (declarative vs. procedural confusion).** Watching ten videos on cold calling is declarative knowledge — you know it exists. You don't know how to do it. There is no substitute for the first hundred reps.

6. **Quitting at the valley of despair.** The valley is not evidence it's broken — it's the price of entry. Most people exit at despair and restart at optimism with something new, forever cycling without upside.

7. **Chasing views as your primary content metric.** Views don't pay payroll. Views with the wrong audience actively mislead your content strategy by making bad content feel like it's working.

8. **Scaling a leaky bucket.** If churn is high and you're spending more on ads to compensate, you're running harder to stay in place. Fix the retention problem first. Then scale.

9. **Spreading resources across multiple priorities simultaneously.** "These are our objectives — also keep doing everything else" is not strategy. It's abdication. Discretionary effort must flow to one thing.

10. **Changing price to close a deal.** Once you do it, every price you quote is negotiable forever. Change terms or components if you must — never discount unprompted.

11. **Adding features to solve retention problems.** The number one reason customers cancel across industries is overwhelm. Before adding anything, ask: what are people not consuming, and why? The right move is almost always deletion or simplification, not addition.

12. **Raising capital before validating demand with pre-sales.** Pre-sales confirm people want it badly enough to pay and wait — that's a more honest signal than any investor check. If nobody buys the pre-sale, thank god you didn't build it.

13. **Opening a second location before the first runs without you.** The business must maintain or grow for six consecutive months without your direct involvement before you expand. If it craters when you leave, you haven't built a business — you've built a job.

14. **Suppressing rising community contributors.** The natural instinct is to feel threatened when a member gains status. Resist it. Their fame is your retention — the more community leaders you elevate, the more member-to-member edges exist, and the stickier the network.

15. **Networking instead of working — especially early.** Networking is exploration mode behavior. In exploitation mode, every hour at a lunch is an hour not building. When the work works, every door will open. But if the work never gets done, no network will save it.

16. **Buying status before buying leverage.** Watches, cars, and leased Ferraris impress people below you — the exact people whose opinions cost you nothing. Every dollar into status is a dollar taken from compounding.

17. **Serving anyone with a credit card.** If your qualifying criteria is "has money," you'll have diluted marketing, overwhelmed sales, and inconsistent delivery. You are not a real business — you're a freelancer with employees.

18. **Being the answer to everything.** If your business requires your involvement to function, you don't have a business — you have a job that employs other people. The size of your business is capped at the size of your attention.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. The Math-Out-Loud Move**
Hormozi rarely states a conclusion — he walks through arithmetic in real time. "Let's say $77, divide by 33% churn, that's LTV of $231. At $137 with 50% churn, that's $274. So the question becomes: does the lower price convert more than 18% better? Do the math. Pick the price that wins." Confidence comes from the numbers, not the assertion. Use whenever a decision feels emotional — convert it to pure arithmetic and remove the emotion.

**2. Short Declarative Stacks**
Builds arguments as tight, declarative sentences delivered in threes or fours before expanding. "Find something that works. Do as much of it as you can. Eliminate everything else. That includes your personal life." No hedging, no softening, no caveats. State the punchline first; the argument follows.

**3. The "You're Right. So What?" Pivot**
Acknowledge the person's complaint or objection fully — don't fight it — then strip away its power by asking what it actually produces. "You're right. You'll never make this work. Feel better? Now what?" Agree to the conclusion, expose the uselessness of holding it. Reverse-objection pattern.

**4. First-Person Failure First**
Open any prescriptive claim with a personal failure or embarrassing story before giving the advice. "I let a $46M sale almost not happen because I was worried about impressing a single colleague." The vulnerability earns the credibility that makes the prescription land. Never just vulnerability, never just data — vulnerability + data + adjustment.

**5. The Reframe Ladder**
When a person holds a limiting belief, don't argue directly. Run them up a ladder: accept the premise → take it to its logical extreme → show the extreme is unacceptable → let them discover the exit themselves. "Great — so you're right, your mom ruined your love life. Forever. That's what you want? Now she controls every relationship you'll ever have."

**6. Specificity as Authority**
Generic advice gets no airtime. Every claim is attached to a number, a story, or a named example. "The average gym going through GymLaunch makes over $20,000 in additional new revenue in their first 30 days — average. We track it." Specificity signals you were actually there. Abstractions get replaced with exact dollar amounts, timelines, emotions.

**7. Acknowledge → Associate → Ask**
When any objection or question lands: (1) Repeat it back to buy time and show you listened. (2) Associate it with something positive — "totally understandable" or tie it to a success story. (3) Ask your next question. Never rebut directly. Stay in the sale without breaking rapport. This structure is the engine of the entire close.

**8. The "So the Question Becomes" Bridge**
After presenting a framework or data, consistently bridges to the decision with this phrase: "So the question becomes — which of these two EPC numbers is higher?" Trains listeners to think in decision terms, not just information terms.

**9. The Inversion Move**
State the common belief, then invert it with the more useful frame: "I didn't start caring about the first sale — I care about the 10th sale." "You can't actually make something good; you remove what's bad." Sets up the naive assumption, then shows why the math says the opposite.

**10. Triple Emphasis with No Hedging**
Closes arguments by repeating the core claim three times with escalating finality: "The leader is always the problem. Not sometimes. The leader is always the problem. Period. Full stop. No questions." No caveats after the period. Signal that the diagnosis is final.

**11. Numbered Structure, Telegraphed in Advance**
"I'll give you three things. Number one... number two... number three." Announced, then executed exactly. Creates anticipation and makes content scannable even in speech. The listener stays because they're counting to a known endpoint.

**12. Binary Compression of Complex Decisions**
Collapses multi-variable problems into two paths, one obviously worse: "You either do it now when there's low stakes or you do it at the end when you both have high stakes. I promise you it's much better when there's low stakes." Every dilemma gets stated as pain today vs. pain forever. Removes false neutrality and forces a decision.
