## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Value Equation / Deprivation Timing**
Sell at the moment of maximum deprivation, not maximum satisfaction. The steak analogy: you sell the second steak before the first bite, not after the meal. Identify the moment your prospect feels the gap most acutely, then make the offer — don't wait until they've partially solved the problem themselves.

**Declarative vs. Procedural Knowledge**
Declarative = knowledge *about* something (use this to sell). Procedural = knowledge *of how* to do something (this is what you sell). Teach the "what is possible" and "why it works" in free content; reserve step-by-step implementation for paid product. People who get a to-do list walk away satisfied without buying.

**The SPCL Influence Stack (Status, Power, Credibility, Likeness)**
Four independent levers that compound influence. Status = the reinforcers you control. Power = say-do correspondence (give direction → good result follows). Credibility = third-party proof. Likeness = audience similarity to you. Having all four makes compliance near-automatic; if you can only have one, optimize for Power — consistently giving advice that works.

**The Management Diamond**
When someone isn't doing what you want, run through five root causes in order: (1) They didn't know you wanted it. (2) They don't know how. (3) They don't know the deadline. (4) Something is blocking them. (5) They don't want to. Entrepreneurs default to #5 first — it's almost always one of the first four.

**The Idea Parking System**
Capture every idea fully (full memo, financials, how it works) in a dedicated document, then return to it only when the team has slack capacity — when they "have smiles on their faces." Ideas that survive weeks of distance and still gnaw at you are worth revisiting. Ideas you forget were never that good.

**Scale Zero**
The goal: build a company that runs without your direct contribution. Accept a temporary margin reduction (e.g., 50% → 20%) to eliminate keyman dependency. Once you're out of the operating loop, the business can grow past the ceiling your personal bandwidth imposed.

**The Branding Loop**
Brand = association between an unknown thing and a known positive thing, built through repeated reinforced outcomes. Loop: you make a promise → customer acts → good thing happens → they comply again. Run this at scale and you can sell with no explicit promise: "Everything else I've made has been good — trust me."

**The Unicorn Decomposition**
Stop searching for one person who can do everything you do. Break your role into component parts (the horn, the body, the magic), then hire specialists for each. The approximation of a unicorn built from specialists will always be more scalable than waiting for a real one.

**The Rule of 100**
Commit to 100 outreach actions per day for 100 straight days without measuring ROI on individual attempts. Volume and time horizon together defeat frustration tolerance. Most people report dramatic business growth well before day 100 if they actually commit.

**Culture as Conditional Reinforcement Rules**
Culture = the spoken and unspoken rules governing reinforcement in an organization. A rule is an if-then statement: if you do X, you are rewarded/punished/ignored. Codify all ~200 behavioral rules, then chunk them into 3 values. Transpose culture by moving the biggest culture champion to the new location — not by declaring values on a slide.

**The Reverse Obituary**
Write your own eulogy first. Eulogies are 500–1,000 words and focus exclusively on service and character. Every goal you're chasing that wouldn't appear in those 1,000 words deserves scrutiny. Use it as a prioritization razor for every major decision.

**Pattern Recognition as the Only Real Compounding Asset**
The reason second businesses scale faster isn't changed physics — it's accumulated pattern recognition. Every bad hire, every failed sales rep, every wrong VP builds the internal template that lets you pattern-match instantly next time. You cannot buy this; you can only earn it through volume of repetition.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening a sales call or piece of content | **Script:** "My name is Alex Hormozi. I have a portfolio of companies that last year did over $250 million. The reason I make these videos is so I can help you [specific outcome] so that you can [specific result]."

**Situation:** Agitating pain in a pitch | **Script:** "Let me show you the gap between where you are right now and where you could be — and let's make that gap feel as big as it actually is."

**Situation:** Selling at point of deprivation (restaurant framing) | **Script:** "Guy like you? You're going to need two steaks." — Offer the upsell before the first bite, when hunger is highest.

**Situation:** Justifying premium pricing | **Script:** "There is an audience of people who would pay you ten times more for the exact same work — they just don't know you exist."

**Situation:** Prospect objects that they can get information for free | **Script:** "Ninety-nine percent of people are never going to buy from you, but they will judge you based on the quality of your free content. Give away the information; sell the implementation."

**Situation:** Teaching vs. pitching balance | **Script:** "I'm not going to teach you how to set up the domain. I'm going to show you that going from non-personalized to personalized gets this result. [Show the delta.] And then I'll sell you how to do it."

**Situation:** Demonstrating value of a crossing a revenue threshold | **Script:** "If you go from two million to five million, you cross the institutional threshold. Your multiple goes from three to seven — that's the difference between a $4M exit and a $35M exit, just by adding $3M in revenue."

**Situation:** Onboarding a salesperson to script discipline | **Script:** "Thousands of sales calls and thousands of hours went into this script. Men died and gave their lives to build it. You are not better than the script. You say the words because we know these words close people. Period."

**Situation:** Script memorization training method | **Script:** "Print the script. Read it aloud. Black out one word. Read it again. Black out another. By the time every word is blacked out, you've said the script as many times as there are words in it — and you'll know it cold."

**Situation:** Real-time sales roleplay correction | **Script:** "Stop. Try it like this. [Correct delivery.] Do it again. [They do it.] Lock it in. Do it again. Again. Keep going."

**Situation:** Explaining why you're not disciplined | **Script:** "You're not disciplined. You're consistent with things you like. Discipline is doing the things you hate, repeatedly, with no immediate reward, for an extended period of time."

**Situation:** Breaking a prospect's belief about their current model | **Script:** "Here's the old model. Here's what's actually happening in the market now. I'm not telling you how to implement the new model — I'm telling you it exists and what the outcome difference is."

**Situation:** Framing the difficulty of entrepreneurship | **Script:** "Entrepreneurship is someone saying 'run' and telling you they'll let you know when to stop. That's what makes it painful. You don't know when uncertainty ends — and most people won't start a race with no finish line."

**Situation:** Hard conversation with an underperforming employee | **Script:** Run the Management Diamond aloud with them: "Did you know I wanted this done? Did you know how to do it? Did you know when? Was something blocking you?" Exhaust all four before concluding it's a will problem.

**Situation:** Justifying hiring when margins will drop | **Script:** "If I go from $400K profit to $340K by hiring a customer service person, I get 80% of my time back. That 80% is now pointed at the next growth lever. The margin hit today is the investment in scale tomorrow."

**Situation:** Introducing free resource / lead magnet | **Script:** "Before you talk to my team, download the free $100M scaling roadmap — plug in your numbers, find out exactly where you are in the scaling process, and get the specific things that move you to the next level."

**Situation:** Deflecting fear of giving away secrets in content | **Script:** "Give away all the secrets. Sell the implementation. Those are the two things. Secrets are free. Implementation is what people pay for."

**Situation:** Qualifying whether an idea is worth pursuing | **Script:** "If I forget the idea, it wasn't that good. The ones that keep me up at night a week or two later — those are the ones worth fleshing out further."

**Situation:** Explaining why to go up-market | **Script:** "The bottom of the market gives you customers who are spending a huge percentage of their life savings on your service. They will expect you to promise everything and you'll never be able to deliver enough. Go up-market — same work, ten times the pay, ten times the margin, ten times the morale."

**Situation:** Framing volume commitment to a new salesperson | **Script:** "Do ten outreaches. Nothing happens. Do twenty. Nothing happens. Do thirty. Keep going. Do a hundred a day for a hundred days straight — I promise your business will change, usually well before day one hundred."

## DECISION RULES

**When you want to add a new initiative:** Wait until your team has visible slack — smiles, rested eyes, a glint. If they look wrung out, the business cannot absorb more scope.

**When pricing a service:** Go extremely expensive to a select few, or extremely cheap to everyone. The middle kills margins and morale. Services requiring human delivery should default to high-ticket.

**When a salesperson is outperforming but ignoring the script:** Enforce the script over individual performance. A cowboy closer is a culture cancer. Process discipline compounds; individual heroics don't.

**When identifying your business constraint:** Ask "what is the one thing, if fixed, would make everything else easier or irrelevant?" Then bomb that constraint — books, videos, hires, coaching — violently and simultaneously until it is no longer the constraint.

**When an employee isn't performing:** Run the Management Diamond in order (know, how, when, blocked, won't) before concluding it's a motivation problem. Most underperformance is a clarity or systems failure, not a will failure.

**When evaluating whether to pursue an idea:** Write the full memo. Wait. If it still gnaws at you after a week or two and the team has capacity, revisit it. If you forgot it, it wasn't worth pursuing.

**When scaling a team with one star:** Break what the star does into documented, trainable steps first. Then train the whole team to execute those steps with high adherence. A team of eights on a great process beats one star with no process every time.

**When you're the bottleneck in your own business:** Do a time study. List every activity. Give it to GPT and ask for the minimum roles required to replace all of it. Accept the margin reduction required to remove yourself from operations.

**When building content to sell:** Lead with declarative knowledge (outcomes, what is possible, how the system works conceptually). Save procedural knowledge (step-by-step how-tos) for paid product. If they can implement it from your free content, they won't buy.

**When you face an extended period of uncertainty:** Recognize that figuring things out is by nature a "wandering activity." If you knew the answer, you wouldn't need to figure it out. Frustration tolerance is the skill to train — not the knowledge gap to fill.

**When deciding time horizon:** Solve the next level first. Go from $100K to $1M before thinking about $100M. Resources and skills gained at each level change what the next level looks like — you can't see the full board from where you're standing.

**When transposing culture to a new location:** Move your biggest culture champion first. If culture can't sustain itself after that person leaves the origin location, the culture wasn't codified well enough — go back and write down all the rules.

**When someone says they can do it better than a hire:** The model doesn't scale if it requires someone as good as you to run it. Half as good, operating a system you designed, is the goal — accept the quality tradeoff to buy your time back.

**When AI is part of your strategy:** Treat it as a better tool in the same game humans have always played. Until it's man vs. machine and machine wins, your job is to be the person with the best tools — not to panic or ignore the leverage differential.

**When a priority feels urgent but isn't in your constraint:** Ask what it costs to delegate or eliminate it. If it's less than the value of your time redirected toward the actual constraint, pay for it immediately.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Consistency only with enjoyable tasks.** Calling it discipline when you're consistent with things you like. Real discipline is measurable specifically in the activities you avoid — lead follow-up, outreach, hard conversations.

**Selling at the wrong time.** Pitching after the customer's need has been partially satisfied. The steak is the same; the timing kills the close. Always identify the point of maximum deprivation first.

**Teaching procedural knowledge in free content.** When free content gives people a full to-do list they can act on, they don't need to buy. Free content must create the desire to implement, not the ability.

**One star suppressing the whole team.** A top-performing salesperson who ignores the script creates inconsistency, blocks scale, and poisons culture. The star's short-term revenue is not worth the long-term ceiling they impose.

**Serving the bottom of the market without infinite scale.** Undercapitalized businesses trying to sell cheap to the masses will face customers who treat small prices like life savings, demand infinite guarantees, and drain team morale. Not a viable model without massive capital.

**The idea fire hose.** Entrepreneurs have enough new ideas to kill their businesses. Acting on ideas before the business has capacity is a faster way to fail than having no ideas. Ideas are not the scarce resource — execution bandwidth is.

**Shotgun approach to learning.** Reading one book or watching one video on a constraint and calling it solved. The correct move: read many books, consume all video, hire a practitioner, and work the constraint simultaneously from every angle until it is no longer the constraint.

**Skipping the script memorization phase.** Starting roleplay training before the rep can "breathe the script" means the feedback is going into someone who doesn't have the words internalized. Memorization before roleplay — non-negotiable.

**Waiting for perfect talent before building.** Talent is attracted by proof, not potential. Early-stage companies earn A-players by demonstrating what they've already built. Build the proof first; the talent follows.

**Conflating testimonials with product quality.** Selling to 1,000 people and cherry-picking 10 successes as proof, while ignoring the 990 failures, is the internet marketer doom loop. Brand is only built when average results are good — not when cherry-picked results are great.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The pattern interrupt reframe.** State the common belief, then immediately dismantle it with a concrete example. "You're not disciplined — you only do what you like. That's not discipline, that's preference." No softening; direct confrontation of the false frame.

**Escalating specificity.** Start with a broad claim, then drill to an extremely granular example that makes the principle visceral. "If you go from two million to five million, you cross the institutional threshold. Your multiple goes from three to seven. That's not $4M — that's $35M." Numbers create credibility and lock the concept in.

**The rhetorical mock dialogue.** Plays both sides of a conversation to illustrate a dynamic: "'Was the steak good?' 'Yeah.' 'Want another?' 'No.' 'But you liked it?' 'I loved it.' 'Then why won't you buy another?' 'Because I'm full.'" Lets the audience feel the irony rather than being told it.

**Volume stacking.** When emphasizing commitment, lists escalating numbers with no pause: "You do ten, then twenty, then thirty, then fifty, then a hundred." The accumulation itself performs the point — volume feels real by being demonstrated rhetorically.

**Self-inclusion as credibility.** Frequently uses himself as the live case study: "Right now, 80% of my time is heavy recruiting. Yesterday I had four executive meetings. That's what my constraint is." Not abstract advice — lived behavior demonstrated in real-time.

**The "most people" contrast.** Establishes the modal failure pattern, then positions the correct path as the exception. "Most sales teams have a loose script. No one pays attention. They hire a bunch, see who sticks. Exceptional orgs take anyone and make them a savage." Creates clear in-group/out-group without being preachy.

**Death-and-clarity close.** Ends heavy arguments with a forcing function: "Death creates clarity. Eulogies are 500 to 1,000 words. No one mentions your revenue. Service and character. Everything else is noise." Uses mortality not for shock, but as the ultimate prioritization filter — applied to business decisions as much as life ones.
