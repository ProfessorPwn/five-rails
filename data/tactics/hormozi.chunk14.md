## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Four Brand Benefits**
Brand isn't logos and color schemes — it's how many people trust you. Trust translates to four compounding economic levers: higher conversion rates (more people buy when shown the offer), higher CTR throughout the funnel, premium price tolerance (customers pay down the risk of buying something inferior), and repeat purchases. Apply it by asking: "Which of these four am I optimizing for this quarter?" Most businesses only focus on conversion; the compounding is in repurchases.

**2. The Give-to-Ask Ratio**
Every interaction either deposits or withdraws from the trust account. Free content, overd-delivered products, and kept promises are deposits. Asks for money are withdrawals — unless the product is so good the "ask" becomes another deposit. Apply it by engineering your product to turn the moment of purchase into a reinforcement event, not a transaction. The goal is for customers to think "I can't believe how much I got for this."

**3. The Inputs/Outputs Money-Making System**
Every business has a box: activities go in, customers and LTV come out. Most owners fill their calendar with activities that feel productive but aren't connected to either getting more customers or making existing customers worth more. Map every activity to one of those two outputs weekly. If an activity doesn't trace to one, cut it. The discipline is describing inputs as actual physical actions — not "analyze," but "look at rows in a spreadsheet and flag anything 20% above benchmark."

**4. The Three Employee Diagnostic Questions**
When you don't know if someone is valuable, ask three questions in sequence: (1) "What do you do?" — force them past amorphous words to actual physical activities. (2) "What do we do as a business?" — if they can't explain it, they can't connect to it. (3) "How does what you do help what we do?" — this is the only question that matters. Apply it in your next 1:1s to locate where your payroll is actually driving the machine.

**5. The Four Levels of Lead Getters**
There are four leverage tiers for customer acquisition: (1) You alone — capped by hours. (2) Employees/agencies — centralized, scalable but requires management. (3) Affiliates (other businesses) — decentralized, compound passively. (4) Customers themselves — the highest leverage because they advertise for you without being managed. Apply by identifying what tier you're operating at and designing the next tier before you hit the ceiling of the current one.

**6. The Centralized vs. Decentralized Acquisition Distinction**
Employees and agencies are centralized lead getters — you have to scale the relationships. Affiliates and customers-as-advocates are decentralized — they continue operating beyond your ability to manage them. For any consumer business that wants to get very large, you must eventually build decentralized acquisition. Apply it by designing your product and incentive system so customers refer without being asked.

**7. The Product-Incentive Seesaw**
Product quality and incentive strength are a seesaw: a 10/10 product needs almost no incentive (people refer because they want the social credit), while a weak product requires a disproportionately large incentive to generate the same referral rate. The most efficient move is always improving the product first. Apply it by asking: "Would my best customers refer us unprompted if there were zero incentive?" If the answer is no, you have a product problem, not an incentive problem.

**8. The Three Scaling Options for Any Input**
When you've identified an input that drives the business, you have exactly three ways to scale it: (1) Automate — get a machine or software to do it. (2) Delegate — get a person to do it. (3) One-to-Many — shift from 1:1 delivery to 1:N delivery (content, webinars, communities). Apply it sequentially: first ask if the input can go one-to-many, then whether it can be delegated, then whether it can be automated.

**9. The Data Discipline Test**
Track only what changes behavior. For every metric someone proposes tracking, run it through: "If this number goes up, does it change what we do? If it goes down, does it change what we do?" If neither answer is yes, delete the metric. Apply this in any team meeting where someone is presenting dashboards — run every row through the test out loud.

**10. The Brand-as-Compounding-Word-of-Mouth Model**
Media and content are measurement tools for word of mouth, not the thing itself. Brand grows because someone has a positive experience, tells someone, that person experiences it, tells someone else. Content accelerates this by letting one person give fractionally to a million people simultaneously. Apply it by designing each content piece as a "fractional give" — asking: "Does someone who watches this leave with something genuinely useful?" If not, it's noise.

**11. The Reinvestment Imperative**
When you find a referral or affiliate system that works, the mistake is treating it as set-and-forget. The right move is to immediately double down: lower activation energy, remove friction steps, give better training, provide more assets. The system is already working, which means marginal reinvestment here has higher ROI than investing in anything else. Apply it by scheduling a quarterly review: "What is currently working, and what is the most direct investment we can make to make it work faster?"

**12. The Charity vs. Business Distinction**
Charities are unsustainable because they can't feed themselves — they require constant extraction from one side to give to another. A business that does good generates a profit because it creates value for all stakeholders: investors, employees, customers, environment. The cycle is self-sustaining. Apply this framing when you're tempted to undercharge or give away too much — sustainable generosity requires a functioning economic engine underneath it.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Diagnosing whether an employee is actually driving business outcomes | **Script:** "What do you do? — No, not 'analyze' — what does that actually mean, physically? Okay. Now, what do we do as a business? And how does what you do help what we do?"

**Situation:** Challenging a recurring meeting that shares data | **Script:** "What decision will we make differently after seeing this data? If nothing changes based on this number, we don't need to track it, and we don't need to meet about it."

**Situation:** Explaining why brand investment is worth it to a skeptical operator | **Script:** "Brand is just conversion rate, click-through rate, price tolerance, and repeat purchases — compounded. Every one of those either compounds into lower cost-per-customer or higher lifetime value. It's not soft, it's the math."

**Situation:** Evaluating a proposed project or initiative | **Script:** "How does this trace back to more customers or higher LTV? Walk me through the chain. If there are more than three hops, we're probably not doing this."

**Situation:** Someone wants to improve their referral rate | **Script:** "Would your best customers refer you right now, unprompted, if there were zero incentive? If not, you don't have an incentive problem — you have a product problem. Fix the sandwich first."

**Situation:** Someone proposes a new metric to track | **Script:** "If this number goes up, what do we do differently? If it goes down, what do we do differently? If the answer to both is nothing, we don't need to track it."

**Situation:** Pitching why you do free content | **Script:** "The give-to-ask ratio is a history of reinforcement. Every piece of free content is a deposit. When I eventually ask for something, they're not buying — they're returning a favor. That's a different psychology entirely."

**Situation:** Explaining pricing power to someone afraid to raise prices | **Script:** "When someone pays a premium for a known brand over an unknown, they're not paying extra — they're paying down the risk of buying something inferior. You earn the right to charge more by being predictable."

**Situation:** Convincing someone to stop micromanaging their affiliate program | **Script:** "The whole point of affiliates is that they operate independently. If you have to prod and poke them to keep going, you have either a product problem or an incentive problem — not a management problem."

**Situation:** Someone is drowning in tasks and asks what to prioritize | **Script:** "Clear the plate. List everything you're doing weekly. For each item: does this get more customers or make existing customers worth more? Everything that doesn't trace to one of those two — cut it or delegate it."

**Situation:** Explaining why mediocre products + great marketing is a trap | **Script:** "Marketing just accelerates whatever you're doing. You can absolutely tell a million people very efficiently that your product is mediocre — you'll make money in the short run and destroy your acquisition economics in the long run."

**Situation:** Framing the goal of word-of-mouth systems | **Script:** "I want people who are already about to refer all their friends and then ask me 'is there an incentive for this?' That's lubrication. Trying to use incentives to create referrals from zero is buying behavior you didn't earn."

**Situation:** Explaining why you fire bad customers vs. keep them | **Script:** "You can trust someone to do a bad job just as reliably as you can trust them to do a good job. A negative brand is predictive power working against you — those customers cost you more than they pay you."

**Situation:** Someone asks why you focus on existing customers instead of new ones | **Script:** "It cost me the same to acquire you once. After that, every repurchase is pure leverage. I'd rather 10x the LTV of the customers I have than find 10x the number of customers."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether to build an employee team vs. affiliate network | **Script:** "Employees and agencies are centralized — you scale the relationship. Affiliates and customers are decentralized — they compound beyond your ability to manage them. If you want to get very big, you need the decentralized layer."

**Situation:** Someone is stuck doing every sale themselves | **Script:** "The day my sales rep went two-for-four while I was out of the office was the day I realized this could be an asset, not a job. You need to find your two-for-four moment — and then rebuild everything around never being the bottleneck again."

**Situation:** Justifying heavy content output | **Script:** "One person can give to a million people at once in a fractionalized way. If I give ten fractional gives to a million people, I've effectively given a full give to enough of them that they're willing to return the favor."

**Situation:** Someone says their referral rate is low and wants better incentives | **Script:** "How good is your sandwich? Because if it's soggy, I don't care how much you offer me — I'm not going to recommend you to my friends and take the social hit. The incentive is not the constraint."

**Situation:** Someone asks what activities to scale first | **Script:** "Before you scale anything, figure out if it can go one-to-many. If it can, do that first — it's free leverage. If it can't, delegate it. If it can't be delegated, automate it. Only do it yourself if none of those are possible yet."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether a new hire is worth keeping | **Script:** "Can they tell me what we do? Can they tell me how their work connects to us getting more customers or making customers worth more? If they can't articulate the chain, they're probably not in it."

## DECISION RULES

**When** a metric doesn't change your behavior whether it goes up or down, **stop tracking it** — data collection without behavioral output is cost with no return.

**When** you're overwhelmed, **clear your plate and audit every weekly activity** against one question: does this trace to more customers or higher LTV? If not, cut or delegate before adding anything new.

**When** a referral rate is low, **fix the product before improving the incentive** — incentives can only amplify what's already happening, not create it from zero.

**When** you find an acquisition system that's working (especially referrals/affiliates), **immediately reinvest in it** rather than treating it as steady-state — marginal returns there are higher than anywhere else.

**When** you're deciding how to scale an input, **try one-to-many first, then delegation, then automation** — in that order, because one-to-many is leverage without headcount.

**When** evaluating a meeting, **calculate the hourly cost of all attendees** (annual salary ÷ 2,000 = hourly rate) and ask whether the meeting will generate 4-5x that cost in changed behavior or decisions.

**When** building a referral or affiliate program, **design for people who already want to refer** — the incentive should lower activation energy from 1 to n, not attempt to create motivation from zero.

**When** you've validated your inputs, **commit to those activities exclusively** and ruthlessly remove everything that doesn't connect — being deadline-focused reveals what actually matters.

**When** you're deciding whether to charge more, **ask if customers would be upset at a 10% price increase** — if yes, you have a brand or product problem that more marketing won't fix.

**When** scaling a consumer business beyond a certain size, **build decentralized acquisition** (affiliates, customers-as-advocates) — centralized lead getters (employees, agencies) hit a management ceiling.

**When** an employee can't explain how their work connects to business outcomes, **use the three questions** (what do you do, what do we do, how does your work help ours) before deciding whether to retrain or replace.

**When** considering a new project or initiative, **first ask how many hops it takes** to connect to revenue — if it's more than three steps removed from a customer outcome, deprioritize it.

**When** a business is growing, **protect the activities that create it** — most founders stop doing the inputs that caused growth because they're now "too busy" managing the growth they created.

**When** you've made money, **don't stop — reframe hard work as the goal, not the means** — the point was never the money, it was having a vehicle for the thing you'd do anyway.

**When** building brand, **measure it in conversion rate, CTR, price tolerance, and repurchase rate** — if none of those four numbers are improving, the brand activity isn't working regardless of how it feels.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Doing arts and crafts and calling it business.** Reorganizing your project management tool, redesigning your website, cleaning up your CRM — these feel productive because they're business-adjacent. They are not inputs. Unless you can trace them directly to more customers or higher LTV, they are expensive hobbies.

**Meetings that share data without changing decisions.** If the data being reviewed in a recurring meeting has never caused anyone to do anything differently, the meeting — and the data collection that feeds it — should be cancelled. You're paying for theater.

**Using incentives as a substitute for product quality.** If you're obsessing over your affiliate commission structure while your NPS is low, you have the causality backwards. No incentive system survives a bad product at scale.

**Thinking in one-to-one acquisition indefinitely.** Cold outreach, one-on-one sales, manual referral asks — these are valid early mechanisms, not long-term growth strategies. If you've been doing them for two years and haven't built a one-to-many or decentralized layer, you're capped.

**Tracking metrics that don't change behavior.** Vanity metrics (tags, likes, impressions, engagement rate) are only useful if you can show a chain: metric → action → revenue. If you can't show the chain, you're data-distracted, not data-driven.

**Treating referral and affiliate programs as set-and-forget.** Finding a channel that works and not reinvesting in it is one of the most common and expensive mistakes. The right move is immediate, aggressive reinvestment in what's already converting.

**Building centralized acquisition only.** Employees and agencies require you to scale the management relationship. They're necessary but not sufficient. If 100% of your acquisition is centralized, you're one key-person departure away from a crisis.

**Confusing media growth with business growth.** Views, followers, and engagement are proxies for trust and word-of-mouth — they're not the thing itself. Someone with massive engagement and no revenue has the measurement tool without the underlying asset.

**Scaling marketing before fixing product.** Fast feedback loops from early marketing success can train you to keep pouring into acquisition while ignoring retention signals. Marketing accelerates whatever you're doing — including telling the market your product is mediocre, just faster.

**Asking "how do I get crazier incentives?" before "how do I make this better?"** Every hour spent engineering incentive structures for a weak product is an hour not spent on the thing that would actually fix the economics permanently.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The trace-to-revenue discipline.** Every claim, every activity, every metric gets stress-tested with a chain-of-logic back to "more customers" or "higher LTV." Anything that can't survive the chain gets cut. This creates a rhythm of: state the activity → trace the chain → either validate or eliminate.

**The analogy-then-mechanism pattern.** Introduce a concept with a vivid, grounded analogy (bulls being branded, sandwiches, pushing a boulder) and then immediately translate it into the underlying mechanism. Never leave an analogy hanging without the "and here's why this matters for your business" pivot.

**Binary frames for clarity, nuance for precision.** Lead with a clean binary ("are you data-driven or data-distracted?") to provoke attention, then add the nuance ("it's not that data is bad, it's that data that doesn't change behavior is cost with no return"). The binary creates the hook; the nuance creates the trust.

**The compounding sequence.** Arguments are structured as: small thing → repeated → compounds into large thing. This is applied to brand (each positive experience compounds), referrals (one super-referrer 10x's a cohort), content (fractional gives accumulate into full gives). Always show the compounding mechanism explicitly.

**Rhetorical escalation via real numbers.** Ground abstractions immediately in specific figures: 30% Amazon page conversion, 55% of School customers from affiliates, $46.2M exit, 26,000 five-star reviews. Numbers aren't decoration — they're proof that the framework is load-bearing, not theoretical.

**The "but here's what people miss" pivot.** After stating the common understanding of a concept, pause and redirect: "but here's what most people miss" or "this is the part people don't realize." This signals that the real insight is about to land and frames the speaker as ahead of the conventional wisdom.

**Self-referential credibility without bragging.** Establish authority by recounting specific moments of discovery ("the first time I saw the power of brand was when...") rather than claims of status ("I know more than most"). The story does the credibility work; the lesson does the value work.
