## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**BAM FAM (Book a Meeting from a Meeting)**
Every appointment that requires a return visit is an opportunity to book the next one *before the customer leaves*. Relying on them to self-schedule creates friction, drop-off, and extended repurchase cycles. Apply it: at the end of every service, or better yet at the *start* before they receive value and want to leave, say "You're going to need to come back in X weeks — let's just get that on the calendar now because I'm getting booked up quick."

**Front-End / Back-End Offer Architecture**
Promotional / intro offers (e.g., $99 promo) are *lead magnets*, not profit centers. They exist to acquire a buyer. The profit lives entirely in the upsell — the upgraded service, the membership, the prepayment. If you break even on the front end and nobody takes the back end, the business has no margin. Apply it: measure, train, and obsess over Step 1 → Step 2 conversion rate, not front-end close rate.

**Default Close (Opt-Out Framing)**
Countries with opt-out organ donation get 80%+ participation vs. 10% for opt-in. Structure every upsell so the desired outcome *appears to be what they're already doing*. Apply it: for membership upsells to existing repeat customers, frame it as "You've already been paying for this — you just haven't been getting the bonuses. We just need to formalize it."

**ICE Prioritization Framework**
Impact × Confidence × Ease. Score every initiative and execute highest-ICE items first. Maps directly to the Value Equation: Impact = Dream Outcome, Confidence = Perceived Likelihood of Achievement, Ease = Time + Effort. Apply it: when you have a list of 10 possible improvements, rank them on all three dimensions and attack the top three ruthlessly before touching the rest.

**The Four Components of Equity**
Equity has four elements: Risk, Control, Profit, and a future Sale event. Most operators who want "equity" in a location actually only want Profit — they don't want risk, they won't get control, and a sale is theoretical. Apply it: offer profit share bonuses (monthly cash out) rather than equity grants to managers and key operators. Preserves your ability to remove underperformers and avoids legal complexity.

**Supply / Demand Surge Pricing**
Price is the cross-point of supply and demand. When demand exceeds supply at specific times (peak days, peak hours), prices should rise. Apply it: implement weekend / peak-hour pricing at +10–20%, then offer non-surge pricing as a *membership benefit* — this simultaneously drives membership conversion and smooths the demand curve to fill off-peak slots.

**The $100M Scaling Roadmap**
Business growth has 10 discrete stages, each with its own constraints and graduation criteria. The mistake most operators make is applying Stage 8 solutions to a Stage 6 problem. Apply it: identify your stage first (acquisition.com/roadmap), then focus *only* on the constraints that block graduation to the next stage — everything else is distraction.

**Shotgun Offer for Partnership Exits**
When a partnership needs to dissolve, the fairest mechanism is: "I will buy you out at $X on these terms — but I'll also *accept* that same offer if you want to buy me out instead." The person making the offer sets the price and terms, and must be willing to be on either side. Apply it: use this when you want a clean, fair exit without a protracted negotiation, especially with friends.

**Career Path / Title Progression for Retention**
Retention is not about maximum pay — it's about visible progression with frequent milestones. Create 6+ title levels with corresponding pay bumps every 3 months. Each level unlocks both a pay increase *and* a new billable skill. Apply it: Junior Tech → Tech → Senior Tech → Junior Artist → Artist → Senior Artist, each with a compensation step and a new service they can charge more for.

**Nail It Then Scale It**
The infrastructure required to run one great location is the same infrastructure that enables scaling to 50. Shortcuts (equity partners, unvetted managers) that let you open faster destroy the foundation needed to scale later. Apply it: before opening the next location, ask "Can I replicate this without me being present?" If not, fix the model first.

**The Service Business Arbitrage**
Service businesses make money by acquiring low-skill labor, training it to a higher skill level on their system, and collecting the spread between what the market pays for that trained skill and what the employee agreed to on entry. Apply it: the key lever is training throughput — how fast can you take someone from 0 to billable, and how much of the spread do you capture?

**Prepayment as Retention Mechanism**
Customers who prepay for a membership churn at dramatically lower rates, pull cash forward for growth capital, and are effectively locked out of going to a competitor for the prepaid period. Apply it: after a customer says yes to a monthly membership, immediately ask "Want to save money?" then present 6-month and 12-month prepay as the obvious next step, not as an afterthought.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Customer finishes service and is about to leave without rebooking | **Script:** "Your lashes are going to be ready in about 3 weeks — I'm getting booked up pretty quick so let's just get that on the calendar right now while you're here. Does the Saturday in 3 weeks work, or the one after that?"

**Situation:** Customer says "I'll book later, I'm busy" | **Script:** "Totally — if you want, I'm here and you're here, let's just pull up our calendars and get it sorted right now so you don't have to worry about it later."

**Situation:** Customer doesn't have card handy at rebooking | **Script:** "Don't worry, we have the card on file — let me just pull it up. Does next Saturday work?"

**Situation:** Upselling existing repeat customer to membership | **Script:** "Hey, I was looking at your history — you've been in six times in the last six months. I realized you haven't been getting [the bonus benefits]. All you have to do is sign up as a member. You pay nothing more than you're currently paying, but you get all of these things you should already have been getting."

**Situation:** Closing membership at checkout — framing as default | **Script:** "You've already basically opted into this — we just need to formalize it so you start getting the benefits."

**Situation:** Offering prepayment upgrade after membership yes | **Script:** "Want to save some money? If you prepay today, you can save [dollar amount]. Would you prefer twelve months or six?"

**Situation:** Making prepayment ultra-compelling at checkout | **Script:** "Do you want today to be free? The discount you'd get prepaying the year basically covers today. We already have your next appointment on the books — just check the box and you're good."

**Situation:** Weekend/surge pricing objection from loyal customer | **Script:** "If you sign up for the membership, your pricing stays the same — no surge pricing ever. It's one of the main benefits of being a member."

**Situation:** Lead follow-up call within 1 minute of form submission | **Script:** "Hey, saw that you booked our $99 promo — what time do you want to come in? … Great, what card do you want to use?"

**Situation:** Lead asks why you need card upfront | **Script:** "It's how we've always done it. That's how we always do it."

**Situation:** Partnership conversation — wanting clean break | **Script:** "I'll buy you out at [X] on these terms — but I'll also accept that same offer if you want to buy me out. Either way I want to make sure this is fair for both of us."

**Situation:** Offering equity-swap exit on co-owned locations | **Script:** "You take that location outright, I take this one outright. No money changes hands, no taxes, clean break — and we can still talk business and share what's working."

**Situation:** Pitching career path to new hire | **Script:** "Put your two years in, you'll continue to make more and get fully trained up. Once I have a new location to open, the person who's performed best here gets first shot at running it — 10% profit share year one, 20% year two."

**Situation:** Framing a role-play training session | **Script:** "Don't just hand them a script and say say these words — it won't work. It's repetition. As soon as they mess up, say: 'Cool, say it like this.' Try again. 'Say it like this.' Try again. 'You did great, do it five more times.' Then start from the top."

**Situation:** Overcoming "let me book online later" | **Script:** "We have sixty minutes right now — let's just get one thing done, which is booking the next time you're in. Does [date] work?"

**Situation:** Check-in attribution capture at front desk | **Script:** "Can you sign in right here? And where'd you hear about us? Did anyone send you? Cool, let me grab who referred you."

**Situation:** Giving a partner a clean buyout while keeping the relationship | **Script:** "I'll give you a buyout over twelve to eighteen months so you have stability — and the one favor I'm asking in return is help me replace you. That's it."

**Situation:** Framing a price increase internally to staff | **Script:** "We're not raising prices — we're adding peak-hour pricing. Weekdays and off-peak stay the same. And members never pay peak pricing. So this is actually going to drive more people into memberships."

**Situation:** Manager isn't doing upsells | **Script:** "You already have a hundred percent close rate on people walking in the door — those are assumed closes, you're not going to mess those up. The real money is in the upsell. That's the business."

**Situation:** Structuring a bonus offer on a membership | **Script:** "Each individual bonus should feel like it alone is worth the full price of the membership. If someone would say 'I'd do it just for this one thing' — that's the right bonus. If they'd only maybe do it, it's not meaty enough."

## DECISION RULES

**When demand exceeds supply at specific time slots → implement surge pricing (+10–20%) for peak hours/days.** The cross-point of supply and demand *is* price. Don't leave that on the table.

**When you have a list of growth initiatives → score them on ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease) and execute highest-ICE first.** Strategy is prioritization of resources, not doing everything.

**When someone says yes to any recurring service → book the next appointment before they leave the building, ideally before they receive the current service.** Once value is delivered, they want out the door.

**When structuring a membership offer → add free bonuses instead of discounts whenever possible.** Your cost on tangible goods is near zero; a 10% discount costs real margin. A free facial costs almost nothing and is worth far more in perceived value.

**When offering a membership upsell to repeat customers → frame it as the default they've already opted into, not a new offer.** Opt-out framing gets 80%+ conversion; opt-in framing gets 20–40%.

**When someone agrees to a monthly membership → immediately ask if they want to prepay 6 or 12 months.** This is a separate sale. Don't conflate it with the membership close.

**When you need to dissolve a partnership → use a shotgun offer.** You set the price and terms but must be willing to be bought out at those same terms. Forces fairness.

**When two partners co-own multiple locations → explore equity swap before cash buyout.** Clean break, no taxes, no monthly payments stinging either party.

**When the working partner is doing unpaid management work → formalize it as a paid role at market rate.** If you can't backfill your own role with a hire at that salary, the model doesn't scale.

**When expanding to new locations → ask "Can this location run without me present?"** If no, the model isn't ready to scale. Fix the training and management system first.

**When staff churn is a problem → build a visible 6-level career path with 3-month milestones.** People don't leave money — they leave invisibility. Frequent small wins with titles and pay beats a single big raise.

**When tracking ad spend → require attribution at the point of service, not later.** A dropdown at sign-in, captured before they receive the service, is the only clean data source.

**When you're running above 50% on a key metric (e.g., upsell rate) → don't stop — get it to 70–80%.** The delta between 50% and 80% is not incremental; it's the difference between a profitable business and a transformational one.

**When a partnership problem exists alongside growth problems → solve the partnership first.** If 80% of mental bandwidth is on partnership friction, none of the downstream tactics will get executed.

**When making big capital bets on growth → collect clean data first.** Irreversible bets require reversible data-gathering infrastructure. Post-it notes and duct tape are fine early; clean attribution is required before scaling ad spend.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Taking on equity partners to solve a manpower or capital constraint.** This is the costliest shortcut in business. You're paying equity — permanent, compounding, relationship-laden equity — for what should have been a hired role or a loan. The role they fill will eventually need to be backfilled at market rate anyway, and now you've got a co-owner who may not share your vision.

**Rushing to open new locations before you've nailed the model.** The fastest way to five locations is not the fastest way to 50. Rushing creates hodgepodge deals, inconsistent execution, and a brand that can't scale because the foundation was never built.

**Running discount-only memberships with no added value.** A membership that's just "10% off" is not an offer — it's a price concession. It attracts price-sensitive customers, trains them to think about your price, and churns when a competitor goes cheaper. The membership needs to be so good they'd feel stupid saying no.

**Not tracking ad attribution at the point of service.** Spending even $1,000/month on ads without knowing which customers came from which channel is burning money. You cannot make intelligent scaling decisions without this data, and it takes almost no effort to capture.

**Giving equity instead of profit share to key operators.** Equity carries four components: Risk, Control, Profit, Sale. Most operators only want Profit. Giving full equity for a role they could be compensated for with a profit bonus creates future governance problems and removes your ability to course-correct.

**Leaving partnership disagreements in the gray.** "Whatever you say behind someone's back, just say it to each other." Every month a difficult partnership conversation is avoided, the stakes get higher and the resolution gets uglier. The problem doesn't disappear — it compounds.

**Treating rebooking as an administrative conversation instead of a sales conversation.** The scheduling conversation *is* a sales conversation with the stakes of your entire retention rate. If you treat it like paperwork, you get paperwork-level results.

**Measuring front-end close rate instead of Step 1 → Step 2 conversion.** If your intro offer has a near-100% close rate, that tells you nothing. Everyone who walks in the door already has their card down. The only number that matters for profitability is what percentage of those people take the upsell or the membership.

**Not paying yourself (the working owner) market rate for your operational role.** This artificially inflates the P&L and makes the business look more profitable than it is. When you try to hire someone to replace yourself, you'll find the location isn't actually viable. Build the true cost in from the start.

**Attempting to scale before resolving the supply constraint.** Running more ads into a business where technicians aren't properly trained, managed, or retained just accelerates churn and dilutes brand quality. Fix the supply side (career path, training, retention) before pouring more demand into the funnel.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Zoom out, then zoom in.** Hormozi consistently steps back to the macro principle ("the oldest law in business is supply and demand") before applying it to the specific situation ("she's running a business where she has temporal supply constraints"). This earns intellectual credibility before the tactical prescription lands.

**Personal story as shortcut to credibility.** "I've done this same thing. I had six locations, multiple partners, made the same mistake." He deploys his own failures preemptively, before the subject gets defensive, to collapse the distance between advice-giver and advice-receiver. Use first-person failure stories before delivering hard feedback.

**"And so..." as a transition into consequence.** Not "therefore" or "which means" — "and so." It's casual but maintains logical chain. Used to connect a principle to its business implication without sounding lecture-y.

**Three-option framing for hard decisions.** "Path A. Path B. Path C." When a situation has tension, list all exit options with neutral labels. Then give your actual recommendation *after* the options are laid out. The options make the recommendation feel like a selection, not a command.

**Math on the spot, narrated aloud.** "She's spending $12,000 on a $2.3 million business. If she spent 1% of revenue that'd be $23,000. So she's at half a percent." Walk the arithmetic live, in plain language, to show how small a change looks on the top line but massive it looks on the bottom line. Always bridge from revenue impact to margin impact.

**The rhetorical challenge.** "Do we think the future version of this business is her with 100 different partners? Probably not. And if that's not the future version, why start doing that today?" Forces the listener to answer their own question, so the conclusion feels self-derived, not imposed.

**Implied stakes via inversion.** "If she doesn't deal with this thing, it's just going to get bigger and nastier." Don't just sell the benefit of acting — describe the cost of *not* acting. The cost of inaction is almost always more visceral than the benefit of action.

**Label the sale even when it doesn't look like one.** "Getting a customer to come back is absolutely a sales conversation. Though it walks and talks like a scheduling conversation — it's not." Renaming a common activity as a sales moment changes how people prepare for it, how they practice it, and how seriously they take it.
