## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Value Equation / Value-Based Pricing**
Cost-plus pricing asks "what did it cost me?" Value-based pricing asks "what is someone willing to pay?" These are completely divorced concepts — your vendor raising prices is irrelevant to your sell price. Apply it by testing higher prices against actual conversion rates, not against your cost sheet.

**Price Elasticity Testing**
When you raise price, sales volume drops — but the question is *by how much*. If you double price and triple margins while only losing 25% of buyers, you win. Run split tests on highest-velocity SKUs, optimize for gross profit × units sold, not just units sold.

**The ICE Prioritization Stack (Impact, Confidence, Ease)**
For any list of tactics, rank each on how big the impact is, how likely it is to work, and how easy it is to execute. Start with the triple-high items first. This is how you sequence a growth plan — not by gut feel, not by what's exciting, by ICE score.

**LTV:CAC Ratio as a Dollar-In Machine**
If your LTV:CAC is 11:1 or higher, you've found a machine where putting in $1 returns $11. The only rational move is to put in as many dollars as possible. When someone has a 13:1 on an organic/SEO channel, asking "how do I max this out?" is more important than launching three new channels.

**The Reference Group Deprivation Theory**
Motivation = gap between where you are and where your reference group is. If you're the wealthiest person you compare yourself to, your drive to earn more is near zero — even if you're objectively poor. Raise your reference group ceiling to raise your motivation floor.

**Passive vs. Active Income Continuum**
Passive to you is always active to someone else. The myth of passive income keeps people poor because they spend 1,000 hours building a $100/month stream instead of taking those same hours to earn $10,000 active. Max out active earning first, then let excess flow passive.

**The Four Sources of Leverage (Money Types)**
Debt (costs you now and tomorrow), savings (drawing on past self), cash flow (drawing on present self), new opportunity (create new income to fund new goals). The fourth is elite-level thinking: "I'll write a new song to pay for the swimming pool." Don't rob past or future self — generate new flow instead.

**The "What Business Are You Really In?" Reframe**
You don't sell trophies. You sell recognition, status, and compensation. You don't sell software. You sell time reclaimed. Naming the vehicle you sell (trophies) instead of the outcome (recognition) causes you to misprice, mismarket, and missell. Reframe first, everything else downstream changes.

**Return Customer Economics (The Free Sale Test)**
Return customers cost zero to acquire. If your margin on free-acquisition customers is still terrible, you have either a fixed-cost problem or a pricing problem — nothing else. Diagnose which one before spending a dollar on new customer acquisition.

**The Skill-as-Asset Model**
A skill that earns you $1,000/month more permanently is worth more than any ETF at similar cost. Skills are untaxable, divorce-proof, government-proof, and compound faster than capital at early-stage wealth. Invest in SME 500 (yourself) before S&P 500.

**The Story Arbitrage (Rockefeller's Premium)**
The delta between what something is worth and what you pay for it can be justified entirely by the story it creates. Rockefeller overpaid for #1 market position and did 22 M&A deals in 60 days off the back of it. Always price in the story's downstream leverage, not just the asset's intrinsic value.

**Consolidate Before You Scale**
Every extra location, brand, or model is a tax on attention. Strategy means deciding what you *won't* do. An unprofitable retail location bleeding $50k/year isn't a real estate problem — it's a focus problem. Cut it first, then scale the surviving unit.

## DECISION RULES

1. **When margins are low and return customers are still unprofitable → diagnose pricing before advertising more.** Free acquisitions should be your most profitable sales. If they're not, you have a pricing or fixed-cost problem.

2. **When a channel has >10:1 LTV:CAC → max it out before diversifying.** Putting a dollar cap on a 13:1 return machine is irrational.

3. **When a location or division is barely breaking even and isn't the future → cut it.** The $50k in rent plus the attention tax is always worth more than the marginal revenue it generates.

4. **When you have two business models competing for your attention → pick one and cut the other off.** "Decide" means to kill an option. No-man's-land kills more businesses than wrong decisions.

5. **When you want to raise prices → raise them 5-6% per month and test, don't ask permission.** A 6% price increase on near-zero margins doubles profit. Entrepreneurs are more afraid of pricing changes than customers are.

6. **When evaluating a business to acquire → check for keyman risk and customer concentration first.** Either one alone is a yellow flag. Both together is a no.

7. **When ranking tactics → apply ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease) and start with triple-high items first.** Don't start with exciting or new. Start with certain and high-leverage.

8. **When you have a customer list you haven't emailed → email them with an offer immediately.** The acquisition cost is already sunk. Revenue from that list is pure profit.

9. **When creating content that converts → find the winning hook and reuse it repeatedly.** Hook-centric creation on short-form: once you have a winner, iterate on it, don't abandon it for novelty.

10. **When deciding how to fund a new opportunity → generate new income for it, don't draw on savings or debt.** "Write yourself a swimming pool" — create the income stream that funds the thing.

11. **When you have $1,000 and no clear path → buy a skill, not an asset.** Skills compound, are untaxable, and can't be taken from you. An S&P play on $1,000 is irrelevant; a skill that earns $1,000/month forever is transformational.

12. **When evaluating a person in your life → ask only: do they increase or decrease the likelihood I hit my goals?** Ignore intention. Track outcomes. If they decrease it, limit exposure regardless of history.

13. **When someone gives you their problems and calls it your fault → own it anyway.** It doesn't matter if it's your fault. It is your problem. Ownership is the only path to action.

14. **When deciding what to buy → ask if it buys time or stuff.** If it doesn't free up productive hours, strongly reconsider.

15. **When a business has pricing set by someone other than the market → treat it as a massive unlock.** Businesses with emotional or cost-plus pricing often have 25-50% price room they've left on the table. This is the lowest operational-drag profit move available.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Cost-plus pricing.** "Every business I know that does cost-plus doesn't make a lot of money." You're handing the customer your cost structure and asking them to negotiate. Price is about willingness to pay, full stop.

2. **Trying to serve two customer avatars simultaneously.** You can't win at B2B recurring and B2C viral at the same time. You dilute everything — media, messaging, offer, ops. Pick one and cut off the other.

3. **Adding locations/brands before mastering one.** Each additional entity fragments your attention. Attention ROI is the highest leverage in an early-stage business. More footprint ≠ more value.

4. **Obsessing over passive income before earning power is built.** This is how people spend 1,000 hours to make $100/month passive instead of $10,000 active. It's a category error — optimizing for the wrong vehicle entirely.

5. **Buying stuff that signals wealth before building wealth.** "Do you want to look rich or be rich?" Rich people are impressed by how rich you'll be in 20 years, not your Rolex today. Visible consumption while still building is a wealth leak.

6. **Waiting for perfect conditions to start.** Starting *is* the perfect condition. Waiting for readiness is a form of avoidance dressed as strategy.

7. **Blaming environment instead of owning outcome.** Even if it's not your fault, it's your problem. Outsourcing blame is outsourcing agency — and it teaches your kids to do the same.

8. **Saving your way to wealth on a low income.** At $30k/year and 100% savings rate, the math just doesn't work. You have an income problem, not a frugality opportunity. Fix the numerator.

9. **Not testing prices.** "Not testing your price is the most expensive thing you can do." If you've never run a price increase test, you don't actually know what your customers will pay. You're leaving profit on the table for certainty.

10. **Letting a customer buy once and never following up.** If you're acquiring customers and not emailing them, you've paid for the acquisition and thrown away the asset. The email list is the business inside the business.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. The setup-then-reveal.**
State the conventional belief, then flip it. "People think they can save their way to wealth. The truth is, at absolute max you can save 100%. If you're making $30k, it just takes forever — even at 100%. This isn't an efficiency problem. This is a volume issue." Sets up an obvious frame, destroys it with math.

**2. The "so what I'm really saying is" drill-down.**
After stating a tactic, zoom out to the principle. "You're not in the trophy business. Trophy is what you sell. You're really in the recognition, status, and compensation business." Forces a reframe mid-explanation.

**3. Hypothetical stress-tests.**
"What if you got rid of all new customers and only served return customers — would you make money? If not, you have a pricing problem." Uses a counterfactual to isolate the actual variable in question.

**4. Math-first compression.**
"6% price increase doubles profit. 6% times 6% more than doubles it. That's it." Strips reasoning to arithmetic. No metaphor needed when the numbers are stark enough.

**5. The binary path forcing function.**
"There are two paths. Path A gives you problem X to solve forever. Path B gives you problem Y. Which problem would you rather have?" Never sells a solution — forces the person to choose their preferred constraint. Eliminates waffling.

**6. Compression via contrast pairs.**
"Poor parents teach kids to get along with everyone. Wealthy parents teach kids to ruthlessly curate their circle." "Average parents blame the economy. Wealthy parents say I don't have the skills — but I want you to." The paired contrast is fast, memorable, and argument-proof.

**7. Stakes escalation then return to simplicity.**
Build up complexity ("you've got two brands, two locations, five channels, and you're thinking about an acquisition—") then snap back to the simple frame: "You actually have one of the simpler plans I've seen. Three levers. That's it." The contrast makes the simplicity feel like a gift.

**8. First-person story as proof, not as inspiration.**
Stories aren't told to be relatable — they're told to demonstrate a specific mechanism. The Chipotle story isn't "I worked hard." It's "I outsourced food prep to reclaim selling hours, and one 30-minute sale was worth more than a week of cooking." The lesson is the last sentence, not the anecdote.
