## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Value Equation**
Every message — ads, emails, webinars, pitches — must simultaneously maximize dream outcome and minimize three things: risk (use proof), time delay (show speed), and effort (show ease). If any lever is ignored, conversion suffers. Apply it: before writing any piece of copy, run it through all four levers and ask which is weakest.

**2. The Money Model (Four-Offer Sequence)**
A deliberate sequence of offers — Attraction → Upsell → Downsell → Continuity — structured to generate maximum upfront cash and pull revenue forward so you can outspend competitors on acquisition. Apply it: map every customer interaction to one of the four buckets; the goal is to be profitable *before* continuity kicks in, not after.

**3. Proof-Promise-Plan**
The opening of any webinar, video, or sales pitch must do three things in order: show proof with a relatable avatar ("I helped someone just like you"), state the promise clearly ("here's exactly what you're getting"), and set the plan ("here's how the next 60 minutes will go"). Apply it: write any opener with these three elements; if you're spending more than 60 seconds before hitting all three, you're losing 40% of your audience.

**4. The Pipeline Audit**
Break your acquisition funnel into granular stages — CTR → opt-in rate → schedule rate → show rate → close rate — and find the *actual* bottleneck before changing anything. A 33% improvement at show rate has the same revenue impact as a 33% improvement in close rate, but is usually faster and automatable. Apply it: run the audit monthly; only fix the widest constraint.

**5. Congruence / The Baton Pass**
Every handoff in the customer journey — ad to opt-in page, opt-in to webinar opener, webinar to sales call opener, sales call to onboarding — must echo the same promise and language the customer originally responded to. Disconnect anywhere in the chain causes friction and kills conversions. Apply it: pull the exact headline from the ad and verify every subsequent step uses that same language.

**6. The Three-Pillar Pitch**
For done-with-you or DIY offers: simplify your pitch to exactly three pillars — group two or three related steps into one bucket. For done-for-you: show every step to create "I can't do this myself" overwhelm. Apply it: if you have more than three steps in your current pitch, chunk up; if you have fewer than five steps for a DFY offer, show more granularity.

**7. Structural Churn**
Churn baked into your customer's nature — businesses going under, beginners quitting — has nothing to do with your product quality. Serving volatile customers makes *you* volatile. Apply it: before acquiring a new customer segment, ask "would this segment cancel even if I performed perfectly?" If yes, that's structural churn — price to their worst month or exit the segment.

**8. The Customer Pyramid**
Start selling at the top of the market (highest-spend, most stable customers) and work down as you build infrastructure and reputation. Small customers have volatile revenue, short commitment windows, and high structural churn. Large customers know their numbers, honor contracts, and compound. Apply it: niche your avatar up one or two levels above where you currently sell; same product, higher stability.

**9. Best-to-Best Lead Routing**
Assign your highest-quality leads to your highest-performing closers. Assign your weakest leads to your lowest-performing closers. Never mix. Apply it: rank closers by cash-collected close rate weekly; build a tiered calendar that gates access to premium call slots by performance.

**10. Measurement as Intervention**
The act of measuring a metric changes behavior. You don't need a complicated incentive system — just make the number visible and require daily reporting on a cadence tight enough to influence behavior before the opportunity closes. Apply it: if a team is underperforming, add a daily stand-up where they read back their own metrics before you coach them.

**11. The Before-During-After Framework**
For any product or customer experience change, examine what happens immediately before they buy, during delivery, and immediately after. Leverage lives in those three windows. Apply it: when iterating a product or service, map friction at each of the three moments rather than auditing the feature list.

**12. The Delegate vs. Abdicate Test**
Delegation succeeds when the person you hand off to has equal skill plus more time, so stats hold or improve. Abdication occurs when stats drop because you handed off without training, checklists, or oversight. Apply it: if any metric declines after a handoff, immediately return to working side-by-side until the stat recovers, then hand off again with a documented system.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Webinar registrant no-show prevention | **Script:** Send SMS at 24 hours before, day-of morning, and 1 hour prior. Text outperforms email for show rates — 95–99% open rate. Don't skip these three; everything else is optional.

**Situation:** Behavior-triggered follow-up after email engagement | **Script:** "I saw you [opened/clicked] — here's something exclusive we don't share publicly." Treat engagement as a signal and reward it with behind-the-scenes content.

**Situation:** Webinar intro (first 60 seconds) | **Script:** "I helped [specific avatar just like you] achieve [specific result]. Today you're going to get [clear promise]. Here's how the next [X] minutes will go: [plan]." Get to this within 60 seconds — every second of meandering loses 40% of your audience.

**Situation:** Establishing that a live webinar is actually live | **Script:** State the date, the time, the weather outside, a current event. "It's Tuesday the [date], it's [weather] here in Vegas..." The perceived liveness improves results; capture the benefit by stating it.

**Situation:** Webinar takeaway (qualifying the offer) | **Script:** "We only work with people who are [X, Y, or Z]. If you're below [threshold] or above [threshold], this isn't for you." State who it's NOT for. This removes wrong fits and magnetizes right ones.

**Situation:** Webinar scarcity (ethical) | **Script:** "We only have [N] call slots because I only have [N] guys on my team. Typically we book out within [timeframe]. It's first come, first served." State the *actual* operational constraint — that's all the scarcity you need.

**Situation:** Webinar live social proof during booking window | **Script:** "Let me know in the chat as soon as you book." Then call out: "I see Rosie booked. I see John booked. Wednesday morning is now sold out. Thursday afternoon is now sold out." Chunk scarcity into time slots, not just total seats.

**Situation:** Sales call opener (replacing "how's your week going") | **Script:** "My name's [Name]. I have a background in [specific problem domain]. I work closely with [founder/expert they saw in the webinar]. I'd love to know — what resonated most with you on the call?"

**Situation:** Reframing the sales call away from a pitch | **Script:** "The real purpose of this call is to see if you qualify for our guarantee — not everyone does. It comes down to three factors: [business size / industry / you being the owner / etc.]."

**Situation:** Pre-pitch invite (getting permission to sell) | **Script:** "Given everything you've told me — you're doing [X revenue], you've got [Y challenge], and you [Z qualification] — I think you'd be a great fit. We solve exactly this. If you want, I can walk you through what that looks like. Where do you want to go from here?" Wait for them to say yes.

**Situation:** Rollover upsell (crediting a prior purchase toward the next) | **Script:** "We can take your [paid amount] and credit it forward toward [higher-tier offer]. How's that sound?" The higher the original payment, the more leverage you have as a credit.

**Situation:** Pay-in-full discount close | **Script:** "If you prepay the year today, I'll save you another 10–20%. You're already committed — this just locks in a better rate." Run this at the natural transition point (end of trial, renewal, week six of a challenge).

**Situation:** Closer incentive structure | **Script:** Design comp so cash-collected close earns 5x more than a payment-plan close. Example: 10% of $2K (payment plan) = $200. 10% of $10K (PIF) = $1,000. This eliminates flaccid closes.

**Situation:** Interviewing lost prospects for product intel | **Script:** "I'll pay you $100 just to hop on a 20-minute call and tell me what we could have done better." The intel is worth 100x that — and half the time you get a second shot at the sale anyway.

**Situation:** Forcing best-customer behaviors onto new customers | **Script:** Match your guarantee conditions to the behaviors your best customers took: "We guarantee [result] as long as you [specific onboarding action] within the first 30 days." This signals what matters and creates compliance.

**Situation:** Product iteration — finding the non-negotiable | **Script:** "If I deleted every component of our service except one, which one would you want me to keep?" Then the inverse: "If I kept everything except one thing, which thing would you not care about?" Delete the answer to the second question quietly and see who complains.

**Situation:** Below-KPI performer management | **Script:** "You're below KPI this week, so I'm cutting your calendar in half. Hit the number and you get it back. If you're still below in [7–14 days], we'll have a different conversation."

**Situation:** Discovery — keeping it chunked up | **Script:** Ask about pain, desired outcome, current gap. Do not go into tactical detail. The moment they're asking you questions about execution, you've lost control of the call. Stay at the level of "what do you want and what's stopping you."

**Situation:** Closing a DFY offer with complexity as value | **Script:** Walk through all 11–38 steps. Then: "Right. So you can see why most people can't do this on their own — and exactly why we exist." Let the complexity sell the done-for-you component.

**Situation:** Selling supplementary products to existing buyers | **Script:** "You just invested in your [gym membership / program]. Most people also grab [supplement / accessory] because [reason tied to their stated goal]. Want to add that while you're here?" Customers have separate mental wallets — the gym membership wallet and the supplement wallet don't overlap.

## DECISION RULES

- **When show rate is below 25%:** Add SMS at 24h, morning-of, and 1h prior. That sequence alone typically produces the biggest lift; everything else is secondary.

- **When you can't diagnose what's wrong with a webinar or presentation:** Pull the analytics. Find where people drop off. Fix the first five minutes before touching anything else — that's where most conversions are won or lost.

- **When a sales script isn't converting:** Look at the opening frame, not the close. Reframe discovery so you're the qualifier, they're the applicant.

- **When your best stat is close rate but show rate is 18%:** You're leaving more money on the table in show rate improvement than you could ever recover through close rate optimization.

- **When improving an existing system vs. launching something new:** Default to better. Improving a proven system is lower risk and disproportionately falls to the bottom line. New is sexy but unproven.

- **When you have real scarcity (limited slots, limited capacity):** State it explicitly. "We only have four consultants, so we can only take 20 calls" — if you don't say it, you lose 100% of the benefit.

- **When a live event is actually live:** Say the date, time, weather, local events. Liveness improves conversion for reasons that don't need to be understood — just captured.

- **When doing a DFY pitch:** Show every step of the process. Complexity creates perceived value and justifies the done-for-you premium.

- **When doing a DIY or DWY pitch:** Reduce to three pillars. Complexity creates overwhelm and kills the close.

- **When churn stays high despite product improvements:** Ask if the churn is structural (the *customer type* is failing, not your product). If yes, you must either change the customer segment or price to their worst month with near-zero delivery cost.

- **When evaluating a new customer segment:** Ask: "What percentage of these customers would cancel even if I performed perfectly?" That number is your structural churn floor — build your economics around it.

- **When a closer's stats slip after initial strong performance:** They're getting cute. Pull recordings. They've started improvising instead of running the script. Return to roleplay and fundamentals.

- **When two closers have identical scripts but different results:** It's a who problem. Put the best leads in front of the best closer. Train or exit the weaker closer — don't waste good leads on low performers.

- **When you want to increase close rates:** Watch the first 5 minutes of recorded calls first. That investment returns more than drilling the objection-handling section.

- **When a metric looks bad:** Look at it twice. The instinct to avoid a painful number is the reason it stays painful. Make it visible and report on it daily until behavior changes.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **One webinar per week with multi-day gaps before it runs.** The longer the gap between registration and the event, the more drop-off. People forget within hours. If show rate is below 25%, the scheduling cadence is the first suspect.

2. **Opening a sales call with "how's your week going / do you have a pen and paper."** This is the most common way to signal you're a vendor, not an authority. It frames the call as a pitch before you've established whether the prospect qualifies for anything.

3. **Feature-stacking pitches for a DIY offer.** Overwhelming a prospect with complexity and then asking them to do the work themselves is the fastest path to "let me think about it." Match pitch depth to how much work the client is responsible for.

4. **Closers getting "cute" — improvising, playing consultant, going into the weeds.** This is the leading cause of top performers plateauing after a strong start. As soon as a closer starts answering detailed delivery questions, the prospect is in control and you're losing.

5. **Paying commissions on contract value instead of cash collected.** Misaligned incentives produce payment-plan-heavy closes, higher refund rates, and weaker conviction in the close. Pay 5x more for cash-collected — structure always beats culture.

6. **Serving beginners or small businesses without pricing to their worst month and having near-zero delivery cost.** You will inherit their volatility. Every month they have a bad month, you're at risk of cancellation for reasons you cannot control.

7. **Tracking only close rate without sub-metrics.** One top-level stat tells you something is wrong; it tells you nothing about *where* or *why*. Break it into: offer made %, obstacle %, show rate %. Personalize coaching from there.

8. **Trying to label your business as something it isn't to get a higher multiple.** Valuations are derived from the math — margin, retention, revenue quality — not the label. A service business with software-like retention gets software-like multiples. A service business wrapped in software UI does not.

9. **Assuming customers understand the value you created in prior months.** Perceived value resets to zero every billing cycle. "I made you $9K last month" does not buy you goodwill this month. They evaluate only what you've done for them relative to what they're paying *right now*.

10. **Handing off a function before building a documented system and training to match your own skill level.** Premature delegation is abdication. If stats drop after a handoff, you didn't delegate — you abandoned. Return and stay until the system produces equal or better output.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

1. **Before/After Math with Specific Numbers.** Never say "we improved a lot." Say: "We went from 18% to 24% — that's a 33% increase, same as getting 33% more leads for free." Numbers make claims credible and shareable. Always state before, after, and the percentage delta.

2. **Rule of Three in Lists.** Arguments land in threes: "ease, speed, and risk." "Pain, proof, and dream." "Attraction, upsell, continuity." If a list has four items, chunk two of them. If it has two, find the third.

3. **"Said Differently" as a Reset.** After a conceptual explanation, restate it from a different angle: "Said differently — if you don't have an offer, there's nothing I can do for you." This handles the 40% who missed the first framing.

4. **Rhetorical Question → Obvious Answer → Principle.** "Why would you sell them supplements right after they bought a membership? Because they're going to buy them either way. Might as well buy them from you." Sets up agreement before the principle lands.

5. **Story → Extract → Apply.** Never lead with the principle. Lead with a specific story (the gym, the Solar Company, the CRM). Extract the lesson. Then apply it to the listener's situation. The story does the persuasion; the extraction does the teaching.

6. **Self-Interruption for Parentheticals.** "And by the way — if you hate looking at this stat, you should look at it twice." Mid-sentence asides are used to drop high-value one-liners that wouldn't survive as standalone statements.

7. **Takeaway Frame Before Pitch.** Before making any offer — in a webinar, on a sales call, in copy — first establish who it's NOT for and what you're NOT doing. "I'm not here to sell you; I'm here to see if you qualify." The takeaway creates the desire. The pitch fulfills it.

8. **Chunked Structure with Explicit Signposting.** "So we did four things. Number one... number two..." Every argument is numbered and the count is stated upfront. This creates a listener contract ("I know there are four things coming") that keeps attention through the list.
