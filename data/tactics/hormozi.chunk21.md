## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Grand Slam Offer Ladder**
Never cold-sell your highest-ticket offer. Build a front-end product (5–10% of your core price) that solves one specific, fast-ROI problem and converts prospects into customers. Then upsell into your core and continuity offers. Apply it by mapping: Lead Magnet → Front-End Offer (one-call close) → Core Offer → Retainer/Continuity.

**The Value Equation (4 Levers)**
Perceived value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort/Sacrifice). Use these four axes to generate unlimited copy angles — amplify the dream, reduce the timeline, reduce the effort, and increase certainty. Apply by running each lever as a separate ad variation.

**The Three Types of Lead Magnets**
Every lead magnet does one of three things: reveals a problem the prospect didn't know they had, gives a free sample of your solution, or delivers one step of a multi-step process. Apply by picking whichever builds the most urgency toward your front-end offer — never stack them; A/B test instead.

**The What/Who/When Copy Framework**
Explode the Value Equation into copy by asking: what's the dream outcome, who already has it, and when (what moment) does the prospect feel the pain or benefit? Write to specific moments, not abstract features — "no more disaster back-of-bar" beats "functional operating processes." Apply this to ads, landing pages, and VSLs identically.

**Good Friction vs. Bad Friction**
Good friction qualifies leads (avatar callouts, application questions); bad friction repels everyone (double opt-ins, slow pages, asking the same info twice). The more expensive the product, the more good friction you need. Apply by auditing your funnel for any form field, page load, or step that slows down both qualified and unqualified leads identically.

**The Pain Articulation Trust Transfer**
If you can describe a prospect's pain more precisely than they can describe it themselves, they will trust you to solve it more than any promise you could make. Apply by listing the five most recent, visceral moments of failure your avatar experiences and writing those as specific copy lines — not categories of pain, but exact incidents.

**The Rollover Upsell with Waved Fee**
Sell step one of a multi-step process explicitly framed as step one. Seed the upsell at the beginning: "My real goal isn't to just do X — it's to help you do Y. This is just the fastest way to get you there." Upsell at three milestone moments: first win, one week later, and last-chance. Apply by scripting all three touch points before you begin fulfillment.

**The Wave Fee Offer (AB Close)**
"I charge $30K for this. If you commit for the year, I'll wave it. If you want out early, you pay the breakup fee. If you want quarter-to-quarter, no problem — that's the risk premium." Always present as an AB choice, never a yes/no. Apply when a continuity offer has high perceived setup cost — convert that cost into a commitment incentive.

**Front-End Offer as Cash-Flow Mechanism**
A front-end product isn't primarily about revenue — it's about pulling cash forward to offset ad spend and reduce payback period. Cold traffic requires more information before a large purchase; a $5–8K front-end captures cash during that education period. Apply whenever CAC exceeds 30-day cash collected from a new customer.

**Spread Discounts, Don't Lump Them**
Lumping a discount into the first payment trains buyers to cancel after that payment ends. Spread discounts across all payments (e.g., $1,500 off per quarter for four quarters) to create commitment gravity. Apply any time you're crediting a front-end purchase toward a continuity offer.

**The Pre-Call VSL System**
Record the full presentation you repeat on every sales call and send it before the call. Open every call with "Did you watch the video?" If no: "Watch it now — I'll grab a coffee." This eliminates presentation fatigue, adds visuals you can't convey on phone, and lets the call start at decision-making, not education. Apply by listing every objection you handle before the 20-minute mark of your current calls — that's your VSL script.

**The $100M Scaling Roadmap Stages**
Businesses get stuck in predictable stages. Stage four ("Prioritize") looks like: saying yes to any avatar, unqualified leads coming in, high CAC, offer-market mismatch. The fix at stage four is always: specialize the avatar, create qualifying friction, improve the lead magnet, and tighten copy to one ICP. Apply by identifying which stage symptom matches your current constraint before attempting to scale spend.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect hasn't watched the pre-call video | **Script:** "The first question I'm going to ask you on the call is whether you watched the video. So you might as well just watch it now — it has charts and figures that'll take us three times as long to cover verbally."

**Situation:** Opening a sales call | **Script:** "Let me set the agenda: I'm going to walk through the [lead magnet topic] with you, understand a few metrics about your business, and then at the end, if it looks like a good fit, I'll explain how we might be able to help. Sound good?"

**Situation:** Upselling at the first win moment | **Script:** "Dude, if you like that, you're going to love what I have for you next. You just made an extra $X. What I really want to do is build this into a consistent machine — and the only reason I'm mentioning it now is I'll apply the $5,800 you just paid toward your first quarter."

**Situation:** Seeding the upsell at the start of the front-end engagement | **Script:** "I'll be real with you — my whole goal isn't just this pricing audit. I'm going to ROI myself easily and everyone will be happy. What I really want is to help you transform the bar. You can only do a pricing change once. After that, wouldn't it be cool to make an extra million?"

**Situation:** Presenting the wave fee close | **Script:** "Listen, I normally charge $30K for the full build. If you commit for the year, I'll wave that. If you want out in two quarters, there's a fee. Or if you'd rather stay quarter-to-quarter, I'm happy to do that — you just pay the premium. Which works better for you?"

**Situation:** Spreading a discount across a retainer | **Script:** "Rather than cutting your first payment in half, what I'd prefer to do is knock $1,500 off each quarter for four quarters. That way you're locked in, and the savings compound as you stay."

**Situation:** Justifying why the video must come before the call | **Script:** "I already have facts and figures I could describe with words, but it'll take us three times as long as you just watching it. This has graphs and visuals that make it more digestible. I don't want to just vomit information at you — you don't want that either."

**Situation:** Prospect is price-shocked at your core offer | **Script:** "It sounds like that number landed wrong. Let me ask — at what number would this be an obvious yes for you, assuming we deliver exactly what I just described?"

**Situation:** Framing the front-end offer to close fast | **Script:** "I just want to calculate how much more profit you would have made last year if you had these prices. If you're like most of our clients, that number is going to shock you — and the only real cost to you is reprinting the menus."

**Situation:** Moving from front-end to core offer | **Script:** "We made you an extra $100K. The next thing I want to show you costs about a third of what I already made you. So I've already made room for you to afford me."

**Situation:** Writing ad callout copy for a specific avatar | **Script:** "[Avatar descriptor] — are you doing over $4M per year? If your cocktail program is less than 10% of your revenue year-over-year, keep reading."

**Situation:** Pain-based ad hook | **Script:** "Is your cocktail program flaccid? When was the last time anyone posted something good about your drinks? If you look at your reviews, are you just seeing Long Island iced teas and vodka sodas?"

**Situation:** Qualifying the right ad audience | **Script:** "$4M+ per year restaurant owners — [single line of specific pain]. If that sounds familiar, here's what we did for [client result in numbers]."

**Situation:** Explaining why you're starting with a lower-ticket offer | **Script:** "It's too cold of a sale right now. Either we run a much longer sales process, or we lower the bar to get you in the door so I can prove myself first. I'd rather prove myself and earn the right to ask for the bigger number."

**Situation:** Prospect asks why they should care about your lead magnet | **Script:** "Most bar owners don't know that alcohol is one of the largest profit drivers in the business — and it's the one thing they're the least focused on. I took the top 10 worst drinks to serve and I'll show you 10 better replacements that work for just about any bar. It's free."

**Situation:** Handling a low conversion rate on continuity | **Script:** "You're not showing them the value. They need to see week-over-week proof that the program is working. Get a login to their system, pull their numbers yourself, and show them on a monthly call. When they see you know all their data, they won't want to cancel."

**Situation:** Justifying increased ad spend after funnel fixes | **Script:** "Your funnel was broken — that's why your numbers are bad. Now that we've fixed it, test at two times your allowable CAC per campaign. That's your stomach-it budget while you find what converts. Once you find it, blow the doors off."

**Situation:** Introducing yourself on a cold outbound call | **Script:** "Hey, this is [name] from [company] regarding [specific topic]. By the way, this call is recorded. Before we get going — how are you doing today? Great. Let me set the agenda..."

**Situation:** Responding to "I'll think about it" at end of discovery call | **Script:** "Totally fair. Can I ask — is it the ROI math that needs more time, or is there something specific about the delivery that's still unclear? Because those are two different conversations and I want to make sure I'm giving you the right information."

**Situation:** Asking the prospect to watch the VSL mid-call | **Script:** "I'm going to grab a coffee — you can watch the video. You're going to do it either way, so you might as well do it now."

## DECISION RULES

**When CAC exceeds 3–4x the 30-day cash collected from a customer → there is an offer mismatch, a broken sales process, or both. Diagnose before increasing spend.**

**When your back-end upsell rate is below 20% → the front-end offer is not creating the right expectation, or the back-end offer is not the logical next step. Fix the offer structure before fixing ads.**

**When selling to cold traffic → always insert a lead magnet. Going from low trust directly to a $30K sale loses the majority of people who would have bought if you'd demonstrated value first.**

**When you have a high-ticket offer ($10K+) and cold traffic → add a front-end product at 15–20% of core price. Use it to pull cash forward and convert prospects into customers before making the real ask.**

**When customers are churning after 2 payments → fix churn before scaling acquisition. Adding leads to a leaky bucket accelerates cash burn, not revenue.**

**When a funnel collects the same information twice → remove the redundancy immediately. Bad friction repels qualified buyers as effectively as unqualified ones.**

**When testing new ads → budget 2x your allowable CAC per campaign before shutting it off, as long as you're generating leads. This is the cost of finding what works.**

**When you want to discount a continuity offer → spread the discount across all payments, never front-load it. Front-loaded discounts incentivize a one-quarter buy-and-cancel.**

**When founder is not selling → reinsert the founder until close rate and ICP feedback are well-understood. Founders outperform salespeople on conviction and cost less.**

**When your landing page has your logo above the fold on cold traffic → remove it. They don't know who you are; use that real estate for a pain-based headline instead.**

**When a prospect hasn't watched your pre-call VSL → don't start the pitch. Either ask them to watch it now or reschedule. A cold-start call wastes both parties' time.**

**When testing lead magnets → A/B test one vs. the other, never stack them. Track all the way to LTV, not just opt-in rate.**

**When churn is driven by clients not seeing value → get a login to their systems and report their own numbers back to them monthly. Ownership of the data creates stickiness.**

**When a service naturally "graduates" clients (they learn and stop needing you) → either accept it's a one-time project business and price accordingly, or find a consumable that resets the need (new staff, new season, new data).**

**When a prospect is at low trust → offer a lead magnet that either reveals a problem, gives a sample of the solution, or delivers step one of a multi-step process. Never skip this step on cold traffic.**

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Selling your highest-ticket offer to strangers with no prior trust-building.** Cold traffic + $30K ask = arm wrestling. The right offer at the wrong time is still the wrong offer.

**Collecting the same information twice in a funnel.** Double opt-ins, redundant forms, and re-entry steps create bad friction that drops qualified and unqualified leads equally. Most business owners have never gone through their own sales process and don't know this is happening.

**Recurring revenue for recurring revenue's sake.** Some products graduate clients — that's natural. Forcing continuity onto a one-trick engagement creates churn, resentment, and a bad reputation. Accept the nature of the product and price it accordingly.

**Running paid ads before fixing funnel conversion.** Pouring spend into a broken funnel amplifies the losses. Fix conversion rate first; then scale spend.

**Letting a pre-call VSL go unwatched.** If clients aren't watching the video, your call starts from zero every time, you repeat the same pitch with diminishing conviction, and you lose the visual/data advantage of a well-produced presentation.

**Optimizing for opt-in volume instead of LTV.** A lead magnet priced at $97 may convert identically to one priced at $47 while also pre-qualifying buyers who spend 2x more downstream. Never judge an offer only by top-of-funnel metrics.

**Having your business logo as the hero element on a cold-traffic landing page.** Cold traffic doesn't know or care who you are. Every character of above-the-fold real estate should be doing persuasion work, not branding.

**Using a salesperson before the founder understands close rate and ICP.** You can't diagnose whether poor results come from leads, pricing, copy, or delivery if a rep is in the way. Founder-sold = fastest feedback loop.

**Describing features instead of moments.** "Data-driven menus" and "functional operating processes" require mental translation. "No more disaster back-of-bar when you open" doesn't. Anytime copy describes a category of benefit rather than a specific experience, it's doing less work than it could.

**Discounting the first payment of a continuity offer without securing commitment.** An easy yes on payment one followed by a 2x jump to payment two is a churn machine. Spread incentives across the commitment horizon.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Ding, ding, ding — the alarm call pattern.** Hormozi signals a critical insight mid-conversation with a verbal or conceptual "alarm bell," then narrows immediately into the specific data point that triggered it. Use this structure to flag pivots: "Here's the number that tells me everything I need to know → here's what it means → here's the fix."

**The "and so" cascade.** Arguments build through tight chains: "And so... and so... and so..." Each clause extends logically from the previous one, creating a sense of inevitable conclusion. Avoid conjunctions that feel like jumps; every step should feel like it follows from the last.

**Binary frame before close.** Before any decision, collapse options to two: "Option A is X, Option B is Y. Which works better for you?" Never leave a prospect in open-ended consideration space. The AB close applies to offers, timelines, and discounts equally.

**Specific numbers over ranges.** Don't say "somewhere between 2x and 4x." Say "30% cocktail sales, 40% return on beverage, 50% speed of service." Specificity signals that you've done this before and you know exactly what happens. Ranges signal guessing.

**Pain before promise, always.** Lead with the exact moment of suffering, then attach the outcome. "When you walk in and the bar is a total mess because no one knows how to set it up → no more disaster back-of-bar" is more powerful than leading with the outcome alone. People don't buy the destination; they buy the relief from the departure point.

**Preemptive objection handling via sub-headline.** After a bold promise, immediately voice the most obvious disbelief: "Launch an elite high-profit bar — without redesigning your location, without expensive ingredients that go bad, without a six-month training process." Name what the skeptic is thinking before they say it.

**The "I make no guarantees, but..." hedge-and-commitment pair.** Close high-conviction recommendations with explicit uncertainty framing: "I make no guarantees — these are high-likelihood shots." This signals honesty, not weakness, and inoculates against overpromising while preserving the conviction behind the recommendation.

**Teach-by-narration.** When walking through a framework, Hormozi reads directly from his own books and names them ("This is page 136 of the Leads book"). This isn't ego — it reinforces that the advice is systematized, not ad hoc. Reference your systems and tools by name to build the same authority signal.
