## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Value Equation**
Four levers determine price tolerance and conversion: Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement ÷ Time Delay ÷ Effort & Sacrifice. Maximize the top two, minimize the bottom two. Every ad, upsell, and offer repositioning runs through these variables: "How do I make it faster, easier, less risky, and closer to exactly what they want?" When conversion stalls, diagnose which lever is weakest — it's rarely all four simultaneously.

**2. The BSL Tripod (Build, Sell, Lead)**
Every business requires all three: building products people want, selling (moving people from unaware to paying), and leading people to do both at scale. Miss any leg and the stool falls — builders who can't sell rot in a garage; sellers with nothing real eventually face consequences. Identify which leg is weakest right now; that's where your next dollar and hour goes.

**3. Rule of 100**
Pick one acquisition channel and do 100 units per day: 100 outreaches, OR 100 minutes making content (and post it), OR 100 minutes building/optimizing paid ads. Volume is the only cure for perceived volatility. If you're closing one customer every 3–5 weeks, the mission is compressing four weeks of advertising activity into one day. Apply immediately when results feel inconsistent.

**4. Nail It Then Scale It**
You cannot scale a broken unit. Sell just enough to figure out what keeps customers happy, paying, and referring. Company A retaining 100% and adding 100/year beats Company B selling 300/year but churning 300/year — by year three they're equal in headcount, but A has no re-acquisition cost and far better margins. Fix retention before touching acquisition.

**5. The Grand Slam Offer Build (5 Steps)**
Step 1: Define the dream outcome with precision. Step 2: List every problem blocking that dream — the excuse list IS the problem list. Step 3: Rename each problem as a solution with a descriptive name. Step 4: Brainstorm every delivery mechanism for each solution with no cost constraints. Step 5: Trim and stack — prioritize high-value/low-cost combos, but high-value/medium-cost has far less competition. Apply whenever pricing feels stuck or customers aren't "in love."

**6. The Virtuous Cycle of Pricing**
Higher price → higher gross margin → hire better talent → deliver better service → better reputation → more demand → raise price again. The break-in point is always raising price. When demand exceeds supply, raise price before adding capacity. Most sub-$5M businesses are underpriced, not overpriced. An 80%+ close rate is a direct signal you're leaving 2–4x margin untouched.

**7. Supply vs. Demand Constraint Diagnosis**
Before fixing anything: if you doubled lead flow, could you handle it? Yes = demand-constrained, fix marketing. No = supply-constrained, build infrastructure first. Never pour leads into a broken delivery system. Supply-constrained businesses require the same acquisition creativity applied to talent, not customers — calculate gross profit per employee to size the recruiting bounty correctly.

**8. Customer-Financed Acquisition (CFA)**
Structure your economics so the first customer pays for themselves AND the next customer within 30 days. Formula: 2× CAC + COGS must equal ≤ 30-day cash collected. Engineer upfront cash collection — prepay incentives, bundled packages, immediate upsells — so capital is never the growth constraint. When your cash conversion cycle exceeds 30 days, redesign the offer to pull cash forward.

**9. Permanent Customer vs. Churn Factory**
Two businesses selling 300 customers per year are not equal. Company B keeps every customer; Company A churns and re-acquires. With zero increase in marketing spend, Company B compounds indefinitely. Measure: of 100 customers on January 1, how many are still buying December 31? That percentage is your true business health signal — distinguish logo retention from revenue retention.

**10. The "How to Yay Without Boo" Headline Formula**
Fill in: *How to [desired outcome] without [feared obstacle], even if [biggest insecurity].* This is the single highest-leverage sentence in any piece of copy — it leads with the dream, neutralizes the objection, and pre-handles the excuse in one breath. Write this before every other headline variant. It should be your control until something beats it.

**11. The Underdog Advantage Frame**
You're not competing against the brand — you're competing against their junior account rep running a checklist. The advantage of being small: unlimited shots on goal, nothing to lose, pure risk-free upside. When a prospect name-drops a bigger competitor: "To them, you're a number. To me, you're my whole business." Apply in every sales conversation where size differential is raised.

**12. Top-Down Brand Anchoring (The Tesla Model)**
Start with an unscalable, expensive, premium offer before building accessible tiers. This creates downward brand logic: "we made an expensive thing, here's an affordable version." The reverse — budget brand going premium — is nearly impossible. Launch your highest-ticket offer first, even if done-for-you and unscalable. Use early case studies and revenue to fund the next tier down.

**13. BANT Qualification**
Four gates, all must open before you invest sales time: Budget (do they have the money?), Authority (can they decide alone?), Need (do they actually need this?), Timing (do they want to move now?). Embed BANT into your intake form — by the time you get on a call, the decision should be mostly made. If your sales team is underutilized, loosen filters; if overloaded, tighten them.

**14. The STAR System (Why People Don't Do Things)**
Five sequential failure modes before concluding someone lacks motivation: they didn't know you wanted it done → didn't know *what* you wanted → didn't know *how* → didn't know *when* → something *blocked* them. Run through all five in order before delivering judgment. Motivation is the last variable, not the first.

**15. Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate (D3 Training)**
To transfer any skill: write the exact checklist, do it in front of them, have them do it in front of you. Every training failure is a failure to operationalize. "Be kind" isn't trainable; "smile when you answer the phone, repeat back what they said, raise your voice when you enter a room" is trainable. Use whenever you're tempted to say "they just don't get it."

**16. The Iron Man Suit Upgrade**
Your business starts as a suit that enhances you — it still requires you to run it. The goal is to upgrade it so it fights without you. A business that needs you to operate has zero transferable value; one that operates without you trades at a multiple. Each quarter ask: "Does this burn down without me?" If yes, you're still wearing the suit.

**17. Feature vs. Bug Diagnosis**
Every business has permanent structural costs (features) masquerading as solvable problems (bugs). Service businesses always struggle with talent quality — that's the operating system, not a broken business. Before recommending any model change, ask: "Is this problem endemic to your business category, or a specific execution failure?" If endemic, optimize how you deal with it — don't try to eliminate it.

**18. The Constraint-First Growth Model (More → Better → New)**
The priority order for scaling: do more of what's already working → make it better → add new channels. Most founders jump to "new" while leaving "more" completely untapped. Before launching a new acquisition channel, ask why you can't 10x your best existing one. The answer to that question IS the constraint. Solve the constraint, don't route around it.

**19. Giveaway → Backend Monetization**
Give away something so good your competitors can't match it — something they're charging $10K–$20K for. Absorb the cost. Drive leads. Then monetize through what the giveaway logically leads to (software, memberships, supplements, licensing). The giveaway must create a next problem your paid offer solves. If it fully resolves the pain, you've eliminated the reason to buy.

**20. Fear vs. Regret / Short Pain Long Gain Matrix**
Four quadrants: short gain / long pain (avoidance), long gain / short pain (courage), short gain / short pain (neutral), long gain / long pain (grind). The only winning trade is short pain → long gain. Avoiding a hard conversation is a short gain / long pain trade — the coward's bet that compounds against you. Before every comfort-seeking decision, map it to the quadrant explicitly.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect says "You're not [market leader]" | **Script:** "Exactly — and here's what that means for you. With them, you'll get assigned a rep three levels down running a checklist. You'll never talk to anyone who actually built the system. With me, you get my cell. You're not comparing our companies — you're comparing me to their rep. I'm more invested in your success than they'll ever be."

**Situation:** Prospect pushes back on price | **Script:** "It sounds like that number landed wrong. What does fair look like to you?" [Stay silent for 3+ seconds. Never justify price before understanding the real objection.]

**Situation:** Delivering a high price and worried about choking | **Script:** "Before I tell you the price — it's super expensive." [pause, let them set their own internal anchor, then deliver the number calmly. Say nothing after.]

**Situation:** Explaining ad spend logic to a skeptical client | **Script:** "If for every dollar you gave me, I gave you $5 back — what would your budget be? As many dollars as you possibly could. Once I give you more back, you take those dollars and give them back to me. That's how good advertising works."

**Situation:** Market saturation objection | **Script:** "Which channel are you using? Because you're not competing in the market — you're competing on one platform. There are eight other ways to get your customer that have almost no competition right now."

**Situation:** Someone has sunk cost in a bad direction | **Script:** "That's called sunk cost fallacy. Some of the best money you ever spend is deciding to stop spending bad money."

**Situation:** Someone wanting to diversify into multiple businesses | **Script:** "Do you remember that guy who got rich dropshipping, day trading, buying crypto, and wholesaling all at the same time? Yeah. Me neither. Of all the things you're doing, which one is actually working? Kill the others."

**Situation:** Reactivation email, high open-rate version | **Script:** Subject: "I owe you $175." Body: "Hey [name], it turns out when you signed up we didn't communicate a component of our offer that we did to other customers. I want to make sure we do right by you — can we make it up to you?"

**Situation:** Reactivation email, savings angle | **Script:** "Hey [name], Casey was paying $600/month on her energy bills before we worked together. She's now at $150. Your situation is probably doing the same thing. Want me to take a look?"

**Situation:** Someone can't train salespeople | **Script:** "You're using words nobody can act on. 'Have more charisma' means nothing. 'Raise your voice, talk faster, shoulders back' — those are behaviors. Other people will call it charisma. Describe what you want in behaviors only — things you can see — and your training time will collapse."

**Situation:** Webinar intro (first 60 seconds) | **Script:** "I helped [specific avatar just like you] achieve [specific result]. Today you're going to get [clear promise]. Here's how the next [X] minutes will go: [plan]." Get here within 60 seconds — every second of meandering loses 40% of your audience.

**Situation:** Sales call opener | **Script:** "My name's [Name]. I have a background in [specific problem domain]. I work closely with [founder/expert they saw]. I'd love to know — what resonated most with you on the call?" [Never open with "how's your week going?"]

**Situation:** Qualifying who gets the free lead magnet | **Script:** "Before I do this for you — do you have the budget to move forward if it goes well? Are you the one who makes that call? Do you actually need this right now? And are you ready to act if we deliver?" Only proceed if all four are yes.

**Situation:** Pitching after lead magnet is consumed | **Script:** "Did you like the free thing? Good. You're going to love the paid thing — because all we did was solve step one. Here's what's left."

**Situation:** Addressing obvious weakness in your offer | **Script:** "We're not the biggest [gym/firm/agency] in the area. We don't have [X]. But when you walk in, you're going to forget about all of that — because [specific compelling experience]. Most of our clients say it's not even close."

**Situation:** Underperforming employee | **Script:** "Did you not know I wanted you to do it? Did you not know what I wanted? Did you not know how to do it? Did you not know when? Or was there something blocking you?" Run through sequentially before assuming motivation.

**Situation:** Behavioral feedback to an employee | **Script:** "I need you to stop [specific behavior]. I need you to start [specific behavior]. Keep doing [specific behavior]." Never use trait labels.

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it" | **Script:** "Of course — what specifically do you need to think through? I want to make sure you have the right information to make the decision." [Then stay in the conversation, don't end the call.]

**Situation:** Offering a guarantee to justify a 20–40% price premium | **Script:** "We're going to charge more than everyone else, and here's why you should love that: if we're not [on time / on budget / hitting these metrics], I'll give you all my profit back. Which means you pay exactly what everyone else charges if we fail. But if we hit it, you keep the difference in performance."

**Situation:** Affiliate pitch | **Script:** "I don't want 20% of your referrals. Here's what I'd rather do: sell my [X-session] package to your customers for whatever price you want — keep every dollar. I just need the price to be at least $[floor] so we're attracting the right people."

**Situation:** Writing ad copy — benefit without stating it | **Script:** "Don't say 'you'll feel better.' Say 'You'll bend down, tie both shoes standing up — no more holding your gut in to lean over.' Describe the moment, not the label."

**Situation:** Someone says "no one can do it like me" | **Script:** "That's not a competitive advantage. That's a prison sentence. Let me reframe: 'I don't yet know how to find someone who can do X like me.' That's solvable."

**Situation:** Founder frustrated a slow-solve fix isn't working yet | **Script:** "You made the right call. You started the right solution. Nothing has changed yet — that's not failure, that's the timeline. The danger right now is that you feel like you should do something. But you are doing something. Don't break the thing that works while you wait for the solution to land."

**Situation:** Prospect stalling — justifying acting now | **Script:** "If you genuinely believe you're going to do this sooner or later, do it sooner so you can start enjoying the benefits now. You're going to enjoy them either way. The only reason to delay is to delay the cost of potential failure."

**Situation:** Someone starting from zero with no audience | **Script:** "Leave the best comment on posts that are attracting your exact avatar. You're hitching a ride on someone else's views. It's totally legal, and it's almost uncapped when DMs and posts are limited."

**Situation:** Customer wronged by your mistake | **Script:** "I'm sorry this happened. Here's what we're going to do — [overcompensate specifically]. We want to make sure you walk away better off than if this never happened." The people you wrong and then super-compensate become your biggest ambassadors.

**Situation:** E-commerce operator hitting ~$10M ceiling | **Script:** "You're running a media arbitrage business. It works great up to about $10M, then it very quickly stops working. Your margins go to nothing, cash flow gets tight, and dupes flood in. The only way out is brand — people who buy from you because of who you are, not because you had the best ad."

**Situation:** Reveal-a-problem close | **Script:** "Your load time is nine seconds. For every second of load time you lose 3% of conversions. We can get you to three seconds — that's an 18% conversion lift. What's 18% more revenue worth to you this year? What I charge is a fraction of that. How soon do you want to start?"

**Situation:** Sell continuity after one-time product delivery | **Script:** "You've got the skill now. What you don't have is what changes every month — [fresh data/curated list/tested creative]. That's what the membership is. Without it, the skill goes stale."

**Situation:** Closing a DFY offer with complexity as value | **Script:** Walk through all steps of the process. Then: "Right. So you can see why most people can't do this on their own — and exactly why we exist." Let the complexity sell the done-for-you component.

## DECISION RULES

- **When your close rate exceeds 80%:** Raise prices. An 82%+ close rate means you have 2–4x untapped pricing. Raise 10% — you only need close rate to stay above ~65% for net profit to increase.
- **When diagnosing why something isn't working:** Run STAR first — know, what, how, when, blocked — before assuming motivation. Motivation is the last variable, not the first.
- **When a channel feels saturated:** Map every channel you're NOT on. Saturation is almost always a single-channel illusion. Warm outreach, cold outreach, content, paid, affiliates, referrals are almost entirely separate pools.
- **When ads show positive ROI:** Spend more immediately. The constraint on ad spend should be attribution confidence, not emotional discomfort with large numbers.
- **When deciding to make an operational change:** The projected upside must exceed 30–50% to justify the guaranteed ~20% temporary performance drop during transition. A 5% improvement idea is not worth changing.
- **When you have more demand than capacity:** Raise price before adding capacity. This is how the supply/demand curve actually works.
- **When a customer churns fast:** Stop scaling acquisition immediately. Fix retention first. Every new dollar of ads accelerates losses.
- **When building an affiliate program:** Give affiliates 100% of a peeled-off component rather than 20% of your main offer — enough to change their behavior, not just acknowledge the relationship.
- **When split testing:** Prioritize in order: offer → headline → images → third-party integrations. Most businesses never test #4, which produces the largest absolute gains.
- **When training a new hire on a performance skill:** Never use adjective-based instructions. Convert every instruction to an observable behavior. If you can't film someone doing it, the instruction is too vague.
- **When evaluating whether to add a new activity (speaking, new market, side project):** Calculate hard opportunity cost: daily sales × price × days away = minimum fee to make it worth it.
- **When a business has a persistent recurring struggle:** Diagnose it as a feature, not a bug. Optimize how you deal with it — don't try to eliminate what's structurally endemic.
- **When tempted to switch business models mid-constraint:** You've already started the solution. Changing now pays the cost of transition on top of still having the original problem. Sit with it.
- **When reactivation emails underperform:** Run 2–3 email mini-sequences per angle (savings, guilt, case study), 3–4 angles per year — not single monthly blasts. A themed sequence has story arc and follow-up logic. Expected lift: 20–30% of revenue, nearly all profit.
- **When customer says no to primary offer:** Make a completely different offer solving the same underlying problem before ending the conversation. A "no" to the offer is not a "no" to solving their pain.
- **When building a pricing menu:** Always include one anchor offer priced at 10x your primary offer. It makes everything below it look reasonable — even if you rarely close it.
- **When measuring LTV:** Use gross profit, not revenue. Software literature assumes 100% margins. Yours probably doesn't. A fully manual business needs 20:1 LTV:CAC minimum; fully automated can run at 3:1.
- **When you know something needs to happen eventually:** Do it today. Don't let the market or tide force it — that version costs more.
- **When your best affiliate/referral source exists:** Prioritize it over all new untested acquisition channels. It already proved ROI — the only question is volume.
- **When hiring into any role:** Ask how their specific function generates revenue. If they can't answer, they won't know what winning looks like.
- **When a founder wants to chase multiple channels simultaneously:** Ask which one has the highest leverage (lowest time or money per customer) and 10x that one before touching the others.
- **When cold traffic doesn't close:** You're trying to take off with a runway too short. Add the lead magnet, add the VSSL, give them the runway. A prospect needs a fixed quantity of information to buy — compress it into the funnel, not across 3 calls.
- **When setting continuity pricing:** Price it at what it would sell for standalone, not as a payment plan for the one-time value. If someone can't say "it's worth it for this one thing alone," the continuity product isn't real.
- **When a business still requires you to generate customers:** You cannot leave and you cannot sell. Fix acquisition first, then build the suit that runs without you.
- **When evaluating two candidates:** Pick the faster learner over the more experienced one. Intelligence is rate of learning.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

- **Competing on price.** 85% of companies try it; 1% win. There is no second-place prize for cheapest. The moment you win on price, someone cheaper emerges tomorrow.
- **Scaling before nailing.** Scaling a broken unit accelerates losses, thins margins, and creates operational chaos. The retention flywheel must exist before acquisition is turned up.
- **Building before posting.** Preparation is procrastination in disguise. "I'm working on it" without shipped content is invisible. Ship first, optimize later.
- **Pricing off your cost, not customer value.** If your margins feel impossible, you're pricing like a plastic table while delivering a hand-carved one. The fix isn't cutting costs — it's repositioning.
- **Portfolio thinking at the wrong stage.** Founders at $3–6M talking about building 4–5 brands before they have the margin, cash flow, or management bandwidth. Find the one winner and go all in.
- **Multiple businesses with no dedicated CEO per unit.** Revenue spread across entities with one operator is a vanity metric. "CEO of multiple companies" only works when you have actual CEOs running each one.
- **Sending cold traffic directly to a purchase CTA without a lead magnet and VSSL.** You're trying to take off on a runway too short. You'll kill conversion rate and blame the ads.
- **Amorphous training language.** "Be more confident" or "have more charisma" is a telephone game where everyone translates it differently and no one improves. If you can't film someone doing it, it's not an instruction.
- **Paying affiliate commissions that don't change behavior.** If it doesn't hurt a little to give, you haven't given enough. A 10–20% cut doesn't move anyone. Give them 100% of a peeled-off component.
- **Continuity priced as a payment plan for one-time value.** People exit the moment they've extracted the one-time value — usually before month 12. Price the ongoing piece at what it would sell for standalone.
- **Sunk cost continuation.** Spending money to justify past spending rather than to generate future return. Stop immediately.
- **Treating the sales call as a consultation.** Calling it a consultation frames you as the person being evaluated. Name it what it is — a sales call — and run it accordingly.
- **Constant small operational changes.** You're guaranteeing permanent 20% underperformance because nobody ever masters their current job. The business never gets to breathe. Improvement through stability compounds silently.
- **Honoring scarcity you don't enforce.** The moment you extend a deadline or sell past a cap, every future scarcity signal is worthless. You lose the compounding power of meaning what you say.
- **Chasing virality in B2B where customers have no incentive to share.** Virality requires both incentive to share AND high communication frequency between your customers and potential buyers. If either is absent, obsess over retention instead.
- **Adding features to solve churn in an education business.** More calls, more libraries, more Q&As won't fix structural churn if the underlying product is one-time value dressed as recurring. Solve the model, not the feature set.
- **Starting a second business because the first one is hard.** The reason you want a second business is usually that growing the first is hard. That's not a reason to start a second one — that's the problem to solve.
- **Revenue ego attachment to a local maximum.** Refusing to take a temporary revenue step down to fix the foundation. The fastest path to $10M is sometimes temporarily leaving $1M.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

- **Math first, implication second.** Never assert without the number that earns the assertion. State the hard number ("82% close rate," "38% margins," "10% price raise equals 25% increase in net profit"), let it land, then draw the business implication. Numbers make claims credible and replicable. Use them in every substantive point.

- **"And so" as the pivot into the point.** Hormozi uses "and so" constantly to signal the punchline is about to land: observation → "and so" → what that means for you. Replicate by structuring all arguments as: setup → consequence → application.

- **Story → Extract → Apply.** Never lead with the principle. Lead with a specific story (a named person, a specific situation). Extract the lesson in one clean sentence. Then apply it to the listener's situation. The story does the persuasion; the extraction does the teaching. No story without a direct lesson at the end.

- **Short declarative punch, then the explanation.** Lead with the claim in one sentence, then prove it. "Proof will always outdo promise. Here's why..." The memorable line comes first; the argument follows. Never bury the lead.

- **Binary choice frame.** Strip false nuance: "buy or build," "can't or won't," "have to or choose to." Reduce every complex decision to two named options, define each, ask which the person wants. This collapses ambiguity and forces commitment.

- **The "right?" affirmation loop.** Closes every example with "right?" to invite agreement before escalating. Functions as a verbal handshake — you nod, he escalates. "We fix the funnel first, then we increase spend, right? So you're not buying more traffic into a broken page, right?" It's punctuation, not a question.

- **The specific failure admission as credibility setup.** Not "I've made mistakes" — the exact mistake, the exact number. "I had nine failed businesses — six at the same time." Specific, embarrassing, quantified. This earns the floor before the lesson.

- **"I've had this conversation every week."** Uses frequency markers to signal a pattern is universal, not a personal judgment: "I have this conversation with someone doing e-commerce every single week." Removes ego threat from the diagnosis — you're not uniquely wrong, you're exhibiting a known pattern with a known fix.

- **Preempt the objection before it's raised.** States the objection the listener is forming, then defuses it: "Now you might be wondering why doesn't anyone do this — well it's usually an entirely different skill set." This controls the frame and builds credibility simultaneously.

- **"Said differently" as a reset.** After a conceptual explanation, restate it from a different angle: "Said differently — if you don't have an offer, there's nothing I can do for you." Handles the 40% who missed the first framing without slowing down.

- **The brutal reframe without apology.** "That's called sunk cost fallacy." "You're running a high-liability nonprofit." No softening, but no cruelty — naming the thing clearly and moving immediately to what to do next. Directness signals respect, not disrespect.

- **Compounding logic chains.** Arguments build on themselves with explicit connective tissue: "So that means... which means... and that's why... so the only question is..." Each step locks the listener in before the next one is added. By the end, the conclusion feels inevitable rather than sold.
