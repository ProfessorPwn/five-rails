## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Core Four**
Every business has exactly four ways to acquire customers: warm outreach (1-to-1, people you know), cold outreach (1-to-1, strangers), paid ads (1-to-many, strangers), and content (1-to-many, existing audience). You don't need all four — pick one, go all in, spend your first four hours every day on it. The mistake is dabbling across all four at 25% each instead of dominating one at 100%.

**BOOM — Business Order of Magnitude Change**
An optimization can only take you to 100% — it's capped by definition. Advertising (volume of reach) can 100x the number of people who know you exist. When you're stuck tweaking conversion rates from 10.1:1 to 10.2:1, you're playing a capped game. Get 100x the leads and the math handles itself. Apply this: before optimizing anything, ask whether simply doing more volume would compound harder.

**Proof Over Promise**
A business with 11,382 reviews at 4.7 stars and a plain offer will always beat one with a Grand Slam offer and one review from their mom. Proof is your single highest-priority asset. Before entering any new market or launching any product, run a free beta in exchange for testimonials, feedback, and referrals. Apply this: collect proof before you spend a dollar on advertising. Show don't sell — "state the facts, tell the truth."

**Clear Not Clever**
The president who speaks at the lowest grade level wins the election — it's not about dumbing down, it's about removing friction from comprehension. Every extra word of jargon is a tax on conversion. Apply this: run all ad copy, landing pages, and email sequences through a reading-level tool and get them below 5th grade. Restate complex ideas with analogies drawn from your audience's lived experience (mechanics → cars, realtors → houses).

**The 70/20/10 Steal-From-Yourself Rule (Sergey Brin ratio)**
70% of all creative and strategic effort should be carbon-copying what already works. 20% should be adjacent variations — same hook, different shirt, different background. Only 10% should be genuinely new experiments. Most entrepreneurs have this inverted: 70% new ideas, 20% slight variations, 10% repeating proven stuff. Apply this: identify your one highest-converting hook and use it relentlessly until the numbers break.

**The 3.5:1 Give-to-Ask Ratio**
For every one "ask" (buy, click, sign up), you need 3.5 "gives" (education, entertainment, free value). This ratio is validated across TV ad breaks, Facebook feed algorithms, and TikTok's early growth model. Apply this: audit your content calendar and ad mix — if more than 22% of impressions are asking for something, you're burning audience faster than you're building it.

**The Five Levels of Awareness (Eugene Schwartz)**
Buyers exist on a spectrum: Unaware → Problem Aware → Solution Aware → Product Aware → Most Aware. Direct response only works on the tiny "Most Aware" sliver. To scale, you must allocate budget to educate buyers further up the funnel. Apply this: map your ad creative to awareness levels — broad curiosity hooks for unaware audiences, symptom-based hooks for problem-aware, comparison content for solution-aware.

**High Info vs. Low Info Buyers Continuum**
It's not "logical vs. emotional" buyers — that's a false binary. It's a spectrum of information required before purchase. Low-info buyers are rare and fought over. High-info buyers are an order of magnitude larger. Apply this: if you only run direct response, you're fishing from a tiny pond. Build content and brand that moves high-info buyers down the continuum over time.

**The New Balance 70/30 Brand Flip**
CMO Chris Davis flipped New Balance from 30% brand / 70% direct response to 70% brand / 30% direct response. Revenue took 18 months to respond — then it compounded. Apply this: if you're serious about scale (not just survival), 70% of your marketing impressions should be giving value, building association, and earning trust — not asking for a sale.

**More / Better / New**
Three and only three growth strategies exist: do MORE of what works, make what works BETTER, or do something entirely NEW. "More" is the highest risk-adjusted return because you've already proven it works. Apply this: before greenfielding anything, ask whether simply doubling volume of the proven thing would outperform any optimization or new initiative.

**Volatility = Low Volume**
When sales feel random — one deal this week, nothing for two weeks — it's not bad luck, it's insufficient activity. Take your annual customer count, spread it daily, and calculate how much volume would be required to normalize that into a daily result. Apply this: if you feel like your results are inconsistent, 9 times out of 10 you need to 10x activity, not fix your offer.

**Masters Have More Metrics (Leading vs. Lagging Indicators)**
Beginners measure binary outcomes: sale / no sale, leads / no leads. Masters measure the pipeline of micro-events that precede those outcomes: list quality, call pick-up rate, hook-to-minute-one retention, show-up rate for second calls. Apply this: map every step between "prospect exists" and "sale closed," assign a metric to each, and move the bottleneck systematically.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Someone asks why your advertising isn't working despite a great offer | **Script:** "Before we change the offer — who's seeing it? If I showed winter coats to people in South Florida, nothing about the offer would save it. The wrong audience makes every offer look broken."

**Situation:** Responding to a competitor or critic publicly | **Script:** "You're right. I'm a super flawed person. That said, it looks like you've built a real audience here and they seem to like you — congrats on the success." [Then stop. Say nothing more.]

**Situation:** Someone pushes back that you're repeating yourself / recycling content | **Script:** "Seneca's been dead 2,000 years. I still love seeing his quotes. You think they're tired of it — but most people haven't heard it once yet."

**Situation:** Someone says your market is too competitive | **Script:** "You only need 200 members to run a profitable gym. Your city has a million people. Even if there are 100 other gyms advertising on the same platform, you need 0.002% of the audience. The market isn't the problem."

**Situation:** A team member says results from a new strategy aren't there yet | **Script:** "What's the test size? You can't know anything from 300. My mentor tested with 5,000 a day for 30 days — 150,000 total — before calling it. We're nowhere near a real sample."

**Situation:** Pitching yourself in a crowded commodity category | **Script:** "Slice the data until you find something true and unique — even if others do it too but don't say so. 'It's toasted.' Nobody else was saying it. First claim wins."

**Situation:** Launching a new product or entering a new market | **Script:** "I'm not charging you. I'll do this for free. All I ask is you tell me exactly what works, what doesn't, and if it goes well, maybe a testimonial. That's the trade."

**Situation:** Someone is spending $1K/day on ads and thinks they're maxed out | **Script:** "There are businesses spending $2 million a day. You have more runway than you think. Nothing breaks — the platforms want you to spend, they built the system for it."

**Situation:** Defending why you're still running the same hook after years | **Script:** "Nike didn't retire 'Just Do It' in year two. When you find a message that converts, you keep hammering it. You'll get bored of it long before your customers do."

**Situation:** Introducing yourself / establishing credibility on a new platform | **Script:** "I'm here to make money — I'll say it upfront. And because I say that, when I do something useful, nobody can say I had a hidden agenda. Everything after that lands cleaner."

**Situation:** Explaining why you work for free at the start | **Script:** "Never work for free is advice from people who don't make that much money. The free phase is when you make the product right. That's the highest-leverage thing you can do."

**Situation:** Pitching why content volume matters more than content variety | **Script:** "I launched a book with 7 million subscribers across all platforms, emailed everyone, ran ads, had affiliates — and people I stopped on the street didn't know the book existed. If you think you've said it enough, you haven't."

**Situation:** Selling B2B through content instead of ads | **Script:** "There are three degrees of proof: I did it myself, I helped someone like you do it, or you used my free stuff and did it yourself. Zero degrees of separation always wins. That's why the books are free."

**Situation:** Explaining why you're cutting a slow-building brand investment | **Script:** "You're not pulling forward demand — you're draining the well someone else dug. And when it dries up, it takes 18 months to refill, not one quarter."

**Situation:** Framing a seemingly weak offer as uniquely positioned | **Script:** "You don't need to be the best in the world. You need to be the best in a puddle. It's more important that you ARE the best than what you are the best OF."

**Situation:** Defending running the same ad creative again | **Script:** "All our competitors are copying what's working. If I change the thing that's working, I'm doing their job for them and breaking my own building in the process."

**Situation:** Explaining to a team why a new channel looks slow | **Script:** "Heart surgery looks like murder to someone who doesn't know the milestones. We fixed the list, then the call pick-up rate, then the hook, then the offer, then the show-up rate. Look for directional correctness, not binary results."

**Situation:** Justifying overpaying for a market position (acquisition, sponsorship, collab) | **Script:** "Rockefeller overpaid for that refinery on purpose. Once he could say he was the biggest, 20 M&A deals closed in 30 days. He bought the story, not the asset. The brand value more than paid for the premium."

**Situation:** Coaching a business owner to improve email conversion | **Script:** "Don't change what you're saying. Change the grade level you're saying it at. I rewrote the same email sequences below fifth grade — didn't change the offer, didn't change the angle — and got a 50% lift in conversion."

**Situation:** Someone says you need to beat competitors | **Script:** "You only have two options: grow so big nobody can hear them — or kill them with kindness. Either your shadow blocks their sunlight, or you respond with grace and they look small by comparison. There's no third option worth your time."

## DECISION RULES

- **When under $1M in revenue:** Dedicate the first four hours of every day to solving obscurity — cold outreach, warm outreach, content, or paid ads. Pick one. Go all in.

- **When choosing between optimization and volume:** Default to volume. A 10% improvement in close rate is a 10% lift. Hiring three more salespeople at the same close rate is a 300% lift. Volume wins until proven otherwise.

- **When you find a hook that converts:** Carbon-copy it. Allocate 70% of all creative effort to replicating exactly what works, 20% to adjacent variations, 10% to genuinely new experiments.

- **When entering a new market or launching a new product:** Work for free first. Get testimonials, feedback, and referrals before spending on acquisition. Proof collection is job one.

- **When your ad copy is written:** Run it through a reading-level tool. Rewrite until it's below 5th grade. Only then send it.

- **When your avatar is narrow (niche industry):** Use analogies from their world — mechanics get car analogies, realtors get house analogies. Broader audience = broader human analogy (food, sleep, driving).

- **When results feel volatile and inconsistent:** Don't change the offer. Calculate what daily volume would be required to normalize your annual close rate into a daily result. You don't have a conversion problem; you have a volume problem.

- **When a competitor is gaining visibility in your market:** Stop tracking them. Grow loud enough that their signal disappears under yours. The response to competition is always more output, not defensive positioning.

- **When evaluating whether a strategy is working:** Reject binary success/failure framing. Map all micro-steps between activity and outcome, assign metrics, and look for directional correctness. Move the bottleneck.

- **When scaling advertising spend:** Assume you have 10x more runway than feels comfortable. Platforms want you to spend. Audiences haven't heard of you yet. Most perceived limits are mental, not real.

- **When allocating marketing budget at scale:** Target 70% to brand/awareness/education and 30% to direct response. Expect an 18-month lag before the brand investment compounds. Don't touch the ratio when it looks like it's not working.

- **When targeting an ad:** Match the person in the ad to the avatar you're targeting. If you want women to convert, don't feature yourself. The visual signals who the product is for before the copy does.

- **When someone attacks you publicly:** Start your response with "You're right." Don't enumerate counterpoints — that only invites more rounds. Concede the surface, redirect to kindness, stop.

- **When deciding whether to do something new vs. repeat what works:** Demolishing a building takes five minutes. Building it took two years. Every deviation from a working system is more likely to destroy it than improve it.

- **When measuring a function you want to improve:** First identify all leading indicators — the upstream micro-events that predict the lagging outcome. If you can only name the lagging outcome, you're not yet equipped to fix anything.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**The Optimization Mouse Trap.** Obsessing over 10.1:1 to 10.2:1 ROAS while ignoring the fact that 100x the volume would transform the business. Optimizations are capped at 100%. Volume is uncapped. If you're micro-tuning, you've already lost the bigger game.

**Shiny Object Syndrome.** Spending 70% of energy on new ideas, 20% on variations, and 10% on proven repeatable execution — the exact inversion of the correct ratio. The new idea feels productive. It almost never outperforms the thing that already works.

**The "I've Said It Already" Fallacy.** Believing that because you've stated something, your audience has absorbed and acted on it. Most people in your audience haven't heard you say it once. Repetition is not a flaw — it is the strategy.

**Confusing Volatility for Bad Positioning.** When sales are inconsistent, most people change their offer or creative. The real culprit is almost always insufficient volume. Diagnosing variance as a signal problem when it's actually a sample size problem.

**Leading With Promise, Not Proof.** Spending resources on crafting a better guarantee or a more compelling offer before assembling social proof. A grand slam offer with one review loses to a plain offer with 11,000 reviews every time.

**Advertising to the Wrong Avatar.** Blaming the offer, the copy, or the price when the real issue is showing the right thing to the wrong people. If conversion is zero, check targeting before touching creative.

**Only Advertising to Most-Aware Buyers (Direct Response Only Trap).** Direct response reaches the tiny sliver of people ready to buy right now. Over-indexing on this channel caps your growth ceiling and forces you to compete in the most crowded, most expensive segment of your market.

**Complexity as Intelligence.** Using advanced vocabulary to seem credible. It signals status to no one and loses conversions from everyone who can't follow. Speaking above your audience's grade level is ego serving your vanity, not your bank account.

**Pulling Forward Demand Without Digging the Next Well.** Cutting brand investment to hit short-term revenue targets. The well runs dry, and refilling it takes 18 months — not one quarter. This is the move that marks when founders exit and executives destroy the business.

**Changing a Control to Test Creativity.** Replacing a proven ad, hook, or script because you're bored of it. Your boredom is not a data signal. The audience's behavior is. Change is most likely to break what works, not improve it.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**"And so" as the connective tissue.** Nearly every logical transition is bridged with "and so." It creates a causal chain that makes the argument feel inevitable rather than asserted. Listeners follow without resistance because each step appears to flow from the last.

**State the flawed assumption, then detonate it.** Setup always names the conventional wisdom first ("So here's what most people think..."), lets it sit for a beat, then destroys it with a specific counter-example or data point. The listener feels smart for having believed the wrong thing and smarter for now knowing the right one.

**Specificity as credibility.** Never "a lot of reviews" — always "11,382 reviews at a 4.7." Never "we grew fast" — always "$1.2M/month by end of year one." The precision signals that the speaker lived this, not theorized it. Use exact numbers even when approximations would do.

**The embedded self-correction.** Mid-sentence, catch yourself using a complex word, flag it explicitly, and replace it: "...which is comprehensible — that's a fancy word for understandable." This pattern performs humility while demonstrating mastery. It shows the speaker is rigorous enough to police their own language in real time.

**Story → Principle → Reapplication.** The rhetorical loop: tell a specific story (flyers, Rockefeller, New Balance), extract the principle in one tight sentence, then immediately apply that principle back to the listener's situation. The story earns attention, the principle transfers value, the reapplication makes it actionable before the listener can object.

**"The real is..."** Used to signal that everything before was preamble and what follows is the actual insight. Functions as a verbal punch to the jaw — listeners who zoned out snap back in. Deploy sparingly, once per major point maximum.

**Preemptive self-disclosure to disarm critics.** Claim your own weaknesses before anyone else can: "I'm here to make money — I'll say it upfront." By owning the attack surface, you neutralize it. Every subsequent action is judged against your disclosed, accurate framing rather than an adversarial one.
