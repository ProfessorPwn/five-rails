## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The One-Thing Ramrod**
Before optimizing anything else, identify the single variable that — if true alone — solves the core constraint. Everything else is noise until that one thing is proven. Apply it by asking: "If only X were true, what stops me?" Work backwards from there and kill everything else until it's done.

**Cash-Flow-Positive Offer Engineering**
Structure every offer so that cash collected on transaction day exceeds your hard cost of goods plus your cost of acquisition — fully loaded. Don't design for margin over time; design so Day 1 is positive. To apply: list hard costs (labor, materials, CAC, commissions), sum them, then reverse-engineer the offer price and commitment structure to exceed that number on the first payment.

**The AB Anchor Offer**
Always present two tiers: a premium anchor (e.g., annual commitment, free bonus item) and a core offer. Even if only 20% take the anchor, the blended average cash-per-door rises enough to fund operations. Present anchor first — "Would you like X for free if you commit to a year?" Then step down: "If not, here's the core deal."

**The Value Equation Lever Audit**
When stuck on pricing, list every tangible asset or friction you control: the physical product, setup fees, processing fees, commitment length, QR codes, branding. Each is a lever. Pull them one by one to build toward your cash-positive threshold rather than cutting price. Apply by listing all levers before touching the base price.

**The Fewest Need-to-Believes Rule**
A business plan is only as strong as the number of assumptions it requires. Every additional "and then this also has to be true" exponentially reduces execution probability. Strip your plan to the minimum viable set of assumptions and attack only those. If it works with one assumption (e.g., "10 commission-only reps close 5 doors/day"), don't add a second until the first is proven.

**Commoditization Escape: Efficiency Is the Product**
In any market that auctions on lowest bid, the only durable edge is operational efficiency — not price. If you cannot escape the auction (e.g., HOA contracts), re-frame your strategic identity as an efficiency business, not a service business. Your pricing and acquisition should then reflect speed, density, and route optimization rather than feature differentiation.

**Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate**
The only three steps for training commission salespeople at scale. Write the script (document). Do it in front of them on live doors (demonstrate). Ride shotgun while they do it and give real-time feedback (duplicate). Do not advance to the next step until the previous one is owned. Role-play between every set of doors; repeat until they say it in their sleep.

**The Hunger Screen for Commission Hires**
For commission-only roles, interview skill is irrelevant — it doesn't correlate with door-to-door performance. Screen for circumstances that create urgency: someone who recently had a kid, has bills they can't pay, just left a dead-end job. Hungry people in hard situations self-select for work ethic. Conduct group calls, not one-on-ones. Invite the normal-and-hungry ones. Give offers on the spot.

**Blended Average Cash Per Door**
The real unit economics metric for door-to-door is not close rate — it's total cash collected divided by total doors knocked. Factor in your offer mix (e.g., 80% core, 20% anchor) to calculate the blended average. Design the offer until this number exceeds your all-in cost. Apply by modeling different conversion splits before going to market.

**The Star Trainer System**
Never let your best closer train alone. Shadow them constantly — two birds, one stone: you're producing revenue AND training. Pay the trainer a premium ($60/deal vs. $50) to compensate for the drag of a trainee. The moment someone else becomes the better closer, they become the new lead trainer and you step back.

**The Double-Dip on Effort**
Any high-leverage skill you possess should never be done solo — always with a learner watching. You don't lose output; you multiply it. Your time budget stays the same; your team's skill compounds. Apply to any repeatable, in-person, trainable task where you are currently the only practitioner.

**Pipeline = Date + Cadence, Not Volume**
For long-cycle, low-volume, high-value prospects (e.g., HOA contract renewals on 3–5 year cycles), the acquisition strategy is not cold email at scale — it's a simple calendar with personal touches. If you have 500 targets on 3-year cycles, you need to visit ~2 per day. That's it. Volume-based outreach is the wrong tool for this sales motion.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening a door-to-door pitch for a commodity service | **Script:** "If you're tired of increasing bills every year, unreliable pickups, and surprise fees — I might be able to help. Here's the deal: [pain → pain → pain], then [offer]. Does that sound like something worth two minutes?"

**Situation:** Presenting the AB offer at the door | **Script:** "We give everyone three months free just for switching — no matter what. But if you're willing to commit to a full year, I'll throw in the bin on us. It's a $99 value. Or if you'd rather just do three months upfront, totally fine — you just pay for the bin separately and we waive the setup fee."

**Situation:** Customer says "my current provider matched your price" | **Script:** "That's fair — but they won't give you three months free. That's ours. They can match the rate; they can't match that."

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it" | **Script:** "Totally. What's the one thing that would need to be different for this to feel like a no-brainer right now?"

**Situation:** Justifying an upfront bin fee | **Script:** "The bin is actually yours — you keep it. It's $99 and it's yours forever as long as you're a customer. If you ever cancel, we take it back. But most people just keep the service, so it ends up being your bin."

**Situation:** Handling "I don't want to pay an initiation fee" | **Script:** "No worries — if you sign up today I can waive that. I just need you to take the first quarter upfront and we're good."

**Situation:** Collecting payment at the door | **Script:** "We do charge a small processing fee on card — it's just because when a card declines it interrupts your service and creates work on both sides. Honestly, no one ends up paying it — they just give us a backup form of payment."

**Situation:** Recruiting commission-only door-to-door reps via job post | **Script:** "If you can memorize four questions and you're willing to be outside, you can make six figures a year. We don't care about your resume. We care about your work ethic. It'll sometimes be hot. It'll sometimes be cold. That's the trade. If you're someone who just needs an opportunity to prove yourself, we want to talk."

**Situation:** Group interview opener for commission hires | **Script:** "You've got two minutes — tell me who you are, why you want this, and what you're trying to accomplish. That's all I need."

**Situation:** Training session between doors | **Script:** "Okay — we've got 45 seconds to the next door. You're the homeowner. I knock. Go. [Role play.] Good. Now you knock. I'm the homeowner. Go."

**Situation:** Customer pushes back on price, citing competitor | **Script:** "What are you paying now? [Pause.] Okay, so here's the math — even at our standard rate you're saving [X]. Plus the three free months on top. What would have to happen for this to make sense today?"

**Situation:** Pitching the QR-code opt-out upsell | **Script:** "We put a QR code on the bin for marketing. A lot of people don't mind — but if you'd rather it look clean, it's just five more a month and we pull the sticker. Most people just say leave it."

**Situation:** Presenting the financing option when customer balks at the bin purchase | **Script:** "No problem — we can finance it over three years for you. It just comes out to $150 over the term instead of the $99 upfront. Your call."

**Situation:** Closing a hesitant HOA prospect on a contract bid | **Script:** "We know you're locked in until [date]. We're not here to pressure you — we just want to be the first call you make when it's time. What would make us the obvious choice when that window opens?"

**Situation:** Diagnosing a business owner's core constraint fast | **Script:** "If only one thing were true in order for this to work — just one — what would it be? And if that were true, what stops you? And if that were solved, what would stop you then?"

**Situation:** Reframing a complicated offer as simple for the sales floor | **Script:** "Here's what you tell them: 'You can pay for the year and we give you the bin free. Or you pay for three months and the bin is $99. That's the whole decision.' Done."

**Situation:** Countering "I don't have the card I want on me" at door close | **Script:** "No problem. Give me a second form of payment as a backup — doesn't need to be the one you prefer. We just need something on file so your service never gets interrupted."

**Situation:** Explaining why you're going commission-only for growth | **Script:** "I'd rather give more people a shot to prove their work ethic than hire someone who's good at interviewing. Different skill."

**Situation:** Talking to a prospect who's been burned by their current provider | **Script:** "That's exactly why we're here. We're local, we're owner-operated, and if something goes wrong you call me directly. Not a 1-800 number."

**Situation:** Pitching a senior salesperson a trainer role | **Script:** "You'd still be closing deals — I'm just asking you to have someone with you. I'll bump your commission to $60 because it does slow you down a bit. But you're basically building the team while you sell. That's a different kind of leverage."

## DECISION RULES

**When you have two avatars and five acquisition channels and you're under $1M revenue, pick one of each and cut the rest.** Spread is the reason you feel stretched thin and get mediocre results everywhere.

**When your gross margin is low due to one-off costs (e.g., a bad repair year), don't make permanent pricing decisions on distorted numbers.** Strip out the anomaly, model normalized margins, then decide.

**When you're deciding between a high-margin/low-volume customer type and a low-margin/high-volume one, ask which one lets you win on acquisition speed — and weight that heavily.** Speed of customer acquisition compounds faster than per-unit margin at early stage.

**When a market is 70%+ dominated by contract-based HOAs that will always auction on price, don't make HOA acquisition your growth strategy.** Compete there defensively; grow offensively in the open scatter market.

**When you can't figure out why you're cash-flow negative, solve for Day 1 cash collected > (hard COGS + CAC + commission).** If Day 1 is positive, the business math works. If not, restructure the offer before scaling anything.

**When your ad spend starts returning diminishing results in a narrow geo, don't increase ad budget — switch to a channel with lower geographic saturation (door-to-door, direct mail, personal outreach).** Meta frequency kills efficiency in small markets faster than you expect.

**When a sales rep is commission-only and door-to-door, don't do one-on-one interviews.** Run group calls, screen for sanity and hunger, and give offers on the spot to the ones who seem normal and motivated.

**When you're training a new rep, never let them go solo until they can run the full script without hesitation in a role-play.** Don't let live doors be their practice reps.

**When building an offer, structure it so that even if 80% take the lower tier, you're cash-flow positive — then design an anchor that makes the top 20% blow your economics wide open.**

**When a commodity service is price-matched by a competitor, don't lower price — add a time-locked bonus that the competitor structurally cannot match (e.g., free months, physical asset included).** Competing on price in a commodity market is a race you will always lose.

**When you have a high-converting channel (e.g., 1-in-12 door conversion), don't dilute attention by running five other channels simultaneously.** Double down on what's working and let volume prove it before diversifying.

**When hiring for physically demanding, commission-driven outdoor sales, weight circumstances over credentials.** Someone who has to make money will outwork someone who wants to.

**When a prospect is locked in with a competitor, don't push for a close — get on the renewal calendar and deliver one high-touch touch point per quarter.** You're farming, not hunting.

**When you add a new offer tier, always calculate the blended average across your expected conversion mix before presenting it to salespeople.** If the blended number doesn't exceed your threshold, rework the offer, not the reps.

**When cash flow is the binding constraint, solve for it in the offer structure before touching marketing spend.** More leads into a cash-flow-negative model just accelerates the hole.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Doing door-to-door without collecting payment on the spot.** A signed contract with no payment instrument is a lead, not a sale. If they're willing to sign, they're willing to pay. Get the card.

**Pricing quarterly but thinking monthly.** Misaligned billing periods distort your cash flow math. Know your exact cash timeline per customer before designing offers.

**Running cold email at scale to a market of 500 HOAs.** When the total addressable universe is tiny, cold email creates noise and burns goodwill. Personal, high-touch outreach at a sustainable cadence (2/day) dominates.

**Training salespeople without a documented script.** Modeling and feedback are useless without a baseline script to deviate from. No script = no measurable improvement.

**Splitting attention across two avatars before proving unit economics on one.** This doubles your acquisition complexity, halves your learning speed, and makes it impossible to optimize.

**Anchoring your self-perception on your worst months' economics.** One-off costs (equipment failures, bad hires) distort margins. Never make strategic pricing or growth decisions on anomaly-driven numbers.

**Building a business model that requires debt to grow.** If every new customer requires upfront capital that won't return for 90 days, your growth rate is capped by your balance sheet. Engineer Day 1 cash recovery instead.

**Letting the competitor's pricing define your floor.** When you say "we had to lower prices because of the competitor," you've ceded your pricing power. Add a structural offer element they can't match instead of racing to the bottom.

**Doing one-on-one interviews for commission-only roles.** It optimizes for interviewing skill, not hustle. You end up hiring articulate people who can't close doors.

**Doing everything yourself because you're "not sure" you can find people to do it.** Commission-only sales is zero-risk hiring. There's no payroll exposure. The constraint isn't "can I find people" — it's "have I actually tried running a real job ad to the right audience."

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Pain → Identity → Offer sequence.** Lead with the specific pain the prospect is living ("if you're tired of X, Y, Z"), then establish credibility briefly, then land the offer. Never lead with your name or your company. The prospect doesn't care until they recognize their pain in your words.

**Numbers first, then logic.** Hormozi never makes a qualitative argument when a quantitative one is available. "If 10 guys close 5 deals a day, that's 1,500 sales a month, that's 20,000 customers by year-end." Always anchor an assertion with the math that proves it before moving on.

**The "if only X were true" reframe.** When a situation is complex and multi-variable, strip it to the single highest-leverage assumption. State it explicitly: "If only that one thing were true, what else would need to be true?" This isolates the real constraint from the noise.

**Verbal math in real time.** Hormozi does arithmetic out loud as he builds the offer — "89 plus 99 is 188, call it 189, and your hard cost is 158 plus 50 commission is 208, and you're collecting 220, so you're net positive." This is not sloppy — it's deliberate. Showing your work in real time builds trust and keeps the counterpart tracking rather than doubting.

**Damaging admission as credibility.** When recruiting or selling, proactively surface the real downside before they ask: "It'll sometimes be hot. It'll sometimes be cold. That's the trade." Prospects and recruits who don't flinch are the right ones. It also disarms skepticism about the pitch being too good.

**"That's it" as a pattern interrupt.** After laying out a complex plan, compress it: "AB offer. Two guys. Four questions. That's it." The contrast between complexity and simplicity signals mastery and creates relief. Use it as a closing beat after a long build.

**The rhetorical question that answers itself.** "You know why married men with kids make more money than single guys without? Because they have to." The structure asks a question the listener is already leaning toward, then confirms it immediately. It creates the feeling of insight without the friction of persuasion.
