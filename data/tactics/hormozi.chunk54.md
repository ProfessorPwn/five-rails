## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Four Components of Mental Toughness**
Mental toughness isn't one thing — it's four measurable stats: Tolerance (how much hardship before behavior changes), Fortitude (how far you drop when you do snap), Resilience (how fast you return to baseline), and Adaptability (whether your new baseline is higher or lower). Stop telling yourself or your team to "toughen up" — that helps no one. Diagnose which of the four stats is low, then drill that specific component.

**2. The Theory of Constraints**
Every business has exactly one bottleneck that is limiting all growth right now. Solve it and everything else gets easier or becomes irrelevant. Identify it relentlessly; once removed, immediately reassess — the next constraint will be different than you expected.

**3. The Tree / Pruning Model**
Picture your business as a tree. Every B+ problem you work on is a branch shoot — it siphons resources sideways instead of up the trunk. Revenue stays flat not because you're lazy but because your energy is dispersed. Commitment means cutting the shoots so everything feeds the one vertical that makes the tree taller.

**4. Big Wheel vs. Small Wheel**
Most people ask "how do I spin my wheel faster?" The better question is: "how do I swap to a bigger wheel?" One revolution of the right lever covers more ground than ten rotations of a minor task. Before starting any sprint, ask: is this a big wheel or a small wheel? Only sprint on big wheels.

**5. The CLOSER Sales Framework**
Clarify they're qualified → Label their problem out loud → Overview their past experience → Sell the vacation (three-step pitch) → Explain away concerns → Reinforce the decision. This is the skeleton. Everything else — tone, pauses, questions — serves this skeleton. Never deviate from the skeleton.

**6. The Five Voice Modulation Elements (3 Constants, 2 Variables)**
Constants you never change: (1) volume loud enough to be heard clearly, (2) pace between 135–185 wpm, (3) full articulation of every word. Variables that create natural rhythm: pauses (short = draw attention; long = solicit response) and pitch (voice rising = asking a question, inviting reply; voice flat/falling = declarative statement). Every "tone" note on a script reduces to these five levers — nothing else.

**7. Script Memorization by Deletion**
Print two copies of your script. Read the full script aloud once. Cross out one word. Read again. Repeat until the page is blacked out and you're saying the script from memory. By the end, you've run the script as many reps as it has words. Outcome: you stop thinking about what you're saying and start listening to the prospect — which is where sales actually happen.

**8. Discovery → Chunk Up → Match → Close**
Discovery is where most salespeople lose deals, not the close. Pull specific examples out of vague complaints ("Can you give me an example of that?"). Once you have two or three concrete pain points, chunk them up to the categories you sell: "So it sounds like you have a marketing problem and a pricing problem — is that fair?" Get agreement on the diagnosis. Then sell them the cure they just agreed they need.

**9. Commitment = Elimination of Alternatives**
Saying you're committed is easy. Showing commitment means removing every path that isn't the priority. Marriage: eliminate all other people. Diet: pour out the alcohol, clear the fridge. Business: explicitly tell the team that everything else can burn. If the alternatives still exist, the commitment is performative.

**10. Prioritize → Urgency → Eliminate → Reassess (4-Step Focus Loop)**
Step 1: Name the one thing that moves the needle most. Step 2: Set a deadline and meeting cadence that signals urgency (end-of-day, not end-of-week). Step 3: Publicly remove everything else from your team's plates — tell them which fires are allowed to burn. Step 4: After solving, reassess the list fresh — don't assume the old priority #2 is still relevant.

**11. The Universal Reframe Close**
When a prospect gives any non-logistical objection (time, money, need to think, talk to spouse), respond: "I think that's actually the very reason you should do this." If they say "I don't have money," say the lack of money is exactly why they need the thing that generates money. This isn't a trick — it's true for any real solution. Use it to earn the right to ask one more time.

**12. Proof Above Everything**
The best way to get someone to believe is to have already done it. The entire pre-event experience — the content, the team, the campaign — should exist as proof before you ever make the ask. If they believe you, price is easy to justify. If they don't, no script saves you.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening a prioritization meeting where people are afraid to admit problems | **Script:** "As of this moment, there is Amnesia from the past. No one here will be responsible for anything that happened before today. If I ask who caused something, I'm asking to understand the problem — not to blame anyone. Going forward, we're all on the same team."

**Situation:** Communicating to a team that they must let lower-priority fires burn | **Script:** "There is one massive fire right here that will burn everything to a crisp far more than these B fires will. That means it's okay that these burn. [Name], if CS complains about slow turnarounds, tell them I said it's fine. [Name], if legal is pushing on contracts, tell them I said it's fine — I pay everyone's paychecks and I'm telling you: let it burn."

**Situation:** Assigning tasks and verifying comprehension | **Script:** "Tell me back what you're going to do next — right now." [If they get it wrong:] "Let me say it again. You're going to do X, then Y, then Z. Say it back to me. Now write it down."

**Situation:** Prospect gives a vague one-word answer in discovery | **Script:** "Can you give me an example of that?" [After they answer:] "Can you give me a specific example — like what's the difference between this year and last year?" [Repeat variations until concrete.]

**Situation:** Chunking up discovery into a diagnosed problem | **Script:** "So just to make sure I have this right — you're not getting leads consistently. Is that right? So it sounds like you have a marketing issue. Is that fair to say? Great. What else do you think is holding back your growth?"

**Situation:** Transitioning from discovery to pitch | **Script:** "Real quick before I dive in — it sounds like you said you have inconsistent lead flow and your profit per sale isn't where it needs to be. So it sounds like we've got a marketing issue and a pricing and packaging issue. Is that fair? Okay. If we solve both of those, do you think it would help grow the business?"

**Situation:** Getting agreement before the pitch | **Script:** "Now that you've agreed that what I'm about to show you would help you grow — can I tell you about our thing? Because I think it'll help you out given what you just told me."

**Situation:** Matching solution to each diagnosed problem | **Script:** "For the marketing issue — you said lead flow is inconsistent. Do you think it would help if you sat down with someone who's done $106 million in sales and only works with businesses like yours? Great. Problem one solved. Second issue: we can't really do the marketing without fixing the gross margin first, because you need margin to fuel the machine. Is that right? So if we brought in our pricing expert who's worked with 2,000 home services businesses for six years — do you think they could help with that? Yes? Great. Then I think you'd be a perfect fit. Ready to rock and roll?"

**Situation:** After asking for the close, prospect goes silent | **Script:** [Say nothing. Wait up to 8 seconds. Do not fill the space.] Research shows waiting up to 8 seconds after asking someone to buy closes 30% more sales.

**Situation:** Prospect raises a non-logistical objection after the close | **Script:** "I think [the reason they gave] is actually the very reason you should do this. The fact that you don't have money is exactly why you need something that solves that. The problem you're giving me as a reason not to buy is the exact problem this solves."

**Situation:** Prospect stalls with "I need to think about it" | **Script:** Follow any of the seven universal closes. The goal is not to be right — it's to earn the right to ask again. Reframe, then re-ask. Do not argue. Winning an argument never closed a sale.

**Situation:** Coaching a salesperson who sounds unnatural or nervous | **Script:** "You're not speaking loudly enough. You're talking too fast. Fully pronounce every word — that alone will slow you down. You don't sound unnatural because you're following a script; you sound unnatural because you haven't memorized it yet. Go run the deletion drill."

**Situation:** Salesperson claims they need a custom rebuttal for every objection | **Script:** "You don't need 40 custom overcomes. Memorize seven universal closes. Every objection except a real logistical problem is just an excuse to ask again. Reframe their reason as the reason to buy, then ask."

**Situation:** Team member resists dropping their current work to focus on the #1 priority | **Script:** "I know you want to do a good job — that's why you're still working on those things. But here's the reality: if we don't fix this one thing, none of the other stuff matters. Once we fix this, we reassess everything. You are not in trouble for letting those burn. I'm telling you to let them burn."

**Situation:** Deciding what to prioritize next after solving the current constraint | **Script:** Pull up the full list, look at it with fresh eyes. Ask: "Given what just changed, which of these moves the needle most now?" Don't assume the old #2 is still relevant. Reprioritize every time.

**Situation:** Selling something expensive with a skeptical buyer | **Script:** "The best way to protect your downside here — if that alone could work, would that be worth it? I'll sort this out for you and in 30 minutes we'll call you back and figure it out."

**Situation:** Asking someone to commit to a deadline | **Script:** Switch from weekly meetings to end-of-day or twice-a-day check-ins. The cadence communicates the urgency more than any speech.

**Situation:** Making winning visible to a team | **Script:** Put a progress tracker somewhere everyone sees it. Name a pot of gold: "The day we fix this, we all go to dinner / everyone gets the afternoon off." Make the target obvious, make winning feel real, and make what's in it for them visible.

**Situation:** Framing a traumatic or crushing setback | **Script:** "If you were the main character in a movie that has a happy ending — and the protagonist just got passed up for the promotion, lost the deal, got broken up with — what would the character do right now to move the plot toward the happy ending? Do that."

## DECISION RULES

**When you have a list of five priorities, do only #1.** After solving it, reassess the entire list. Items 2–5 may have become irrelevant or a completely different #1 may have emerged. Working 2–5 first is almost always wasted effort.

**When something on your list makes you feel productive but doesn't move the needle, cut it.** Ask: "Would solving this make any of my other problems irrelevant?" If no, it's a B+ problem. Let it burn.

**When the pixel (or core tracking/data infrastructure) is broken, fix it before touching anything else.** Conversion rate optimization on top of bad data is permanent waste. Infrastructure before optimization, always.

**When a prospect gives a non-logistical objection, reframe and ask again.** Never try to win the argument. Every "overcome" is just an excuse to ask one more time.

**When a salesperson sounds nervous or mechanical, diagnose before coaching.** "Sound more confident" is useless feedback. "Speak louder," "slow down," or "fully pronounce every word" are actionable.

**When you ask for the close, shut up for at least 8 seconds.** The first person to talk after the ask loses the most leverage. Silence is not awkward — it's working.

**When a team member has too many things on their plate, don't add the new priority — subtract everything else first.** It's not fair to make something "most important" while leaving the full workload in place.

**When your cadence for a priority is weekly, it's not actually a priority.** Move to daily or twice-daily check-ins to signal real urgency.

**When you're in discovery and getting one-word answers, don't pitch.** Keep pulling thread: "Can you give me an example?" and "Can you be more specific?" until you have two or three concrete, chunked-up problems.

**When you feel like you're making progress by crossing things off a list, check whether #1 is still untouched.** A full day of B wins with #1 still outstanding is a losing day.

**When bad things happen, separate how you feel from how you act.** You're allowed to be in a bad mood. You're not allowed to let anyone know. If you get through the conversation without them seeing it, that's a W.

**When choosing between asking for custom objection handles or memorizing universal closes, choose universal closes.** You only need seven. The goal of every rebuttal is to earn one more ask, not to be right.

**When resilience is the weak stat, prioritize bouncing back over going deep into the problem.** As soon as you hit your lowest point of the bad behavior, do a full 180 back to normal. Speed of recovery matters more than depth of fall.

**When someone won't believe you, lead with proof — not promises.** The entire pre-sell experience (content, testimonials, track record) should do the believing work before the conversation starts.

**When your team's information is siloed, open it up.** The more people who know the financials and constraints, the more people can help solve the problem. Information hoarding is a fear, not a strategy.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Running multiple priorities simultaneously.** This is not multitasking — it's building half-bridges. Resources disperse, nothing completes, nothing crosses the river. Every unfinished initiative is a sunk cost with no return.

**Doing the easy problems first to "build momentum."** The dopamine of fast wins is a trap. By end of day you've crossed off B problems, the A+ problem is still untouched, and tomorrow there are two more B problems in its place. Momentum without the right direction is just spinning.

**Telling people to "toughen up" or "be more confident."** Vague coaching instructions are useless. Name the specific behavioral change: "speak louder," "slow down," "stop filling the silence," "get back to your normal behavior faster."

**Trying to win an argument with a prospect.** Winning the argument ends the relationship and the sale. Nobody buys from someone they feel has defeated them. Reframe, never rebut directly.

**Filling the silence after the close.** The moment you talk after the ask, you've signaled anxiety, removed the pressure, and lowered the likelihood of a yes. Silence is a sales tool. Use it.

**Reading the script instead of breathing it.** If you're thinking about what to say, you're not listening to the prospect. The prospect's words contain the sale. You can only hear them if the script is automatic.

**Treating objection handling as the main event.** At most 20–30% of sales come from post-no recovery. The sale is won or lost in discovery and in everything before the conversation starts. Obsessing over clever overcomes is a distraction.

**Skipping the discovery chunk-up.** Pitching before the prospect has verbally agreed on their own problems means they never bought in. Get them to name the problem, agree it's the problem, agree that solving it would help — then pitch. In that order.

**Assigning multiple priorities without removing existing workload.** Saying "this is now the most important thing" while leaving everything else on people's plates guarantees it won't get done. Commitment requires subtraction, not just addition.

**Letting low resilience become an identity.** Believing that how long you mourn reflects how much you loved, or that it takes X days to get over something. These are rules with no basis in physics. The only thing stopping faster recovery is the belief that faster recovery isn't allowed.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The Diagnosis-Before-Label Pattern.** Never lead with the label. Lead with the precise behavioral definition, then name it. "So the percentage chance that when something bad happens, you change how you act in a way that's against your goals — that's what mental toughness actually measures." This inoculates against vague interpretation and makes the concept actionable immediately.

**The Three-Part Scaffold.** Nearly every argument is structured as: (1) here's the thing most people do wrong, (2) here's the underlying mechanic of why it's wrong, (3) here's the specific action that fixes it. Each part is short. No meandering. "Most people do A → A fails because of B → instead do C."

**Volume-Speed-Pause Variability.** In delivery, Hormozi speaks fast in general, then drops to a noticeably slower pace on the key point — forcing the listener's attention without announcing it. He combines this with a sharp pause before anything he wants to land. The contrast does the emphasis work so he doesn't have to say "this is important."

**The Inversion Frame.** When a concept feels like it contradicts common sense, he names the contradiction first, then resolves it. "The salespeople who close the most are the ones who ask the most times — now, you might think that makes you annoying. No. Here's why." Naming the objection before the audience has it keeps them from mentally leaving.

**Concrete Anchors Before Abstractions.** He never introduces a model without grounding it in a specific story or number first. "We broke the Guinness record. $106 million in 72 hours." Then: "Here's why the tonality system I used was the reason." Proof gates the theory, not the reverse.

**The Self-Disclosure Loop.** He regularly volunteers his own failures or weak stats ("fortitude is the one that hurts the most for me to fix") before prescribing the solution. This prevents the prescription from landing as judgment and positions him as a practitioner, not a commentator.

**Short Declarative Stacks at the Point of Instruction.** When it's time to give the actual rule, sentences compress to their minimum: "Ask for the close. Shut up. That's it." No hedging, no qualifiers. The shortness signals conviction and makes the instruction quotable and memorable.
