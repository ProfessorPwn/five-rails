## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The "How to Yay Without Boo" Headline Formula**
Fill in: *How to [desired outcome] without [feared obstacle], even if [biggest insecurity].* This is the single highest-leverage sentence in any piece of copy — it leads with the dream, neutralizes the objection, and pre-handles the excuse in one breath. Apply it before every other headline you test; it should be your control until something beats it.

**2. The Awareness Stack (5 Levels)**
Prospects exist on a ladder: Unaware → Problem Aware → Solution Aware → Product Aware → Most Aware. The colder the audience, the higher up the ladder your hook needs to land — curiosity-first for unaware, problem-moment-first for problem aware, comparison-first for product aware. Never write copy before you know which rung you're starting on.

**3. Damaging Admissions (The "But" Pivot)**
Lead with a true, believable flaw, then pivot with "but" to the benefit you need them to believe. The flaw makes the benefit credible. *"Our websites are ugly as hell, but they convert."* Reverse the order and you emphasize the flaw. Write down every complaint customers have and every weakness you know exists — then claim them before the prospect does.

**4. Benefits-to-Status (What/Who/When Framework)**
Every benefit has a status dimension. Ask: *what* is the outcome, *who* is giving them the status (spouse, friends, rivals, colleagues), and *when* (past, present, future). A cookbook isn't fast — it's so fast that *your friends wonder how you find the time.* Mapping all three axes gives you nine different angles from a single benefit.

**5. The Awareness-to-Action Funnel (One Step Sells One Step)**
The headline sells the click. The click sells the opt-in. The opt-in sells showing up. Never ask someone to take two steps at once. State exactly what happens next, then deliver it. Each fulfilled promise increases say-do correspondence — they become more likely to follow the next instruction because the last one paid off.

**6. ICE Prioritization (Impact / Confidence / Ease)**
Every improvement idea gets scored on: how big is the upside if it works, how confident am I it will work, how easy/cheap is it to execute? Tabulate and rank. Use this to ruthlessly kill the 5%-upside ideas that feel urgent, and focus resources on the 30-50% bets. Build a running "GROWTH" list to park every idea so the team doesn't get whipsawed.

**7. Cost of Change (The 20% / 5% Rule)**
Any manual process change costs you a guaranteed ~20% temporary performance drop. If you do nothing, you get ~5% free improvement over time from people just getting better. Therefore: only change things when the projected upside is >20%. If it's a 5% improvement idea, don't touch it — you're taking a 20% loss for a 5% gain. Let things breathe.

**8. LTGP to CAC with Leverage Adjustment**
Lifetime value must be measured in *gross profit*, not revenue. The target ratio scales with how manual your business is: fully automated (paid ads + checkout + software) → 3:1 is viable. Fully manual (outbound SDRs + one-on-one sales + concierge delivery) → target 20:1 minimum, shoot for 30:1. Lumpiness in hiring and ramp time destroys thin margins during scaling.

**9. Revenue Retention as North Star Metric**
Measure: of the 100 customers you had on January 1, how many are still buying on December 31? That percentage is your true business health signal. Distinguish logo retention (headcount) from revenue retention (dollars) — the latter is what determines whether you can scale. Virality is a bonus, retention is the floor.

**10. Virality Preconditions (B2B vs. Consumer)**
Virality requires two conditions: (1) no disincentive to share (B2B competitors rarely refer each other), and (2) high communication frequency between your customers and potential customers. If either condition is absent, virality is not a realistic growth lever — stop chasing it and obsess over retention instead.

**11. The 1-3M Swamp Diagnosis**
At $2M revenue with 20% margins you have $400K profit. A competent hire costs $250K all-in. That's risking >50% of your net on one person who may not work out. The only exits: overdrive personally, make a high-conviction hire knowing the first shot rarely lands clean, or raise prices to expand margin before hiring. There is no easy path — the swamp is structural.

**12. Rush is Imaginary / End-State Anchor**
FOMO comes from an arbitrary timeline you invented. Counter it by finding people 20 years ahead of you in your business and studying them — almost all of them have one massive business, not four. Every "yes" to a new thing is a "no" to the current thing. Decide means to cut off. You cannot take four paths to the extreme simultaneously.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Writing an ad headline from scratch | **Script:** *"How to [desired outcome] without [feared obstacle], even if [biggest insecurity]."* Fill slots before writing anything else.

**Situation:** Opening an ad to a cold audience | **Script:** Grab visually and verbally in the first 3 seconds. Ask: *"What is the moment someone experiences [pain]?"* Describe that exact moment — not the abstract problem, the sensory experience.

**Situation:** Calling out your ideal customer | **Script:** *"Hey — if you're below [threshold], this isn't for you. Go consume the free content. This is only for people doing [X, Y, Z]."* Use this as the disqualifier, not the qualifier.

**Situation:** Adding a reason why to an offer or deadline | **Script:** *"Because [any plausible reason], we're running this [offer/price/bonus] through [date]."* The reason doesn't need to be profound. The word "because" does the heavy lifting.

**Situation:** Structuring a damaging admission | **Script:** *"[True flaw], but [benefit you need believed]."* Never reverse the order. Lead with the flaw, land on the benefit.

**Situation:** Describing a benefit in copy | **Script:** Don't say "get more sales." Say: *"You'll be running out of order forms because customers are reading their credit cards to you over the phone too fast."* Describe the moment, not the category.

**Situation:** Tying a benefit to status | **Script:** *"So fast and easy that all your friends will wonder how you found the time to [outcome]."* Identify who awards the status — spouse, peers, rivals — and name them in the copy.

**Situation:** Writing a PS statement for email | **Script:** Option A — Reward the reader (joke, fun fact, light content) to train them to click the PS link. Option B — Disqualifier: *"By the way, if you're just getting started, this isn't for you."* Option C — Summary: *"We've helped [X] people do [Y]. If you're serious about [Z], click here."*

**Situation:** Writing the CTA | **Script:** *"If you want [X], click the button. On the next page, fill in your information. Then select a time. Here's exactly what happens after that: [step 1, step 2, step 3]."* Spell out every next step and what follows it.

**Situation:** Cutting complex copy down | **Script:** Run every draft through a free reading-level tool. Edit until it reads at third grade. Replace adverbs + weak verbs with single precise verbs (*"sprinted"* not *"ran quickly"*). Remove every word that doesn't earn its place.

**Situation:** Justifying implied authority without sounding braggy | **Script:** *"We've spent tens of millions in paid ads across our portfolio — not an agency billing clients, actual risk capital in our own companies."* Lead with what makes the claim verifiable and specific, not vague.

**Situation:** Opening a sales conversation using an award or list | **Script:** *"Did you find us through the [local/industry award] list?"* Even if no one knows the list exists, it establishes authority before the pitch begins.

**Situation:** Using humor in ads | **Script:** Test it like a comedian: run material to many small audiences, identify the 8 minutes out of 60 that land, frontload those. Never cut humor from an ad until you've tested it — but if you have to prioritize, cut humor before cutting the core persuasion structure.

**Situation:** Describing the 1-3M swamp to a client stuck there | **Script:** *"You need the help but you don't have the money to afford the help. Either you go overdrive, you make a high-conviction hire, or you do both. There is no option C. That's why it's the swamp."*

**Situation:** Countering FOMO on a new opportunity | **Script:** *"Show me the person 20 years ahead of you in this business. Does she have four businesses running at once, or one massive one? That's your answer."*

**Situation:** Evaluating whether to add virality to a B2B product | **Script:** *"Two questions: Does your customer have an incentive to tell a competitor about you? And how often does your customer talk to people who could buy from you? If either answer is bad, virality is not your growth lever — retention is."*

**Situation:** Evaluating a change initiative with ICE | **Script:** *"What's the impact if it works? What's my confidence it'll work? How easy is it to execute? Score all three and rank. Only run the 20%+ upside bets — everything else stays on the list."*

**Situation:** Pitching scarcity that's actually real | **Script:** *"I'm only selling [X] spots. When they're gone, they're gone. If someone asks to buy after that, the answer is no — because that no is what makes the next launch sell out in five minutes."*

**Situation:** Explaining why specificity beats generic copy | **Script:** Instead of "Are you overweight?" — *"Do you get winded going upstairs? Do you get a rash between your legs on a long walk? Do you sweat in places you didn't know you could?"* Moments, not labels.

## DECISION RULES

**When writing any ad:** Write the headline first. If it doesn't grab by the throat, nothing else matters — a 1% to 3% CTR improvement triples every downstream result.

**When unsure if a headline will convert:** Look at organic content and competitors' ads in your space and adjacent spaces. The first 3 seconds (visual + verbal) tell you what's working. Steal like an artist from a different industry.

**When writing copy for a cold audience:** Assume the prospect is in a rush, distracted, below third-grade reading level, and doesn't care about your product. Write to that person.

**When you have a claim to make:** Lead with the damaging admission first. What you sacrifice in promise you gain in trust, and trust compounds.

**When you have scarcity or urgency:** Make it legitimate or don't use it. Honor every deadline and cap — the customer who missed out becomes your first buyer next time.

**When evaluating a business improvement idea:** Ask: is the expected upside greater than 20%? If no, don't change it. The guaranteed cost of change is ~20%. A 5% gain does not justify it.

**When measuring LTV:** Always use gross profit, not revenue. Software literature assumes 100% margins. Your business probably doesn't.

**When deciding LTV:CAC target:** Fully leveraged (automated ads, checkout, delivery) → 3:1 is workable. Any manual component → raise the floor. Fully manual → 20:1 minimum, 30:1 is the real target.

**When your product is B2B:** Don't build virality assumptions into your growth model unless customers have both (a) incentive to share and (b) high communication frequency with potential buyers. Default to retention obsession instead.

**When tempted to start a second business:** Ask — do you know why the first business is not 3x its current size? That is the problem to solve. Starting a new business is avoidance.

**When hiring into the 1-3M swamp:** Know you're risking >50% of your net profit on one hire. Plan to take multiple shots, not one perfect one. Pair the hire with personal overdrive — don't bet on one or the other.

**When a prospect seems problem-aware:** Skip curiosity hooks. Open with the moment they experience the problem — make them think "how does he know my secrets?"

**When setting any deadline or promo reason:** Use "because" plus any plausible reason. The word "because" is worth more than the reason itself.

**When feeling FOMO about an opportunity:** Find the person 20 years ahead of you in your current business. Study what they look like. That is your job. Everything else is distraction.

**When measuring business health over 12 months:** Ask one question — of every customer I had on January 1, what percentage is still buying on December 31? That number tells you everything.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Running copy without a written headline.** Every ad has a headline — a bad one if you didn't write a good one. "We have the best sessions" is a headline. It's just terrible.

**Using marketing lingo instead of moment-specific language.** "Overweight" is trite. "Getting a rash between your legs on a long walk" is copy. If it reads like an ad, rewrite it until it reads like a conversation.

**Vanilla positioning ("everything to everyone").** When you appeal to all, no one feels spoken to. Polarize by explicitly excluding wrong-fit customers — that pull is what makes right-fit customers lean in.

**Claiming benefits in reverse order around "but."** *"Our system works, but it's boring"* emphasizes boring. *"It's boring, but it works"* emphasizes it works. Reversing the pivot is one of the most common copy errors that costs conversions silently.

**Measuring LTV off revenue instead of gross profit.** This inflates your ratio, makes unit economics look better than they are, and causes businesses to scale into losses they didn't model.

**Chasing virality in a B2B context where customers have incentive to NOT share.** No amount of referral tactics overcomes a structural disincentive. Misidentifying your growth lever wastes years.

**Perpetual change.** If you're always changing something, you're permanently 20% below where you should be. The business never gets to breathe. Improvement through stability compounds silently — let it.

**Starting a second business because you don't know how to scale the first.** The reason you want a second business is usually that growing the first one is hard. That's not a reason to start a second one. That's the problem to solve.

**Using scarcity or urgency you don't enforce.** The moment you sell past the cap or extend the deadline, every future scarcity signal is worthless. You lose the compounding power of a reputation for meaning what you say.

**Assuming your rush is real.** The timeline that's making you panic is one you invented. Unless you're in a winner-take-all network-effects business, there is no actual deadline. Arbitrary urgency applied to *yourself* is the engine of bad decisions.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Short declarative punches, then the explanation.** Lead with the claim in one sentence, then prove it. *"Proof will always outdo promise. Here's why..."* He never buries the lead — the memorable line comes first, the argument follows.

**The "right?" affirmation loop.** Closes every example with "right?" to invite agreement before moving on. Functions as a verbal handshake — you nod, he escalates. *"Our websites are ugly, but they convert. Right?"*

**Self-interruption for credibility anchors.** Pauses mid-argument to establish why he can say this: *"We spend tens of millions per year in paid advertising — not an agency billing clients, actual risk capital in our own companies."* Earns the floor before continuing the lesson.

**Concrete over abstract, always.** Never says "increase revenue" when he can say "running out of order forms." Never says "hard work" when he can say "16 hours a day, 7 days a week." The abstraction exists to introduce; the concrete is what lands.

**The "now watch this" pivot.** Signals a reframe or gotcha moment. Used to flip an example (reversing the "but" pivot), reveal a counter-intuitive insight, or show why the obvious answer is wrong. Creates mild theatrical tension before delivering the insight.

**Stacking triads and numbered lists out loud.** *"Impact. Confidence. Ease."* *"Attract. Convert. Deliver."* Structures ideas in threes or short numbered sequences so listeners can follow without taking notes. The structure IS part of the persuasion.

**Owning the uncomfortable math explicitly.** Doesn't soften the hard truth — he quantifies it and names it. *"You're risking more than half of your income on one person."* Saying the scary number out loud, plainly, before the audience does, is his version of the damaging admission applied to teaching.

**Ending lessons with a restatement of the rule, not the example.** After a long illustration, he returns to the principle in plain language: *"That's the swamp. Cash flow and time. You don't have either, but you need both to grow."* The example is scaffolding; the rule is what sticks.
