## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Four Business Risks Framework**
Every sub-$10M business has four hidden risks that cap its value and killability: keyman risk, single-channel risk, key-customer risk, key-vendor risk. Audit each one. The more of these you have stacked, the more fragile the business — and the less it's worth to anyone, including you. Fix them in order of highest impact.

*How to apply:* Run a quarterly audit. Ask: "If X disappeared overnight, would revenue drop >20%?" If yes, you have a risk. Classify it and start solving.

---

**2. Keyman Risk: The Asset vs. Job Test**
A business where one person's absence would materially reduce revenue isn't a business — it's a job with overhead. Keyman risk shows up in four zones: marketing (how you get leads), sales (how you convert), delivery (how you serve), and operations (the glue holding the other three together). The more zones one person covers, the riskier the whole machine.

*How to apply:* For each core function, ask: "If this person left tomorrow, would this function continue at 80%+ capacity?" If no, you have keyman risk in that zone. Solve it with process + people, or (if it's someone else) with incentives.

---

**3. Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate (3D Training Method)**
This is how you transfer any skill to any human. Step 1: Document the checklist of every action required for the function — only what you'd do, nothing off-script. Step 2: Demonstrate by doing it in front of them, strictly following the checklist. Step 3: They duplicate it in front of you. Adjust checklist granularity to the person's starting skill level.

*How to apply:* Every time you're keyman in a function, run the 3D process before delegating. Delegation isn't complete until performance stays flat or goes up after handoff. If it drops, repeat the cycle — you failed to teach, not they failed to learn.

---

**4. The Supply Constraint Sequence**
When demand exceeds your capacity and you can't take on more, you have three options in order of least effort to most: (1) Raise prices immediately — same output, more money, zero operational change. (2) Increase service ratio — serve more clients per unit of effort through process or tech. (3) Hire and delegate — removes you from the function entirely. Always do them in this sequence. Never jump to option 3 before maxing out 1 and 2.

*How to apply:* If you're at capacity, raise prices first. If people still say yes at the higher price, raise again or shift service ratios. Only build a team once the economics justify it.

---

**5. Single Channel Risk: The Fishing Boat Test**
If >50% of customers come from one source, a single platform change, ban, or algorithm shift can crater the business overnight. Run the fishing boat test: if your best channel went dark tomorrow, what percentage of lead flow survives? Anything under 50% means you're fishing with one rod over the side of the boat.

*How to apply:* Always have at minimum one paid channel + organic content presence + either outbound or affiliates. Even one post a week on organic gives you a pulse and amplifies whatever primary channel you're running.

---

**6. The Long-Nurture + Referral Engine First Rule**
Before investing in a second acquisition channel, first activate your existing list (email/content follow-up to existing leads) and install a structured referral system. These are zero-cost, highest-margin customer sources. They generate cash flow and breathing room to then invest in channel number two without panic.

*How to apply:* Build an email cadence to your existing list before touching anything else. Ask for referrals multiple times across the customer journey in different and creative ways. Only after these are firing do you put resources into a new cold channel.

---

**7. The "One New" Rule for Channel Expansion**
When adding a second acquisition channel, never launch it with an untested person. You won't know if the failure is the channel or the operator. Pair a new channel with a known person, or pair a known channel with a new person — never both new at the same time.

*How to apply:* Before adding the new channel, transfer ownership of the first channel to someone else. Confirm that channel holds performance without you. Then, and only then, go learn the new channel yourself or put a proven operator on it.

---

**8. Key Customer Risk: The 20% Threshold**
If any single customer (or closely associated group) accounts for >20% of revenue, you have key customer risk. Even a 40% margin business loses half its profit if that customer leaves. The higher the percentage, the more existential the risk.

*How to apply:* Four solutions, pick the one with highest likelihood of success: (1) add more small customers until the whale's % drops, (2) lock the whale into a long-term contract with breakup fees, (3) go get more whales so no single one dominates, (4) let the whale swim — decline the deal if it requires custom infrastructure you can't replicate.

---

**9. Key Vendor Risk: Redundancy + Mutual Destruction + Covenants**
Any vendor controlling a core business function (acquisition, delivery, processing) holds leverage over you proportional to how dependent you are on them. The solution stack: (1) Always have backup vendors spun up and ready, (2) grow large enough as a % of their revenue that they can't afford to lose you either, (3) bake lead times, breakup fees, and service-level fines into every vendor contract.

*How to apply:* For every critical vendor, ask: "If they tripled their price or disappeared tomorrow, what would I do?" If the answer isn't "switch seamlessly," you have vendor risk. Build the backup before you need it.

---

**10. The R&D Process for Removing Yourself from Innovation**
To eliminate keyman risk in product/delivery, systematize the innovation process itself. Step 1: Survey customers to identify and rank the biggest current problem. Step 2: Deploy resources at a scale they individually can't (fractionalize cost across your whole customer base). Step 3: Test in representative markets across diverse demographics. Step 4: Roll out only the winners.

*How to apply:* Create an explicit R&D department or function that follows this exact sequence. The founder's irreplaceable "genius" is usually just pattern recognition — and pattern recognition can be codified into a process someone else can run.

---

**11. Sell-Ready = Operate-Better**
Building a business as if you're going to sell it — removing keyman dependencies, diversifying channels, locking in customers, protecting against vendor risk — makes it better to operate regardless of whether you ever sell. The act of fixing the house for sale reveals how good the house actually is.

*How to apply:* Run a quarterly "acquirer stress test": What would a PE firm or strategic buyer find wrong with this business in a diligence process? Fix those things proactively, not reactively.

---

**12. Mutually Insured Destruction with Vendors**
As you grow, you become a larger percentage of your key vendors' revenue. At a certain point, they need you more than you need them — and the leverage flips. Track this. At scale, consider acqui-hiring critical vendors to permanently align incentives.

*How to apply:* Know exactly what % of each critical vendor's revenue you represent. When that number gets meaningful (>20%), use it as negotiating leverage on pricing, service levels, and contract terms.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Explaining why a business isn't worth what the owner thinks | **Script:** "You don't have an asset, you have a high-paying job. If you walked away tomorrow, what happens to the revenue? If it drops, that's what a buyer sees — and they're right."

**Situation:** Owner resists documenting processes | **Script:** "Every task you do that you haven't written down, you own forever. The checklist is the transfer mechanism. No checklist, no handoff."

**Situation:** Founder is skeptical about building a second acquisition channel | **Script:** "Your current channel is one rule change, one account ban, one algorithm shift from zero. Not if — when. The question is whether you've built the second rod before the first one breaks."

**Situation:** Operator wants to test a new channel by hiring someone new | **Script:** "Don't do two news. If you put a new person on a new channel and it fails, you don't know if it's the channel or the person. You've just paid to learn nothing."

**Situation:** Someone hasn't activated their email list yet | **Script:** "You have 10,000 people who've already raised their hand for you and you're spending money on cold traffic. Email returns 36-to-42 dollars per dollar spent. That list is the most profitable thing you own and you're not touching it."

**Situation:** Business owner is proud of their one big whale client | **Script:** "That whale is not an asset — it's a liability. If they left tomorrow, what does your business look like? If it looks bad, you don't own a business, you own a client relationship dressed up as a business."

**Situation:** Owner wants to take a big enterprise contract that requires custom delivery | **Script:** "What's your reliable channel for getting more of these? If this is a one-off from your network, you're building a custom machine for one client. What business do you actually want to be in?"

**Situation:** Key employee wants equity | **Script:** "If you're not planning an exit, equity is complicated. There's a better way — pay them off the bottom line. When their P&L goes zoom, their personal income goes zoom. No equity dilution, no departure complications, same incentive alignment."

**Situation:** Asking about delegation progress | **Script:** "Delegation isn't complete when you hand it off. It's complete when the performance of that function is flat or up. If it dropped, you delegated abdication, not responsibility."

**Situation:** Business owner worried about investing in redundancy | **Script:** "You're looking at it wrong. If you never need it, you wasted some money. If you do need it — and you will — every dollar was worth it. This is insurance for existential risk."

**Situation:** Evaluating a new business initiative | **Script:** "Does it have margin? Does it have a scalable acquisition path? Does it justify the bandwidth of your best people versus what those people would produce staying on what's already working? If you can't answer yes to all three, let it die in the test tube."

**Situation:** Vendor is pushing back on contract terms | **Script:** "I need lead time, a breakup fee, and SLA fines written in. Not because I don't trust you — because I'm building a real business and this is how real businesses protect themselves."

**Situation:** Owner is managing keyman risk in someone else (not the founder) | **Script:** "You have two options: process them out of the keyman position, or incentivize them to stay. If it's you, you only have option one. So start building the system."

**Situation:** Encouraging organic content as a small business | **Script:** "You don't need content to go viral. You need a pulse. One post a week. When someone Googles you after getting a cold email or seeing an ad, they need to find something. Existence is the goal, not virality."

**Situation:** Post-acquisition handoff | **Script:** "The business I sold had keyman risk in me. I fixed it by creating a system that replicated my thinking process step by step. If I couldn't write it down, I didn't own it — it owned me."

**Situation:** Owner is stuck at capacity | **Script:** "You have three moves. Raise your prices — that's the highest-leverage, zero-effort move. If they still say yes, great problem to have. Then shift your service ratio. Only then build a team. Don't jump to hiring to solve a pricing problem."

**Situation:** Explaining vendor leverage risk | **Script:** "You are not in a negotiation with someone who's abusing their leverage. You are in a hostage situation. The only solution is a backup you built before you needed it."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether a new thing is worth doing | **Script:** "Test it quickly. Iterate before you kill it. But when you lose hope that the margin and scale will justify the resources committed, be a grown-up and let it go. Being emotionally attached to a bad idea is expensive."

**Situation:** Explaining in-person vs. remote for early career people | **Script:** "The most valuable learning of your career happens in the unstructured moments — overhearing the conversation next to you, eating with someone from a different team, watching how decisions actually get made. Remote kills all of that. You can't learn in a vacuum."

**Situation:** Founder realizes they're still the keyman after trying to delegate | **Script:** "That means you delegated the task, not the skill. Go back through the 3D process. Document the checklist — every step. Demonstrate it. Have them do it in front of you. If performance dropped, you skipped a step in the teaching."

## DECISION RULES

**When you're at capacity**, raise prices first. Don't hire to solve a pricing problem.

**When a channel produces >50% of your leads**, start building a second channel immediately — not urgently, but consistently. Invest before you need it.

**When a single customer represents >20% of revenue**, treat it as an existential risk, not a success. Install one of the four whale-risk remedies now.

**When considering a new channel**, pair it with a known operator, not a new one. Never run two unknowns at the same time.

**When delegating a function**, the test isn't whether you handed it off — it's whether performance held. If it dropped, the delegation failed and you repeat the cycle.

**When keyman risk is the founder**, the only solution is process and people. Incentives won't work if the person leaving is you.

**When keyman risk is an employee**, use both: systemize their role AND incentivize them to stay (vesting, profit share). Don't rely on loyalty alone.

**When evaluating a vendor contract**, always negotiate for: minimum lead-time notice before they can exit, breakup fees, and SLA fines tied to their core deliverables.

**When you have multiple vendors for a critical function**, you have negotiating leverage. Use it. No redundancy = no leverage.

**When a promising new venture requires building custom infrastructure for a non-replicable client**, let the whale swim. Don't rebuild your core business around one lottery ticket.

**When building an acquisition strategy as a small business**, do one primary cold channel plus organic content minimum. Organic isn't for customers — it's to confirm existence and amplify everything else.

**When testing a new profit center**, run a beta with real metrics: customer acquisition cost, conversion rates, margin, bandwidth required. If you lose hope that any of those numbers will hit the threshold, kill it and move on.

**When a vendor becomes large enough that they're dependent on your revenue**, use that leverage in contract negotiations and consider an acqui-hire to permanently resolve the risk.

**When transferring a skill**, break it into smaller steps based on the trainee's current level. Advanced operator = higher starting point. Beginner = start from first principles. The length of the checklist reflects the gap between their current skill and the required skill.

**When choosing between raising prices, improving service ratio, or hiring**, do them sequentially, in that order. Each requires more operational complexity than the last.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Red Flag: The "one great client" celebration.** If your biggest win is also your biggest % of revenue, you haven't won — you've created dependency. Celebrate it privately, then immediately start solving for it.

**Red Flag: Building custom delivery for one whale you can't reliably acquire more of.** If the whale came through your network or a referral and you have no repeatable channel to get more of them, you're building a business around luck.

**Red Flag: Delegating by announcement.** Handing something off and calling it delegation without a feedback loop is abdication. Real delegation ends when performance confirms the handoff worked.

**Red Flag: Two news at once.** Launching a new channel with a new person is paying to learn nothing. You won't know which variable caused the failure.

**Red Flag: Building a business that only runs when you're in it.** If you can't leave for 30 days without revenue declining, you're not running a business — you're holding a job hostage.

**Red Flag: Vendor contracts with no exit terms.** No lead time, no breakup fees, no SLA fines = full exposure. They can hold you hostage at will. You will be held hostage.

**Red Flag: Single-processor dependency.** One payment processor with no backup means zero negotiating leverage and one bad day away from total shutdown. Have two fully approved and ready to run the full volume.

**Red Flag: Ignoring the email list while spending on cold acquisition.** A 10,000-person opt-in list generating zero revenue while you're buying cold traffic is a capital allocation failure. The list converts at far higher margin.

**Red Flag: Letting organic content go completely dark as a small business.** Even minimal presence (one post a week) serves as social proof and confirms you're a real operation. Total silence creates doubt that kills warm leads.

**Red Flag: Confusing keyman retention with business-building.** If someone in your business is so critical that you're afraid of what happens when they leave, that fear is the problem — not their potential departure. Either systematize the role or you're sitting on a time bomb.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. The "two-step analogy" structure.** Hormozi almost always pairs a named concept with a visual or physical metaphor, then immediately names the business consequence. Pattern: "[Here's the concept] → [Here's the picture] → [Here's what it costs you if you ignore it]." Example: fishing boat with one rod → rod breaks → no fish → you die.

**2. Quantified thresholds over vague warnings.** Rather than "too dependent on one customer," he specifies "if any customer represents >20% of revenue." Rather than "diversify your channels," he says "if >50% of leads come from one source." Always anchor advice to a number.

**3. Self-implication as credibility.** Before giving the lesson, he names the personal loss first: "$400K stolen from the manufacturer," "lost my payment processor at 25 years old," "keyman risk was the reason I almost couldn't sell for $46M." The loss establishes authority; the lesson is the redemption arc.

**4. The "this is not an insult" pre-frame.** When delivering hard truths, he pre-empts the sting: "I don't say that to insult you — I say it so you can fix it." Use this structure when delivering diagnoses that might land as criticism.

**5. Sequential optionality.** When presenting solutions, he always ranks them by effort and speed of return, and specifies the order matters. "First do X, because it requires the least work and immediately makes money. Only after you've maxed out X do you move to Y." Never presents options as a menu — presents them as a sequence.

**6. The "double-edge sword" pattern.** Whenever a strength also creates vulnerability, he names it explicitly: "Keyman risk is a double-edged sword — the more valuable you become, the more of a keyman you are. So the most valuable thing you can do next is learn to transfer the skill." This creates the tension that motivates the action.

**7. The "clear-eyed reframe" close.** Hormozi ends most frameworks by reframing the effort as worthwhile beyond the obvious reason. Fixing keyman risk isn't just about selling the company — "it makes the business better to own." Adding channel redundancy doesn't just reduce risk — "it also makes you sleep great at night." Tie the tactic to quality of life, not just business metrics.
