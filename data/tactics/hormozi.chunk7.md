## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**Solve for Zero**
Build the business as if you have zero time and need immediate profit. This premise forces you to eliminate fat, outsource correctly, and only keep what's core. When you start with no time and must be profitable, the business scales more cleanly because you never built in the bloat. Apply it to every new product line or division: "How do we make money on this with the least possible friction and overhead?"

**The Who Principle**
Early stage is about what and how. Beyond a threshold, it's entirely about who. The compounding leverage isn't in learning a skill yourself — it's in finding the person who already has 15–20 years in it and combining their knowledge with your distribution. Apply by identifying the kill-shot unknown in your business (how do you ship meat in dry ice?) and finding the one person who already solved it.

**Pre-Sale as PMF Test**
Never raise capital to validate a product when you can pre-sell instead. Pre-sales confirm people want it badly enough to pay and wait — that's a more honest signal than any investor check. Structure it: offer goes up before product ships, collect payment, ship on schedule. Bonus: the customers finance your inventory.

**HBT Reverse Engineering (High-Value Target Extraction)**
When you need a cold outreach list, don't build one from scratch. Find a competitor or analogous company already doing what you want, reverse-engineer their database (public affiliate pages, directories, partner pages), and contact their audience directly. Apply: concatenate sequential partner URLs, scrape public directories via VA, build a lookalike audience from that list before you run a single cold ad.

**The Trojan Horse Outreach**
Lead with something the target actually wants (a recipe feature, a mention, a collaboration) rather than asking for the favor directly. Get a reply by dealing in their preferred currency. Once they respond, transition to the real conversation. Apply any time you're doing cold outreach to influencers, operators, or potential partners — lead with their win, not yours.

**Lifetime / For-Life Offer Stack**
The most powerful retention mechanic is a perpetual benefit that disappears the moment the customer cancels. Start with one for-life item, then sell additional ones to existing subscribers. The subscriber psychologically cannot cancel without "losing" something they already own. Apply to any subscription: identify a recurring cost-of-goods item under $5–10, offer it as a permanent add-on for staying subscribed.

**Barbell Hiring**
Hire very young (gritty, hungry, nothing to lose) and recently retired (domain expertise, legacy drive, no title obsession). Avoid the middle. The middle wants labels, career progression, and the résumé line. The ends want to do the actual work. Apply by default: for every open role, ask "Is there a 60-year-old who mastered this and has a non-compete expiring?"

**The $500M Trap**
At hypergrowth inflection points, companies reflexively hire managers to solve operational chaos. This flips the culture from entrepreneurial ("pirates hacking through the jungle") to process-bureaucratic. Headcount grows faster than value creation; the square root of employees still does 50% of the output. Apply defensively: when something breaks at scale, ask "can we solve this with a process before we solve it with a person?"

**CPG Unit Economics Formula**
30% gross margin is the floor. 10% goes to ops (salaries, tech). That leaves 20% for marketing or profit. At scale you market aggressively for years, build brand, then collapse marketing spend and live on word-of-mouth to capture the margin. Know your gross margin per unit cold — everyone in the company should be able to recite it.

**Content as Top-of-Funnel Compounding**
Gated content protects nothing — users don't log in anyway. Public content lets the platform algorithm identify your ideal buyer, build your retargeting pool for free, and creates warm traffic that converts at lower CAC than cold ads. Apply: put all educational/demonstration content public, let the algorithm amplify the top performers, retarget 50–75% video viewers with conversion creative.

**Show Don't Sell**
Demonstration is the most elegant form of selling. If you can show the product working — solving a real problem in real time — the sale is already made before the pitch starts. Apply to content, ads, and sales calls: lead with the transformation, let people self-select before you ask for money.

**Switching Sides of the Table**
Before any major strategic decision, mentally act as if you just bought the business today. List everything you'd immediately fix. Then go fix those things before you go raise money, run ads, or hire. The new-owner lens removes the emotional attachment that keeps you defending bad decisions.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Business owner asks how to validate a new product idea without raising capital | **Script:** "Run a pre-sale. Put up a page, take deposits, tell people delivery is in 90 days. If nobody buys, thank god you didn't build it. If they buy, you've got a funded launch."

**Situation:** Operator asks how to find expert talent without paying top-of-market salaries | **Script:** "Find the person who's the best in the world at the one thing that would kill your business if you got it wrong. LinkedIn, while on the toilet. Usually someone 60+ with a non-compete that's expiring. They want to be useful. You want their 20 years. Everybody wins."

**Situation:** Founder is hesitant about money-back guarantees | **Script:** "What percentage of customers actually ask for refunds? It's so small you don't even track it. And the conversion increase is worth it. If you're worried about refunds — fix your product."

**Situation:** Someone pitches a VC round for a normal business | **Script:** "Have you considered just making money? VC's business model is deployment of capital and management fees. Their incentive is to put money to work as fast as possible. That's not aligned with building a profitable business. You'll end up spending money to spend money."

**Situation:** Influencer outreach — asking for promotion | **Script:** "Hey, we're building [X] and noticed you've talked about [specific thing]. We're including a recipe from your site in our box — mind if we credit you with a link? P.S. — what's your shipping address, we'd love to send you a box."

**Situation:** Cold outreach to influencer — asking for affiliate deal | **Script:** "We can't pay you $5,000 upfront. What we can do is $10–15 per subscriber, every month they stay — for life. If your audience converts, this compounds every month."

**Situation:** Customer just received first shipment — convert to subscription | **Script:** "Hey, how's it going? Do you need any recipes? We'd love to get you set up as a subscriber — [offer: first box discount / free item]." (Call first. Don't just email.)

**Situation:** Someone asks how to get influencers to respond to cold outreach | **Script:** "Deal in the currency they actually want. Most influencers want fame more than money. Give them credit, a mention, a co-branded thing. Get them to reply. Then pitch."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether to build a capability in-house vs. outsource | **Script:** "Will we ever be better at [X] than a company that's been doing it for 75 years? No. So why bother? Control only what would bring revenue to zero if you lost it. Everything else — tight specs, feet to the fire, long-term partner."

**Situation:** Operator is losing subscribers and panicking | **Script:** "You need 12,000 new subscribers a month just to tread water. That's 400 a day. Right now, while we're talking, people are leaving. The math is fixed — solve for acquisition, solve for churn, or both."

**Situation:** Founder wants to retain customers via content | **Script:** "Don't put it behind a paywall. Nobody logs in. Your subscribers won't attribute the value of your content to whether it was behind a paywall — they'll zoom out and ask 'did this subscription give me $X of value this month?' Make it public, let the algorithm find your buyer, retarget the warm audience."

**Situation:** Someone is hiring for a key operator role | **Script:** "I want people who've failed at something. I want athletes. I want people with a chip on their shoulder who are going to hack through the jungle with me day and night. Title obsession is a dealbreaker."

**Situation:** Founder asks how to structure a referral program | **Script:** "Don't give the referrer money. Give them something they can give their friend — a nearly-free trial box, $20 shipping-and-handling. They want to be the hero to their friend, not collect a commission. Referred customers retain better. Let it compound."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether to raise venture capital | **Script:** "You're going to give up control of what you're building, spend money faster than it can compound, and end up owning 8% of something you used to own. The person with the most to gain from the transaction will win the argument every time. That's not you."

**Situation:** Partner performance review | **Script:** "There's going to be problems with every partner. We're not afraid to talk about them. Quarterly business reviews — here's what you're doing wrong. Let's fix it. We want to lock arms and move forward together."

**Situation:** Founder has never done the unscalable early work | **Script:** "I know it doesn't scale. Do it anyway. You need to know it works before you automate it. Write the handwritten cards. Make the 1,100 phone calls — or pay someone $10/hour and a $5 spiff to make them. The business wouldn't exist without it."

**Situation:** Team is debating gross margin targets | **Script:** "30% is the floor. Below that you can't run the business. Know the number on every single unit — dollars per box, dollars per transaction, whatever your unit is. Everyone in the company knows it. You optimize every line item against it."

**Situation:** Someone is burned out / considering exit | **Script:** "Treat it like a marriage where divorce is never on the table. If it's an option in your mind, you won't have the hard conversations. If it's not an option, you approach every problem differently — I either die or we keep going. I'm still alive. Therefore we solve it."

**Situation:** Debating whether to add a bonus to an existing offer | **Script:** "What's the cost of the add-on? What does it do to conversion and retention? If adding four dollars of bacon drops CAC by more than four dollars and extends LTV — it's not a cost, it's a lever. Stack it."

**Situation:** Founder asks about agency vs. in-house for paid ads | **Script:** "Speed is the constraint. If you're having multiple meetings to approve creative and you can't iterate fast enough — you'll lose the arms race. Outsource until the agency fee exceeds what in-house would cost at the same speed. Bake it into CAC."

## DECISION RULES

**When a customer hasn't been called after the first purchase, call them** — email is a last resort. A $10/hour caller with a $5 spiff will convert 40% to subscribers. That's not marketing math, that's just math.

**When you can pre-sell, don't raise capital** — use your customers' money to fund the launch. No investor check validates demand the way a credit card transaction does.

**When you're debating in-house vs. outsourced on a core competency question, outsource** — unless it would bring revenue to zero if the partner disappeared. Own only the one chokepoint.

**When you're about to hire a manager to fix an operational problem, pause** — ask if a process or a system solves it first. The $500M trap is triggered by reflex-hiring into middle layers.

**When you're cold-outreaching to influencers, lead with their currency** — find out if they want fame, money, or access. Deal in that currency first. Get a reply before you pitch.

**When churn is high, don't chase new acquisition first** — calculate the monthly treadmill number. If you need 12,000 new customers to stay flat, that's 400 a day. Fix retention so acquisition compounds rather than refills.

**When structuring a for-life offer, make it cancelation-triggered loss, not a sign-up bonus** — the psychology of losing something you already own retains better than the excitement of gaining something new.

**When evaluating a hire, look for hunger over credentials** — athletes, people who've failed, people with chips on their shoulders. Barbell the team: very young + recently retired. Skip the middle.

**When doing referral programs, give the referrer a gift for their friend, not cash for themselves** — people want to be the hero to someone they care about. The near-free trial box converts better than a $50 commission.

**When building a content strategy, make everything public** — don't gate educational content behind logins. Nobody logs in. Let the algorithm amplify winners, retarget viewers, and convert through demonstration.

**When a pre-sale doesn't convert, kill the product** — don't interpret low pre-sale conversion as a marketing problem. It's a signal the product doesn't have pull. Be grateful you found out before you built it.

**When gross margin drops below 30%, don't increase marketing spend** — solve the unit economics first. You'll be advertising your way into insolvency.

**When someone asks how to validate a business idea, ask: what's the kill-shot question?** — identify the one thing that, if you can't solve it this week, means you have no business. Solve that first, before everything else.

**When partners disappoint, do a quarterly business review** — tell them exactly what's wrong. Don't fire the relationship; fix the performance. Long-term holds compound on good supplier relationships.

**When the business gets hard, check if you have a mental exit option** — if you're secretly entertaining selling or walking away, you will not make the hard calls. Commit fully or exit cleanly. Partial commitment produces the worst outcomes.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Raising capital before validating demand with pre-sales.** Pre-sales are a product-market fit test with real money. If you skip that step and go straight to investors, you're outsourcing your conviction check to people with misaligned incentives.

**Listening to investors about what your business model should be.** VCs invest in narratives. If "marketplace" gets a meeting, you'll become a marketplace whether it makes business sense or not. Their incentive is to deploy capital, not to build your company.

**Building everything in-house to "control" your supply chain.** Owning every node is a capital trap. You don't get better at farming by buying a farm. Work with best-in-class partners on tight specs and hold their feet to the fire.

**Hiring your way out of operational chaos.** Adding bodies — especially middle management — when growth creates problems is the $500M trap. You'll end up with 85 managers for 230 people and nobody doing anything.

**Gating content behind paywalls or login walls to "protect" member value.** Nobody logs in. You're not protecting value; you're limiting distribution. Make it public, let the algorithm do the targeting, retarget the warm audience.

**Optimizing for GMV or revenue multiples when you're burning cash.** Revenue without unit economics is a story for investors, not a business. Know your gross margin per unit. Obsess over it. Everything else is a narrative.

**Giving equity to early advisors and service providers at founder-level valuations.** Onetime work compounds into permanent dilution. If you must give equity, structure it with buyout provisions from day one.

**Overlooking unscalable early work because "it doesn't scale."** The handwritten cards, the 1,100 cold calls, the elbow grease — this is what builds the initial foundation. Don't skip it because it can't be automated yet. Do it, then automate it.

**Title obsession as a proxy for output.** People in the middle of their career optimize for titles and résumé lines. Barbell your team. If someone is negotiating VP vs. Director before they've done the work, that's the wrong person.

**Running referral programs with cash incentives for the referrer.** People don't refer because they want $50. They refer because they want to look good to a friend. Give them something to give, not something to receive.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The Double-Click:** Hormozi constantly interrupts to zoom in on a specific mechanism — "I want to double-click on that offer real quick" / "Let me hit on the meta part of this." Forces granularity. Never lets a tactic pass without extracting the underlying principle.

**The Contrast Frame:** Sets up a binary — the path taken vs. the path not taken — then forces the listener to see the counterfactual. "If you had stayed with the original model and not raised... it could have been a very profitable business." Used to teach through regret without moralizing.

**The Math Anchor:** When making a point, immediately grounds it in arithmetic. "3% churn a month means 12,000 new customers before you tread water. That's 400 a day. Right now, while we're talking, people are leaving." Always converts rates to concrete daily/unit numbers.

**The Currency Reframe:** Takes abstract concepts and redenominates them in what people actually care about. "Influencers care more about fame than money — deal in their currency." "They charge 10% on customers they brought you for free — what are we even talking about?" Strips pretension, finds the actual trade.

**The Principle Extraction:** After a tactical story, immediately labels the underlying principle. "So basically — you reverse-engineered a competitor's database to build your lead list. I call them HBTs. High-value targets." Turns anecdotes into frameworks.

**Compressed Timeline Acknowledgment:** Names the time cost explicitly before moving past it. "So we just covered eight years in a pretty short window — just for anyone listening." This validates the difficulty without dwelling on it. Used to pace narrative without losing tactical density.

**The Rhetorical Self-Answer:** Poses a question, then immediately answers it in the same breath. "Have you considered just making money? No — but real, right?" Creates the effect of thinking out loud while controlling the conclusion.
