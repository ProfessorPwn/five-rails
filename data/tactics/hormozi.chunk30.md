## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Platform Evaluation Matrix**
Score any platform across five vectors: Who (% of $100k+ households on platform), How Many (US market share), Growth (discoverability for new accounts), Depth (avg time spent per content unit), and Conversion (ability to DM, link, or transact directly). Don't pick platforms based on vanity metrics or cultural buzz — run them through all five vectors, weight by your avatar, and follow the output. A platform can win on Who and Growth but still be an F if Depth and Conversion are trash for your business model.

**The Potency-Per-Follower Ratio**
Raw follower count is a vanity metric. What matters is traffic generated per follower and downstream conversion quality. Calculate: traffic % ÷ follower % across all platforms. When a platform with 1/6th the followers generates 9x the revenue-per-contact, that's where to reallocate. Run this quarterly and let the ratio tell you where to double down — not intuition, not trend headlines.

**Owned vs. Rented Audience**
Social platforms are rented land — the algorithm can cut your reach overnight. Email and podcast subscribers are owned. Build rented audiences for growth (discoverability, virality), funnel them into owned audiences for monetization. The rented audiences fill the bucket; email and podcast keep the bucket from leaking. If you're heavy on rented and light on owned, you're one algorithm update from a revenue crater.

**The Hub-and-Spoke Content Model**
One platform is your creation hub — the place where you produce raw, authentic, in-the-moment content (for Hormozi, that's X/Twitter). Everything else is distribution. Capture the raw material in one place, then repurpose across spokes. This lets you scale your voice without ghost writers diluting authenticity — you wrote it, someone else just assembled it in a new order.

**Depth-to-Conversion Funnel**
The more time someone spends with your content, the higher-intent they are when they hit your site. YouTube viewers spend 91 seconds on-site vs. TikTok's 8 seconds — 10x. Don't just optimize for traffic volume; optimize for session quality. A platform sending 1/10th the traffic but 10x the session time can produce 3-4x the qualified contacts.

**The Niche-Down Arbitrage**
A 5,800-follower account can do $1M/year. An 8,000-follower account can do $5M/year. The variable isn't size — it's specificity. The more you niche your content to the exact problem your ideal buyer has, the higher the conversion rate per impression. Likes don't matter; relevance to the right who matters. If the metal fabricator shows up in my feed because Instagram knows I love gym equipment, I'm clicking even if the post has 5 likes.

**The Podcast/Email Listener Share Math**
On Instagram you compete with 500-3,000 accounts for someone's scroll. On podcast you compete with 3-5. Once you earn an ear on podcast, the competition for attention collapses. The downside is growth is nearly impossible organically — podcasts must be fueled by other platform audiences. Build the audience elsewhere, park them on email and podcast for long-term monetization.

**The Amazon Conversion Arbitrage**
Average Shopify conversion: ~2%. Amazon book page conversion: ~30%. If you send traffic to Amazon and earn 10% commission, vs. your own product at 20-30% commission converting at 2%, you're still ahead on absolute dollars. Amazon's trust layer does the conversion work for you. Treat a book or physical product on Amazon as a traffic source — not just a product — because buyers discover you and self-identify as customers.

**Session Duration as a Proxy for Intent**
You can't always measure conversion directly. Use on-site session duration as a leading indicator of purchase intent. Email subscribers: 133 seconds. YouTube: 91 seconds. LinkedIn: 40+ seconds. Facebook: 53 seconds. Instagram: 28 seconds. TikTok: 8 seconds. The higher the session time, the more they're engaging with your offer. Allocate resources proportionally to platforms that produce the longest sessions for your specific avatar.

**The Large Account Comment Collab Hack**
On LinkedIn (and applicable elsewhere), a comment from a large account functions like a collab — it exposes your content to their entire following. Engineer this: write genuinely great content about them, tag them, and make it so substantive they feel compelled to respond. A single detailed comment from someone with a large following can produce 50x your average engagement on that post.

**The 8-Second Hook Rule**
You lose most of your audience in the first 30 seconds. The first 8 seconds should confirm — not tease — exactly what the thumbnail and title promised. No warm-up, no intro music, no "welcome back." Get to the proof of premise immediately. If you're spending more than 8 seconds telling people what the video is about, you're burning the attention you already earned from the click.

**Data-Driven Reallocation**
Don't defend platform choices based on ego, history, or where competitors are. Pull the actual traffic, session duration, and conversion data from your CRM and analytics. When the data says a platform is outperforming at 9x potency-per-follower, move resources there — even if culturally it seems less exciting. The data is the boss.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening a DM on Twitter/X | **Script:** "Hey, I loved [specific thread title] — especially [specific point]. The way you framed [X] actually changed how I think about [Y]."

**Situation:** Someone doesn't respond to a compliment DM | **Script:** Don't follow up. If someone won't acknowledge a genuine compliment, they won't engage with an ask. Move on.

**Situation:** Doing a CTA in a YouTube video without killing watch time | **Script:** Keep it short and make it a small ask — one sentence, drop the link in the description, pin it in the first comment. Don't belabor it mid-video.

**Situation:** Building email list CTA | **Script:** "If you're a business doing over a million bucks a year, hit the Scale button at [URL]. If you're not a business owner, that's not for you."

**Situation:** Driving Instagram story conversions | **Script:** Use keyword DM automation (MiniChat) — "DM me the word [X] and I'll send you [the thing]." Starts the conversation without a public ask.

**Situation:** LinkedIn content growth tactic | **Script:** Write a substantive post about a large account — not "great post," but a full breakdown of *why* a specific piece of content they made mattered. Tag them. Make it long enough that a real response from them generates algorithm signal.

**Situation:** Amazon affiliate content | **Script:** "I was reading this and thought you'd like it — here's the link." No pitch energy. Pure recommendation framing. The Amazon trust layer handles the conversion.

**Situation:** Pitching a podcast CTA | **Script:** Give the URL verbally AND put it in show notes AND remind them "I know you're probably driving — the link is in the description, come back to it." Reduce the friction of the multi-step conversion.

**Situation:** Responding to someone who found your content but hasn't converted | **Script:** Use email — you already have their contact. Make a direct, single-action ask: "We just opened two dates for [X]. If you're doing over $1M, go here." One clear CTA, specific qualifier, no fluff.

**Situation:** Deciding which platform to prioritize with limited bandwidth | **Script:** "Run your avatar through the five vectors — who, how many, growth, depth, conversion. Weight conversion and depth highest if you're selling services. Go where the math points, not where the hype is."

**Situation:** Building a LinkedIn following from scratch | **Script:** Connect with every high school classmate, college contact, and coworker. Max out your 500 connections. Post high-value content about the people you want to attract. The connection-graph seeding is your growth engine early.

**Situation:** Evaluating whether a platform is working | **Script:** Pull your CRM source data. What % of traffic comes from each platform? What's the session duration? What % of those turn into contacts? If a platform has 10x the followers but 1/4 the conversion potency — that's your answer.

**Situation:** Encouraging someone to start YouTube with no team | **Script:** "Start with a webcam at your desk. Post three videos a week. Every week, look at the three you made last week and improve one thing. That checklist compounds into what a great video actually is."

**Situation:** Email send hesitation (perfectionist block) | **Script:** "You're not protecting quality — you're leaving money on the table. The average return on email is 36:1. The only cost is delivery and copy. Send it."

**Situation:** Content that goes viral but doesn't convert | **Script:** "Viral content that doesn't match what you sell is noise. If you slap strangers on the street you get views. But unless you sell 'how to slap strangers,' those aren't the right views. Make content about the problem your buyer has."

**Situation:** Posting frequency on Instagram | **Script:** Two sessions a day — one morning, one evening. Don't go beyond that or you'll get penalized. These are your only shots; curate accordingly.

**Situation:** Building a podcast audience | **Script:** You can't grow a podcast organically at scale. Build the audience on YouTube or Instagram, then funnel them to podcast for depth and long-term trust. The podcast is the moat, not the acquisition channel.

**Situation:** Reactivating a dead/polluted social account | **Script:** Start a new account. Don't try to fix a following you bought or built wrong. A fresh start with the right audience outperforms a large corrupted one every time.

**Situation:** Someone asks if they should do TikTok | **Script:** "Who's your avatar? If they're business owners, 27% of $100k+ households are on TikTok, and your traffic from it converts at 1/4 the rate of other platforms. The math doesn't support it unless you have unlimited bandwidth."

**Situation:** Justifying content investment to a skeptic | **Script:** "I had 300,000 YouTube subscribers and was getting 3,000 warm inbound applications a month. I was doing paid ads before that. Tell me how much paid traffic you'd need to buy to get 3,000 warm applications every 30 days."

## DECISION RULES

- **When choosing platforms with a limited budget:** Pick one or two. Use the five-vector matrix (who, how many, growth, depth, conversion) and go where the math points for your specific avatar. Don't spread thin.

- **When a platform has 10x the followers but generates 1/4 the revenue per contact:** Immediately audit time allocation and consider reallocation. Follower count without conversion potency is a vanity trap.

- **When you haven't started creating content yet:** Start with a webcam. Post three times a week. Improve one thing per week. The compounding skill gain beats waiting for production quality.

- **When your email list isn't generating 30%+ of revenue:** Treat email as broken infrastructure, not a secondary channel. Fix before adding new platforms.

- **When building a LinkedIn presence:** Write about large accounts in your space. Get their comment. That comment is more valuable than running LinkedIn ads for most early-stage creators.

- **When you have a book or physical product:** Put it on Amazon and embed CTAs inside the product. Treat Amazon as a discovery and traffic platform, not just a sales channel.

- **When posting on Instagram:** Never exceed two sessions per day. Quality over frequency — you get penalized for overposting, unlike TikTok or X.

- **When your CTA disrupts viewer experience (YouTube):** Keep it to one sentence mid-video. Put the real CTA in the description above the fold and pin it as the first comment. Never sacrifice watch time for a long pitch.

- **When doing outreach on any platform:** Lead with a specific, substantive compliment — not "love your work," but "loved this specific thing and here's why." If they don't respond to that, don't escalate to an ask.

- **When the data contradicts your assumption:** Update your behavior. That's what learning is. If Facebook is outperforming TikTok 9x on potency-per-follower, move resources regardless of what you believed before seeing the data.

- **When building trust before selling:** Use podcast and long-form YouTube to build depth. Use email and Instagram DMs to convert. Don't try to sell from the same channel you use for deep trust-building.

- **When your content goes viral but doesn't convert:** Audit content-to-offer alignment. Viral ≠ relevant. Relevant-to-your-buyer is the only metric that drives revenue.

- **When an influencer asks what to sell:** Sell what you actually use and love — link to it on Amazon. The trust is already there, the platform converts at 15x a typical Shopify store, and it doesn't feel like selling.

- **When you want to grow on LinkedIn from zero:** Seed your first 500 connections from real-world networks (school, jobs). The connection graph is the growth engine on LinkedIn in early stages.

- **When YouTube community tab is available:** Use it for CTAs. A community post generating 2.5M impressions is not nothing — it's a high-leverage, low-effort CTA channel most creators ignore.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

- **Chasing platform followers without tracking conversion potency.** 900k on TikTok generating less traffic than 150k on Facebook is a resource allocation disaster hiding behind a big number.

- **Buying followers or running low-quality traffic to build a following.** It poisons the algorithm targeting and can make organic growth nearly impossible later. You'll spend years cleaning up a metric you paid to fake.

- **Posting the same content identically across all platforms.** Each platform now has its own algorithm preferences. What works on Facebook Reels doesn't work on TikTok doesn't work on YouTube Shorts. Platform-specific formatting is no longer optional.

- **Posting too frequently on Instagram.** The algorithm penalizes over-posting. Eight posts a day doesn't work here like it might on TikTok. Two sessions a day is the ceiling that performs.

- **Starting a DM with an ask.** "Hey can we hop on a call?" with no prior relationship or context gets ignored. Lead with specific value, earn the response, then move to the ask.

- **Ignoring email.** If you're heavy on social and light on email, you're on rented land with no owned asset. The 36:1 ROI on email is not a theoretical benchmark — leaving it underdeveloped is leaving the highest-ROI channel idle.

- **Optimizing for views over relevance.** If your content attracts people who will never buy what you sell, you're building an audience with a low ceiling. A 19-like post that converts 10 times a day beats a 100k-view video that converts zero.

- **Long intros.** If you spend the first 2 minutes of a video explaining what the video is about, you've already lost most of your audience. The first 8 seconds must confirm the thumbnail promise and start delivering.

- **Building podcast without a feeder audience.** Podcast growth alone is nearly impossible in a crowded market. Without YouTube, Instagram, or another high-growth platform funneling listeners in, a podcast is a great product with no distribution.

- **Neglecting the thumbnail.** It's the first conversion point before anyone clicks play. Split-testing nine thumbnails on a single video isn't overkill — it's table stakes at scale. If you're not testing thumbnails, you're leaving viewership on the table before the algorithm even has a chance to distribute.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Data sandwich:** State the surprising stat → give the implication → give the counterintuitive action. E.g., "150k on Facebook generates more traffic than 900k on TikTok. That means Facebook is 9x the potency. So if you're debating where to start — you now have your answer."

**Avatar specificity as proof:** Always name the exact niche when citing a case study. Not "a nutritionist" but "a registered dietitian who taught insurance companies how to bill more profitably." The specificity signals real knowledge and makes the principle stick.

**The rhetorical question pivot:** "Want to know what it is for TikTok? 27%." Drop the number after the setup. Build tension with the question, release it with the stat. Repeat this rhythm throughout.

**Self-correction on the fly:** "Before I made this video I thought X. But when I looked at the data, I changed my mind." Model intellectual honesty explicitly — it builds trust and gives permission for the audience to update their own beliefs.

**The compound payoff close:** End a platform analysis not with a summary but with the unexpected downstream implication — the thing they didn't see coming. "And the crazy part? My email traffic converts at 133 seconds on-site. I already have their contact info. This isn't me trying to get them. This is me closing them."

**Direct address with qualifying exclusion:** "If you're a business owner doing over a million — go here. If you're not a business owner, don't." Explicitly excluding non-buyers signals that you're talking to real people, not everyone, which paradoxically increases the credibility of the pitch for the people who do qualify.

**Numerical precision as credibility:** Never round when you have the real number. Not "about 90 seconds" — "91 seconds." Not "almost 50%" — "48%." Precision signals that you actually looked at the data and aren't approximating from memory or guessing.
