## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. Core 4 Acquisition Channels**
There are exactly four ways to get customers: content, outreach, paid ads, and affiliates. Every business fits differently into these four — your job is to pick the one or two you're actually suited for and go deep, not spray all four. When advising a cash-pay local clinic, paid ads with a geo pin-drop beats content by a mile. Know which channel matches your avatar density, your team's capability, and your timeline.

**2. Front-End Offer + Ascension Architecture**
You will never scale by advertising all 15 of your services. Pick one to three offers that convert profitably on the front end — your "door into the business" — then cross-sell and upsell everything else once the customer is inside. The business isn't selling 15 things; it's acquiring customers efficiently through a single entry point and ascending their LTV from there.

**3. The Guarantee Formula**
Every guarantee has the same structure: "If you don't [X] in [Y time], we will [Z]." The Z doesn't have to be a full refund — it can be a partial refund, added time, or your gross profit back. A 10% money-back guarantee that closes 20% more deals nets you more than a 100% guarantee that closes 22% more and doubles refunds. Find the minimum guarantee that changes behavior.

**4. Three Affiliate Integration Tiers**
Tier 1: give away your lead magnet free with their purchase (easiest, zero revenue, gets you leads). Tier 2: let them sell your lead magnet and keep 100% of the proceeds — your cost is just delivery, your gain is the customer relationship and the upsell. Tier 3: standard commission kickback (least favorite — generic, rarely changes behavior). Always try to get to Tier 2 before defaulting to Tier 3.

**5. Launch and Integrate Model for Affiliates**
Getting an affiliate to send you one trial customer is not activation. Launch = a one-time big campaign push to their entire audience. Integrate = your process and theirs merge so referrals happen automatically without anyone reminding anyone. Most people stop at the trial and wonder why it doesn't scale. The sequence is: trial → close the loop → propose launch → build toward full integration.

**6. Lead Getters vs. Lead Getting**
There are two modes: you getting leads (Scenario 1) and you getting people who get leads (Scenario 3). Both cost the same time from you. In Scenario 3, each month you're adding nodes that generate revenue without you. The math compounds differently — the line for your effort stays flat, but revenue underneath it stacks geometrically. High-leverage businesses shift from Scenario 1 to Scenario 3 as fast as possible.

**7. Revenue Retention as the North Star Metric**
In software and education, the single most important number is Month-12 retention. 40% is interesting. 60% is product-market fit. 70%+ is a growth engine. Before adding acquisition spend, before launching new products, before expanding to new markets — solve for M12. Once retention compounds, acquisition becomes a multiplier rather than a treadmill.

**8. Levels of Change Management**
The greater the change, the greater the risk the change doesn't produce the intended outcome. When a business is working — even at $140M revenue with 40% gross margins — don't change five things simultaneously. Stack changes from lowest-risk to highest-risk: centralize operations first, confirm the lift, then adjust the offer. Change the thing most likely to work, wait for signal, then move to the next thing.

**9. The Business Stage Diagnostic**
Businesses behave in predictable patterns by headcount and revenue stage. "Product Tie" stage means customers have nothing else to buy after the initial purchase — they graduate and leave. "Optimized" stage means talent can't be hired because cash flow won't support senior compensation. Identifying the stage instantly clarifies the constraint: retention problem, hiring problem, or acquisition problem — and each has a specific solve.

**10. CAC Offset via Education / Lead Magnet Liquidation**
If you have a high-ticket offer and a lower-ticket educational product, the education isn't the business — it's your customer acquisition machine. The $12K offer breaks even (or loses slightly). The $3K/year continuity membership after it is essentially pure margin that stacks year over year. Structure it so your high-ticket acquisition is subsidized by the education funnel: you can then outspend competitors who need to profit on the front end.

**11. Affiliate Activation Loop**
An affiliate who sends you one customer isn't an affiliate — they're running a test. Your job after that first referral: close the loop explicitly ("Sandy was amazing, here's what happened"), then immediately re-open the door ("Which of your patients this week would be a good fit?"). Note the phrasing shift: not "do you have any" — "which ones." Systematize this callback into every patient's post-treatment workflow.

**12. Premium Cash-Pay Transition Playbook**
If you're leaving an insurance-based model for cash-pay, you're not evolving your business — you're starting a new one that happens to share your existing infrastructure and staff. That framing matters because the skills required are different: insurance rewards operational efficiency and low costs; cash-pay rewards marketing, offer construction, and sales. Know which game you're switching into before you make the move.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Client wants to transition from insurance-based to cash-pay | **Script:** "You basically have to think about this as starting a business over — but with resources already. You don't have to think about the service, you have to think about the packaging. What's the Grand Slam Offer for this?"

**Situation:** Selling at intake before treatment | **Script:** "Do you want to use the card we have on file?" — get the card before the appointment. No-show rate drops to 20-30%. Charge a no-show fee if needed. The card on file makes every downstream sale frictionless.

**Situation:** Running a discounted front-end offer | **Script:** "You run a highly discounted assessment — say, a $200 X-ray for $19. You don't care about that transaction. You want the credit card on file and the body in the door."

**Situation:** Converting assessment to sale | **Script:** "Do the assessment. Don't treat them. Do the sale appointment. Sell at point of greatest need, not point of greatest satisfaction."

**Situation:** Activating a dormant affiliate | **Script:** "Hey, we just dealt with Sandy — she was amazing, we did [X], she loved it. By the way, which patients did you see this week who'd be a good fit for what we do?" Not 'do you have any' — 'which ones.'

**Situation:** Proposing an affiliate commission structure | **Script:** "I've got 20% I can play with here. You can take all 20 — I'll price up for direct consumer. Or we do a 10% discount for your clients, you keep 10%. This is my bare-bottom number. I'll make it work either way — you tell me what you want."

**Situation:** Pitching a Home Services guarantee | **Script:** "I guarantee delivery on time and on budget, or I give back my gross profit — which is 20%. You still have your roof. I have skin in the game. All they want is to know you care enough to make sure it gets done."

**Situation:** Affiliates not generating consistent referrals | **Script:** "You're doing the hardest part — getting them to refer at all. The missing link is the continuous activation. Somebody needs to be walking in that front door with donuts, asking how they're doing, and reminding them you exist. That's the whole job."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether to build a recurring revenue model | **Script:** "If you want to sell it, you need Revenue retention — something that makes it valuable. If you just want to make more money, just run more of what works. Don't add complexity if that's not your goal."

**Situation:** Education business with no backend | **Script:** "The real continuity in education is just more education. First year, then undergrad, then graduate, then masters, then PhD, then double PhD. People just become endless students. What's the next level you can sell them?"

**Situation:** Evaluating acquisition channel priority | **Script:** "Of content, outreach, paid ads, and affiliates — which one are you actually better suited for? Because we have to pick, and then go deep."

**Situation:** Prospect hesitates on price after a quote | **Script:** [Implied by framework] "It sounds like that number landed wrong. What does fair look like to you?"

**Situation:** Introducing a new offer concept to affiliates | **Script:** "Let me sell you my lead magnet — or better, let me let you sell my lead magnet to your audience. You keep 100%. I get the introduction. When I talk to the customer, I've already paid for their first thing, and now I just sell them the rest."

**Situation:** Advising a business on when to expand markets | **Script:** "If I crush my B2B play, I just run my B2B playbook in the new market. I find something that works, then I just do more. I don't go solve B2C first — I clone the winning system."

**Situation:** Pushing back on scattershot advertising | **Script:** "Instead of advertising all 15 services, you're going to have one to three that convert profitably on the front end. The rest of your business is cross-selling and upselling when they walk in the door."

**Situation:** Assessing product-market fit in a subscription business | **Script:** "You retained 40% at Month 12, basically in a pre-product-market-fit market. That's fairly compelling. Best-in-class for prosumer is 60%-plus. You're not that far. The only number I care about is M12 — how do we get from 40 to 60."

**Situation:** Evaluating a multi-business founder | **Script:** "I'm concerned about the four businesses. To win in SaaS you have to be all-in on software. The people you're competing against are very well-funded and very smart. If you think you can split your attention and still beat them, that's a tall order."

**Situation:** Recommending against premature B2C expansion | **Script:** "I'm not saying you can't go B2C. But when I look at this, you're barely optimized on B2B, and it's already working at a margin I'd kill for. There's so much juice left in this. I don't want to start something new. Squeeze this first."

**Situation:** Recommending referral-based content CTAs | **Script:** "Don't change the content strategy — just change the call to action. Alternate between consumer CTAs and agent recruitment CTAs. You can kind of double-dip. The content stays the same — you're just changing who you're talking to at the end."

**Situation:** Personal life / partner alignment | **Script:** "Don't accommodate. Live the way you'd want to live. If she's thrilled about it, you probably have a good life ahead. If she comes out and it's not the case, at least you didn't wait two years to find out after you both stopped acting."

## DECISION RULES

**When your business is profitable and unoptimized, squeeze the current model before starting a new acquisition channel.** Every dollar of improvement in an existing, working system is lower-risk than a new channel from zero.

**When choosing acquisition channels, pick the one that matches your skills and avatar density** — for local brick-and-mortar, paid ads with a geographic pin is almost always the right answer. Outbound works for high-value B2B Affiliates where you can be hyper-targeted.

**When you have a working business at scale ($100M+ range), don't change more than one major variable at a time.** Centralize first. Confirm the lift. Then consider changing the offer.

**When retention at Month 12 is 40% or above in a prosumer software product, you probably have product-market fit** — put all profit into improving M12 before expanding acquisition.

**When your affiliate partners are sending you one trial customer, you haven't activated them** — you've passed a test. Treat every first referral as a trial that requires a full close-the-loop + reopen conversation.

**When an affiliate commission structure doesn't change what people do with their business, the offer isn't compelling enough** — either make the incentive bigger or remove friction.

**When transitioning from insurance-based to cash-pay, treat it as a brand-new business** that happens to share your infrastructure. The required skills are fundamentally different: marketing, offer construction, and sales replace operational efficiency and cost management.

**When you're in an education business, the continuity product is more education, not a subscription** — unless the goal is to sell the company, in which case you need revenue retention metrics.

**When an education business wants to maximize enterprise value for exit, build a downsell continuity tier** — e.g., $12K high-ticket → $3–5K/year ongoing access. The $12K offsets CAC; the continuity is pure stacked margin.

**When evaluating whether to focus on Enterprise vs. prosumer/downmarket customers, choose the one with higher retention** — a prosumer customer who stays is worth more than an Enterprise customer who churns.

**When your sales model is commission-based field reps, cut the bottom third upon centralizing** — top performers absorb the volume, costs drop, and revenue often increases 20-25% from that change alone.

**When someone wants to build a SaaS company while running three other businesses, they have to go all-in** — SaaS is one of the most competitive spaces in existence. Split attention means you lose.

**When setting up a guarantee, identify the customer's two or three biggest fears** — in Home Services it's time and budget. Build your guarantee specifically around those fears, not around money back.

**When a guarantee increases sales more than it increases refunds, it's net positive** — you don't need the highest guarantee, just the minimum one that changes behavior.

**When someone asks "more or better," ask "what do you actually want to happen in 3 years?"** — if the answer is "make more money," more is almost always right. If the answer is "sell the company," then better (retention, recurring revenue) is what matters.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Generic 20% referral kickbacks.** Almost every business offers 20% to anyone who refers them. Almost no one uses it. A referral incentive that doesn't change what the affiliate does with their business is not an offer — it's noise. Redesign it from scratch.

**Advertising all of your services at once.** This is an insurance-model habit that destroys CAC efficiency in cash-pay. You're not a menu. You're a door. Pick one to three offers that convert, and sell everything else after they walk in.

**Chasing multiple rabbits simultaneously.** Three businesses with split attention is not a portfolio — it's an execution deficit. Pick the one with the clearest path and the best existing metrics, shut off the others, and go all-in. You cannot beat a well-funded, focused competitor while distracted.

**Changing five variables at once in a working business.** Especially post-acquisition or post-merger. Centralize first, measure the lift, then adjust the offer. If the business is at $140M EBITDA, you don't make radical offer changes until you've captured the operational efficiency gains first.

**Treating an affiliate's first referral as activation.** One patient sent by a doctor is a trial, not a relationship. If you don't close the loop and reopen the door, you've wasted the acquisition cost of that affiliate.

**Selling at point of greatest satisfaction.** Once a patient has been treated and is happy, the urgency is gone. The sale happens at the assessment — before treatment — when the problem is top of mind and the need is greatest.

**Saying "I want to make an impact" when you actually want to make money.** Hormozi reads through it immediately, and it makes the advice worse. If your goal is revenue, say so — it unlocks cleaner, faster recommendations.

**Building a SaaS marketplace without going freemium or pure subscription.** You can't have a revenue-share model that only monetizes 2.5% of your user base and expect to scale. Either charge subscription for the hope of future success (Shopify model) or go full freemium and monetize on the brand side.

**Starting B2C acquisition before exhausting B2B affiliate capacity.** If your affiliate channel is returning 12:1 ROI and you've only reached 200 out of thousands of potential referrers, there's no reason to build a new channel. Squeeze what you have first.

**Leaving no mechanism for the customer to keep paying.** If you run an education or high-ticket coaching business and every customer graduates with nothing else to buy, you're on a treadmill. Every revenue dollar requires a new front-end sale. Build the backend before the treadmill gets unbearable.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The "two paths, you pick" structure.** Hormozi almost always reduces complex situations to exactly two viable options, articulates what winning looks like in each, and then asks which one the person is more aligned with — rather than telling them what to do. "You could go in-network and make your whole business about operational efficiency. Or you go cash-pay, be the best, be private. Which game do you want to play?"

**The blunt "so basically" pivot.** When someone overcomplicates their situation, he reframes it in one sentence with "so basically": "So basically, you need to learn how to acquire cash-paying customers — which means you run ads and sell stuff." Strips the complexity. No softening.

**The rhetorical math reveal.** He will do on-the-spot unit economics mid-conversation to reframe whether something is actually a problem: "It costs you $500 to acquire a physician, and they refer you $6,000 a year. That's 12-to-1. What exactly is stopping you from doing ten times more of that?" The math is usually the answer.

**Stacked constraint identification.** Before prescribing, he names all the constraints first — usually three to four — then sequences the solves. "Issue one: can't hire senior talent because no cash. Issue two: ads not converting. Issue three: too many products diluting improvement rates. Now here's the order you fix them."

**The "if I were you" hypothetical.** He uses "if I were in your position" to deliver the most direct recommendation without being prescriptive. It creates distance from the ego of the advice while still being clear: "If we swap places, that's where I'd be focusing." Then he explains the reasoning.

**Contrasting the theoretical and the actual.** When someone asks about a sophisticated strategy, he'll often say: "It's not that I wouldn't do it — it's that theoretically it's great, but actually, you haven't squeezed what you have." He distinguishes between what's possible and what's the highest-probability move given current state.

**The stage-diagnostic frame.** Every answer is prefaced by identifying what stage the business is at and what the stage-specific problem is. "He's at the product-tie stage." "She's at the optimized stage." It grounds every recommendation in a structural reality rather than a personal failure. The business isn't broken — it's at a predictable stage with a predictable set of constraints.

**Short declarative closes.** Every explanation ends with a one-sentence summary that sounds like a headline: "That's the business." "That's all he has to do." "The offer has to change what they do, or you didn't make a real offer." These are designed to land as takeaways, not to be argued with.
