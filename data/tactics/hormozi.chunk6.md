## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. Fear in the Fog**
Fear only survives in vagueness. The moment you write down, step by step, exactly what will happen when you fail — who you'll call, where you'll sleep, what you'll actually lose — it becomes survivable. Crisp specificity kills abstract dread. Apply it: before any scary decision, write the worst-case chain in excruciating detail. You'll almost always find it ends with you on a couch, not dead.

**2. Four Components of Mental Toughness**
Threshold (how much bad before behavior changes) → Magnitude (how much behavior changes) → Resiliency (how fast you rebound to baseline) → Adaptation (do you rebound above baseline, meaning the event made you better). Most people measure toughness as one fuzzy variable; this breaks it into four you can actually improve. Apply it: when something bad happens, consciously track which variable is currently under attack and respond to that specific variable, not the general feeling of "this sucks."

**3. More, Better, New (Candy Stack)**
Your working business is a statistically rare configuration of variables. Most configurations don't make money — yours does. Every change risks breaking the stack. The priority order is: do MORE of what already works → then do it BETTER (small variations) → only then do something truly NEW. New takes the most resources and almost always fails. Apply it: give yourself one new thing per year maximum; everything else must be a variation or volume play on what's already proven.

**4. The Textable Offer**
An offer is only ready when you can text it to someone and they reply "I'm in" without needing a call. Each bonus in the offer must be worth more than the total price of the offer by itself — not in aggregate, individually. If a bonus requires explanation, delete it. The mental real estate cost of explaining something exceeds its persuasive value. Apply it: before finalizing any offer, write it in one SMS. If it doesn't convert on its own, compress further.

**5. The Two-Question Decision Frame**
Before reacting to anything — a bad employee, an online critic, a conflict — ask: (1) What do I want to have happen? (2) What actually increases the odds of that? Your gut reaction is almost always antithetical to the answer. The heavyweight champion of responses is usually "do nothing," which is physically efficient but emotionally painful. Apply it: run every interpersonal or business decision through both questions before acting; treat your instinct as a hypothesis to falsify, not a directive to follow.

**6. Five Levels of Friendship**
Level 1: talk about the past. Level 2: talk about other people (gossip). Level 3: talk about ideas. Level 4: execute things together. Level 5: make, lose, and spend money together. The level of friend you have determines what kind of trust you can extend. Apply it: use as a litmus test before entering business partnerships — if you've never done Level 5 with someone, write everything down regardless of how solid the relationship feels.

**7. Third Marshmallow Fallacy**
The research rewards delaying gratification — but indefinite delay is the same failure as zero delay. If the ant saves forever and dies, the crumbs meant nothing. Winners who've learned to delay must then learn the harder skill: when to reap. Apply it: if you are a chronic under-spender and delayer, set an explicit date or milestone — "at X, I begin consuming." Making money for its own sake on the way up is legitimate and fundable.

**8. Scale Zero**
The spiritual path of entrepreneurship is the consistent relinquishing of control. First you delegate the doing, then the managing, then the leading, then eventually the vision. At each stage the instinct is to hold the next level — don't. Apply it: for every function you currently own, ask "how do I remove myself from this?" If the answer is "I can't," that's the bottleneck. Treat it as an engineering problem.

**9. A-Player Culture / The Sixes Problem**
Five eights plus five sixes = ten six-sevens. The sixes don't just underperform — they pull the eights down by demonstrating what's tolerated. Remove the sixes, and the eights become nines; you never needed the sixes. Apply it: identify your sixes by asking whether their performance, if universally accepted, would embarrass you. Remove them before they set the cultural floor.

**10. Supply Before Demand at Equilibrium**
When supply equals demand (full capacity), the instinct is to push more demand and figure out delivery later. Wrong. Increasing demand first costs you reputation as quality degrades. Increasing supply first costs short-term profit (training drag + salary before load) but expands capacity cleanly. Apply it: when you're full, hire before you promote. The book launch only happened because four directors were hired 90 days prior.

**11. Decentralization / Team of Teams**
Past a certain scale, centralized shared resources dilute focus across every revenue line. The right structure: independent P&L owners with uncapped earnings potential, accountable to bottom line, not requiring the CEO for daily decisions. If the leader needs you to drive their revenue, you are still the driver. Apply it: for each revenue line, ask "if I disappeared for 90 days, would this grow or shrink?" The answer tells you if you have a real leader or a glorified executor.

**12. Talent as the Highest-ROI Investment**
A specialist who generates $700K in gross profit annually is worth $150K in headhunter fees — that's a sub-3-month payback on a recurring asset. Most founders underinvest in talent acquisition because they're pricing the cost, not the return. Apply it: frame every senior hire as an investment with a cash-flow yield. If the yield is 3–5x the cost in Year 1, the question isn't "can we afford to hire them" — it's "can we afford not to?"

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Someone on your team asks "what if we don't hit the goal?" before a big push | **Script:** "Dare greatly. If we fuck up, we die trying. What are we actually afraid of? That we made a lot of money and fell short of a number? We try again next year."

**Situation:** Prospect or partner resists putting anything in writing | **Script:** "I want everything in writing so we have zero misunderstanding and no unspoken expectations — because the last thing I'd want is to disappoint you by not doing something you expected me to do."

**Situation:** Opening hard partnership conversation before money is on the line | **Script:** "What happens if you don't want to be partners anymore? What happens if I don't? What happens if one of us dies? What happens if we disagree on where the money goes or whether to sell? Let's answer all of it now, because it's so much easier when there's nothing on the line."

**Situation:** Friend-turned-business-partner pushes back on contracts | **Script:** "People who sleep with each other and make life commitments to each other — half the time it ends in court. If you think the most important relationship in your life still has 50/50 odds, write down a contract."

**Situation:** Evaluating a bonus to include in an offer | **Script:** "Does this bonus, by itself, justify the entire price? If the answer is no, cut it. If I have to explain why it's valuable, cut it. The mental cost of explaining it eats more than the persuasive upside."

**Situation:** Checking if your offer is finished | **Script:** "Can I text this to someone right now — just the offer, no explanation — and have them say 'yeah, I'm in'? If not, it's not done yet."

**Situation:** Founder thinking about switching to a new business | **Script:** "If this business made 10 times the money, would you still want to switch? No? Then we have a revenue problem, not a business problem. Let's fix the business."

**Situation:** Business owner complaining they can't find good people | **Script:** "How much gross profit does that role generate per year? Let's say $700K. What would you pay one time for an investment that cash-flows $700K annually? So why are you spending $2,500 on recruiting? Spend $150K. It's a four-month payback."

**Situation:** Marketing campaign that's gotten too complex | **Script:** "Stop. You have ten things you're selling with one reason each. Flip it. One thing, ten reasons. Run it. Highest-converting campaign you've ever had."

**Situation:** Underperforming employee you want to discipline | **Script:** [Internal frame] "What do I want to have happen? Better performance. Does yelling increase the odds of that? No — anxiety decreases output. What does increase the odds? Publicly rewarding the one thing they did right so they can see what the positive loop looks like."

**Situation:** Online attack or public criticism | **Script:** [Internal frame] "What do I want to have happen? This person moves on and forgets I exist. What increases those odds? Not engaging. The heavyweight champion of my options here is: do nothing."

**Situation:** Evaluating a new hire after two weeks | **Script:** "I have yet to find an A player I didn't immediately know was an A player within the first 14 days. If I'm still unsure at day 14, I have my answer."

**Situation:** Deciding whether to pursue a new opportunity | **Script:** "Is this my one new thing this year? I get one. If it's not, it waits. If it is, it gets 100%. Everything else is more of what's working."

**Situation:** Leader whose team is underperforming | **Script:** "I don't need to run diagnostics on the team. The leader is the problem. Always. Either the leader and the team, or just the leader — but never just the team."

**Situation:** Content creator who's never monetized their audience | **Script:** "Why did you build this brand? You said to make money. So how do you plan to make money if you never ask for it? You've been taught to delay — but delay until when? Today we start reaping."

**Situation:** Explaining why you're doing so much volume in ads | **Script:** "I made 5,000 ads that said basically the same thing. That was it. Most people in my own audience still didn't find out about the launch. Clear beats clever, and volume beats cleverness even harder."

**Situation:** Justifying keeping a simple, 'unscalable' revenue stream | **Script:** "That $4K/month in cash let me delay gratification on everything else. It paid for me to eat while the real business got built. A purist would call it a distraction. I call it fuel."

**Situation:** Someone asks what your brand moat is in an AI world | **Script:** "Give away the secrets, sell the implementation. People know how to fix their toilet — they still call the plumber. Show them exactly how to do it, and they'll pay you to do it for them."

**Situation:** A leader who "needs you" to make decisions | **Script:** "If they need you to drive the revenue, they are not the driver. You are. That's not a leader — that's an expensive employee with a title."

**Situation:** Presenting a component of an offer | **Script:** "Every bonus should be worth more than the entire value of the thing you sell. Not the aggregate — this one thing, alone, should be worth more than the price. Single-issue buyers. They keep listening until they have their one unlock."

## DECISION RULES

**When afraid to act** → Write down the worst-case chain in excruciating detail. If you can read it without it paralyzing you, proceed. Fear only survives in ambiguity.

**When your gut reaction is to counter-punch, retaliate, or engage** → Run the two-question frame first. Your instinct is almost always wrong. The correct answer is usually "do nothing."

**When something is working** → Do more of it before doing it better, and do it better before doing something new. One new thing per year maximum.

**When a business function is underperforming** → Go straight to the leader. Skip tactical diagnostics. The leader is always the problem. Replace or upgrade the leader; reassess the team after.

**When you're at full capacity (supply = demand)** → Hire before you promote. Expand supply first, even at short-term profit cost, to preserve reputation as you grow.

**When evaluating a potential business partner** → Ask: have we made, lost, or spent money together? If not, write everything down. Don't extend trust that hasn't been earned through economic behavior.

**When adding a bonus to an offer** → Ask: does this bonus alone justify the entire price? If no, cut it. If you have to explain it, cut it.

**When a new solution isn't showing results after months** → Give time time. Don't abandon the bridge two months into a four-month build. You chose the solution knowing the timeline.

**When a leader says they can build to target but needs you involved** → Redesign the role. They are not the leader. You are.

**When screening EAs or chiefs of staff** → Non-negotiables in order: response speed (under 60 seconds always), raw intelligence (can draft, decide, synthesize), maker/manager awareness. Pay above market; the leverage is massive.

**When evaluating whether to keep a low performer** → Ask: if this performance level were the standard across the team, would I be embarrassed? If yes, cut now. Sixes cost you more in culture drag than salary.

**When a founder seems burned out** → Ask "if this made 10x the money, would you quit?" If no → revenue fix. If yes → mission fix. Different interventions entirely.

**When a deal can pay you now vs. in 5 years** → Risk-adjust for the present value of time. Cash today can compound for 5 years. Don't let enterprise-value purism cause you to refuse real money.

**When deciding how to structure a large org** → Independent P&Ls, independent leaders, independent revenue lines. Shared services (software, legal, finance) only. Every leader must own their bottom line and not require you.

**When someone says "I can't find good people"** → Translate: "I haven't priced talent correctly." Price the role by its gross profit yield, not by its salary comp.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Vague fear** — If you can't write down exactly what you're afraid of in four sentences, you're letting fiction stop you. Fear is only real when you can't articulate it.

**Handshake deals with unearned trust** — "I only do business on a handshake" is something scoundrels say to avoid accountability. Five years of shared economic history earns a handshake. Week one does not.

**Complexity as sophistication in marketing** — Ten offers with one reason each is not a campaign. It's confusion. If your team had to build a slide deck to explain the campaign to you, the campaign is already broken.

**Bonuses that require explanation** — If you're spending 90 seconds on a webinar explaining why a bonus is valuable, delete the bonus. The explanation cost exceeds the persuasion value.

**Doing "new" to escape a hard "more" problem** — Switching to a new initiative is almost always avoidance of the genuinely hard constraint in the current business. New is exciting; more is harder. Excitement is the tell.

**Keeping sixes to seem fair** — Tolerance for low performance isn't kindness — it's a tax on your A players. They see what's accepted and either leave or lower their own bar.

**Centralizing resources across revenue lines** — Shared teams pulled between divisions produce divided focus and diluted accountability. Every revenue line needs its own leader who owns its own P&L.

**Being the answer to everything** — If your business requires your involvement to function, you don't have a business — you have a job that employs other people. The size of your business is capped at the size of your attention.

**Building a brand via cleverness instead of demonstration** — "Give away the secrets" means demonstrating real skill in public, repeatedly, until it hurts. Not clever positioning. Not witty copy. Proof of competence at volume.

**Reacting from gut every time** — Living reaction to reaction to reaction is seaweed in the ocean. You have no direction. You're just being moved by whatever hit you last. The two-question frame exists precisely to interrupt this loop.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Triple emphasis with no hedging** — Hormozi closes arguments by repeating the core claim three times with escalating finality: "The leader is always the problem. Not sometimes. The leader is always the problem. Period. Full stop. No questions." No caveats after the period.

**Concrete numbers as credibility anchors** — Abstractions get replaced with specifics immediately: not "a lot of slides" but "1,700 slides"; not "we ran ads" but "5,000 ads"; not "a lot of profit" but "$700,000 in gross profit per dentist per year." Numbers are how he signals he was actually there.

**The "Right?" confirm-and-advance move** — After laying out a logical chain, he drops "Right?" — not as a question but as a micro-commitment. Forces the listener to silently agree before the next claim lands. Used 3–5 times per argument.

**Binary compression of complex decisions** — "You either do it now when there's low stakes or you do it at the end when you both have high stakes. I promise you it's much better when there's low stakes." Everything collapses to two paths; one is obviously worse.

**"Said differently" re-entry** — When he feels the first formulation didn't land, he pivots with "Said differently" or "Here's a different way to think about it" and reruns the same idea from another angle — never admitting the first pass was unclear, just adding a new path to the same destination.

**Self-implication before the lesson** — He leads with personal failure or embarrassment before delivering the rule: "I'm 0 for 12 on executive assistants." "I had eight lawsuits this year." "My income went down for two years." The admission is load-bearing — it makes the rule credible instead of preachy.

**Rhetorical escalation to absurdity** — When he wants to expose how small a fear is, he voices it at its most embarrassing: "I didn't achieve my goals because I was afraid that somebody I don't know would tell me that I suck. When you say it like that, you're like... fuck." Scales the fear down by speaking it out loud at full fidelity.

**The internal monologue as self-coaching script** — He narrates his own decision process in real time: "And I asked myself, what kind of man do I want to be? Am I the man who cuts the corner? No. Get back to it. That's it." This is intentional — it models the exact self-talk the listener should run.
