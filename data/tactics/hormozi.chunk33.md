## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Rule of 100**
Every day, pick one acquisition channel and apply 100 repetitions: 100 warm outreaches, 100 cold prospect contacts, 100 minutes making and posting content, or $100/day minimum on ads. Consistency of inputs produces consistency of outputs — if you want one sale per day, you need 100 touches per day, not 10. Apply this before anything else when starting or restarting growth.

**The Rule of 300 (Warm-to-Lukewarm Lead Gen)**
Post one piece of content → make 100 thoughtful comments in communities where your ideal buyers already congregate → then do 1:1 outreach to everyone who liked your post, commented, liked your comment, or replied to your comment. These four groups have self-selected as engaged, which converts them from cold strangers to warm leads at zero cost. Open every DM by referencing the specific action they took: "Hey, I saw you liked my comment — are you trying to [outcome]?"

**Proof Stack Before Offer**
Before advertising, collect testimonials that mirror the specific avatars of buyers you want. You need proof that is "similar enough to them that they'll believe what you did for that person will also work for them." A weak offer with 100 pieces of proof beats a perfect offer with none. Work for free or at cost until you have the social proof to make paid acquisition convert.

**The Value Equation**
Four levers determine perceived value: (1) dream outcome the buyer wants, (2) perceived likelihood of achieving it, (3) time to result, (4) effort/sacrifice required. To increase value, increase 1 and 2 while decreasing 3 and 4. When a sales process has a gap — like a 24-hour insurance verification delay — that's a friction cost attacking lever 3 and 4. Eliminate it upstream.

**Sell at Point of Greatest Pain**
Buying intent is highest at maximum pain, not after relief. Present the offer before delivering the first dose of value — not after. If you adjust someone's back and then ask them to buy a 12-week plan, you've already reduced their urgency. Reverse the sequence: assessment → sale → first treatment. Apply this whenever your sales conversation comes after the value has already been delivered.

**Document / Demonstrate / Duplicate**
To transfer a skill from a top performer to a team: (1) Document — make a checklist of exactly what the top performer does; (2) Demonstrate — have the top performer follow the checklist in front of the trainee, proving it works; (3) Duplicate — have the trainee execute the checklist in front of you. Never skip steps. Used to scale sales processes, intake scripts, or any repeatable workflow across locations.

**Ascension Ladder (Have a Next Offer)**
After every sale, the customer needs a logical continuation — more of the same outcome, at a new level of pain. A guy who added 30 lbs to his bench doesn't think "I'm done"; he wants 30 more. If you don't have a second offer, you are forcing yourself to replace every customer instead of compounding them. Build the next offer before you close the first one.

**The Four-Part Ad Structure (What-Who-Win + Offer)**
Every paid ad has: (1) Callout — interrupt the right person (use a yes-question or location + pain combo: "Everett residents with chronic back pain?"); (2) What — describe their situation/nightmare in their own words; (3) Why — proof you can solve it; (4) Offer + CTA — state the specific thing they get and what to do next. If the offer doesn't appear in the first scroll, rewrite. Compress copy to bullets; test headline as the offer itself.

**Absolute vs. Relative ROI (Spend More on What Works)**
Optimizing for efficiency (ROAS ratio) at low spend is a trap. A 10:1 ROAS on $1,000 yields $9,000 net. A 5:1 ROAS on $10,000 yields $40,000 net. When you have a profitable channel, ask "how much CAN I spend before it breaks?" not "is the ratio good?" Give agencies a target CPA/CPL with a built-in buffer — share what you're willing to pay, not your true ceiling.

**Niche-Down for Template Efficiency**
The narrower your avatar, the more templated your delivery becomes — which means both higher quality (more specific solutions) and lower cost to deliver. Don't niche to be clever; niche because it lets you build systems that make your solution feel custom while actually being repeatable. The niche compounds: same problems → same solutions → faster execution → more proof → higher prices.

**Founder Magic vs. Scalable Systems**
The first location always outperforms because your fingerprint is on it — old relationships, your personal energy, word of mouth. Every location opened after that needs to run on systems, not personality. When performance varies wildly across locations, the constraint is almost never the strategy; it's the people executing the system. Fix ops and training before adding marketing spend to weaker locations.

**Big Bag of Why**
Write down every reason you're going to stick with the work — what you're running toward AND what you're running away from — and put it in front of your face where you do the boring repetitive work. Motivation fades in days. A written inventory of reasons outlasts it. Review it specifically when you want to skip a day.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect asks why you need their credit card for a free or low-cost offer | **Script:** "That's just how we've always done it." — say it matter-of-factly, don't over-explain. People stop questioning policy they can't change.

**Situation:** Opening a DM to someone who liked your post | **Script:** "Hey, saw you liked my post — what about it drew you in? Are you looking to [specific outcome your content covered]?"

**Situation:** Opening a DM to someone who commented | **Script:** "Hey, thanks for that comment — what made you engage with that? Are you currently trying to [outcome]?"

**Situation:** Opening a DM to someone who replied to your comment in a group | **Script:** "Hey, noticed you replied to my comment in [group] — I figured you might be dealing with [problem]. Are you looking for help with that?"

**Situation:** Asking for referrals after delivering results | **Script:** "Hey, I've got your before-and-afters now. I need a few more case studies — do you know anyone who's seen your results and might want the same thing?"

**Situation:** Transitioning a free client to paid | **Script:** "You know how you've been paying [charity/cost] for this? Want to just redirect that to me instead?" — frame it as a redirect, not a new ask.

**Situation:** Launching publicly after accumulating proof | **Script:** "For the last [X months], I've been doing [thing] for free. Here are the results. I now have [X] slots open — DM me or comment if you're interested." Lead with transformation images, not text.

**Situation:** Selling at point of greatest pain (intake) | **Script:** "Before we go over your results, I want to make sure we have a plan ready for you. Based on what I'm seeing, here's what I'd recommend…" — present the package before first treatment, not after.

**Situation:** Confirming appointment and reducing no-shows | **Script:** Send 3 texts + 1 call the night before + 1 call/text 2 hours before. If they haven't confirmed after all five touches, double-book the slot.

**Situation:** Introducing insurance verification on intake call | **Script:** "Just while I have you — do you have your insurance card handy? I want to make sure we have everything ready so your appointment is smooth." — frame it as convenience, not a sales step.

**Situation:** Doctor hesitates to present pricing | **Script:** Give them a visual checklist/clipboard script — they read the words, circle the number, turn it toward the patient. Machine the process so they don't have to improvise. The top performer's words become the team's script.

**Situation:** Explaining to an agency what you need | **Script:** "I can pay up to [number] per acquired customer. I don't care about the ROAS ratio — if you can stay under that CPA, spend as much as you want. Find me more keywords and make it rain."

**Situation:** Prospect pauses or pushes back on price | **Script:** Don't drop the price. Say "The price is what it is — I've thought hard about what this is worth to me. If it doesn't work for you, I understand." Never negotiate down; optionally offer free if you need proof.

**Situation:** Someone says your tactic "doesn't work" | **Script:** "You built the bridge with nine bricks instead of ten. It's not that building bridges doesn't work — you just haven't found the missing step yet. What specifically happened when you tried it?"

**Situation:** Upselling at point of satisfaction | **Script:** Don't upsell the same thing — open the next pain loop. "Now that you've solved [problem A], a lot of people in your position run into [problem B]. We actually help with that too. Want to hear how?"

**Situation:** Someone asks about Chick-fil-A pricing | **Script:** "It's full price or it's free. I don't negotiate with terrorists. If I'm going to do it for less, I'll make a strategic business decision to lower the price for everyone — not make a one-off deal that tells you and everyone you tell that my prices aren't real."

**Situation:** Convincing someone to put a card down for a $29 offer | **Script:** "We just put a card down to hold the spot — the doctor's time is set aside specifically for you. It's just how we confirm appointments. Any issues, we'll sort it out when you come in."

**Situation:** Coaching someone who is inconsistent with their outreach | **Script:** "Your business is inconsistent because you are inconsistent. Businesses are machines for consistency. If you put in 10 reach-outs a day, you get one sale every 10 days — it feels volatile, but it isn't. Do 100 inputs a day and get one a day. You're just extending the time horizon."

**Situation:** Team member says leads are bad quality | **Script:** "You're seeing 100% coverage insurance patients and skipping the sales muscle. Now when you get a patient who actually has to pay something, you've got nothing. These aren't bad leads — you just stopped training."

## DECISION RULES

**When your close rate is above 60-70%, raise your prices.** High close rates signal the market will bear more. Healthy close rate range for optimal price/volume balance is 30-40%. Above that, you're leaving money on the table.

**When you have no proof, work for free before running any paid acquisition.** A testimonial from a result is worth more in future revenue than whatever you could have charged for that job. Stack proof until it's undeniable, then monetize.

**When a referral comes in, do not lower the price for them.** Referrals buy at higher prices and refer others more readily than any other lead type. Treat them as full-price buyers from day one.

**When a location/channel/tactic isn't working, check if the inputs are actually being done consistently before changing the strategy.** Businesses fail at execution before they fail at strategy. Audit the team before auditing the model.

**When you find a profitable ad channel (3:1 ROAS or better), increase spend before optimizing.** Absolute return beats efficiency ratio. Double the budget first, then optimize later.

**When you're doing something free or below market to build proof, set non-monetary expectations upfront.** Testimonials, referrals, feedback, before/after photos — state these as the compensation before starting the engagement.

**When a sales process has a multi-step delay (verify insurance → wait 24 hours → present plan), move every possible step earlier in the sequence.** Friction before the sale equals lost revenue. Move verification to the phone, print insurance plans the morning of, hand the doc a pre-loaded clipboard.

**When expanding to multiple locations, fix the weakest location's people problem before adding any marketing spend to it.** Marketing amplifies what's already working. Spending more on a broken operation just burns budget faster.

**When you want to train a skill into someone else, document first, demonstrate second, have them duplicate third — in that order.** Never skip the checklist step. If you can't follow your own checklist consistently, the checklist isn't finished yet.

**When building a recurring business, have the next offer ready before the current engagement ends.** Don't close the 12-week program and then scramble to find new clients. Offer continuation before the client reaches satisfaction and urgency drops.

**When someone objects to a process step (why do I need to give my card?), respond with "that's just how we've always done it."** Appeal to policy, not logic. Over-explaining creates negotiation. Policy closes the loop.

**When commenting in communities to generate leads, make the single best comment on the post — not a pitch.** You're not competing with everyone online; you're competing with everyone in those comments. A thoughtful response piggybacks on the post's reach and creates inbound engagement to outreach against.

**When you feel unmotivated, don't count the work you do while excited.** Work starts when excitement ends. The discipline gap — doing the boring inputs on day 47 with no visible result — is the only thing that separates you from the people who quit.

**When a business is profitable but the owner wants to stop doing it for something new, don't.** Ask: "What if I just worked 10x harder at the thing that's already working?" Switching to a new business is almost never better than scaling what already converts.

**When SEO is available in a local market and you're playing a long-term game, start it immediately even if it takes a year.** It is the highest-ROI channel at scale because the marginal cost of each lead approaches zero. Every new location you open, start local SEO on day one.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Discounting to close.** Every discount communicates that your stated price was fake. Every person that buyer tells now knows your prices are negotiable. You've trained the entire market to haggle you. Either full price or free — never in between.

**Selling after delivering value.** If you adjust someone's back, help them make money, or solve their immediate problem — and then ask them to buy — you're pitching to a satisfied customer. Urgency is gone. Present the offer while the pain is real.

**Starting a new business instead of scaling what's working.** The moment something clicks (making $4k/month online working 4 hours/week), the instinct to "start something legit" will destroy it. The correct move is 10x the inputs. Recognize this trap before it costs you years.

**No ascension offer.** If every client terminates and you have to replace them from scratch, you're running on a treadmill. The absence of a second offer isn't a product problem — it's a strategic failure that compounds over time into a growth ceiling.

**Optimizing for ROAS ratio instead of absolute profit.** A 10:1 return on $1,000 is $9,000 net. A 5:1 return on $100,000 is $450,000 net. Don't let a pretty efficiency number keep you from pouring money into a profitable machine.

**Making one-off deals.** Pricing exceptions aren't isolated — they propagate through word of mouth and through your own psychology. Each exception makes the next one easier. Your price is either real or it isn't.

**Starting paid acquisition before you have proof.** Running ads with no testimonials means you're spending money to tell strangers you're unproven. Get results first, even for free, so the ad has something to point at.

**Building infrastructure before demand exists.** Signing a 5-year brick-and-mortar lease when your online business is already profitable and low-overhead is solving for legitimacy rather than economics. Validate demand, then build fixed costs — never the reverse.

**Variability in sales process across team members.** When top-performing salespeople carry the revenue, you don't have a sales system — you have a person dependency. If you can't document and transfer the process via checklist, you haven't finished building it yet.

**Quitting the inputs when results don't show immediately.** The weakest operators require immediate reward for effort. Long-cycle businesses — SEO, content, referral compounding — pay out in months or years. Stopping at week three means you paid all the costs and collected none of the returns.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The numbered list as forced velocity.** Hormozi almost never explains one thing — he explains 3, 5, or 10. "There are four stages... here's number one... here's number two..." This signals momentum, builds authority, and signals that stopping early costs you the most valuable parts. Use numbered structures to front-load value and prevent disengagement.

**Analogy then application, always.** Abstract concept → vivid, concrete, often absurd analogy → direct application to the audience's situation. Selling at point of pain becomes: you walk into a restaurant starving → I sell you two steaks → you walk in full → now you don't want one. Then: "Does this sound familiar? Because I hear sales calls that do exactly this." Never explain in theory without immediately grounding it.

**Self-deprecation as credibility.** "That was dumb." "I was just dumb." "I would have told myself..." He regularly narrates his own past mistakes in plain language — no hedging, no spin. This pattern builds trust faster than achievements because it signals the lessons were actually paid for with real failures, not borrowed from a book.

**"To be clear" as a reset before specificity.** Used before narrowing a broad claim: "To be clear — you don't just spend 100 minutes making content and do nothing. You post it." Signals that what follows is the precise, executable version of the general statement just made. A verbal flag that the tactical instruction is incoming.

**Constraint-first business analysis.** When diagnosing a business, Hormozi always asks for the bottleneck explicitly ("what's staying in the way?") before making any recommendations. He then sequences improvements by: (1) low effort / high impact first, (2) high effort / high impact second, (3) nothing structural until people and process are validated. Don't recommend a growth tactic before confirming operations can execute it.

**The "and so" chain.** Arguments are rarely more than two steps — premise → implication → action. "Referrals are free. And so if you're poor, great place to start. And so reach out to everyone in your contacts before you spend a dollar on ads." Short causal chains keep logic auditable and prevent the listener from losing the thread. Avoid subordinate clauses; use sequential declarative sentences instead.

**Stakes escalation through contrast.** "Imagine a competitor who needs a result the moment he does the work, versus one who can work for a year with no result. Who wins?" He creates a vivid mental image of the weak version first, then the strong version, so the listener self-selects into the strong version identity. Contrast beats instruction for behavior change.
