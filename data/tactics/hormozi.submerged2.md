## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Grand Slam Offer Ladder**
Never cold-sell your highest-ticket offer. Build a front-end product (5–20% of core price) that solves one fast-ROI problem and converts strangers into customers. Then ascend: Lead Magnet → Front-End Offer (one-call close) → Core Offer → Retainer/Continuity. Apply by mapping all four levels before running a single ad.

**The Pricing Ladder (Close Rate → Price Multiplier)**
Close rate is a direct pricing diagnostic. 80%+ = underpriced 3–4x. 60–80% = underpriced 2–3x. 40–60% = underpriced 1.25–2x. 30–40% with a proper pre-sell motion = correctly priced. Below 30% = fix avatar or sales motion first. Apply it: run your close rate this week — if above 40%, raise price before diagnosing anything else.

**The Give-to-Ask Ratio**
Every interaction either deposits or withdraws from the trust account. Free content, over-delivered products, and kept promises are deposits. Asks for money are withdrawals. The amount of value given before the ask is almost directly proportional to revenue generated when you ask. Butcher Box ran full cooking classes with zero CTAs for weeks; the ask converted at multiples of a cold pitch. Rule: 3.5 gives per ask minimum. If your ratio is inverted, flip it and hold for 18+ months before judging.

**The Core Four Acquisition Channels**
There are exactly four ways to get customers: warm outreach (1:1, known), cold outreach (1:1, strangers), paid ads (1:many, strangers), content (1:many, existing audience). Pick one, go all-in, spend the first four hours of every day on it. Dabbling across all four at 25% each is how you get mediocre everywhere. For local brick-and-mortar, paid ads with geo pin-drop almost always wins. For high-value B2B, outbound. For creators, content.

**The Value Equation (4 Levers)**
Perceived value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay × Effort/Sacrifice). Every piece of copy, every ad, every pitch either amplifies the numerator or shrinks the denominator. Use these four axes to generate unlimited angles: faster result, easier path, higher certainty, bigger dream. Run each lever as a separate ad variation.

**The Pain Articulation Trust Transfer**
If you can describe a prospect's pain more precisely than they can describe it themselves, they will trust you to solve it more than any promise you make. List the five most recent, visceral moments of failure your avatar experiences and write those as copy — not categories of pain, but exact incidents ("no more disaster back-of-bar when you open," not "operational challenges"). Zero degrees of separation between their language and your copy is the closest thing to a guaranteed close.

**The Guarantee Formula**
Every guarantee: "If you don't [X] in [Y time], we will [Z]." Z doesn't require a full refund — partial refund, added time, or gross profit back all work. A 10% money-back guarantee that closes 20% more deals nets more than a 100% guarantee that closes 22% more and doubles refunds. Find the minimum guarantee that changes prospect behavior, not the most dramatic one.

**The 30-Day Payback Period**
Cash collected in the first 30 days must exceed CAC + COGS. This enables credit-card-funded growth: borrow interest-free, acquire customer, collect before the bill is due, repeat. Apply by front-loading cash via initiation fees, setup fees, prepay discounts, layaway, or physical product bundles. If Day 1 is cash-positive, the business math works at any scale.

**Gross Margin as the Business Ceiling**
Net margin cannot exceed gross margin — it is the hard cap on everything downstream: marketing, sales, admin, profit. 70% vs. 90% gross margin looks like a 20-point difference; it's actually a 2.5x difference in per-customer profit. 80% is the minimum viable floor for any service business. Before fixing ops, sales, or scaling, check gross margin. If it's under 80%, the entire business math is broken at the foundation.

**Revenue Retention as the North Star Metric**
In software and education, the single most important number is Month-12 retention. 40% = pre-product-market-fit but interesting. 60%+ = product-market fit. 70%+ = growth engine. Before adding acquisition spend, before launching new products, before expanding markets — solve for M12. Once retention compounds, acquisition becomes a multiplier rather than a treadmill.

**The Three Affiliate Integration Tiers**
Tier 1: give away your lead magnet free with their purchase (easiest, zero revenue, gets leads). Tier 2: let them sell your lead magnet and keep 100% of proceeds — your cost is delivery, your gain is the customer relationship and the upsell. Tier 3: standard commission kickback (weakest — generic, rarely changes behavior). Always try Tier 2 before defaulting to Tier 3. An affiliate who sends you one customer hasn't been activated — they're running a test. Close the loop explicitly, then immediately reopen the door: "Which of your [clients/patients/customers] this week would be a good fit?"

**The Barrel Framework**
Most founders are single-barreled — they're the only one who can generate output from scratch. Bullets (capital, hires) are useless without a barrel to fire them through. When you find someone who can create something from nothing at your level, that's a barrel — you don't hire barrels, you partner with them. Ask: do I have more ammo than barrels to fire it? Undertalent, not overexpansion, is the real constraint.

**Proof Over Promise**
11,382 reviews at 4.7 stars + a plain offer always beats a Grand Slam offer + one review from your mom. Proof is your single highest-priority asset before entering any market or launching any product. Run a free beta in exchange for testimonials, feedback, and referrals. Collect proof before spending on acquisition. Show don't sell — state the facts, tell the truth.

**The 70/20/10 Steal-From-Yourself Rule**
70% of all creative and strategic effort should replicate what already works. 20% should be adjacent variations — same hook, different surface. Only 10% should be genuinely new experiments. Most entrepreneurs invert this: 70% new ideas, 20% slight variations, 10% repeating proven work. Identify your one highest-converting hook and use it relentlessly until the numbers break — you'll get bored of it long before your customers do.

**The Consumption Gap Problem**
People cancel not because the product lacks value, but because they perceive they're not using enough of it. Adding features widens the gap between what exists and what they consume. Planet Fitness learned 80% of users only use cardio and weights — stripped everything else, dropped price, retention improved. Rule: give 100% of the one thing they want and charge accordingly. Every component that wouldn't be missed should be cut.

**The Fewest Need-to-Believes Rule**
A plan is only as strong as the number of assumptions it requires. Every additional "and then this also has to be true" exponentially reduces execution probability. Strip your plan to the minimum viable set of assumptions and attack only those first. If it works with one assumption (e.g., "10 commission-only reps close 5 doors/day"), don't add a second until the first is proven.

**The Three Outcomes Model**
When facing a hard stretch, only three endings exist: you quit, the circumstance gets easier, or you get harder. Two of three resolve in your favor. Persistence is statistically dominant. Apply by naming which of the three is occurring — it removes emotion and converts the problem to a waiting game.

**Same Offer, New Wrapper**
Once you have a winning offer, never retire it — rename it. Holiday Promo, Super Bowl Special, Spring Sale, Back-to-School Deal. The offer stays identical; the reason changes. The reason creates novelty and urgency without rebuilding what's already converting. Apply any time you need a promotional hook without reinventing the product.

**The Management Diamond**
When someone isn't doing what you want, run through five root causes in order: (1) They didn't know you wanted it. (2) They don't know how. (3) They don't know the deadline. (4) Something is blocking them. (5) They don't want to. Entrepreneurs default to #5 — it's almost always one of the first four.

**BOOM — Business Order of Magnitude Change**
An optimization is capped at 100% by definition. Advertising (volume of reach) can 100x the number of people who know you exist. When stuck tweaking ROAS from 10.1:1 to 10.2:1, you're playing a capped game. Before optimizing anything, ask whether simply 100x-ing volume would compound harder. More / Better / New are the only three growth strategies: More is the highest risk-adjusted return because you've already proven it works.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect objects to price or pauses after hearing the number | **Script:** "It sounds like that number landed wrong. What does fair look like to you?"

**Situation:** Launching a promotion without a real holiday | **Script:** "I'm celebrating [anniversary / milestone / dog's birthday] — and because of that, everything [category] is [offer]. Good through [specific date]."

**Situation:** Prospect hasn't watched the pre-call video | **Script:** "The first question I'm going to ask on the call is whether you watched the video. It has charts and figures that'll take us three times as long to cover verbally. Watch it now — I'll grab a coffee."

**Situation:** Seeding the upsell at the start of a front-end engagement | **Script:** "My whole goal isn't just this — my real goal is to help you do [Y]. This is just the fastest way to get you there. I wanted to name that upfront."

**Situation:** Upselling at the first win moment | **Script:** "If you like that, you're going to love what I have for you next. You just made an extra $X. What I really want to do is build this into a consistent machine — and I'll apply the $[front-end price] you already paid toward your first quarter."

**Situation:** Prospect asks for split payments | **Script:** "Sure — as many splits as you want. We schedule your start date once it's paid in full. Most people do half now, half in 30 days and get going next month. Want to do that?"

**Situation:** Diagnosing whether an employee is driving business outcomes | **Script:** "What do you do? — No, not 'analyze' — what does that actually mean, physically? Okay. What do we do as a business? And how does what you do help what we do?"

**Situation:** Challenging a data-review meeting that doesn't change decisions | **Script:** "What decision will we make differently after seeing this data? If nothing changes based on this number, we don't need to track it and we don't need to meet about it."

**Situation:** Getting a first testimonial / launching a new product | **Script:** "I'm not charging you. I'll do this for free. All I ask is you tell me exactly what works, what doesn't, and if it goes well, maybe a testimonial. That's the trade."

**Situation:** Selling at point of maximum deprivation | **Script:** "Guy like you? You're going to need two steaks." — Offer the upsell before the first bite, when hunger is highest. Present the next step before the current problem is solved.

**Situation:** Opening a pain-based ad hook for cold traffic | **Script:** "Is your [X] [visceral problem word]? When was the last time anyone [posted something good / noticed / praised] your [thing]? If you look at your reviews, are you seeing [low-tier version of your product]?"

**Situation:** Motivation has faded on a project | **Script:** "This is exactly where everyone else stops. Which means staying is where you separate from them. Work begins once motivation stops — this is where the reps actually count."

**Situation:** Training a salesperson to stop freelancing the script | **Script:** "Thousands of calls and thousands of hours went into this script. You are not better than the script. You say the words because we know these words close people. Period."

**Situation:** Opening a door-to-door AB offer | **Script:** "We give everyone three months free just for switching — no matter what. But if you commit to a full year, we throw in the bin at no cost. It's a $99 value. If you'd rather do three months, no problem — you pay for the bin separately and we waive setup."

**Situation:** Activating a dormant affiliate | **Script:** "Hey, [client name] was amazing — we did [X], they loved it. By the way, which [patients/clients/customers] did you see this week who'd be a good fit for what we do?" Not 'do you have any' — 'which ones.'

**Situation:** Competitor has more budget than you | **Script:** "They compete with capital. We compete with creativity and things that don't scale. A handwritten note from the CEO sells 10 units and costs 8 minutes. They can't do that."

**Situation:** Someone calculates TAM as share of existing buyers | **Script:** "You're calculating TAM as a share of existing buyers. I'm calculating TAM as everyone I could convert from doing nothing, or from a worse alternative. Those are different numbers by an order of magnitude."

**Situation:** Diagnosing a business's core constraint fast | **Script:** "If only one thing were true for this to work — just one — what would it be? And if that were true, what stops you next? And if that were solved, what would stop you then?"

**Situation:** Referral rate is low and client wants better incentives | **Script:** "Would your best customers refer you right now, unprompted, if there were zero incentive? If not, you don't have an incentive problem — you have a product problem. Fix the product first."

**Situation:** Prospect resists group format due to privacy/shame | **Script:** "You're afraid of being embarrassed because you think you're the only one who deals with this. The truth is, the only thing that's not normal is that people think it's not normal."

**Situation:** Pitching a guarantee for a home services business | **Script:** "I guarantee delivery on time and on budget, or I give back my gross profit — which is 20%. You still have your roof. I have skin in the game. People don't want their money back. They want the roof."

**Situation:** Prospect says your niche is too small | **Script:** "You don't need to be the best in the world. You need to be the best in a puddle. It's more important that you ARE the best than what you are the best OF."

**Situation:** Defending running the same content / ad creative again | **Script:** "Seneca's been dead 2,000 years. I still love seeing his quotes. You think people are tired of it — but most haven't heard it once yet."

**Situation:** Justifying high ad spend to someone nervous about scale | **Script:** "There are businesses spending $2 million a day on the same platforms. You have more runway than you think. Nothing breaks — the platforms want you to spend, they built the system for it."

**Situation:** Writing a cold-traffic identity callout hook | **Script:** "[Location/identity descriptor] are getting [unexpected result] — here's why." No product mention. Pure curiosity.

**Situation:** Opening a sales call with a clear agenda | **Script:** "Let me set the agenda: I'm going to walk through [topic] with you, understand a few metrics about your business, and at the end, if it looks like a good fit, I'll explain how we might be able to help. Sound good?"

**Situation:** Telling your team you're bringing in a senior leader | **Script:** "Guess who hasn't taken a company to a billion dollars before? Me. So I'd like to bring on someone who has."

**Situation:** Hard close at an in-person event | **Script:** "If today was valuable, do you mind if I tell you a little about what we do?" [15-minute stack.] "We've got one table at dinner tonight — it's for everyone who's ready to go. What do you say?"

**Situation:** Agitating the pain gap in a pitch | **Script:** "Let me show you the gap between where you are right now and where you could be — and let's make that gap feel as big as it actually is."

**Situation:** Justifying why you're starting with a lower-ticket offer | **Script:** "It's too cold of a sale right now. Either we run a much longer sales process, or we lower the bar so I can prove myself first. I'd rather earn the right to ask for the bigger number."

## DECISION RULES

- **When close rate exceeds 80%**, raise price 3–4x before doing anything else — the market is telling you it's underpriced.

- **When close rate is below 30%**, fix avatar targeting or sales motion before touching price in either direction.

- **When gross margin is below 80% in a service business**, stop scaling — the entire business math is broken at the foundation.

- **When payback period exceeds 30 days**, redesign the money model before adding acquisition spend.

- **When annual retention is below 80%** (B2B), you are filling a leaky bucket — fix retention before buying more acquisition.

- **When a metric doesn't change your behavior whether it goes up or down**, stop tracking it — data collection without behavioral output is cost with no return.

- **When results feel volatile and inconsistent**, calculate what daily volume would be required to normalize your annual close rate into a daily result. You likely have a volume problem, not a conversion problem.

- **When referral rate is low**, fix the product before improving the incentive — incentives can only amplify what's already happening, not create behavior from zero.

- **When you find an acquisition system that's working**, immediately reinvest in it rather than treating it as steady-state — marginal returns there are higher than anywhere else.

- **When giving a discount**, make the customer earn it with information (quiz, challenge, completion) so you capture data that funds future remarketing. Never discount for free.

- **When your conversion window opens** (buyer enters hyper-buying mode), eliminate all friction and do not redirect. Extend the buying window; do not break it.

- **When entering a new market or launching a new product**, work for free first — collect testimonials, feedback, and referrals before spending on acquisition.

- **When a competitor has more capital**, compete with things that don't scale: handwritten notes, founder video, personalized outreach. These compound into word-of-mouth their ad budget cannot replicate.

- **When an ad has been running unchanged for 12+ months**, treat it as a proven winner and protect it — your boredom is not a data signal.

- **When running a promotion**, reduce site navigation to 3–4 items max, all pointing to the offer. Paradox of choice kills conversion.

- **When sales calendar utilization exceeds 85%**, hire another rep instead of jamming the calendar — fully booked looks productive while CAC silently rises.

- **When under $1M in revenue**, dedicate the first four hours of every day to one acquisition channel — not split across four at 25% each.

- **When adding a human to any funnel stage**, double your required LTV:CAC ratio as cushion for ramp inefficiency.

- **When motivation fades on a project**, don't stop — two of three outcomes (it gets easier, you get harder) resolve in your favor. Work begins once motivation stops.

- **When an employee can't explain how their work connects to outcomes**, run the Management Diamond in order (know, how, when, blocked, won't) before concluding it's a motivation problem.

- **When supply constrained**, resolve directly — raise price, change delivery ratio, productize, or hire. Do not build a cheaper productized version with the same CAC.

- **When considering a new business or initiative**, first ask whether the constraint can be solved in another way that doesn't require starting from scratch.

- **When scaling an input**, try one-to-many first, then delegation, then automation — one-to-many is leverage without headcount.

- **When testing a new acquisition channel**, apply Rule of 100: 100 actions per day for 100 days before drawing conclusions.

- **When allocating marketing budget at scale**, target 70% brand/awareness/education and 30% direct response — expect an 18-month lag before brand compounds. Don't touch the ratio when it looks slow.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

- **Calling leads more than 60 seconds after opt-in.** You are paying 4x market rate for customers and don't know it. Every hour of delay multiplies acquisition cost. This is the single most expensive habit in sales operations.

- **Scaling before nailing retention and LTV:CAC.** Pouring spend into a broken model amplifies every crack. "When you scale problems, they get meaner and uglier and they have more faces on them."

- **Asking before giving.** Launching a hard CTA before the audience has received disproportionate value. The ask lands hardest when it follows genuine, sustained generosity — not before it.

- **Measuring brand investment at month 3.** The New Balance model didn't pay off until month 19. Measuring brand spend on a direct-response timeline kills legitimate strategy before it can compound.

- **Using incentives as a substitute for product quality.** If your NPS is low and you're obsessing over affiliate commission structure, you have the causality backwards. No incentive system survives a bad product at scale.

- **Changing a proven control because you're bored of it.** Your boredom is not a data signal. The audience's conversion behavior is. Change is more likely to break what works than improve it.

- **Discounting without a reason.** A discount without a story is just margin erosion. A discount with a story (milestone, holiday, event) is a buying event.

- **Showing all products during a high-intent moment.** 24 jam jars get more browsers, fewer buyers. During peak buying periods, narrow to 3–4 hero offers. More choice = fewer decisions.

- **Doing arts and crafts and calling it business.** Reorganizing your project management tool, redesigning your website, cleaning your CRM — these feel productive because they're business-adjacent. Unless they trace directly to more customers or higher LTV, they're expensive hobbies.

- **Building only centralized acquisition (employees/agencies).** Centralized lead getters require you to scale the management relationship. They're necessary but not sufficient. If 100% of acquisition is centralized, you're one key-person departure from a crisis.

- **Teaching procedural knowledge in free content.** When free content gives people a full to-do list they can act on without buying, they don't need to buy. Free content must create the desire to implement, not the ability.

- **Unlimited messaging access as a product feature.** Direct-message access at any time trains learned helplessness, prevents renewals (clients feel they haven't used what they bought), and makes the business unscalable. It's a retention liability dressed as a feature.

- **Confusing what people love after buying with what makes them buy.** Slack is loved after purchase. It doesn't drive renewals. Features that feel like value inside the product may be invisible — or irrelevant — to a prospect making a purchase decision.

- **Advertising all your services at once.** You're not a menu; you're a door. Pick one to three offers that convert profitably on the front end. Sell everything else after they walk in.

- **One star suppressing the whole team by ignoring the script.** A top-performing rep who freelances kills consistency, blocks scale, and poisons culture. The star's short-term revenue is not worth the long-term ceiling they impose.

- **Modeling the plateau, not the rise.** Copying what successful people do after they're rich — not what they did to get there. You're watching the outcome, not the input. The pattern that matters is in the rise.

- **The "I've said it already" fallacy.** Believing your audience has absorbed a message because you've said it. Most people in your audience haven't heard it once. Repetition is not a flaw — it is the strategy.

- **Building for hypothetical customers instead of testing with real ones.** Put experimental offers inside an existing campaign (advent calendar, bundle, challenge reward) before committing to full production. Get real buyer signal from your warmest audience first.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

- **Pain before promise, always.** Lead with the exact moment of suffering — specific, visceral, named — then attach the outcome. "No more disaster back-of-bar when you open" > "improved operational efficiency." People buy relief from the departure point, not just the destination.

- **The ratio reframe.** Converts abstract strategic advice into specific numbers, percentages, or time windows. "If you're at 70% asks and 30% give — flip that." "If you're closing 80%, you're underpriced 3–4x." Makes the abstract immediately executable.

- **The triple-beat declarative.** Staccato three-part lists land rules with finality. "No regressions. No missing imports. No broken routes." "Clear. Not clever. Proof over promise." The rhythm itself communicates urgency and completeness.

- **Binary frame for clarity, nuance for precision.** Lead with a clean binary to provoke attention ("data-driven or data-distracted?"), then add the nuance ("it's not that data is bad — data that doesn't change behavior is cost with no return"). The binary creates the hook; the nuance creates the trust.

- **Story → extract principle → generalize.** Personal story (e.g., Butcher Box, New Balance, Gymshark) → "here's what that actually taught me" → "and here's how it applies to any domain." Never lets a story sit without explicitly pulling the transferable rule.

- **The "what people miss" pivot.** After stating the common understanding of a concept, pause and redirect: "But here's what most people miss…" Signals the real insight is about to land and frames the speaker as ahead of conventional wisdom.

- **Rhetorical escalation via real numbers.** Grounds abstractions in specific figures: "11,382 reviews at 4.7 stars," "$3.4 million a minute," "28,000 orders per minute." Numbers aren't decoration — they're proof the framework is load-bearing, not theoretical.

- **Short declarative closes.** Every explanation ends with a one-sentence summary designed to land as a takeaway: "That's the business." "That's all he has to do." "The offer has to change what they do, or you didn't make a real offer." Not argued with — absorbed.

- **The trace-to-revenue discipline.** Every activity, metric, and claim gets stress-tested with a chain-of-logic back to "more customers" or "higher LTV." Rhythm: state the activity → trace the chain → validate or eliminate. Anything that can't survive the chain gets cut.

- **Stacked analogies from unrelated industries.** Never relies on one metaphor — stacks two or three until the pattern is undeniable. Gym membership + Planet Fitness + Who Wants to Be a Millionaire all illustrate one principle. This is how heuristics are taught, not just tactics.

- **The compounding sequence.** Arguments are structured as: small thing → repeated → compounds into large thing. Applied to brand (each positive experience compounds), referrals (one super-referrer 10x's a cohort), content (fractional gives accumulate into full gives). The mechanism is always shown explicitly, not implied.

- **Self-referential credibility without bragging.** Establishes authority by recounting specific moments of discovery ("the day my sales rep went two-for-four while I was out was the day I realized…") rather than status claims. The story does the credibility work; the lesson does the value work.
