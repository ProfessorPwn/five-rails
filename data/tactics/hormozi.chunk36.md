## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Extinction Curve (Determination as Behavior)**
Most people stop after one null result — no response, no sale, no traction. Determination isn't a feeling; it's measured by how many times you act without visible reward before quitting. The maniacs who knock down the door believe someone's on the other side even when there's no signal. Apply it: when a channel or initiative shows nothing after 30 days, diagnose it rather than abandon it — ask what the next door is, not whether to keep going.

**2. The Input-Output Equation**
Every business must define: what specific actions directly increase revenue? If you can't name the core activities, your employees definitely can't either. Apply it: before adding complexity (new products, new channels), verify you can state the equation clearly — "When we do X amount of Y, we get Z in revenue" — and that the team can recite it.

**3. Supply vs. Demand Constraint Diagnosis**
Before prescribing any growth tactic, determine whether the bottleneck is supply (capacity, delivery, talent) or demand (leads, awareness). Marketing into a supply constraint wastes money and breaks trust. Apply it: ask "Do I have more leads than I can handle, or fewer leads than I need?" — then prescribe accordingly and never mix up the sequence.

**4. Drop Low / Go High (SMB Pricing Positioning)**
For SMB-facing businesses, there are exactly two viable models: go ultra-cheap with near-zero delivery cost and high volume, or go premium with a higher-sophistication buyer who already has a sales process and proven metrics. The $1,500–$3,000/month mid-market price point has a natural 4–6 month average stick, compressing margins until the business feels like a nonprofit. Apply it: if you're in the middle, pick a direction or accept the slow bleed.

**5. Regret Asymmetry**
People experience regret when they imagine the upside of an unchosen path without accounting for the downside cost they avoided. The girl that got away, the deal you passed on — you only picture the upside, never the trade-off you'd have suffered. Apply it: when someone is frozen by fear of missing out, ask them to name the full cost of the alternative, not just the upside. Reframe: "either want less or trade more."

**6. The Talent Funnel (Parallel Recruiting Machine)**
Just as you have a customer acquisition funnel — lead gen, nurture, close, onboard, retain — you need an identical parallel structure for hiring: applicant gen, applicant nurture, interviewing, onboarding, retention and ascension. You have to spend on talent supply as reliably as you spend on demand. Apply it: build recruiting KPIs that mirror your sales KPIs — cost per applicant, close rate, ramp time, retention rate.

**7. Intelligence vs. Determination (Paul Graham's Trade-off)**
Drop determination one point and intelligence stays high — you get a very smart person who convinces themselves nothing is possible. Drop intelligence and keep determination at 100 — they still tend to win. Intelligence without action is inert; determination finds a way around every obstacle. Apply it: when hiring or evaluating yourself, weight track record of follow-through over credentials and raw intelligence.

**8. Short-term Low Status → Long-term High Status**
Sam Altman's framing: willingly trade short-term status (looking like an idiot, getting laughed at, being wrong in public) for long-term asymmetric upside. The bet against conventional wisdom only pays if you're willing to be wrong-seeming for a long time first. Apply it: when facing criticism for a contrarian move that has a sound thesis, label it explicitly — "I'm trading short-term embarrassment for long-term position" — and hold the line.

**9. One of Zero**
Not just "one of one" — doing what only you can do — but doing things you didn't know were possible, living what the best version of you would live. The standard isn't your current ceiling; it's what you'd be capable of if you stretched beyond known potential. Apply it: in content, offers, and positioning, ask "has anyone done this before?" — if yes, go further.

**10. The Pricing-First Cash Flow Fix**
When a business is in the swamp (stuck revenue, squeezed margins), the order of operations is: fix pricing and packaging first to free up cash flow → use that cash flow to fund paid acquisition → layer content and brand building third. Most founders want to market their way out, but the real fix is margin. Apply it: before spending on ads, calculate whether the current price can profitably support a customer acquisition cost.

**11. Data-First Before AI-First**
You cannot build an AI-first company without first being a data-first company. AI runs on data; if you don't have complete, structured, reliable data flowing through every function, there is no AI layer to build. Apply it: sequence the stack — complete data architecture → AI layer on top → automation compounds the leverage. Using ChatGPT for emails is not being AI-first.

**12. Active vs. Passive Income Separation**
Keep active income active and passive income passive. The moment you spend time making passive investments "more active" to squeeze 5% better returns, you've incurred a time cost that would return far more in your core business. Apply it: if you're tempted to optimize a passive investment, calculate what the same hour in the business would produce — it will almost always be higher.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect stuck on pricing / you suspect price is wrong before tackling marketing | **Script:** "Before we even talk about ads, what's the pricing and packaging look like? Because if the margin isn't there, every customer we buy makes the problem worse, not better."

**Situation:** Client wants to market more but is actually supply-constrained | **Script:** "Hold on — do you have more leads than you can handle right now, or fewer leads than you need? Because those are completely different problems and they have completely different solutions."

**Situation:** Diagnosing a stalled service business | **Script:** "What percentage of your revenue comes from ads, referrals, content, and outbound? Walk me through all four buckets. Because right now I'm just trying to find which one's actually moving the needle."

**Situation:** Business owner refuses to sell while "figuring things out" | **Script:** "I can promise you: you will make more money if you sell people. Making the number of new sales per month a goose egg will not grow the business. That one I'll guarantee."

**Situation:** SMB-serving agency at mid-tier pricing ($1,500–$3,000/month) asking how to grow | **Script:** "You're right in the sweet spot of churn. Average stick at that price with an SMB is 4 to 6 months. So back of napkin — if you're closing 10 a month, you'll hit a ceiling around 100K MRR and stall. Your only moves are go way down in price with near-zero delivery, or go way up-market to a buyer who actually knows their metrics."

**Situation:** Founder distracted by side businesses and real estate | **Script:** "Keep active active, keep passive passive. If the real estate isn't eating your time, I don't care. But the second you're optimizing a passive investment with active hours, you're trading your highest-leverage time for your lowest-leverage return."

**Situation:** Business owner scared to hire because market might soften | **Script:** "Let's do the math. One sales rep generates X in gross profit. You pay them Y. That's a [ratio]-to-one return. You can put that same money in the S&P or you can buy this. Which one would you take?"

**Situation:** Founder building courses/products to avoid fixing the core service | **Script:** "Let's erase those for a moment — the books, the courses. They're not real for the purpose of this conversation. If you didn't have those things, what would you do to grow the business? Because I think you already know the answer and you're avoiding it because it's hard."

**Situation:** Business owner worried about AI disrupting their market prematurely | **Script:** "What business metric is actually off right now? Because if churn isn't meaningfully up and revenue's growing, you might be solving a problem that doesn't exist. The person who's already all-in on AI wasn't your customer to begin with — they built their own before AI made it easy."

**Situation:** Attribution tracking problem — spending on ads without knowing ROI | **Script:** "You don't have a marketing problem. You have an input-output equation problem. You don't know if you're putting in a dollar and getting five back or fifty back. Fix attribution first — then you can make confident decisions about spend."

**Situation:** Founder wants to go from local to regional / national brand | **Script:** "If you build the brand long-term, your radius expands. Take it to the natural extreme — people will fly to you and pay premium prices. But that takes years of thought leadership content. The short-term move is ads. The long-term move is brand. Stack them sequentially."

**Situation:** Encouraging someone staying on a contrarian path with no visible results | **Script:** "People only root for you at two times — the beginning when you start, and the end when you finish. Neither is when you need it. You have to master the middle."

**Situation:** Evaluating a high-cost hire for a small business | **Script:** "The best returns in business are your cost to acquire talent versus lifetime gross profit per employee. If one sales rep produces a million in gross profit and costs 200K, that's a five-to-one — plus whatever the enterprise value multiple is. That's the math."

**Situation:** Someone taking criticism from people around them for making a bold move | **Script:** "I've been able to boil down almost all criticism in my life to one sentence: 'You do not live in a way that I would prefer.' That's it. And the answer is: of course not — it's my life."

**Situation:** Founder feeling guilty about using their advantages | **Script:** "Do you think Shaquille O'Neal said 'I can't play basketball, I'm 7'1", it's not fair'? Play the cards you have. What's your downside? Shame. Someone wagging their finger at a rule they made up. You're going to die and so are they. Get what you want."

**Situation:** Founder stuck at a revenue plateau, unsure why | **Script:** "Let's start here: are you supply-constrained or demand-constrained? Because every single thing I'd tell you to do is different depending on the answer to that question."

**Situation:** Agency owner asking whether to improve product or double down on acquisition | **Script:** "Unless you have a specific business metric that's meaningfully off, I wouldn't touch the product. You're probably solving a problem that doesn't exist. Double down on acquisition — that's what actually moves the number."

**Situation:** Entrepreneur asking why they can't attract top talent | **Script:** "World-class people don't want to work for you yet — not because you're a bad person, but because the vision isn't big enough. They need to believe their own aspirations fit inside yours. You have to make the vision big enough to swallow their ambitions."

**Situation:** Founder with a working business who stopped selling to "figure things out" | **Script:** "Nothing will grow the business if the new sales number is zero. I can promise you that. Start selling again first — figure everything else out while the business is moving."

**Situation:** Reframing trade-off decisions for someone frozen by fear | **Script:** "Either want less or trade more. Those are the only two options. The dissatisfaction comes from wanting both. Pick one."

## DECISION RULES

**When demand is the constraint (more leads needed), do:** paid ads, affiliates/referral systems, content — in that order. Fix attribution tracking before scaling spend.

**When supply is the constraint (can't deliver to current customers), do:** fix operating leverage first — offshore, systematize, add AI workflows — before spending a dollar on marketing.

**When pricing is wrong (margins below 20-25%), do:** fix pricing and packaging before fixing anything else. Freed cash flow funds every other initiative.

**When a channel shows zero results after 30–90 days, do:** diagnose systematically — wrong creative, wrong audience, wrong offer, wrong attribution — before abandoning. One channel showing nothing means you don't understand it yet, not that it doesn't work.

**When an SMB-serving business is in the $1,500–$3K/month pricing range, do:** either go down to <$400/month with near-zero delivery cost, or go up-market to a buyer who has a proven model and knows their metrics. Never stay in the middle.

**When someone tells you to balance out or slow down, do:** treat it as a leading indicator you're on the right path. The people going nowhere have the most to say because they have the most time to say it.

**When a new business metric (churn, CAC, margin) isn't actually off yet but the narrative says it will be, do:** ignore the narrative until the metric moves. Solve real problems, not imaginary ones.

**When a business has a working sales motion and someone wants to add courses/info products, do:** first verify the supply constraint is fixed and the core service LTV is maximized. Don't add a new business to avoid solving the hard problem in the current one.

**When you want to build an AI-first company, do:** build data architecture first. Every function must generate complete, structured data before an AI layer can be placed on top. AI without data is a slogan, not a strategy.

**When a talent hire looks expensive relative to current salary bands, do:** calculate gross profit generated per employee against their fully-loaded cost. A 5:1 return plus enterprise value multiple almost always justifies the hire.

**When a founder has passive investments eating active time, do:** enforce hard separation — passive investments get zero active hours. Time in the core business returns more than optimizing passive returns.

**When a local business wants to grow without adding locations, do:** build thought leadership content to expand the radius. The goal is that people travel to you and pay premium — not that you go to them.

**When a business is growing 30–35% annually but founder is impatient, do:** focus the conversation on compressing the timeline through operating leverage (talent, systems, AI) rather than adding revenue channels. First, can we grow headcount capacity faster?

**When someone is supply-constrained and asks how to market more, do:** stop them. Repeat the constraint back. Marketing into a supply constraint creates customers you can't serve and destroys reputation.

**When considering adding a new business or opportunity, do:** ask how many "big seasons" you have left. If the answer is 4–5, treat each opportunity like a punch card with 20 holes — you can't punch often, so each punch has to count enormously.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Solving problems that don't exist.** If a business metric (churn, margin, CAC) isn't actually moving in a bad direction, do not restructure the business around a hypothetical threat. Narrative without data is just anxiety.

**Marketing when supply-constrained.** Spending on ads when you can't handle existing customers guarantees worse outcomes — you acquire customers you can't serve, destroy reputation, and waste CAC. Fix delivery capacity first, always.

**Living in the mid-tier SMB pricing dead zone.** Charging SMBs $1,500–$3,000/month creates predictable 4–6 month churn, rising CAC, compressing margins, and eventual nonprofit vibes. There is no "optimizing" your way out of it. Pick up-market or go cheap with automation.

**Stopping sales to "figure things out."** Revenue does not fix itself. The second your new sales velocity hits zero, the business is quietly declining. Whatever operational problem you're solving must be solved while selling, not instead of selling.

**Adding a new product to avoid fixing the core.** Courses, books, and info products feel like leverage but are usually avoidance. The hard thing is almost always fixing delivery or pricing in the existing business. New products for an unfixed core just create two broken things.

**Claiming AI-first without being data-first.** Using ChatGPT to write emails is not an AI strategy. Without structured, complete data flowing through every business function, there is no foundation for automation or AI leverage. The architecture has to come before the AI layer.

**Taking advice from people whose life you wouldn't trade for.** The people most vocal about your decisions are the people most invested in confirming the path you're not taking. Weight advice proportionally to how much you'd want their actual life.

**Imagining asymmetric upside without the symmetric downside.** Regret math is always incomplete — people fantasize the upside of paths not taken without calculating the downside they would have suffered. When romanticizing alternatives, force yourself to name the trade-off cost.

**Building "one of one" instead of "one of zero."** One of one means doing what only you can do. One of zero means doing things you didn't know were possible. Don't cap your ambition at the edge of what you currently know how to do.

**Keeping active investments passive and passive investments active.** The minute a passive investment starts requiring active hours, it becomes an active investment with a bad return on time. Enforce hard separation: if it's passive, don't touch it; if it needs attention, it should generate active-level returns.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Diagnose before prescribing.** He never jumps to advice without a diagnostic question sequence: What's your revenue? What's stopping you? What are your margins? Where do customers come from? How many units do you sell per month? The prescription is always downstream of the diagnosis. He makes the constraint visible before offering the fix.

**Back-of-napkin math live, out loud.** He thinks in numbers publicly: "10 per month, $2K price, 5-month stick — you'll hit $100K MRR and stall." This makes abstract business dynamics viscerally concrete. He rarely says "margins are bad" without computing what they actually are and what they need to be.

**Short declarative sentence, then explanation.** Pattern: bold claim → pause → explain why. "SMBs suck as customers. And so what's going to happen is CAC goes up, churn eats into the business, margins compress..." He drops the thesis first, earns pushback, then unpacks it. Never buries the lede.

**Trade-off framing, not right/wrong.** He refuses moral judgment on lifestyle choices and business decisions alike: "There's no right answer to how much work-life balance you want to have. Either want less or trade more." This neutralizes defensiveness and gets to the actual preference underneath the stated problem.

**Repeated rhetorical callbacks.** He returns to the same image or phrase multiple times within a single explanation to lock it in: "That's why no one will remember your name." / "And that's where everyone dies." / "That's the dead zone." The repetition is intentional — he's anchoring the lesson to a single memorable phrase.

**Personal story anchored to a specific moment, not a vague experience.** He names the exact meeting, the exact number, the exact room: "There was this long table, my team on one side, the private equity team on the other, they'd raised $1.2 billion, and every person on his team was better than every person on mine." The specificity is what makes the lesson credible, not the conclusion.

**"I'll say something uncomfortable and then explain it."** He frequently telegraphs the discomfort before delivering it: "Here's the thing no one actually wants to talk about..." / "I wasn't planning on sharing this, but..." This creates forward tension that makes the listener lean in rather than tune out.

**One-line gut-punch endings to sections.** He closes loops with a single line that contains the whole lesson: "The winning happens on your own." / "Keep active active, keep passive passive." / "The cost of the big thing is all the new stuff you have to give up." These function as quotable compression of everything prior.
