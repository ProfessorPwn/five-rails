## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The LTV:CAC Ratio (Economic Arbitrage)**
Every business has an economic arbitrage at its core: how cheap it is to acquire a customer vs. how much they pay you over time. If your LTV:CAC is 16:1 or better, the only question is why you aren't feeding the machine more money. When you've confirmed the machine works, scale spend without hesitation — there's no rational limit.

**The Funnel Leak Audit**
Map every stage: clicks → opt-ins → leads → appointments → shows → closes. Wherever volume drops sharply, that's your highest-leverage fix. Don't add new channels until you've plugged the leak. 1,800 clicks producing 8 sales is a conversion problem, not a traffic problem — solve conversion first.

**BANT Qualification (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing)**
The four questions that tell you whether a lead is worth your time: Can they pay? Can they decide? How bad do they want it? When do they want to start? Build these into your intake form as friction — they filter out bad leads while letting good leads self-qualify.

**The Affiliate Compounding Node**
Affiliates are not referrals. Referrals are one-time. Affiliates are recurring traffic nodes — each one sends you 1–2 customers per month indefinitely. The one-time outreach effort compounds forever. Map the audience-aligned businesses in your market (coaches, matchmakers, attorneys, trainers) and treat each as a channel acquisition, not a relationship favor.

**The Chinese Rule of Three and Ten**
Systems break every time a business triples. Don't plan for 10x in one jump — plan for 3x, stabilize, then 3x again. Set goals in triples so that tripling feels like success, not failure. This prevents both despair ("I only 3x'd") and system collapse.

**The Lead Magnet Pre-Digestion Model**
Your lead magnet should do the work so the prospect doesn't have to. Think of it as pre-digesting food — you chew it, they just swallow. If the prospect has to upload a photo, fill out a mood board, or take measurements, they'll abandon. The value must be immediate, effortless, and specific. Fast + less valuable beats slow + more valuable every time.

**Good Friction vs. Bad Friction**
Good friction filters bad leads without deterring good ones — income qualification, start-date questions, budget ranges. Bad friction repels everyone — slow page loads, photo uploads, multi-step forms. Design intake to increase lead quality, not to reduce it. Only add friction that bad leads are unwilling to pass.

**The Avatar → Offer → Channel Sequence**
Get Avatar clarity first. Then shape your offer to that Avatar's specific pain, trigger moment, and desired outcome. Then choose channels that reach that Avatar where they already are. Skipping Avatar work means your offer is generic and your channel targeting is guesswork.

**The Affiliate Activation Loop**
Getting Affiliates is the easy part. Keeping them active requires a Cadence — host events quarterly, put them in rooms together, make it worth their while to keep sending. Without activation, Affiliates go cold within 90 days. Treat them like customers: they need consistent value to stay loyal.

**The Trigger Moment Framework**
Buyers in high-ticket services enter the buying cycle at a specific life transition: divorce, promotion, baby, weight loss, status mismatch. Identify the 3–5 trigger moments for your Avatar and orient all messaging around catching them at that moment — not selling features, but meeting them in their transition.

**The Before/After Gap Expansion**
Your sales process should first establish the current state ("here's what's broken"), then show the desired state ("here's where you can go"), and then expand the gap between them. The wider the gap feels, the more urgency to act. Don't pitch features — pitch distance traveled.

**The Sequenced Prioritization Model**
Order your action items by: speed of implementation × magnitude of leverage. Quick wins (change a headline, adjust ad spend) go first because they compound and free up mental bandwidth. Heavy work (Affiliate outreach at scale) goes second. Optimization (billing cycles, pricing) goes last when you have more data and cash flow to experiment safely.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

## DECISION RULES

- When you have a strong LTV:CAC ratio (>10:1) and a working channel, increase spend immediately — the only bottleneck is your willingness to feed the machine.
- When two channels are generating revenue, track percentage contribution per channel before touching either — the answer is always "double down on what's working."
- When a lead magnet requires the prospect to do work, cut the work — make it faster and simpler even if it means less initial value delivered.
- When a channel produces 10x better ROI per outreach than industry benchmarks (as Ashley's affiliate emails did), that's your primary lever — don't dilute it with new channels until you've exhausted it.
- When you can't attribute sales to specific channels, pause ad spend — you're spending in the dark. Get attribution before scaling.
- When a business has low churn and high recurring revenue, the problem is almost always volume, not the offer — don't change the offer; open the front end.
- When a customer segment represents less than 30% of revenue and you hate working with them, cut them and double down on the segment you love.
- When you're choosing between two lead magnets, always pick the faster one over the more valuable one.
- When your intake form requires more than 4–5 questions, cut it — only ask what you need to make a qualification decision.
- When you're setting a growth target, set it in triples, not multiples — systems break at 3x; plan for that before planning for 10x.
- When an affiliate or referral source sends you business after one-time outreach, make that channel the immediate priority before any paid channel.
- When a business has good unit economics but low volume, solve distribution, not product.
- When a continuity model is working with low churn, don't touch it — maximize volume into a proven system before optimizing the system.
- When a prospect asks about pricing on the landing page, don't show it — qualify first, price on the call.
- When you find a lead magnet idea, ask: does the prospect have to do any work to receive the value? If yes, simplify until the answer is no.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Selling out of your own wallet.** Pricing to what you'd personally pay rather than what the market will bear or what the value actually is. High-end clients don't buy on your budget — they buy on theirs.

**No attribution tracking while running ads.** Spending $3,400/year and not knowing which clicks become customers. You're generating returns by accident. Stop spending until you know what's working.

**A lead magnet that requires effort from the prospect.** Asking for photo uploads, mood boards, or measurements as part of the opt-in process. It destroys conversion rate. Pre-digest the value so they just receive it.

**Focusing on new channels before fixing the existing funnel.** 1,800 clicks producing 8 sales is a funnel problem. Opening Facebook ads before fixing Google conversion is adding water to a leaking bucket.

**Ignoring your highest-ROI channel.** Ashley sent 40 emails and got a third of her revenue. She then spent most of her time on $600/month in paid ads. The emails were 10–15x more efficient per dollar.

**Not tracking customer source percentages.** If you don't know what percentage of customers come from each channel, you can't allocate time or budget correctly. You'll spend 50% of effort on 10% of customers.

**Two-tier offerings with no price anchor logic.** Having monthly and quarterly options without understanding that the higher price exists to anchor, not primarily to sell. Every premium option makes the next tier feel reasonable.

**Activating affiliates once and never maintaining them.** One-time outreach gets them. Quarterly events, shared wins, and ongoing communication keep them sending. Affiliates go cold without a Cadence.

**Using "TODO" or vague process steps instead of templated deliverables.** If you're repeating the same pitch on every call, that's a video, not a conversation. Put the script before the call so the call can be human.

**Scaling before the core economics are confirmed.** If margins are bad, churn is high, and LTV is low — more volume just loses money faster. Lock in unit economics before spending on growth.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The setup-then-reverse.** State what looks like the problem, then reveal it's actually an opportunity: "You sent 40 emails. That sounds like nothing. But that got you a third of your revenue. What if you sent 400?" The contrast lands the scale of the opportunity.

**The math-as-proof close.** Hormozi builds small arithmetic on the fly to make the opportunity feel undeniable: "Six affiliates = $100K. Each email = $2,500 in revenue. Forty more emails = $100K more. Send 400 emails. Done." No abstraction — raw numbers.

**The rhetorical question that answers itself.** "If you put a dollar in and got $16 back in 30 days — why would you ever limit what you put in?" He asks the question, pauses, and lets the logic close itself. He doesn't lecture; he Socrates.

**Short declarative stacks.** Long concepts get broken into rapid, short declarative sentences: "Low churn. Strong LTV. Good margins. Solid close rate. The problem is just volume. We need more at-bats." Rhythm creates conviction.

**The "I don't want to mess with X" move.** When something is working, he explicitly names it and says he won't touch it. This builds trust and focuses energy: "I don't want to mess with the continuity. It's working. Let's add volume to a good system instead of optimizing a system that doesn't have the volume yet."

**The "you don't know this number yet" redirect.** Whenever a founder reveals a data gap, he doesn't judge — he reframes it as the next thing to fix before spending more: "You don't have attribution tracking. That means you're spending in the dark. Step 1.5 is to get that in place."

**The compounding logic build.** He layers multipliers to show the total opportunity: "If the funnel fix gives us 2–3x and the affiliates give us 10x of one-third, that's a 12x total. $300K becomes $3.6M." Always shows the math of the combined moves, not just individual ones.
