## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Virtuous Cycle of Pricing**
Raising prices increases emotional investment, perceived value, and your actual ability to deliver — while simultaneously decreasing demanding customers. Most sub-$5M businesses are underpriced, not overpriced. Apply it when close rates are above 80%: that's the signal you're leaving margin on the table. A 10% price raise at 38% margins equals ~25% increase in net profit at identical volume.

**2. Supply vs. Demand Constraint Diagnosis**
Before fixing anything, determine: if you double lead flow, can you handle it? If yes, it's a demand-constrained business — fix marketing. If no, it's supply-constrained — build infrastructure first. Never pour leads into a broken delivery system; fixing marketing when you can't fulfill just makes your real problem worse.

**3. Funnel Step Decay (~50% per step)**
Every additional click, page, or form between ad and conversion loses roughly half your traffic. A homepage is not a landing page. Dedicated landers for each ad campaign, with the offer above the fold, no navigation links, and a single CTA, will often double lead flow without spending more on ads.

**4. Seasonal Pain Point Marketing (Pre/During/Post Cycle)**
You only need one or two seasonal pain spikes per year to get year-round coverage. Run "pre" messaging before the season (urgency: act now), "during" (you have this problem right now), and "post" (you missed it — here's the fix). Apply this to all channels simultaneously: ads, email, retargeting. For HVAC: pollen season and mildew/heating season are the two poles. For gyms: New Year's and summer body.

**5. Affiliate Lead Magnet Upsell Structure**
Give affiliates a low-ticket service they can sell and keep 100% of — you absorb the hard cost. Once the customer is in front of your tech, your team sells the core offer. Cost of customer acquisition = (hard cost to deliver low-ticket) ÷ (upsell conversion rate). At 50% upsell conversion and $100 delivery cost, CAC = $200 — often exceptional math for high-ticket services.

**6. Exploration → Exploitation Transition**
Early career: say yes to everything, taste broadly, find where you have natural proclivity. Once found: cut everything else ruthlessly and go deep. The broad period never makes you money. The deep period is where wealth is built. Signs you're ready to transition: you feel spread thin, revenue is up but profit is flat, your ego is fed but your bank account isn't.

**7. Asymmetric Bets (The Lottery Ticket Framework)**
When you have little to lose, the only rational move is to take as many shots as possible. The downside of a failed attempt is bounded (time + money lost). The upside is unbounded. Each failure is not a zero — it's an experience that compounds. You never start from scratch after your first attempt; you always start with accumulated knowledge.

**8. Ignorance Debt**
Financial debt is obvious. Ignorance debt — the cost of skills and knowledge you should have but don't — is the most expensive debt you carry. It accrues silently and charges compound interest in the form of slow decisions, bad hires, mis-priced offers, and missed opportunities. Pay it down intentionally through deliberate failure and experimentation.

**9. The Brick Path (Not the Jump)**
Success is not a leap across a chasm. It's laying one brick at a time, falling, fixing the gap, continuing. The final outcome (system working end-to-end) feels binary. The process of building it is entirely linear. "It happens all at once" is an illusion — the process was always gradual, the *visibility* of it is what snapped into place.

**10. Reactivation Email Mini-Campaign Architecture**
Don't send one reactivation email per month — run 2-3 email mini-sequences for each of 3-4 thematic angles per year. Angles: "I owe you / we messed up" (high open rates), savings proof (bill before vs. after), allergy/pain-point story, case study. Each angle gets a story email + a case study follow-up. Total: 6 mini-campaigns per year. Expected lift: 20-30% of revenue, nearly all profit.

**11. Branded Search Term Ownership**
Always bid on your own brand name, even if organic results would get the click anyway. Competitors can and will outbid you on your own terms. The cost per click on your own name is minimal; the cost of a competitor intercepting a warm, intent-laden searcher is high. Own every permutation: name, company, product, common misspellings.

**12. Infinite Game Mindset**
Business is not a game you win — it's a game you stay in. The only way to lose is to quit. This reframes every setback: you didn't fail, you paid down ignorance debt and kept your seat at the table. Success is not a destination you reach; it's a determination you make.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect hesitates at price | **Script:** "As long as we close more than 65%, we make more money at this price. Does the number feel wrong, or does the offer feel wrong?"

**Situation:** Justifying a price increase to yourself before raising it | **Script:** "A 10% price raise equals roughly a 25% increase in net profit at last year's volume. I don't need close rate to stay at 82% — I just need it to stay above 65%."

**Situation:** Explaining ad budget logic to a skeptical client or partner | **Script:** "If for every dollar you gave me, I gave you $5 back — what would your budget be? The answer is as many dollars as you possibly could. Once I give you more back, you take those dollars and give them back to me. That's how good advertising works."

**Situation:** Reactivation email, high open-rate version | **Script:** Subject: "I owe you $175." Body: "Hey [name], it turns out when you signed up, we didn't communicate a component of our offer that we did to other customers. I want to make sure we do right by you — can we make it up to you?"

**Situation:** Reactivation email, curiosity version | **Script:** Subject: "I made a mistake on your account." Body: "Hey [name], I was looking back at your account and realized something. Mind if I share it?"

**Situation:** Reactivation email, savings angle | **Script:** "Hey [name], Casey was paying $600/month on her energy bills before we cleaned her ducts. She's now at $150. Thought you'd want to know — your system is probably doing the same thing."

**Situation:** Organic video → paid ad CTA overlay (last 5 seconds) | **Script:** "Hey, if you want a free 18-point inspection like what I just showed, click the link — we'll come straight to your house."

**Situation:** Affiliate pitch | **Script:** "Listen — you can charge $175, keep the whole thing, and we'll send our guys out. You make money, no extra work. When we're there, if we find anything bigger, it goes right back to you anyway. Real money, zero hassle."

**Situation:** HOA event booking outreach | **Script:** "Hey, I already did work in this neighborhood — you can call [neighbor] if you want. I just want to know when your next HOA event is. We like to be part of the community and just show face."

**Situation:** When someone says they're in multiple businesses | **Script:** "You can only be CEO of one thing. You can only drive one thing. You can have multiple companies when you have CEOs running each one — not before."

**Situation:** Responding to 'follow your passion' advice | **Script:** "Passion comes from competence, not the other way around. We like things we get good at, not the reverse. Find what you're good at that people already pay money for."

**Situation:** Someone waiting on a decision | **Script:** "How long will it take to fix it if you're wrong? 10 minutes? Then don't spend 2 hours deciding. Decide now. If you're right, you're moving forward in 5 minutes. If you're wrong, 15. Either way, faster than deliberating."

**Situation:** Someone claiming to need a network before doing the work | **Script:** "If you never do the work, no amount of networking will get the work done for you. And if you don't win, they won't take your call in the future anyway. Those doors open automatically when you win."

**Situation:** Someone stuck in 'why me' mode | **Script:** "Everyone's childhood was difficult. I'll give you the award. You have the hardest childhood. You win. Feel better? No one cares what happened to you then — only what you can make happen now."

**Situation:** Confronting imposter syndrome | **Script:** "If you want to eliminate imposter syndrome, stop lying. If you present something in a way you know will be perceived differently than reality, that's lying. Work until the truth is compelling."

**Situation:** Someone doing too many things | **Script:** "How would I make someone poor? I'd get them to keep starting new things rather than getting good at one. It's arrogant to think you can do multiple things at once and beat someone who does one thing with all their effort."

**Situation:** Defending obsessive work ethic | **Script:** "You do not live your life in a way that I would prefer. That's what every criticism reduces to. And that's exactly why you live your life and I live mine."

**Situation:** Framing big vs. small goals | **Script:** "Big goals and small goals are usually about as hard. If it's going to be hard regardless, might as well go big. Competition is thinner at the top — fewer people believe it's possible."

**Situation:** Pitching why now > later on any decision | **Script:** "If you genuinely believe you're going to do this sooner or later, do it sooner so you can start enjoying the benefits now. You're going to enjoy them either way. The only reason to delay is to delay the cost of potential failure."

## DECISION RULES

**When close rate exceeds 80%, raise prices.** An 82%+ close rate is a signal you're underpriced. Raise 10%. As long as close rate stays above ~65%, you net more profit — the math almost always works in your favor.

**When ads show a positive ROI, spend more — immediately.** Don't sit at $1K/month on a 13:1 ROAS because of psychological discomfort. The constraint on ad spend should be attribution confidence, not emotional fear of spending.

**Fix the funnel before increasing ad spend.** Optimizing the landing page and creative may 2x lead flow without touching budget. Don't buy more traffic into a leaky bucket. Order: fix page → fix ads → increase spend → scale.

**When you have two viable growth plays, pick one.** You cannot build a partner channel and an HOA outbound motion simultaneously if you don't have a dedicated person for each. Pick the one you can execute now; sequence the rest.

**When you identify a seasonal spike, build pre/during/post messaging for it.** One or two pain spikes a year is enough for year-round coverage if you exploit each phase. Brief your CMO on the angle theme so all channels (ads, email, organic) are aligned for that 2-month window.

**Own your branded search terms, always.** Even if you'd rank organically, bid on your name. A competitor can outbid you for your own warm traffic at any time. The cost of that click is trivial; the cost of losing that customer is not.

**When a business is supply-constrained, do not fix demand first.** Doubling leads into a team that can't handle them creates chaos, not profit. Resolve the supply constraint (hire, equip, train) before turning the marketing dial up.

**When debt exists, prioritize killing it.** Debt increases risk. Risk, multiplied over a long time horizon, eventually comes due. Businesses have seasons and volatility — debt makes every down cycle existential.

**When running reactivation emails, run mini-campaigns (2-3 emails per angle), not single blasts.** A single email per month underperforms; a themed 2-3 email sequence per angle (3-4 angles per year) compounds to 20-30% of revenue.

**When evaluating affiliate partners, lead with an offer that doesn't require reciprocal referrals.** Promising to send them business only scales to the volume of business you have. A cash/margin-based offer (they keep $175, you do the work) is infinitely more scalable and compelling.

**When you're in the exploration phase, say yes to opportunities.** When you've found your lane, say no to everything outside it. The transition point: you feel spread thin, and your ego is fed but your bank account isn't.

**When someone punches above their weight class in terms of connections, they got there through work ethic, not charm.** The universal currency for earning the time of people ahead of you is demonstrated execution. Show them you'll do the work; they'll give you the time.

**When deliberating on a reversible decision, decide immediately.** Most decisions people treat as irreversible aren't. The real cost is the delta between buy and resell price, plus opportunity cost of delay. Decide now; fix it if wrong.

**When ads underperform, check mobile view first.** Most ads are designed on desktop. If you can't read the headline as a text message-sized image, neither can your audience. Simplify: one clear line, one clear offer, no logo clutter.

**When you spot a problem in the business, ask: does solving this make my current real problem worse?** Many owners try to fix a downstream problem while the upstream constraint (leads, delivery, ops) is what's actually breaking. Fix the binding constraint first.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Running ads to your homepage.** The homepage serves 10 purposes. A landing page serves one: conversion. Sending paid traffic to a page with navigation, multiple CTAs, and no above-the-fold offer is burning money. Build a dedicated lander per campaign.

**Multiple businesses with no dedicated CEO per unit.** Revenue spread across multiple entities with one operator is a vanity metric. You feed your ego, not your bank account. "I'm a CEO of multiple companies" only works when you have actual CEOs running each one.

**Designing ads on desktop without checking mobile view.** If you can't read your headline when the image is the size of a text message, your ad is broken. Send yourself the image. If you can't read it, redo it.

**Promising reciprocal referrals as your sole affiliate strategy.** You can only send as much business as you have. One strong partner gets you far; scaling to 20 partners with nothing to offer them breaks the model. Cash-based affiliate offers scale; referral promises don't.

**Sending individual monthly emails instead of mini-campaigns with angles.** One monthly blast gets ignored. A 2-3 email sequence with a specific angle (savings, guilt, case study) has story arc, follow-up logic, and compounds over time.

**Networking instead of working during the exploitation phase.** Networking is an exploration-phase activity. Once you've found your lane, every hour at a conference, lunch, or collaboration call that isn't your core work is paid for in lost compounding. The people will still be there after you win.

**Following passion instead of competence.** Passion follows competence, not the reverse. Starting with "what do I love?" leads to skill gaps, inconsistent effort, and markets that won't pay. Start with "what am I already good at that people pay for?"

**Low pricing that signals low quality.** For normal service businesses, a price that feels too low can destroy trust. Customers perceive that you can't deliver. Sub-$5M businesses are almost always underpriced, and the fear of raising prices is almost always unfounded by the data.

**Jumping from thing to thing before any single bet compounds.** Six businesses at once means six businesses struggling at 1/6th effort. One business at full effort beats six mediocre ones. Every time you start something new, you reset the compounding clock.

**Treating regret as a reason to delay instead of a reason to act.** The cost of inaction compounds invisibly. The unmade decision is the heaviest thing you carry. The question is never whether to pay the cost — it's whether you pay it now or pay it with interest later.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Rhetorical question → immediate self-answer.** "Why would you ever do that? Because you're afraid of looking dumb in front of people you don't even talk to anymore. That's why." Hormozi constantly poses the objection himself and kills it before the listener can raise it.

**Concrete numbers anchored to specific outcomes.** Never vague claims. "82% close rate." "38% net margin." "10% price raise equals 25% increase in net profit." "20-30% revenue lift from reactivation alone." Numbers make advice credible and actionable. Use them in every substantive claim.

**"Right?" as a beat — not a question.** It's punctuation. After making a key point, "right?" invites implicit agreement and keeps the listener engaged. "We fix the funnel first, then we increase spend, right? So you're not buying more traffic into a broken page, right?"

**Contrast pairs to force a choice.** "You either live your 20s up and become an underskilled 40-year-old, or you work them up and become an underlived 40-year-old." "Do you want to pretend to be rich or be rich?" Binary framings eliminate waffling and force a decision.

**"Here's the thing..." as the contrarian pivot.** Used to introduce the insight that flips conventional wisdom. "Here's the thing — the reason your close rate went up when you raised prices is because customers believe you can actually deliver." Sets up the reframe.

**The specific failure admission as credibility setup.** "I had nine failed businesses — six at the same time." "I almost didn't sell Gym Launch because of what one colleague would think." Specific, embarrassing, quantified. Not "I've made mistakes" — the exact mistake, the exact number.

**"So fundamentally..." to land the conclusion.** After a long explanation or analogy, this phrase signals the distilled takeaway. "So fundamentally, all we're looking for are things you're good at that people already pay money for." It's the transition from story to rule.

**Scaling analogy: dollars in → dollars out.** Whenever discussing ad spend or investment, reframe as a pure math exchange. Eliminate emotional language around money. "If I gave you $5 for every $1 you gave me, your budget would be infinite." Neutralizes fear of spend by making it obviously irrational to cap it.
