## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Four Levers of a Subscription Business**
Traffic × Conversion × Price ÷ Churn = growth. Every initiative you run must ladder up to one of these four. If you can't name which lever it moves, don't do it. When stuck, ask: can I do more of what's already working before inventing something new?

**EPC (Earnings Per Click) as the Universal Metric**
Price ÷ Churn = LTV. LTV × Conversion Rate = EPC. Run every pricing and conversion experiment through this single number. The business that maximizes EPC — not headline price, not raw conversion — wins.

**Big Head / Long Tail Pricing**
Separate one-time value (education, blueprints, frameworks) from ongoing consumable value (community, calls, live ads, fresh content). Price one-time value upfront with a lump payment; price ongoing value as a recurring subscription. This stops the value-decay problem that kills high-ticket payment plans.

**Value-Price Decay Curve**
When you front-load all your value (a course, a playbook) into a high-ticket payment plan, value descends to zero after delivery while price continues. That gap is where cancellations live. The fix: only bundle recurring deliverables into the recurring price.

**Free → Paid Group Funnel**
Drive all traffic (content, Outreach, SEO) → free community → auto-DM → indoctrination module (broken-up webinar) → CTA to call or direct DM close. This single funnel converts at 14%+ because you're selling to already-warm, self-selected leads. Free community is the most efficient lead magnet that exists.

**More → Better → New (Prioritization Order)**
Before trying something new, exhaust "more of what's working," then "better execution of what's working." Only escalate to "new" when you've hit a structural ceiling. Most entrepreneurs skip to New and dilute the business that was already working.

**Broccoli Wrapped in Bacon (Content)**
Posts should look stupid and fun so people click out of curiosity, but deliver real insight by the end. It's a bargain on the reader's time or money. Pure value posts (listicles, opinions) get ignored. Posts that made you laugh AND taught you something go viral.

**Guru Model vs. Network Model (Community Structure)**
Guru model: all edges connect to you. Network model: members connect to each other. Network model wins on retention because if your relationship with any one member breaks, they stay — they have five other connections. Every engagement tactic (maps, 15-minute calls, intro posts) exists to build member-to-member edges.

**Culture = Rules That Govern Reward and Punishment**
Culture isn't vibes — it's explicit and implicit rules about what gets rewarded and what gets punished. Categories tell members what to post. Pinning tells them what earns recognition. DMs reward top contributors. Not liking bad posts signals what's unwanted. Design the rules deliberately.

**Talent Acquisition = Customer Acquisition (Same Funnel)**
Lead gen → nurture → interview (the sale) → onboarding → retention/ascension. You're competing for great talent the same way you compete for great customers. Brand counts on both ends. If you don't have a compelling vision, you can't close A-players.

**LTV-to-CAC × TAM = Avatar Selection**
When choosing between customer segments, multiply (LTV ÷ CAC) by total addressable market. The segment with the best ratio AND the biggest pool wins — even if unit economics look worse than a niche with higher LTV but tiny market. Then niche all delivery around that one avatar.

**Maker Time vs. Manager Time**
Makers need 3–6-hour uninterrupted blocks; one interrupted block is 1/14 of the week gone. Managers work in 15-minute increments. Entrepreneurs do both, so protect maker time first. Batch all calls, meetings, and coordination to one day. Everything else: empty calendar = more revenue.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Someone joins your free community | **Script:** Auto-DM fires immediately — open with a question about their goal, then direct them to the "Start Here" module in the classroom.

**Situation:** Someone watches your 5-part webinar-style classroom but hasn't booked a call | **Script:** DM: "Hey — saw you went through the training. What's the thing standing in your way right now?" Then close from there.

**Situation:** Prospect can't afford your high-ticket offer | **Script:** "What if instead of doing [big outcome] we just focused on [specific problem they have right now]? Would solving that alone be worth it to you?"

**Situation:** Pricing objection on a subscription | **Script:** Don't negotiate — run the math out loud: "At [price], if you stay [X] months, your LTV is [Y]. If I cut the price to [lower], you'd have to stay [Z] months just to break even. Which scenario do you actually believe?"

**Situation:** Someone wants to cancel | **Script:** Auto-DM a funny GIF with: "Leaving so soon?" No wall of text — just the GIF. Response rate beats every other win-back message.

**Situation:** Prospect says "How do I know it'll work in my market?" | **Script:** "I've run this in [N] markets. The ones it doesn't work in are the ones where nobody actually implemented it. Are you going to implement it?"

**Situation:** You want to close a B2C customer you've already helped for free | **Script:** "I just had a spot open up — someone wasn't ready to [do the hard thing]. If you're actually willing to [do the hard thing], I can get you in."

**Situation:** You need a community member to post an intro | **Script:** "Drop three things: something you do for fun, where you're from, and a photo of your workspace. Then comment a GIF on someone else's post." Give them explicit permission and a simple format.

**Situation:** You spot a top community contributor | **Script:** DM: "I've been watching your posts — you're making this place better. I want to bring you in closer. Can we jump on a call?" Then give them a role, a paid slot, or access.

**Situation:** Refunding a customer you don't want | **Script:** "Here's your money back — no problem at all. If things change and you're ready to go all in on [outcome], come back. We'll be here." Zero friction out = higher re-buy rate.

**Situation:** Pitching a high-ticket workshop to a paid community | **Script:** "I'm doing a [specific topic] workshop — capping it at 30 people, [date/location]. [Price]. First [N] who grab it get [bonus]. Reply here or DM me." Don't over-explain. Scarcity + specificity.

**Situation:** Turning down a bad-fit prospect | **Script:** "Honestly, I think you'd get more out of [free resource / someone else's program]. What I do is specifically for [avatar]. When you're at [stage], call me."

**Situation:** Setting expectations with a new videographer/hire | **Script:** "I'm going to say we're doing something big, you'll set up, and then I'll cancel. That'll happen constantly. I can be in a bad mood. Plans will change last minute. If that sounds good to you, we're going to work great together."

**Situation:** Introducing the Mastermind Principle to community members | **Script:** "There's a reason MasterMind groups outperform solo work — the collective intelligence compounds. Here's specifically why I want you engaging with each other: [story/reason]. Now go book a 15-minute call with two people in here this week."

**Situation:** Responding to "What should my price be?" | **Script:** "I don't know. Here's how to find out: take your best guess, run it for 30 days, track LTV and conversion, then do the math. Compare EPC at the new price vs. the old price. The higher EPC wins. That's the price."

**Situation:** Upselling an onboarding call | **Script:** "What are your goals? What have you struggled with? Where are you now? What do you think has been stopping you?" — then naturally transition to: "We actually have something that addresses exactly that."

## DECISION RULES

**When traffic is your constraint:** Don't touch conversion, price, or churn until you have enough volume to measure. Scale what's working — more spend, more posts, more Outreach. Only when you hit a structural ceiling do you move to optimization.

**When churn is >20% monthly:** Stop acquiring. The bucket is leaking too fast. Talk to churned members (check their LTV in the platform), talk to retained members, find the behavioral delta, fix the retention problem first.

**When you have two price points to test:** Raise price only after you've sufficiently raised perceived value. If you raised price without adding value and refund requests went up, that's your signal — not to lower the price, but to rebuild the value case.

**When someone asks for a refund:** Give it. Immediately. No wall-of-text, no retention attempt if they're hostile. Chargebacks and disputes compound into FTC exposure; easy refunds compound into re-buys and goodwill.

**When a lifetime deal is on the table:** Don't take it. Ongoing delivery requires recurring revenue. If you've already sold lifetime deals, refund and relaunch — the focus unlocked by clean books is worth the short-term pain.

**When choosing between two customer avatars:** Run (LTV ÷ CAC) × TAM for each. Pick the highest output. Stop running two front ends — focus all acquisition on the winner and cross-sell the loser's buyers later.

**When your community is dead:** Don't add more content. Add one engagement category with a fun name, DM your top three contributors with a prompt, and rotate pin posts daily. Engagement is downstream of culture, not content volume.

**When evaluating a new hire:** Skill is table stakes; prioritize genuine enthusiasm for the work and personal chemistry with you. Skill at half the level + great chemistry beats exceptional skill + friction every time.

**When someone in your community starts getting known:** Pull them closer — DM them, give them a role, put them on a call. Never suppress rising community members. Their fame is your retention engine.

**When a prospect can't buy at your price:** Give them the entry-level version, or give the general framework away for free and charge for the implementation help. "Watch the free thing. When you have a business, call me."

**When you feel compelled to start something new:** Ask first: can I just do more of what's already working? Then: can I do what's working better? Only if both answers are no do you start something new.

**When onboarding a new community member:** Fire a group or 1:1 onboarding call within the first week. Treat it as: goals → obstacles → current state → why they haven't succeeded → natural segue to your next offer.

**When your content metrics show views going down but revenue going up:** You're probably targeting better. Narrowing audience increases conversion even as reach shrinks. Stay the course — don't widen the content back out to chase vanity metrics.

**When hiring for a role where you need someone to follow you:** The question to ask in the final interview: "I'll be unpredictable, plans will change constantly, and you'll sometimes need to just figure it out. Does that sound good?" If they hesitate, they're the wrong fit.

**When a promising initiative doesn't map to one of the four levers (traffic, conversion, price, churn):** Drop it. It doesn't matter how exciting it is — if it can't move one of these four, it can't grow the business.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Spreading payments over 12 months for one-time value.** You're collecting 10k on a 30k promise because churn is 25%/month. Worst of both worlds: low conversion because the sticker is high, low collection because churn is high. Charge a lump sum upfront for one-time value; separate ongoing deliverables into a subscription.

**Posting five times a day in your own community.** Communities with high engagement have members posting — not founders posting constantly. If you're the only one creating content, you're running a blog, not a community.

**Building a monster course before you have members.** Classroom content has near-zero correlation with retention. Overwhelm is a churn driver. Start with three simple posts, one community call, and no classroom. Add content only after you understand what they actually need.

**Widening your content to reach a broader audience.** More views from wrong people dilutes conversion across every downstream metric — site conversions, optin rates, applications, sales calls. Narrow focus beats broad reach every time in a business that sells something specific.

**Running two front-end offers simultaneously.** Pick the avatar with the best LTV-to-CAC times TAM. Stop running two front ends. Cross-sell the other segment to existing buyers, but don't split acquisition focus.

**Not refunding people.** Disputes compound into chargebacks, chargebacks compound into Stripe kicks, Stripe kicks attract FTC attention. The people who get in trouble aren't making wild claims — they're the ones who don't give refunds and piss enough people off to generate complaints to the attorney general.

**Measuring success at a transactional level instead of the customer level.** The tile business owner was calculating per-transaction margin instead of LTV-per-interior-designer. One interior designer sent 30 customers per year. The unit of analysis should be the relationship, not the sale.

**Adding value to the community instead of removing friction.** Before adding another weekly call or another module, ask: what are people not consuming, and why? The right move is almost always deletion or simplification, not addition.

**Scaling a leaky bucket.** If churn is high and you're spending more on ads to compensate, you're running harder to stay in place. Fix the retention problem first. Then scale.

**Suppressing rising community contributors.** The natural human instinct is to feel threatened when a member gains status. Resist it. Their fame is your retention — the more community leaders you elevate, the more member-to-member edges exist, and the stickier the network.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The math-out-loud move.** Hormozi rarely states a conclusion — he walks through the arithmetic in real time, usually with a marker on a whiteboard or paper. "Let's say 77 bucks, divide by 33% churn, that's LTV of 231. At 137 with 50% churn, that's 274. So the question becomes: does the lower price convert more than 18% better? Do the math. Pick the price that wins." Confidence comes from the numbers, not the assertion.

**The pivot to first principles.** Whenever someone asks a tactical question ("Should I lower my price to 87?"), he immediately reframes to the underlying variable: "All you're optimizing for is conversion rate times LTV. That's the whole equation." Use this to disarm complexity — reduce every question to the one variable that actually matters.

**Short declarative stacks.** He builds arguments as tight, declarative sentences, often delivered in threes or fours before expanding. "Find something that works. Do as much of it as you can. Eliminate everything else. That includes your personal life." No hedging, no softening, no caveats.

**The rhetorical reversal.** He sets up the obvious answer, then flips it: "You'd think I'd want fewer customers at $50k. But I'd rather have four times the customers at $15k, because of Word of Mouth, retention, and LTV." This is a signature pattern — state the naive assumption, then show why the math says the opposite.

**The honest concession before the close.** He frequently admits a mistake or limitation before making a point: "I made this mistake too. Most people in the industry still don't get this." It drops defensiveness in the listener and positions the insight that follows as hard-won, not theoretical.

**Specificity as authority.** Generic advice gets no airtime. Every claim is attached to a number, a story, or a named example: "The average gym going through GymLaunch makes over $20,000 in additional new revenue in their first 30 days — average. We track it because it's the most important metric we have." The specificity is the credibility.

**The "so the question becomes" bridge.** After presenting a framework or data, Hormozi consistently bridges to the decision with this phrase: "So the question becomes — which of these two EPC numbers is higher?" It trains listeners to think in decision terms, not just information terms.
