## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. Supply/Demand Constraint Diagnosis**
Every business is either supply-constrained (hard to find people who can deliver) or demand-constrained (hard to find customers). Most owners fight the wrong constraint. Identify yours first — then ask: *how do I gain leverage over this specific bottleneck at scale?* If you're supply-constrained, your real business is talent attraction, not marketing.

**2. The Single Domino Strategy**
When you have five priorities, the correct move isn't to split resources across all five — it's to identify the one that makes the others irrelevant or easier, then throw everything at it. A good strategist asks: *given the resources I have, what one thing makes all the other problems disappear?* Everything else gets deleted until the domino falls.

**3. SCALE Loop (Start → Compound → Augment → Leverage → Expand)**
A universal growth loop: start doing the thing, then do more of it, then get better at it, then automate it for consistency, then expand to the next channel/product/territory — and repeat. Apply this when diagnosing where a team is stuck. "They just started — they need to do more." "They're doing a lot — now how do we make it better?"

**4. Optimization Framework (Question → Delete → Optimize → Pull Timelines → Automate)**
When taking over a broken department: first, question why every activity exists and how it ties to output. Second, delete everything you can't justify — you can always add back. Third, rank remaining activities by output-per-resource. Fourth, compress timelines aggressively (ask hours, not days). Fifth, automate what's proven. This is distinct from SCALE — this is for fixing, not growing.

**5. Problems vs. Missed Opportunities**
Problems are finite — you can list them all. Missed opportunities are infinite. When you have an urge to start something new, ask: *is this a missed opportunity or a problem?* Nine times out of ten it's an opportunity. Opportunities feel exciting; problems feel like work. Solve problems first. Keep a "big money ideas" list to park opportunities so your brain lets go — then revisit with fresh eyes when you have bandwidth.

**6. The Six Horsemen of Business Stagnation**
The six reasons businesses stop growing: (1) underpriced, (2) employees overcompensated relative to market, (3) serving too many avatars, (4) overextension before operators are in place, (5) running more than one business, (6) no data. Each has a rock-and-hard-place scenario — short-term pain vs. pain forever. The short-term pain is always the correct choice.

**7. The Value Equation**
Value = (Dream Outcome × Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort/Sacrifice). To increase value, you either increase the top or decrease the bottom — and the highest-leverage move is almost always *removing* something to make it faster and easier, not adding features. "Customers cause their own demise when you give them everything they ask for."

**8. 70/30 Brand Investment Ratio**
Spend 70% of marketing budget on top-of-funnel brand awareness (storytelling, association, aspiration) and 30% on bottom-of-funnel conversion. The payoff lags 12–18 months, but the compounding is exponential. The corollary: maintain a 3:1 value-to-ask ratio in content — three pieces of genuine value before any ask. Violate this ratio and your audience erodes.

**9. The Flywheel Loop**
Draw a circle where doing A causes B, which causes C, which causes A again. If you can't draw that circle, you have a linear input-output business that requires constant fuel. Your goal is to spark the engine once and let it spin. Identify friction points in the loop and remove them — the wheel should run itself.

**10. What/Who/When Framework**
Every task assignment needs three things: *what* it is, *who* owns it (chest to poke, throat to choke), and *when* — measured in hours, not days. Ask "how many hours will this take?" — not "what day will you finish?" The hours answer reveals whether the timeline is real or invented. Recap every item in the meeting cumulatively so the first item gets repeated the most.

**11. Sawdust Principle**
Look for waste inside your existing operations — excess capacity, idle space, underused skills — and recombine them into something valuable at near-zero marginal cost. Best businesses check four boxes at once with the same asset. Ask: *what are all the things we already do, and is there a way to recombine them into new value?*

**12. Criticism vs. Insult Distinction**
An insult attaches a negative character judgment ("you're lazy"). A criticism states the gap between desired and actual, factually, then gives behavioral steps to close it. Insults create emotional charge and defensiveness. Criticisms enable improvement. Being *nice* means avoiding this conversation. Being *kind* means having it clearly and immediately, with the steps to fix it.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Employee or team says "we can't do that." | **Script:** "Don't tell me you can't — that means it's impossible. Tell me what it would take. Tell me the resources required and what we'd have to take from somewhere else. Then we can decide together if it's worth the trade."

**Situation:** You want to know if a project/decision is viable | **Script:** "What would it take?" — assume the result occurs, work backwards from success. Use this for deals, sales, and internal decisions.

**Situation:** Pricing feels stuck or you're at capacity but not profitable | **Script:** "You get to pick: do you want a few people to say no and you make more money, or do you want to be barely above water forever because your prices are wrong?"

**Situation:** Implementing the 5-and-20 price ladder | **Script:** Sell five customers at current price. If they all say yes, raise price 20%. Repeat. Keep going until net revenue drops. "If you sell three people at twice the price, it's better than selling five at half — even though you'll hear no more often."

**Situation:** Overcompensated employee conversation | **Script:** "The market rate for this role is X. I understand this changes our agreement. I'm not asking you to decide now — go home, talk to your spouse, sleep on it, come back tomorrow. I'll keep your current comp for the next 90 days while you decide."

**Situation:** Delivering criticism on performance | **Script:** "The expectation is [X]. The current result is [Y]. The gap is [Z]. Here's what I need you to do differently: [specific behavior], not [old behavior]." Never attach character judgment. Always end with the directive, not the deficiency.

**Situation:** Assigning tasks in a meeting | **Script:** "What is it, who owns it, how many hours does it take — okay, you'll have it done by [today/tomorrow at noon]. Let me know when it's done." Then recap: "So we've got [A] done by [time], [B] done by [time]..."

**Situation:** Team member not clear on what was assigned | **Script:** "Say it back to me." — use this until you confirm comprehension. One in three times they'll get it wrong. This is not optional for new team members or complex tasks.

**Situation:** Operator not ready for you to step back | **Script:** "Here's the deal: you're going to get [profit share/equity override]. In exchange, this phone doesn't ring. If it rings, the deal's off. Let's role-play: a pipe burst. What do you do? Wrong answer is call me. Call the plumber. Don't call me. Ever."

**Situation:** Building a B2B brand | **Script:** Three buckets: (1) help your Avatar achieve their goals for free via content, (2) show someone *like them* getting the aspirational outcome, (3) achieve the aspirational outcome yourself publicly. "What is that aspirational thing that, if I can do it at the extreme, makes the average version feel low-risk to buy?"

**Situation:** Diagnosing a performance decline | **Script:** "Don't ask why we started shrinking. Ask why we stopped growing. Go back to where the line went flat — what changed in the 30 days before that? Who did we add? What process did we change?"

**Situation:** Evaluating whether data is worth tracking | **Script:** "If this metric goes up or goes down, does it change what we do? If the answer is no, we don't track it. Track only the variables that change behavior."

**Situation:** Hiring a key player who could be impacted by their home environment | **Script:** Consider talking to their spouse. "You want someone whose partner says: 'This is great for our family. You've got this. What can I do to make it easier?' The neutral spouse becomes the negative spouse over time."

**Situation:** Introducing yourself in content | **Script:** Proof-Promise-Plan within 30 seconds: "[Credibility signal]. You're going to learn [specific outcome]. Here's how we're going to do it." Then introduce yourself — you're always making it for someone who doesn't know you yet.

**Situation:** Evaluating a potential solution before implementing | **Script:** "Before we start — how long should we give this before we can honestly say it worked or it didn't?" Commit to that timeline before starting. Do not appraise mid-solution.

## DECISION RULES

**When at full capacity but not profitable → raise price.** Capacity without profit means you're mispriced. Use the 5-and-20 rule: sell five, if they all say yes, raise 20%. Repeat until net revenue drops.

**When a metric declines → look at where it stopped growing, not where it started shrinking.** Find the inflection point. Usually you added someone bad, not lost someone good.

**When you have multiple priorities → delete everything except the one that makes the others easier or irrelevant.** Strategy is choosing; assigning all five is failing.

**When a department is broken → question every activity, delete first, then optimize, then compress timelines, then automate.** Deletion is always step one. You can always add back.

**When considering a new initiative → ask "is this a missed opportunity or a problem?"** If it's an opportunity, add it to the big money list. If it's a problem, solve it now.

**When supply-constrained → invest in talent acquisition systems, not marketing.** Calculate gross profit per employee per year. Pay referral bonuses proportional to that figure (20–25% of first-year GP is rational, not extravagant).

**When considering data to track → only track metrics whose change alters your behavior.** Everything else is noise.

**When performance is good → thin the herd.** Fire low performers when times are good, not when times are bad. Pruning in good times reads as discipline; pruning in bad times reads as panic.

**When a solution is implemented → give it time proportional to the size of the problem before judging.** You're living today with problems you created six months ago. Solutions need the same runway.

**When a team member says something can't be done → replace "can't" with "what would it take."** Impossible is almost never true. Resources required and trade-offs are always true.

**When setting deadlines → ask how many hours, not what day.** Hours force honesty. Days invite Parkinson's Law.

**When onboarding someone new → half calendar, meet KPIs, then full calendar, then uncapped.** Never give full access before performance is proven.

**When compensation structure has perverse incentives → audit what behaviors it actually rewards.** If promotion requires headcount under you, it rewards empire-building, not output.

**When you have a recurring conflict with someone → stop trying to convert them.** Work with people who move toward you. Neutral becomes negative over time.

**When scaling → solve problems first, then scale.** Scaling before fixing compounds your problems, it doesn't outrun them.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Long-term planning obsession.** Creating massive multi-year projections across hundreds of unknown variables is "mental masturbation." Half the plan will be irrelevant in 12 months. Care about the next 12 weeks and one annual target. Planning past that is procrastination cosplaying as productivity.

**Serving infinite avatars.** When your offer, messaging, and advertising aren't working, the most common cause is you're trying to serve everyone. 80/20 your customers: who are the top 20%? Make your offer exclusively for them and price it accordingly. You'll make more money serving fewer people better.

**Splitting resources across multiple priorities simultaneously.** "These are our objectives — also keep doing everything else" is not strategy. It's abdication. Adding more priorities further dilutes what's important. Discretionary effort must flow to one thing.

**Opening a second location before the first runs without you.** The business must maintain or grow for six consecutive months without your direct involvement before you expand. If you're the operator and you leave, the business degrading proves you haven't built a business — you've built a job.

**Overcompensating employees out of fear.** Paying above-market out of fear that someone leaves traps you permanently. Shore up infrastructure by hiring someone who can do the same job at market rate, then have the conversation from leverage, not desperation.

**Conflating an insult with a criticism.** "Your content sucks" is an insult. "Our channel average is 100K, your videos average 75K, here's what we need to change behaviorally" is a criticism. Insults create charge; criticisms create improvement. Being "nice" by avoiding the conversation is not kind — it's cowardly.

**Managing by political hierarchy.** If the only path to promotion is headcount underneath you, you've created four perverse incentives: hoarding knowledge, always requesting more hires, ass-kissing whoever controls promotions, and performing work rather than doing it. Tie advancement to output, not org chart position.

**Pursuing shiny objects when your current business has unsolved problems.** You were rewarded once for pursuing a missed opportunity — when you started the business. That behavior should not repeat. Every subsequent opportunity pull is a half-built bridge. Finish the bridge you're on.

**Chasing wide content for views when you have a specific avatar.** More views from the wrong audience generates fewer absolute conversions than narrower content for the right audience. Book sales and email opt-ins are your leading indicators, not view count.

**Letting solutions marinate past their committed evaluation window.** Deciding mid-implementation that something "isn't working" is almost always premature. You're suffering the cost of the solution while the problem still exists — that's supposed to happen. The question you should have answered before starting: "How long should we give this?"

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Declarative inversion.** State the counterintuitive conclusion first, then defend it. "Rush is imaginary." "Missed opportunities are the gateway drug to mediocrity." Lead with the punchline; the argument follows. This signals confidence and forces attention.

**The Rock-and-Hard-Place Frame.** Every dilemma gets stated as two painful choices: pain today vs. pain forever. "You get to pick: do you want a few people to say no and make more money, or do you want to be barely above water forever?" This removes false neutrality and forces a decision.

**Self-implication before principle.** Confess your own mistake first, then extract the rule. "This is Alex's greatest hits of mistakes." "This is me trying to take on too many things." Credibility via vulnerability, then the lesson lands harder.

**Redneck logic as permission structure.** "In the back-of-knacken redneck logic way that I prefer..." signals that the simple, obvious answer is the right one and intellectual complexity is the enemy. Permission to stop overthinking.

**"And so..." chaining.** Extended chains of "and so... and so... and so..." build inevitability. Each logical step validates the next. By the time you arrive at the conclusion, it feels like the only possible outcome. Use this to lead someone through an argument they'd resist if stated upfront.

**Direct address + permission release.** "You can do whatever you want. I'm just saying..." or "Take it or leave it, it's up to you." This paradoxically increases influence — it removes the defensive posture of the listener and makes the advice feel like observation rather than instruction.

**Analogy laddering.** Build from a physical or universal analogy (gym, lumber mill, bridge, video games), establish the principle in that domain, then map it explicitly to the business context. "Same idea. If you were to think about this like an investment..." The physical world makes abstract business logic visceral and memorable.
