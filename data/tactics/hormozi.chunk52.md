## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. More / Better / New (MBN)**
There are only three ways to grow: do more of what works, do it better, or try something new. More has the highest risk-adjusted return because it compounds a known winner. Apply it in order — exhaust More before touching Better, and never touch New until More and Better are maxed.

**2. Change = Fixed Cost / Variable Reward**
Every change carries a guaranteed ~20% performance dip while the team relearns, plus an uncertain upside that's usually 5%. If you wouldn't take a bet with a 20% guaranteed loss for a 5% possible gain, don't change things that work. Reserve change for moves with ICE scores that justify the cost.

**3. ICE Framework (Impact × Confidence × Ease)**
Score every potential move on Impact (how big?), Confidence (how likely?), and Ease (how cheap in resources?). The perfect bet is massive impact, high confidence, low friction. If any leg is weak, the bet is worse than just doing more of what works.

**4. Core Four Customer Acquisition**
There are exactly four ways to get customers: warm outreach, cold outreach, content, paid ads. Before inventing a fifth, maximize volume on the four that already exist. Scale each by making more creative first, spending more money second, then expanding platforms third.

**5. Maximizers vs. Optimizers**
Optimizers ask "how do I get more from less?" Maximizers ask "how do I get as much as possible?" In absolute-return terms, maximizers win. A campaign at 2:1 ROAS on $1M beats a 10:1 campaign on $100 every time. Don't stay trapped in relative-return math while leaving absolute dollars on the table.

**6. Big Head / Long Tail Pricing**
Charge a high upfront for the transformational one-time skill, then price the ongoing consumable (community, leads, tools) so low it's nearly a no-brainer. Churn in coaching happens because founders try to sustain full price on value the client has already absorbed. Separate what they pay for the skill from what they pay to keep the habit.

**7. VSL Architecture (Hook → Proof → Promise → Plan → Picture)**
Open with a visceral hook ("Have you ever looked at your railing and thought, if I changed this, my whole house would look different?"). Prove you can help. Promise a specific outcome. Walk through the plan step by step. Show a visual roadmap. Close by opening a loop — tell them you'll cover pricing at the end to keep them watching.

**8. Avatar Segmentation (Demographics → Quantifiables → Behaviors)**
Segment your best customers across three axes: who they are (demographics), what size/stage they're at (quantifiables), and what actions they took that correlated with activation and retention (behaviors). Eliminate avatars below the threshold where you can profitably serve them. A smaller, better-fit buyer pool fixes churn faster than any ops change.

**9. Wealth Ratio**
Wealth = earnings ÷ spending. The fastest early lever is cutting spending, not increasing income, because it de-risks your personal balance sheet enough to take asymmetric business bets. Every $250/month saved compounds to $1M over a career. Translate every purchase into its compounded 40-year cost before spending.

**10. Activation = Time to Value**
The only metric that actually predicts churn in coaching or service businesses is whether the client wins in the first window (7–14 days for most offers). Everything in onboarding — sequencing, calls, daily group drills — should be architected backward from "how do we get them their first win fast?" Once activated, clients stay.

**11. Organic Base + Paid Skim**
Think of your audience as a pyramid. Organic content grows the base — that percentage who love you compounds over time. Ads skim the top of the existing warm pool and accelerate conversion but don't grow the base. You need both in parallel: organic for 10-year compounding, ads for short-term revenue now.

**12. Conquer Local Before Global**
You can't dominate a market you've only scratched. 10 sales a month in a local market isn't a local win — it's a beginning. Until you can sell 20–100 units per week in your current geography, national expansion is just anxiety dressed as ambition. Fortify the base; expansion can wait.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect fills out custom order form, pre-call SMS nurture | **Script:** "Hey, just making sure — does your wife/husband need to be on the call to make a decision? We want to make sure we're not wasting anyone's time. We're not a sales company but happy to hop on and figure out exactly what you need."

**Situation:** VSL pre-call price framing | **Script:** "Come to the call knowing your budget because I don't want to waste your time talking about railings made of diamonds — we can do that, by the way — but let's make sure we're in the right ballpark."

**Situation:** Opening the discovery call | **Script:** "I want to make sure this call is worth your time. I'm going to ask you a few questions, show you a few options based on your house style, walk through pricing ranges, and at the end if it makes sense we'll just move forward. Sound good?"

**Situation:** Creating urgency to close on call | **Script:** "I give a $200 discount for people who move forward today — not because I'm being salesy, just because it saves me the admin headache of another call. Either way, no pressure."

**Situation:** AB close on the call | **Script:** "Same to us either way — do you want to put it on your card or would you prefer to finance through [Affirm/Klarna/Shopify]? Which would you prefer?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "no one in my market can afford that price" | **Script:** "That's just not true. You've seen 200 people this year. There are 2,000 more out there. 9% of Americans have a million-dollar net worth — they just look older than you think. Run the ads. Let's find them."

**Situation:** Founder wants to go national before dominating local | **Script:** "You haven't conquered a city yet. You want to conquer the world. In order to conquer the world, you have to conquer a country. Before a country, a city. Right now you're at 10 sales a month. Let's talk about 100."

**Situation:** Client asks if they should lower prices to fight competition | **Script:** "I cut my top line 20% to fight a competitor. Churn changed zero. I just made less money. The first comment in the chat? 'I can't believe I was paying more.' Don't lose $50 million. Let him figure that out for himself."

**Situation:** Coaching client not activating (not getting early wins) | **Script:** "We only email their list if they make a sale in the first seven days. Figure out what the people who activated did in that window that others didn't — and reverse engineer it into the onboarding."

**Situation:** Founder wants to keep testing new marketing channels | **Script:** "You have this thing that works. The likelihood that the next thing you try also works is statistically very low. Think about how many things you tried before this one worked. Jam more into the machine."

**Situation:** Founder complaining about diminishing ROAS at scale | **Script:** "You put in $100 and got $1,000. You put in $200 and got $1,200. You're upset. But you made more money. Net net, you made $1,000 here. You made $1,000 there. Still more. That's the whole point."

**Situation:** Prospect asks "how do I find a brand manager?" | **Script:** "Look at the brands you admire. Find out who's behind them — LinkedIn, ChatGPT, whatever. Reach out and offer to pay them more. Solvable. Next."

**Situation:** Setting up a high-ticket event pitch | **Script:** "We'll bring 100 people into the room, run three sessions, pitch at the end. Close 10–15 at the high ticket, a handful at the mid-tier. That's the business. 200 people a year isn't a market ceiling — it's a traffic problem."

**Situation:** Framing your offer's unique value against commoditized competitors | **Script:** "If you sell to contractors and designers, you're a commodity. They'll shop you against five other vendors. The DIY buyer thinks your stuff is dope and wants to buy from you specifically. Build for them."

**Situation:** Overcoming internal resistance to doing higher volume | **Script:** "Count to 100. Then do that 20 times. That's how long it takes just to count to 2,000. We made 2,000 ads. That's how we did $105 million in 72 hours. What are you making?"

## DECISION RULES

1. **When you have something that works, do more of it before you change it.** A guaranteed 20% loss for a possible 5% gain is a bet you should never take.

2. **When a competitor undercuts you, don't react.** Focus on the customer, not the competitor. The price-cutter will kill their own margins. Let them.

3. **When churn is high, fix the avatar before fixing the ops.** You're either selling to the wrong person or failing to activate the right one. Segment first.

4. **When a prospect objects on price, don't lower it — expand the audience.** More traffic, not a worse offer.

5. **When you want national scale, ask if you've saturated local first.** If you haven't, go local.

6. **When evaluating a new initiative, apply ICE: Impact × Confidence × Ease.** If it can't clear a 20% guaranteed loss threshold in upside, don't touch it.

7. **When building a service business, separate one-time skills from ongoing consumables in pricing.** Charge more for the transformation, less for the continuity.

8. **When building content strategy, treat organic and paid as parallel tracks, not alternatives.** Organic builds the base long-term; ads skim the top short-term.

9. **When ads convert cold traffic poorly, the funnel economics have to change.** Cold traffic needs a different sales motion than warm — expect conversion rate to drop materially and build for it before launch.

10. **When you hire for a core function (brand, sales, content), bring it in-house.** Attraction, conversion, and delivery are never outsourced. Everything else can be.

11. **When evaluating absolute vs. relative returns, always choose absolute.** $1M net on 2:1 beats $900 on 10:1. Always.

12. **When someone activates early (wins in the first 7 days), they stay.** Build everything in your onboarding backward from that first win.

13. **When your CAC is rising, fix attribution before fixing the channel.** You can't optimize what you can't measure.

14. **When building a content email cadence, use before/after, transformation installs, and FAQ rotation.** Two emails per week minimum. No exceptions.

15. **When picking one big bet per year, it must clear a potential 20%+ upside with high confidence.** One big bet beats five mediocre ones.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Cutting prices to fight a competitor.** It does not affect churn at or above a threshold — it just lowers your revenue permanently. The customer's first comment will be "I can't believe I was paying more."

2. **Trying to go national (or scale broadly) before dominating your local or existing market.** This is an army overexpanding before its base is fortified. It collapses.

3. **Using business complexity to satisfy your own ADD.** Constantly changing funnels, scripts, and offers is you satisfying your need for novelty, not what the business requires. The business thrives on same.

4. **Optimizing relative returns at the expense of absolute output.** Obsessing over ROAS while passing on more revenue is optimization theater.

5. **Building continuity around a one-time skill.** Once the client has the skill, the value of teaching it drops to zero. Separate what's recurring (leads, community, tools) from what's a transformation.

6. **Selling to price-sensitive avatars at the cost of margin.** Contractors will shop you against five competitors. DIY buyers who love your product won't. Go where loyalty lives.

7. **Building a brand on someone else's platform without attribution clarity.** If you're spending $40K/month on ads and don't know which campaigns are profitable, you have no business — you have a lottery ticket.

8. **Reacting to competitor moves instead of focusing on the customer.** Your competitor doing one-on-one cheaper is their problem. Your job is to build the thing they can't copy.

9. **Changing your offer mid-launch or in response to early poor performance.** Let the volume work before you diagnose. Most people declare failure after a volume level that was never meaningful.

10. **Blaming the market for your pricing ceiling.** The market you've seen is not the market that exists. 9% of Americans are millionaires. You haven't found them yet.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. The Three-Rung Ladder**
He structures arguments in ascending concrete specificity: city → country → world. Local → regional → national. One → three → ten. Always anchors big claims in a hierarchy the listener can walk up themselves.

**2. The Math Anchor**
Before making a claim, he quantifies it bluntly: "9% of people in America have a million dollars." "I reduced revenue by $500,000 topline per month." "We spent $500,000 a day in the last days of the launch." Numbers precede argument. The number does the persuading; the argument just explains the implication.

**3. The Personal Confession Before the Lesson**
He telegraphs hard lessons by admitting he made the mistake first: "The biggest, most costly business mistake I've made..." "I was a converted optimizer..." "I gave Kemp a hard time..." The admission creates credibility before the prescription, not after.

**4. The Short Denial + Reframe**
When someone states a false limiting belief, he doesn't argue — he just flatly rejects it and moves on: "That is just not true." "No." "That's not achievable anyway." Then he immediately pivots to the real constraint. No debate, no validation.

**5. The Rhetorical Sequence (Question → Blunt Answer → Implication)**
"Do you want to look rich or be rich? I think a lot of you, if you're being honest, want to look rich." He asks the question that makes the audience uncomfortable, answers it for them before they can dodge it, then draws out the consequence. Stacks three to four of these in a row when building toward a principle.

**6. The "It's Just X" Minimizer**
He deflates complexity on purpose: "It's just attribution." "It's just a traffic problem." "It's just the avatar." This reframe signals that the listener is overcomplicating a solvable problem and positions him as the one who sees through the fog.

**7. The Asymmetric Effort Call-Out**
He exposes the gap between what someone thinks is a lot of work and what the benchmark actually is: "He had made 10 posts that day. I had made one. He had made three on Instagram. I hadn't made one." No moralizing — just the raw number. The embarrassment is self-administered.

**8. The Forced Prioritization Frame**
"If I only had one bet this year, what would it be?" He uses constraint as a strategy tool. By forcing the listener to imagine they can only take one action, he exposes whether their current priorities are actually material or just noise.
