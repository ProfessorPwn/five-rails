## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Trim and Stack**
List every deliverable you currently offer, then ruthlessly cut and recombine into the leanest, most valuable package possible. Don't add more stuff — identify the few things people actually want, strip everything else, and make those remaining pieces as compelling as possible. Apply before repricing or relaunching any offer.

**The Value Per Bonus Rule**
Every bonus or add-on should, on its own, be worth the full price of the offer. If it doesn't pass that test, it's diluting your offer's perceived value by creating a consumption gap — customers see unused features as waste, not value. Audit every component: if they wouldn't miss it, cut it.

**Supply Constraint Resolution Matrix**
Four and only four moves when you're out of capacity: (1) raise price so fewer people buy at higher margin, (2) change delivery ratio (1:1 → 1:4 → 1:many), (3) productize/automate delivery, (4) hire. Evaluate which problem you actually want to solve — each path builds a fundamentally different business (recruiting firm, marketing machine, brand business, or ratio play).

**The Fusion Reactor / Ad Machine**
Design your business so normal operations generate marketing. Customer has experience → document it → advertise it → next customer arrives. The goal is one big initial push, then the snowball self-sustains. Actively engineer content and testimonial capture into the product experience (rebates for video testimonials, documented date nights, etc.).

**The Consumption Gap Problem**
People cancel not because the product lacks value, but because they perceive they're not using enough of it. Adding more features widens the gap between what exists and what they consume. Planet Fitness learned 80% of people only use cardio and weights — so they stripped everything else, dropped price, and retention improved. Give 100% of the one thing they want.

**The X Factor Dilution Model (Shot Glass vs. Bottle)**
In coaching/consulting businesses, you are the bottle. Hiring coaches to deliver your method pours your X factor into diluted shot glasses — customers get less of you, spread thinner. The ratio play (more clients per call) keeps the full concentration but serves more people. The hiring play only works if you're building a recruiting and culture firm, not if you're an influencer/creator with a personal brand.

**The Scaling Roadmap Stages**
Businesses get stuck at predictable constraint types: stage 3 (1–4 employees) = supply constraint + bad lead economics + cash conversion problems. Diagnose the stage first, then apply the correct lever. Don't build a new product to solve a supply constraint — solve the supply constraint directly.

**Shame Lives in Darkness Reframe**
When prospects resist group formats due to privacy concerns, reframe: shame requires believing you're the only one with the problem. The group format is the cure, not the threat. Use: "The only thing that's not normal is that people think it's not normal." Works for any stigmatized problem category (couples issues, business failures, debt, etc.).

**The Invisible Cage**
Operators are often imprisoned by beliefs they never think to question. Surface these explicitly before proposing solutions: "What is the belief that makes you think it must be this way?" Once named, the belief can be tested with external evidence. Most business constraints are belief constraints, not resource constraints.

**The Fast Action Bonus Structure**
The fast action bonus must be tied to a deadline that is real and near (upcoming event, limited capacity). It should pull forward cash (first payment) AND delivery start. The bonus should be something they'd pay for separately: first-class event tickets, upgraded experience, immediate deep dive. Never discount — add.

**The What-Gets-Someone-Over-the-Hump Rule**
In any pitch, only one thing closes the sale. Identify it, make that detail as compelling as possible, and ruthlessly cut everything else from the pitch. More benefits in one ad doesn't help — you just failed to prioritize the one that matters for this specific avatar.

**The Big Decisions Frame**
Position high-touch advisory as reserved for the handful of decisions that create or destroy life trajectories. "There are only a handful of decisions that created the life you have. We're here to make the next handful exactly right." This justifies low frequency delivery AND higher price — scarcity of wisdom, not time.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect objects to group format after being in 1:1 | **Script:** "I'm not married to your wife. You married your wife. If I told you exactly what to say to her every day, I'd be doing you a disservice — you'd never learn. We're here for the big decisions, not to be your ChatGPT for relationships."

**Situation:** Pitching shift from 1:1 to small group to existing clients | **Script:** "This isn't as good as what you had — it's better. You're going to hear someone else's question and think, 'Damn, I never would have thought of that.' And when you hit that same problem next month, you'll already know the answer."

**Situation:** Introducing the group format for the first time | **Script:** "One of the hardest parts of being a [avatar] is it's lonely. Wouldn't it be nice to know that the struggles you have are struggles other people share too?"

**Situation:** Handling shame/privacy objection to group format | **Script:** "You're afraid of being embarrassed. Why? Because you think you're the only one who deals with this. And that's self-fulfilling. The truth is, the only thing that's not normal is that people think it's not normal."

**Situation:** Justifying low-frequency delivery cadence | **Script:** "Anyone can give you more things to do — that assumes unlimited resources, which you don't have. You're going to dedicate maybe 5% of your time to this. So let's crush that 5% to get 80% of the benefit."

**Situation:** Opening a date night / event format | **Script:** "Hey everybody, welcome to date night. If you're anything like us, date night is not business or fun — it's both. So we're going to do that together."

**Situation:** Soft repitch on day 2 of live event | **Script:** "My team tells me a few things came up for people yesterday. I want to handle those upfront — but I'm going to frame it in a way that's still valuable for your business too." [Then teach while simultaneously overcoming objections.]

**Situation:** Hard close CTA at in-person event | **Script:** "If today was valuable, do you mind if I just tell you a little bit about what we do?" [15-minute stack and close, then:] "We've got one table at dinner tonight — it's for everyone who's ready to go. What do you say?"

**Situation:** Announcing layaway / payment structure | **Script:** "The deep dive we do is really intense — it costs us the most out of everything we'll provide for you in terms of our time. So we do 9K down before we kick that off. But you can make it as flexible as you want — a lot of people are like, 'Screw it, I'll just prepay the first quarter.'"

**Situation:** Sunsetting a lower-tier offer | **Script:** "We've got completely stacked because we're good. Because of that, we still want to help you — and we actually think this is better. You're going to hear a higher variety of questions answered, so when you hit a problem next month, someone's already solved it."

**Situation:** Setting expectations on support access | **Script:** "You've got two 911 calls for the year. Think of it like Who Wants to Be a Millionaire — use it when it's actually a 911. The goal isn't for you to be dependent on us. The goal is for you to learn the skills to get where you want to go."

**Situation:** Generating video testimonial ads from existing customers | **Script:** "We're offering a $2,500 rebate as soon as you sign on and send us a video of your first date night experience. Don't talk about money — just talk about what it was like. We'll handle the rest."

**Situation:** Adding CTA to organic content for cold traffic | **Script:** "My wife and I have been doing this for [X] years. If you're an entrepreneur couple and this resonated, come join us — we're doing a 4-hour date night and we're trying to make it the world's biggest. It's absolutely free. Put in your info and we'll see you there."

**Situation:** Renewal pitch at in-person event | **Script:** "We've got our next event on [date]. When are you coming back?" [That's it — just ask. Then:] "For everyone who renews today, we're covering your first-class tickets to all three events next year."

**Situation:** Positioning the product when you have a key-person X factor | **Script:** "Would you rather have a little less of my time but with 100% of my concentration — the full me — or the same amount of time with someone I trained on a weekend? I know the answer. And so do you."

**Situation:** Diagnosing a business problem before proposing a solution | **Script:** "What problem are we solving? And can we solve it in another way that doesn't require us to build an entirely new product, an entirely new acquisition strategy, and start from scratch?"

**Situation:** Reframing group delivery to skeptical prospect | **Script:** "You've been to church, right? Ten people in a room, twenty couples talking. Couples problems are couples problems. The only thing that makes yours feel unique is that you haven't heard anyone else say it out loud yet."

**Situation:** Justifying quarterly vs. weekly cadence | **Script:** "If I worked with you one-on-one every single week, I'd have to charge $200,000 a year for it. This still gets you the 80/20 of that — and you get your life back."

**Situation:** Introducing event upsell framing | **Script:** "Think of this as [product category] insurance. Downside — you do absolutely nothing between now and the event, and we could still change your [marriage/business]. Upside — you actually do the work. Either way, you show up twice a year and build the moments you'll remember when the months blur together."

**Situation:** Transitioning offer copy for a format change | **Script:** "You just need a frame. 'Hey, if you're anything like us, [X] isn't just fun or just business — it's both.' And then you're in. That's it."

## DECISION RULES

- **When you're supply constrained**, do not build a cheaper productized version as a workaround — you'll have the same CAC for a lower-margin sale. Solve the supply constraint directly: ratio, price, productize, or hire.

- **When adding a bonus**, ask: would this bonus alone be worth the full price of the offer? If no, cut it.

- **When something is beloved after purchase but doesn't drive purchases**, remove it — you're spending time on retention theater that doesn't close sales (the Slack rule).

- **When ads stop working**, test creative first. Copy rarely needs to change once it's right. Run 100 image variants before rewriting a word of copy.

- **When a prospect resists group format**, name the actual fear (embarrassment/shame) and use social proof that the problem is universal, not unique.

- **When you have a functional 5-day challenge**, test collapsing it to a single session before rebuilding from scratch — you lose nothing (same content, fewer transitions) and gain higher attendance and conversion momentum.

- **When cash flow is the constraint**, pull forward cash via layaway: require a first payment before delivery begins, not before enrollment. Most people will just prepay to start immediately.

- **When considering hiring coaches to scale**, ask: if you can train them in a day or two, the method isn't complex — which means either you're overselling it, or you're setting up a diluted experience. Choose which business you want to build: ratio play or recruiting firm.

- **When pricing a payment plan**, sell based on the first payment amount, not the total. "It's $9K to start" closes differently than "it's $25K on a payment plan."

- **When running in-person events**, structure renewals as the event pitch — it's the highest-probability close you'll ever get. Never leave an event without making the renewal offer explicitly.

- **When organic content is performing**, don't rewrite it for ads — just append a 5-second CTA. The message that resonated organically is the validated ad creative.

- **When choosing between two business models**, identify which problem you'd rather have, not which sounds best. Each path (ratio, hire, productize, price) creates a specific company type with a specific set of hard problems.

- **When a client objects to reduced support access**, reframe dependency as harm: constant access trains learned helplessness and prevents the client from developing the judgment they're paying you to build.

- **When video ads underperform images**, don't abandon video — the videos are just bad. Images win with mediocre creative because imagination fills the gap. Fix the video quality, then video wins.

- **When designing a renewal incentive**, add value (experiences, upgrades, first-class travel) rather than discounting. Keep price the same; shift what they get for it.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Unlimited, unfettered Slack access** — "Ding ding ding, problem number one." Direct messaging access at any time is a time sink that trains clients to outsource all thinking to you, prevents renewals (they feel they haven't used what they bought), and makes the business unscalable. It's a retention liability dressed as a feature.

2. **Building a new product to solve a supply constraint** — If you're maxed out delivering your expensive offer, don't build a cheaper one with the same CAC. You've now created two acquisition strategies for one problem. Solve the constraint in the original product first.

3. **Adding features without asking "will this get used?"** — Planet Fitness ran the research. 80% use cardio and weights. Everything else created a consumption gap. More features = larger perceived waste, not larger perceived value.

4. **Going soft at in-person events** — "We barely pitched. We focused too much on production and providing value." The result: one sale out of fifty attendees. If you're going to pitch, pitch. If you're not, don't. There is no "sort of pitching."

5. **Apologizing for a format change** — When transitioning from 1:1 to group, operators often frame it as "not quite as good but we hope you understand." Never. It IS better. Argue that case or you'll lose the room before you make the offer.

6. **Confusing what people love after buying with what makes them buy** — Slack is loved after buying. It doesn't drive renewals. Features that feel like value inside the product may be invisible (or irrelevant) to a prospect making a purchase decision.

7. **Running 12–16 image ads and wondering why performance is inconsistent** — Run 100+. You don't know what creative will hit. Test volume is the answer. Open your camera roll and run all of it.

8. **Building for an average customer instead of your dream customer** — "You might not have been clear about the problem you were solving." Products built for "everyone" serve no one. Decide who you actually want to serve and build for them specifically.

9. **Over-engineering the pitch structure at events** — Hosts who feel they need to weave every session into a seamless sales narrative are procrastinating the actual offer. Provide value, tell stories, then simply ask: "Do you mind if I tell you a little about what we do?" 15-minute stack and close.

10. **Assuming coaching businesses scale like other businesses** — "I've never really seen anyone get above 30-ish million in that space." X-factor-dependent businesses that try to hire delivery staff almost always dilute the product, disappoint customers, and plateau. Know which type of business you're actually building.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The Ding-Ding-Ding Pattern** — Identify the problem dramatically before explaining it. "Ding ding ding, problem number one." Name it first, narrate it second. This signals clarity and authority without hedging.

**The Confession Before the Claim** — State the hard truth first, then the solution. "Most coaching businesses fail at scale. And it's because if your X factor is easy to teach, it's not valuable." Never soften before delivering the punch — the contrast is the credibility.

**The "Here's a Secret" Setup** — Signal that what follows is non-obvious and insider-only. "I'll tell you a secret about video. Video has higher volatility than images." Primes the listener to lean in and treat the next statement as proprietary knowledge.

**The Numbered Recap Close** — Always close a session or pitch with a numbered recap of exactly what was decided. "Number one... number two... number three." This collapses ambiguity, creates commitment, and makes complex decisions feel executable. Never summarize in paragraphs.

**The "If I Were You" Pivot** — When recommending a non-obvious move, anchor it personally. "If I wanted to enter this industry, I would enter this way." Removes abstraction, signals conviction, and implies the listener would be foolish to ignore it.

**The Binary Reframe** — Reduce complex decisions to two options with asymmetric risk. "Would you rather have a little less but 100% of me, or the same amount diluted through someone I trained in a weekend?" Forces a choice rather than prolonged deliberation.

**The Callback to Belief** — After laying out a tactical solution, zoom out to the underlying invisible belief that was blocking it. "We are limited by invisible chains of our own making." This reframes the conversation from tactics to identity, which is where commitment actually forms.

**Stacked Analogies (Gym Membership, Planet Fitness, Tony Robbins, Church, Who Wants to Be a Millionaire)** — Use multiple concrete examples from unrelated industries to illustrate a single principle. Never rely on one metaphor — stack two or three until the pattern is undeniable. This is how you teach heuristics, not just tactics.
