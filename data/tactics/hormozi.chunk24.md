## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Pricing Ladder (Close Rate → Price Multiplier)**
Close rate is a direct diagnostic of pricing health. 80%+ close rate = underpriced 3–4x. 60–80% = underpriced 2–3x. 50–60% = underpriced 1.5–2x. 40–50% = underpriced 1.25–1.5x. 30–40% with a proper pre-sell motion = correctly priced. Below 30% = fix avatar or sales motion before touching price.
*Apply it:* Run your close rate this week. If it's above 40%, raise price before diagnosing anything else.

**LTV:CAC Ratio Tiers (Human-in-Loop Multiplier)**
3:1 is for fully automated businesses (ads → checkout → software delivery). Add one human in the loop → target 6:1. Two humans → 9:1. All three stages manual → 12:1 minimum. The extra multiple is padding for ramp-up inefficiency when you hire.
*Apply it:* Map your attraction/conversion/delivery as 0 (automated) or 1 (human). Count the 1s. Set your LTV:CAC target accordingly.

**The Rule of 100**
100 actions per day for 100 days on a single acquisition channel. This produces 10,000 directional actions — enough to generate first results on any channel. Works for new channels even at scale: every new platform restarts the clock.
*Apply it:* If you feel feast-or-famine, you're not doing enough volume. Stop optimizing, start executing 100x daily until the channel proves itself.

**The 30-Day Payback Period**
Recover CAC + COGS within the first 30 days of a new customer. This enables credit-card-funded growth: borrow interest-free, acquire customer, collect before the bill is due, repeat infinitely. The goal is: cash collected in 30 days > CAC + COGS.
*Apply it:* Add initiation fees, setup fees, prepay discounts, layaway, or physical product bundles to pull cash forward into month one.

**Layaway as a Pricing Structure**
Reverse the typical payment model: customers pay before they receive delivery. This incentivizes the customer to pay fast, eliminates collections risk, builds anticipation, and eliminates delivery risk for the seller.
*Apply it:* When someone asks for payment plans, offer layaway — "As many payments as you want, we schedule delivery once it's paid off." Watch how fast they compress the plan.

**Gross Margin as the Business Ceiling**
Net margin cannot exceed gross margin. Therefore, gross margin is the hard cap on everything — marketing, sales, admin, profit. 70% vs. 90% gross margin looks like a 20-point difference; it's actually a 2.5x difference in per-customer profit. 80% is the minimum viable floor for any service business.
*Apply it:* Before fixing ops, fixing sales, or scaling — check gross margin. If it's under 80%, the entire business math is broken at the foundation.

**Retention Arbitrage**
CAC is roughly commoditized within an industry — competitors pay about the same to acquire a customer. The real moat is LTV via retention. 50% annual retention = 2x annual revenue per customer. 80% retention = 5x. Same CAC, wildly different business value.
*Apply it:* Calculate cohort retention — of your January customers, how many still pay in December? If you don't know this number, you're flying blind.

**Calendar Utilization (Sales Team Efficiency)**
70% calendar utilization is the sweet spot for a sales team. Below 60% → morale drops, reps get commission breath, close rates suffer. Above 85% → show rates drop, pipeline follow-up collapses, CAC rises. The fix at capacity is hiring more reps, not jamming the calendar.
*Apply it:* If your best week and worst week in leads produce the same revenue, your guys aren't working the pipeline. Hire another rep and watch the floor rise.

**The Two Winning Strategies at Scale**
Every massively valuable company wins via one of two mechanisms: (A) CAC approaches zero through brand or virality, or (B) LTV approaches infinity through retention, upsell depth, or enterprise pricing. You cannot win long-term without one of these.
*Apply it:* Decide which game you're playing before you scale. Trying to win both simultaneously is how companies get mediocre at both.

**Industry Average Rejection**
Using industry averages as your benchmark is an explicit decision to cap your ambition at the median. The median business is broke, struggling, and losing. Physics is the only real ceiling — not what competitors have achieved.
*Apply it:* Every time someone says "that's industry standard margins," ask what the outlier in the industry looks like and use that as the reference point instead.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect asks for split payments | **Script:** "Sure, as many splits as you want — we'll schedule your start date once it's paid in full. Most people do half now, half in 30 days and get going next month. Want to do that?"

**Situation:** Prospect says price is too high | **Script:** "What does fair look like to you? Walk me through your thinking — I want to make sure we're solving the right problem here."

**Situation:** Offering prepay discount | **Script:** "If you pay in full today, I'll [skip the line / double the guarantee / add dedicated concierge]. The discount alone is X% — money today is just worth more to both of us."

**Situation:** Prospect wants monthly payments on a 12-month program | **Script:** "Totally fine. We can do half now and half in 30 days, and you'd start next month. Or if you want to break it into three, we can do that too — just means your start date shifts by one more month."

**Situation:** Diagnosing why close rates are low | **Script:** "Before we touch price, let me ask — are these leads actually the right person with the right problem? Because if they're the wrong avatar, lower price won't fix it."

**Situation:** Sales rep has low close rate | **Script:** "I don't want to change the price until we fix the process. Let's see the last 10 calls that didn't close and find the pattern before we assume it's the number."

**Situation:** Lead response time is slow | **Script:** "Every hour we wait on a new lead, we're paying four times what we should for that customer. Call them in 60 seconds or build the system to do it automatically."

**Situation:** Business feels feast-or-famine | **Script:** "You don't have an inconsistent lead problem. You have an insufficient volume problem. The timeline you're measuring is too short. Show me what you're doing daily and we'll fix the volume first."

**Situation:** Owner resists raising price | **Script:** "If you're closing 80% of the people you talk to, the market is telling you the price is wrong. They're not buying because it's valuable — they're buying because it's cheap. Raise it until 35% close and then we talk."

**Situation:** Business owner cites industry benchmarks | **Script:** "The average in your industry — what does the average business owner in your industry look like financially? Is that who you want to model?"

**Situation:** Owner wants to scale before fixing fundamentals | **Script:** "What's your LTV to CAC right now? What's your annual retention? Before we scale, I want to know we're not just scaling the leak."

**Situation:** Owner struggles to hit 30-day payback | **Script:** "Is there any way to pull cash forward? Setup fee, onboarding package, prepay discount, physical component upfront? Give me everything you deliver and let's see what we can move to day one."

**Situation:** Prospect asks how many payment options they have | **Script:** "You can split it however works for you. The only thing is, we schedule your [start / delivery / onboarding] once the balance is cleared. Most people end up doing it faster than they planned."

**Situation:** Offering financing through a partner | **Script:** "We also work with [partner]. It doesn't change what you pay us — it just means you can start today with smaller monthly payments. Typically increases yes-rate about 35% because it removes the cash barrier."

**Situation:** Sales team is complaining leads are expensive | **Script:** "What's our lead response time? Because if we're calling in 24 hours instead of 60 seconds, we're paying four times what we should. That's not a lead price problem."

## DECISION RULES

1. **When close rate exceeds 80%**, raise price 3–4x before doing anything else.
2. **When close rate is below 30%**, fix avatar targeting or sales motion before considering a price change in either direction.
3. **When adding a human to any part of your funnel**, double your required LTV:CAC ratio as a cushion for ramp inefficiency.
4. **When you feel feast-or-famine**, increase advertising volume 10x before diagnosing lead quality or offer quality.
5. **When gross margin is below 80% in a service business**, stop scaling — fix the offer or pricing structure first. Scaling a low-margin service business makes it worse, not better.
6. **When payback period exceeds 30 days**, redesign the money model before adding new acquisition spend.
7. **When annual retention is below 80% (B2B)**, you are filling a leaky bucket — fix retention before buying more acquisition.
8. **When sales calendar utilization exceeds 85%**, hire another rep instead of cramming the calendar.
9. **When someone quotes industry averages**, ignore them and find the outlier in that industry to benchmark against instead.
10. **When a customer asks for payments**, immediately present layaway — delivery begins after payment is complete — before offering standard installments.
11. **When LTV:CAC is under 12:1 in a fully manual business**, do not scale paid acquisition — nail the model first.
12. **When introducing a new acquisition channel**, apply Rule of 100: commit to 100 actions per day for 100 days before evaluating.
13. **When net margins feel too low**, look at gross margins first — the ceiling is already set there.
14. **When a sales rep has high close rates**, raise price before rewarding performance, because high close = underpriced.
15. **When expanding to a second channel**, treat it as a brand-new business for the Rule of 100 — prior success on channel one doesn't carry over.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Calling leads more than 60 seconds after opt-in.** You are paying 4x market rate for customers and don't know it. This is the single most expensive habit in sales operations.

2. **Scaling before you've nailed retention and LTV:CAC.** Scaling a broken model doesn't fix it — it amplifies every crack. "When you scale problems, they get meaner and uglier."

3. **Measuring feast-or-famine on a weekly timeline.** Short time horizons make random variance look like systemic failure. Zoom out to 30-day cohorts before drawing any conclusions.

4. **Mistaking a 70% close rate for sales skill.** High close rates are a pricing diagnostic, not a performance badge. If you're closing 70%, you're leaving 2–3x revenue on the table.

5. **Using industry average margins as a benchmark.** The average business in most industries is barely profitable. Benchmarking against it is an explicit choice to aim for mediocrity.

6. **Lowering price when close rates drop below 30%.** This is almost always the wrong lever. Fix avatar fit or sales process first — price is usually last.

7. **Selling out of your own wallet.** Undercharging because *you* feel the price is high conflates your financial reality with your customer's value received. You are not your customer.

8. **Letting sales calendars hit 100% utilization.** Fully booked = slower bookings, worse show rates, zero pipeline follow-up. The team looks productive while CAC silently rises.

9. **Treating 3:1 LTV:CAC as a universal benchmark.** 3:1 is for zero-human, fully automated SaaS. Running a service business at 3:1 means you have no cushion for any human inefficiency — you're one bad hire from burning cash.

10. **Optimizing volume before proving the model.** You can't optimize your way to dominance; you scale your way there. Optimizing a channel with insufficient volume is rearranging deck chairs.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

1. **Ratio framing for every concept.** Nearly every point is expressed as a relationship between two numbers: LTV:CAC, close rate:price multiplier, gross margin:net margin ceiling, retention:LTV. Never just "your price is wrong" — always "here's the ratio that tells you *how* wrong and by how much."

2. **Escalating concreteness.** Starts with the principle, moves to a rule of thumb, then to a worked dollar example, then to a personal story of the extreme case. Pattern: concept → benchmark → math → war story. The math is always simple enough to do in your head.

3. **The permission disarm before a hard truth.** Before delivering a counterintuitive point, name the reaction you expect: "This might sound mindblowing to you, but…" or "I know this is going to blow your minds here…" Then deliver the number flat.

4. **Rhetorical adversarial narration.** Steelmans the wrong behavior in vivid, exaggerated form, then destroys it. "You're like, you know what? Let them cool off a little bit. Let them marinate for a couple days." The caricature makes the listener cringe at their own past behavior before the correction lands.

5. **"The only direction is up."** Service businesses have one strategic direction: raise prices. This is repeated as a mantra, not a suggestion. The framing is always that staying flat or going down is a death spiral; going up is a virtuous cycle. Use this rhythm: if you go up → margin up → reputation up → talent up → customer quality up → around you go.

6. **Physics as the only legitimate ceiling.** When rejecting limits, always invoke physics as the one real constraint, nothing else. "As long as the rules of physics allow it to exist, there's no reason we cannot get this outcome." This signals that industry norms, competitor performance, and conventional wisdom are not constraints — only what's physically impossible is.

7. **Volume over optimization, always.** When in tension, volume wins. "Diminishing returns are still returns." "You can want the most efficient, or you can want the most — you can't have both." Frame optimization as a consolation prize for people unwilling to commit to volume.
