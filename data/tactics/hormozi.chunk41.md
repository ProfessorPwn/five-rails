## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The BSL Tripod (Build, Sell, Lead)**
Every business requires three skills operating together: building products people want, selling (getting people from unaware to paying), and leading people to do both at scale. Miss any leg and the stool falls — great builders who can't sell rot in a garage; great sellers with nothing real go to jail (Wolf of Wall Street model). Identify which leg is weakest; that's where your next dollar and hour goes.

**The Constraint Diagnostic**
At any moment, a business is limited by one of three things: product quality/offering, ability to get customers in the door, or inability to scale delivery. Name the constraint explicitly before investing anywhere. Pouring money into traffic when you can't deliver is waste; hiring when you have no leads is premature. Fix the current bottleneck only.

**The Money Path (Traffic → Qualification → Sales Call → Outcome Offer → Downsell)**
Draw a single green line from every traffic source through one monetization vehicle. Old way: one eyeball source split three ways. New way: three eyeball sources all forced through the highest-yield conversion mechanism. Every CTA in every video, description, pinned comment, and profile points to the same action.

**The Six Small Business Mistakes**
Wrong avatar (selling to too many different people), lack of focus (more than one business), overexpansion, wrong compensation (overpaying, underpaying, or mispricing), single product (no front-end/back-end structure), and underpricing. Most failing businesses are hitting three or more simultaneously. Diagnose all six before adding anything new.

**BANT Qualification (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing)**
Do they have the money? Is there someone else who needs to be on the call? How important is solving this to them right now? If we got them set up, how quickly would they want to start? Only take calls with people who have all four. Saves you from selling hard to someone who can't buy.

**The Delivery Cube (Offer Construction)**
For any one problem, explore every axis: level of personal attention, effort required from them, environment/medium, how they consume the solution, speed of response, what a 10x-priced version looks like, what a 1/10th-priced version delivering the same outcome looks like. Psychological solutions beat logical ones when the customer is emotional — mirrors in elevators instead of faster motors.

**The Problem-Solution Cycle**
Properly solving one customer problem always creates the next one. A salty pretzel creates thirst. Solve thirst, and the dessert stomach is now empty. Over years, a good builder develops an ecosystem of products that catches every downstream problem. Plan your product ladder by mapping what problem your solution inevitably creates next.

**Leverage as a Wealth Multiplier**
Skills × Risk = Wealth. The 0.1% don't just have high BSL skills — they take on concentrated risk in the form of equity ownership. Selling shares or diversifying converts compounding upside into a fixed exit. Steve Jobs' 15% Apple stake would be worth $465B today had he held. Every time you reduce risk, you cap your upside. Match your risk tolerance to your conviction in the underlying BSL engine.

**The Eyeball Redirection Play**
YouTube videos are mini-ads that run forever. Your entire content library is surface area you can redirect at will. Retroactively update pinned comments, profile links, and video descriptions to point at the one thing that converts. You don't need to make new content — you need to harvest existing traffic more efficiently.

**Opportunity Cost Has a Hard and Soft Component**
Hard cost: the specific revenue you won't make (e.g., 3 sales × $6K = $18K for 2 days on stage). Soft cost: the focus, attention, and decision bandwidth consumed. Quote both when someone asks you to take your eye off the business. Your speaking fee should cover both, or you decline.

**Wasted Communication = No Behavior Change**
If a conversation doesn't result in a specific changed behavior — something someone will start doing, stop doing, or do differently — it was waste. Every meeting, every DM, every check-in should close with: what will you do differently as a result of this? If neither party can answer, don't have the meeting.

**BSL × Technology = Leverage Compounding**
Technology doesn't replace skills; it multiplies them. Build faster → ship more. Sell at scale via personal brand → 1-to-many instead of 1-to-1. Lead with project management → more output per employee. Every skill gains a multiplier when you layer appropriate technology on top. Refusing to adopt technology is a decision to be outcompeted.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Explaining why you charge by outcome, not by hour | **Script:** "A lot of lawyers charge by the hour. The reason we don't do that is because it disincentivizes us — our incentives are adverse to you. When we charge by the hour we're incentivized to take as long as humanly possible, which means it costs you more and takes longer. So we charge for the outcome. Period. How does that sound?"

**Situation:** Anchoring a high price against an even higher alternative | **Script:** "We can do $5,800 with the percentage, or if you don't want to give up the percentage we can do $25,000 flat. Most people take the $5,800."

**Situation:** DMing a new follower to qualify them | **Script:** "Hey — are you here for free content and entertainment, or are you looking for help with [specific problem]?" (Every 'help' reply is a lead.)

**Situation:** Pre-framing the sales call so prospects show up ready | **Script:** "On this call we're going to have one of two outcomes: either you want more personalized attention and we'll put together the specific outcome you're looking for, or if you're earlier-stage we can point you toward resources. Either way you'll leave knowing the right next step."

**Situation:** Triaging an unqualified person off a services call | **Script:** "It sounds like you might not be quite ready for our full engagement yet — but I don't want to leave you with nothing. Here's what I'd suggest instead: [education product / book]. When the timing's right, come back and we'll take care of you."

**Situation:** Setting a speaking fee when you have daily revenue data | **Script:** "Our calendar generates [X] sales per day at [$Y]. A two-day engagement costs us [2 × X × Y] in hard revenue plus travel. So our speaking fee is [that number] plus expenses."

**Situation:** Cutting through corporate vagueness in a team conversation | **Script:** "What do you want to have happen? What specific behavior do you want us to start doing, stop doing, or do differently as a result of this conversation?"

**Situation:** When someone doesn't understand what a role actually does | **Script:** "I'm not trying to intimidate you — I genuinely don't understand what you do. Help me understand: which of the things you spend your time on directly changes what we build or how many people buy it?"

**Situation:** Redirecting a distraction project (software, side venture) | **Script:** "If they want to use it, have them pay you $100K–$200K for the whole thing outright. That gets you out at cost, saves two years of runway burn, and lets you focus on what's already working."

**Situation:** Explaining why the education side of a business should stay (long-term nurture) | **Script:** "Long-term you might just give this away for free — because people are coming from that group and buying your high-ticket services anyway. This isn't a product line, it's a lead-nurture system."

**Situation:** Justifying a 5× markup to a new client or business owner | **Script:** "The 80% gross margin isn't greed — it's math. Out of every dollar of revenue, marketing, admin, and sales eat most of what's left. If we don't start at 80 points of gross margin, there's nothing left at the bottom. We need at least $1 in profit to reinvest."

**Situation:** Getting a hesitant founder to name a real price | **Script:** "I feel like $5K might be a stretch emotionally. Can you do $5K? ... Great. Then why not $6K? The number doesn't change the work."

**Situation:** Quantifying the cost of underpricing | **Script:** "Supply is low, demand is high — what should happen to price? It goes up. Instead of raising the price you built something cheaper. You just subsidized your competitors and starved your business."

**Situation:** Reframing a multi-product business back to simplicity | **Script:** "Pick the one thing that makes you money. Point everything at that one thing. You don't have a traffic problem — you have a monetization problem. We're not building anything new today."

**Situation:** Explaining why a content library is a permanent asset | **Script:** "I have [X] videos. Each one is a mini-ad that's always running. Underneath each one I control the call to action, and I can redirect all of that traffic across the entire internet to wherever I want — today."

**Situation:** Closing a deal where the prospect resists the percentage | **Script:** "The percentage is what makes the $5,800 affordable. Without it we'd have to charge $25K to make the math work. So the choice is: bet on yourself for $5,800 and we win together, or buy us out of the upside for $25K upfront."

**Situation:** Defining what a 'lead' actually is in a DM-based funnel | **Script:** "Every new follower you get — DM them, ask if they want help. Anyone who says yes is a lead. At 800 new followers per month, that's 800 leads. Close 5% and that's 40 sales. Do the math."

**Situation:** Framing the VSSL's purpose | **Script:** "Before they get on the call, they watch a 5–7 minute video. It covers how we work, what the process looks like, and a few successful outcomes we've delivered. By the time they pick up the phone they already know if this is for them. The call is just to confirm fit and close."

**Situation:** Convincing someone their existing assets are enough | **Script:** "You have a 90,000-subscriber YouTube, a 23,000-person Instagram, a community of raving fans, and a book. You don't need to build anything. You just need to point it all at the right place."

## DECISION RULES

**When demand is high and supply is low, raise the price — don't build a cheaper product.** Creating a lower-priced version in response to excess demand destroys margin and confuses positioning.

**When something is losing money and the reason is "impact" or "community," treat it as a charity and operate it as cheaply as possible, or convert it to a free lead-generation tool — never let it drain the main business.**

**When you have multiple business units and only one is profitable, eliminate or freeze the losing ones before adding anything new.** Revenue from one unit masking losses in another is not a business — it's subsidized distraction.

**When you identify what's working (the channel, the close mechanism, the price point), do more of that first before adding anything new.** Don't break what works chasing elegance.

**When someone comes to you with a problem but no proposed change in behavior, ask: "What specifically do you want us to start doing, stop doing, or do differently?" If they can't answer, the meeting doesn't need to happen.**

**When evaluating whether to add a new activity (speaking, software, new market), calculate the hard opportunity cost first:** number of daily sales × price × days away = the minimum fee to make it worth it.

**When you're not demand-constrained, do not add more traffic sources.** Fix monetization first. More eyeballs through a broken funnel just burns faster.

**When a business is profitable on services and breaking even on education, kill the software — don't tinker.** Three-way splits of attention at an early stage kill businesses.

**When pricing a downsell, multiply the self-service checkout price by 2–3×** because a phone call alone is worth triple the conversion. If the course sells for $500 on a page, it sells for $1,500–$2,500 on a call.

**When hiring, every role must directly support building more product, selling more product, or enabling others to do both.** If it doesn't map to one of those three, it's waste until proven otherwise.

**When you hit a growth ceiling, name the constraint explicitly before spending:** is it product quality, lead volume, or delivery capacity? Invest only in the actual bottleneck.

**When considering equity vs. salary for a key contributor, remember:** risk is a multiplier on returns. Taking more concentrated risk in a strong BSL engine beats diversifying into lower-upside positions.

**When you're not sure whether to shut down a side project**, ask: "If I ended this tomorrow, would the core business survive?" If yes, and it's bleeding cash, end it.

**When a new follower or subscriber enters your ecosystem, reach out immediately.** Warm leads go cold fast; a new follow is the highest-intent moment you'll ever have with that person.

**When you're building an offer, target 80%+ gross margins on services minimum.** Cost of goods should be under 20% of price; everything else (marketing, admin, sales) needs room to come out of the remaining 80% and still leave a profit.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Selling to too many avatars simultaneously.** If your pitch changes significantly based on who walks in, you don't have an offer — you have a consulting practice you haven't priced like one.

**Lifetime access pricing on a course.** You cap LTV on day one, train customers to expect infinite value for one payment, and incentivize churn disguised as "fully served." Recurring problems require recurring revenue.

**Charging $250/hour as a specialist when market value supports $5,000+ outcomes.** Hourly billing misaligns incentives and underprices expertise. Clients pay for outcomes, not time.

**Building new products while existing ones are unprofitable.** Software that burns $7K/month while services barely break even is not a portfolio — it's a liability wearing a roadmap.

**Confusing "understanding" with "action."** Researching, measuring, analyzing, and reporting things that don't change what you do is organizational performance theater. Only track what changes behavior.

**Treating a sales call as a consultation.** Calling it a consultation frames you as the person being evaluated. Calling it what it is — a sales call — lets you run it.

**Spreading CTAs across multiple destinations.** If your YouTube goes to a website, your Instagram goes somewhere else, and your DMs are where you actually close deals, you've built a leaky funnel by accident. One CTA, one destination.

**Using complex qualification criteria (Spotify streams, follower count) instead of BANT.** These proxy metrics don't predict purchase. Budget, authority, need, and timing predict purchase.

**Optimizing branding, logo, and website before you have a repeatable sales mechanism.** This is aesthetics in service of avoidance. You don't need a beautiful website to sell; you need a willing buyer and something to exchange.

**Diversifying equity too early.** Selling shares or diversifying assets before your BSL engine is fully compounding trades maximum upside for false security. The cost isn't visible on any spreadsheet — it's the $465B you never counted.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Diagnose in numbers, not adjectives.** "Revenue: $300K. Profit: negative $100K. Services: profitable. Education: breakeven. Software: burning $7K/month, generating $40K. That's your business." No filler framing — state the raw data, then the implication.

**Rhetorical questions that answer themselves.** "Supply is low, demand is high — what should happen to price?" "Is that what happened?" "Well, what did you do instead?" Forces the person to say the diagnosis out loud.

**The gentle reversal.** "I knew it." / "Well, [ __ ]." / "That's the good news — there's lots of room for improvement." Uses warmth to defuse shame around bad numbers before pivoting to the fix.

**Stack the "obvious" next step.** "We can do $5,800 with the percentage, or $25,000 without. Most people take the $5,800." Presents two options where one is clearly dominant, making the choice feel like common sense rather than sales pressure.

**Name the mistake, neutralize the shame, move fast.** "These are the six mistakes most small business owners make. It's normal. That's why we're fixing it." Label → normalize → redirect. Never dwell.

**The visual math breakdown.** "Let's put $100 in revenue. Subtract 20% COGS — you have $80. Marketing, admin, sales come out of that $80. Now what's left? Maybe 20%. That doesn't sound greedy anymore, does it?" Walk numbers on a whiteboard or in sequence; never just state the conclusion.

**Compress the solution into a single sentence at the end.** "You had all the ingredients. They were just organized wrong." Closes the diagnosis loop and reframes effort already spent as foundation rather than waste.
