## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Value Equation**
Four levers that determine perceived value: (1) Dream Outcome — what they want, (2) Perceived Likelihood of Achievement — do they believe it'll work for them, (3) Time Delay — how fast do they get it, (4) Effort & Sacrifice — how hard is it. Crank up the top two, crush the bottom two. Apply it: for every offer, audit each lever. If conversion is weak, something is suppressing likelihood or bloating perceived effort.

**2. The Grand Slam Offer**
A normal offer gives them the thing. A Grand Slam Offer makes it easy, fast, low-risk, and tied to a dream outcome — then stacks bonuses that attack each remaining objection. Apply it: list every reason someone would hesitate, then create a component that eliminates each one. Your offer isn't done until saying no feels stupid.

**3. Starving Crowd Market Selection**
Four filters before building anything: (1) Are they in pain? (2) Do they have purchasing power? (3) Can you find them easily? (4) Is the market growing? Fail on any one and no offer will save you. Apply it: run every new opportunity through these four before spending a dollar on product or ads.

**4. Whisper → Tease → Shout Launch Sequence**
Whisper: create mystery, hint at scale, show behind-the-scenes — maximize curiosity, minimize specifics. Tease: fuel anticipation, let people guess. Shout: hard call to action, it's live, go buy. Apply it: never go straight to the pitch. Make people want to want it before you tell them what it is.

**5. The 97/3 Brand-vs-Direct-Response Rule**
Only 3% of your market is ready to buy today. Direct response fights over that 3%. Brand advertising moves the 97% along the awareness continuum so your ready-to-buy bucket is 5x–15x larger than competitors'. Apply it: if you're only running conversion campaigns, you're capping your ceiling. Build brand equity through consistent organic content and Affiliates so your 3% pool keeps expanding.

**6. Affiliates as a Second Tier of Customers**
Affiliates don't just promote spontaneously — you have to make them an offer as compelling as any customer offer. Make it turnkey: provide banners, links, scripts, promotional materials. Apply it: audit your affiliate program like you'd audit a product offer. If you wouldn't buy it as a customer, they won't promote it as an affiliate. Bidirectional brand transfer — your brand affects who affiliates, and those affiliates then reinforce what your brand means.

**7. Lead Magnet → Trust Bridge → Conversion**
Offering something highly valuable for free converts dramatically more prospects than going straight to the pitch. Use personalization to reveal a problem they didn't know they had — sunk cost + consistency bias keeps them moving toward purchase. Apply it: instead of "buy my thing," offer a personalized diagnostic, roadmap, or assessment. Collect contact info at the end, reward completion with a discount. You'll convert 3x more.

**8. Skippable Credit-Based Membership**
For products that aren't inherently recurring, a credit-based membership that can be skipped each month solves the core churn problem: it eliminates the "I got billed for something I don't want" cancellation trigger. Credits roll over. Apply it: frame your membership around savings on purchases they'd make anyway. Build in personalized monthly recommendations + a skip window. Delay a bonus past the average churn point (e.g., free item after 5th billing) to create a reason to stay past the danger zone.

**9. Partnering for Deficiencies**
Your business is limited by the collective brainpower inside it. Identify your core deficiencies — manufacturing, distribution, retail, online sales, PR — and find partners who have spent a lifetime building those instead of trying to learn them yourself. Apply it: map your three biggest capability gaps. Ask who already has those as their core competency, then figure out what you bring to the table (distribution, audience, brand equity) to make the deal worth their time.

**10. The Solomon Frame (Self-Advice Protocol)**
The Solomon Paradox: humans give better advice to others than to themselves because emotional context clouds their own judgment. Fix it by writing a DM to your 85-year-old self and having that older self respond. No context-sharing problems, no confidentiality breach, no ability to jade the data to get the answer you want. Apply it: when facing a high-stakes decision clouded by emotion, open a blank doc and write the situation as if explaining it to your future self. Let the older version answer back.

**11. The Veteran Frame**
When something bad happens, ask: if this happened 1,000 times in a row, how would I feel on repetition 1,000? The answer is: neutral. Which means your distress right now is optional — it's a choice you're making before you've habituated. Apply it: instantly defuse disproportionate emotional reactions by projecting forward to the version of you who has seen this a hundred times.

**12. Pain as Price**
Pain isn't a signal to stop — it's the cost of the outcome you said you wanted. Reframe every difficulty as the price tag on the goal. The bigger the goal, the higher the price. Apply it: when something is hard, ask "what am I paying for right now, and do I want that thing?" If the answer is yes, the pain stops being aversive and starts being a confirmation that you're on the right path.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Explaining why you need to vet your market before building offers | **Script:** "Are they in pain? Do they have purchasing power? Can I find them easily? Is this market growing? If you fail on any one of those, I don't care how good your offer is — you're dead."

**Situation:** Explaining the membership model to a skeptical prospect | **Script:** "Here's what happens: you don't even have to buy anything. The first four days of the month, we show you what's available. If you don't want it, skip it. You never get billed. You stay in, and your credit just rolls to next month. Worst case, you're in a free discount program."

**Situation:** Presenting a credit-based membership at checkout | **Script:** "Guest price is $12.95. Member price is $9.95 — but since you're brand new, we drop it to $5.18. That's 60% off. And you can skip any month you don't want something. You can cancel whenever you want. Why would you not do that?"

**Situation:** Describing what a good affiliate deal looks like | **Script:** "The best brand deals are ones where both brands come out stronger — wider distribution, higher goodwill, more positive sentiment on both sides. That's a zero-cost deal with added financial value. If one side is just paying and the other side is just collecting, you've left half the deal on the table."

**Situation:** Convincing a business to build community around their product | **Script:** "You'd pay for the intel a community gives you — but here you get it for free and people actually buy more because of it. They buy more often, refer more friends, and tell you exactly what your next product should be. It's an asymmetric win."

**Situation:** Responding to someone fixating on a loss they can't change | **Script:** "What does this actually change about what you do? Does it change where you live? What you eat? What you work on? If the answer is no — then the only thing that changed is an expectation you had in your head. And you're choosing to punish yourself for that."

**Situation:** Explaining how to compete against big brands with direct response | **Script:** "On a 30-day window, direct response beats branding. On a one-year window, branding smashes it. And then everyone else is still fighting over the 3% who were already going to buy, and you've got this massive pie that you grew that nobody else can touch."

**Situation:** Making the case for skipping vs. cancellation | **Script:** "Most people don't cancel subscriptions because they don't want the thing — they cancel because they got billed for something they didn't want that month. Give them the skip button and you remove the primary cancellation trigger. They stay in. They come back."

**Situation:** Pitching an affiliate on promoting your product | **Script:** "We're not asking you to do anything except post. We'll give you the links, the banners, the promotional materials — turnkey. You keep X% of everything you drive. The only question is whether your audience would benefit from this. If they would, you're leaving money on the table."

**Situation:** Justifying a multi-step lead magnet quiz | **Script:** "Don't be afraid of asking for more information. Yes, fewer people will finish it. But the people who finish it already want what you have. Conversion rate of whom matters more than conversion rate in the abstract."

**Situation:** When someone asks how you handle a business disaster | **Script:** "The only thing I can control is not stopping. I don't know when it's going to work. I don't know if it's going to work. The only thing I know for sure is I'm not going to stop — and that's something I can actually commit to."

**Situation:** Reframing a big financial loss | **Script:** "What are you so afraid of losing when nothing in this world belongs to you? I'm going to have to push all my chips to the middle when the game ends. I'm really just renting these chips. Being upset about losing something that was never mine to begin with — that ruins the only thing that actually is mine, which is the present moment."

**Situation:** Explaining why most people don't make it | **Script:** "Most people don't even know what trying feels like. It's apparently rarer to have a six-pack over 30 than to be a millionaire. We know what to do. We're just not willing to endure what's required to get it and call that pain the price of the thing we want."

**Situation:** Diagnosing whether someone is sad or anxious | **Script:** "Sadness comes from a perceived lack of options — that's why it feels like hopelessness. Anxiety comes from too many options and no priorities — that's why it feels like paralysis. You solve sadness with knowledge. You solve anxiety with a decision."

**Situation:** Someone asks what to do when they don't know what to do | **Script:** "If you don't know what to do, learn. If you know what to do, do it. If you're already doing it, never stop. None of those three things have anything to do with how you feel today."

## DECISION RULES

**When picking a market:** Choose audiences who are in documented pain, have purchasing power, can be found easily through channels you already have, and are in a growing category. Never optimize the offer before validating the market.

**When building an offer:** Stack bonuses that each address a specific objection. Don't stop until every hesitation is neutralized. "Discount only" memberships have enormous churn — layer in media access, personalization, rollover credits, and delayed bonuses.

**When choosing partners:** Partner for deficiencies, not for affirmation. Identify the 3 things that will kill your business if you have to learn them alone, then find someone who already has those as a core competency — and bring them your distribution, audience, or brand in return.

**When launching a product:** Never go straight from zero to hard pitch. Run the whisper → tease → shout sequence. Every day of the tease phase is demand you've built without spending on ads.

**When building an affiliate program:** Treat affiliates as a second customer tier. Make them an offer. Make it turnkey. Send them the materials. The affiliate who has to figure out how to promote you won't.

**When running ads:** Brand advertising outperforms direct response on time horizons over 1 year. If your only campaigns are conversion campaigns, you're fighting for 3% of the market. Build the 97% with organic content, brand associations, and consistent positive sentiment.

**When pricing a membership:** Set your lowest price at the member level and make the non-member option visually subordinate (grayed out, labeled "Guest"). Anchor the savings at checkout. Make joining the obviously rational choice.

**When someone criticizes you publicly:** Summarize it as "this person lives their life in a way I would not prefer — and they live their life in a way I would not prefer. That is why it is their life." Don't catch the ball they're throwing.

**When something bad happens:** Isolate it before it compounds. Ask: what does this actually change about what I do? If the answer is "nothing material," the suffering is optional. Don't let one bad thing give you permission to ruin multiple good things.

**When facing a high-stakes decision with emotional clouding:** Use the Solomon Frame — write it as a DM to your 85-year-old self. You have all the context, no confidentiality problem, and you can't jade the data to get the answer you want.

**When a prospect hesitates after hearing the price:** The silence is data. Probe the specific objection — it's almost never the price itself. It's a likelihood-of-achievement problem or a time-delay problem. Find which value equation lever is suppressed.

**When building a subscription for a non-inherently-recurring product:** Add a skip mechanism. Remove the primary cancellation trigger (getting billed for something you don't want). Credits that roll over turn the membership into a layaway savings program — that's a feature, not a bug.

**When choosing which content to make per platform:** Make platform-native content, not cross-posted content. Instagram: high-fi aesthetics. TikTok: trending sounds, challenges, product showcases, creator collabs. YouTube: long-form hauls, reviews, membership promotion. Wrong format = wrong context = no distribution.

**When in a bad season:** Zoom in to the day, not the season. String together good days. One good day in a bad season is a win. Enough good days converts a bad season back to a good one.

**When assessing whether a hard stretch is worth it:** Ask "what am I paying for right now?" If you want the outcome badly enough to consciously pay the price, the pain becomes a positive reinforcer, not an obstacle.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Only marketing to the 3% ready to buy.** If every campaign is a conversion campaign, you're competing in a tiny pool against everyone else with a comparable offer. You've capped your ceiling artificially. Brand advertising on a year+ horizon wins.

**Generic subscription with no skip mechanism.** The #1 cancellation trigger is getting charged for something you didn't want that month. A skippable model eliminates this. Refusing to add it is choosing churn.

**Building before validating the market.** Pain, purchasing power, targetability, and growth — failing any one of these kills the business regardless of offer quality. Spending on product before running the market through these four filters is the most expensive mistake in business.

**Assuming affiliates will promote you automatically.** "I have an affiliate program" means nothing if the affiliate offer is weak. Affiliates who have to figure out how to promote you won't. No materials, no clear offer, no promotion.

**Learning everything yourself instead of partnering for deficiencies.** A business is limited by the collective brainpower inside it. If you're the only brain, the business is capped at your lifetime of learning. Your job is to know what you're missing and find someone who's spent their life mastering it.

**Discount-only memberships.** Discounts are table stakes. If the only reason to stay is a price break, members leave the moment they find the same price elsewhere or stop needing the product. Layer in VIP media access, personalized recommendations, delayed bonuses, and community to make leaving irrational.

**Making the same content on every platform.** Reposting the same clip to Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube isn't a content strategy — it's noise. Platform-native content respects the format and gets distributed. Cross-posted content doesn't.

**Letting one bad thing give permission to compound into more bad things.** One setback → poor mood → bad to spouse → worse parenting → missed work → now three problems instead of one. Bad things don't come in threes. You made them compound by losing self-control.

**Solving anxiety with more information instead of decisions.** Anxiety is too many options with no priorities. More research doesn't fix it — a committed decision does. If you know what to do, do it. Stop consuming and start deciding.

**Projecting contingency planning into rumination.** Planning for scenarios is useful. Playing out negative futures repeatedly in your head without reaching a decision is just suffering in advance for things that almost never occur the way you imagined.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Declarative sentence → rhetorical question → immediate self-answer.** "Most people don't even know what trying feels like. You know what you need to do to get a six-pack. Why haven't you? Because you haven't really tried."

**Principle stated → extreme example → practical application.** State the rule bluntly. Prove it with a vivid real-world case (Rihanna's 40 shades, a $100M payment in 72 hours). Then snap back to "here's what this means for you."

**"Now here's the thing" as a pivot.** Used to signal the counterintuitive insight. Marks the shift from setup to payoff.

**Rule of threes for emphasis and memorability.** "No untested code. No regressions. No broken features." / "Pain, purchasing power, targetability." / "Whisper, tease, shout." Ideas come in threes because they're meant to be recalled, not just read.

**Zoom out → zoom in → zoom out.** Opens at scale (Rihanna's $1.7B), drills into one tactical mechanism (the skip button, the credit rollover), then zooms back to the principle ("here's how any business owner can use this"). Never stays at abstraction for more than two sentences.

**Self-interrupting correction as authenticity signal.** "I'm not saying she couldn't do this — it's just that she'd have to learn a ton of skills..." / "I'm not trying to say you need to beat anybody..." Hedges prevent strawmanning and make the audience trust the directness that follows.

**Contrasting the naive interpretation against the real one.** "People think affiliates just automatically promote your stuff. That basically never happens." Sets up the wrong mental model, dismantles it in one sentence, replaces it with the correct one. Efficient, confident, hard to forget.

**Math as a grounding device.** "If you had 100 days, top 10% would be 10 smiley face days..." / "23.5% CAGR — that's doubling every four years." Numbers aren't decoration — they prove the principle isn't just vibes. Drop them early in the argument, not as a footnote.
