## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Wealth Pyramid (Why You're Fishing in the Wrong Pond)**
The top 1% of US households holds more net wealth than the bottom 90% combined. If you visualize $163 trillion as $100 distributed across 100 people, the bottom 50 get $2.50 and one person at the top gets $32. You are competing for the $2.50 because those are the people you see every day — not because that's where the money is. Apply it: every business decision — target customer, price point, distribution channel — must start with "am I going after the $32 or the $2.50?"

**2. 5-10x Tier Pricing (The Ladder)**
Each new pricing tier should be 5-10x the prior tier, and you should expect roughly 20% of customers at the current tier to take the next one. Each tier should contribute roughly a full doubling of total revenue, otherwise the operational complexity isn't worth it. Apply it: don't add a $1,000 → $1,500 tier; add a $1,000 → $5,000–$10,000 tier. Run the math: 8 customers at $10 and 2 at $50 already doubles revenue.

**3. Close Rate as Pricing Diagnostic**
Your close rate is a direct signal of how mispriced you are. 60–80% close rate = you have a 3–4x price increase sitting untouched. 40–50% = appropriately priced. Below 30% = either get better at selling, improve the offer, or qualify your prospects more tightly. Apply it: before assuming your offer is broken, look at close rate first.

**4. Top-Down Brand Anchoring (The Tesla Model)**
Start with an unscalable, expensive, premium offer before building accessible tiers. This creates downward brand logic: "we made an expensive thing, now here's an affordable version of it." The reverse — budget brand trying to go premium — is nearly impossible. Apply it: launch your highest-ticket offer first even if it's done-for-you and unscalable; use early case studies and revenue to fund the next tier down.

**5. The Virtuous Cycle of Price and Service**
Higher price → higher gross margin → hire better talent → deliver better service → better reputation → more demand → raise price again. This cycle compounds. The break-in point is always raising price. Apply it: when demand exceeds supply, do not add capacity before raising price — raise price first.

**6. Lead Magnet Funnel (Give Value Before Asking for Money)**
A lead magnet is a complete solution to a narrow problem that reveals the need for your core offer. It converts cold traffic at 3–5x the rate of a direct "buy now" CTA. The three types: (1) Reveal a Problem — show them a problem they didn't know they had or how bad it is; (2) Free Trial — give them the solution then remove it to create deprivation; (3) One Step of Many — solve step one of a multi-step process so they need you for steps 2–N. Apply it: never send traffic straight to "book a call" unless your audience is already warm.

**7. Sell at the Point of Greatest Deprivation**
You want to make the offer the moment someone feels the gap between where they are and where they want to be most acutely. Giving them the solution first and then pitching the upsell is wrong timing — they're no longer deprived. Apply it: the lead magnet reveals or amplifies a problem; the pitch follows immediately after that amplification, not after the problem is solved.

**8. CTA Formula (Clear → Action → Reason)**
Every call to action has three required components: (1) clear and direct, not clever; (2) the exact next action; (3) a reason to do it now. Scarcity and urgency are ideal reasons, but any reason outperforms no reason — even an absurd one. Apply it: embed CTAs before, during, and after lead magnet delivery. Salespeople who ask the most get the most deals.

**9. Keyman Diagnosis via Time Study**
The number-one risk in any service business is being the keyman — you can't sell it and you'll eventually burn it down yourself. Run a 15-minute alarm time study for one full week: every 15 minutes, log what you did. At end of week, rank activities by revenue contribution and uniqueness to you. Everything in the bottom half gets delegated or hired for. Apply it: the question is never "can I let this go?" It's "can't or won't?"

**10. Inbound/Outbound Team Architecture**
Inbound leads are your most expensive, highest-converting asset. They should only go to your proven closers — the absolute savages who take nothing for granted. Outbound is a separate team and is the proving ground. You earn inbound by dominating outbound. Apply it: if you currently have one sales team handling both, your inbound CAC is being destroyed by mediocre closers.

**11. Opportunity Cost Over Asset Valuation**
When evaluating whether to hold or exit a business/asset, the question isn't "what's it worth?" — it's "what could I build with the time I'm spending maintaining this?" If you can generate more enterprise value faster in the next thing than you'd receive selling the current thing, exit at a discount. Apply it: "I fire sold six gyms in 90 days. Made in total what I should have made on one. Within six months I was doing a million a month on the next thing."

**12. Multi-Step Constraint Resolution**
Most business problems are not first-order. If you can't hire, the real constraint is usually cash flow. If cash flow is thin, the constraint is usually pricing. If you can't raise price, the constraint is usually offer differentiation. Apply it: always reverse-engineer to the root constraint before acting — "if it were one thing you probably would've already seen it."

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Delivering a high price and worried about choking on it | **Script:** "Before I tell you the price — it's super expensive." [pause, let them set their own internal anchor, then deliver the number calmly]

**Situation:** You literally choke on prices in person | **Script:** Write the number on paper, slide it across the table face-down, then turn it. Or use a calculator and rotate it toward them.

**Situation:** Prospect says your competitor is cheaper | **Script:** "The reason they're not making that kind of guarantee is because they're not confident they can deliver. I am. That's why I can put teeth in it — if we're not on time and on budget, you get my profit back. Who do you trust more with your job?"

**Situation:** Justifying a premium price to a skeptical buyer | **Script:** "Everyone would like it cheaper. I would like all of you to get paid less. I pay very little attention to that. Here's what I can tell you: [customers who've been with me for X years haven't left]."

**Situation:** Offering a guarantee to justify a 20–40% price bump | **Script:** "We're going to charge more than everyone else, and here's why you should love that: if we're not [on time / on budget / hitting these metrics], I'll give you all my profit back. Which means you pay exactly what everyone else charges if we fail. But if we hit it, you get the difference in performance."

**Situation:** Someone pauses at your new high price | **Script:** [Say nothing. The person who speaks first loses. If they pause 5+ seconds:] "It sounds like that number landed heavy — what does fair look like to you?" [Then reanchor.]

**Situation:** Resetting an outbound team that got lazy on inbound leads | **Script:** "My [screw]-up. I put inbound and outbound together — that's not how it works. Everybody's back on the streets. For the absolute savages among you — you earn the right to get fed leads. That's the Christmas tree. That's the career path."

**Situation:** First high-ticket pitch — overcoming your own fear | **Script:** [Say a number you expect them to say no to. Stay completely silent after. Do not rescue the silence. Do not discount. If they say yes, you just learned your price floor.]

**Situation:** Qualifying who gets the free lead magnet service | **Script:** "Before I do this for you — do you have the budget to move forward if it goes well? Are you the one who makes that call? Do you actually need this right now? And are you ready to act if we deliver?" [Only proceed if all four are yes.]

**Situation:** Pitching after the lead magnet is consumed | **Script:** "Did you like the free thing? Good. You're going to love the paid thing — because all we did was solve step one. Here's what's left."

**Situation:** Reveal-a-problem close (B2B website example) | **Script:** "Your load time is nine seconds. For every second of load time you lose 3% of conversions. We can get you to three seconds — that's an 18% conversion lift. What's 18% more revenue worth to you this year? Because what I charge is a fraction of that. How soon do you want to start?"

**Situation:** Anchor offer framing when very few people will buy it | **Script:** "If nobody buys the top tier, no big deal. But here's what it does: it makes everything else look like a great deal by comparison. It raises the floor on what people are willing to pay."

**Situation:** Recruiting for a hard-to-fill technical role | **Script:** "Run national ads. Offer a generous relocation package. Frame it as a signing bonus — half now, half at month six. That's your $50K recruiter fee paid directly to the person who matters."

**Situation:** Explaining keyman risk to yourself | **Script:** "What breaks when we do more? Not what do I like doing — what actually breaks? Now: can't or won't? Choose to or have to? Those are different problems."

**Situation:** Scarcity CTA when you're a small business | **Script:** "We can only onboard [X] new clients this month — after that there's a wait. If you want in this cycle, you need to decide by Friday." [This is real — you do have a capacity limit. Use it.]

**Situation:** Outbound prospecting for B2B services | **Script:** "Hey, I work with a lot of [target type]. I'd love to quote a few of your jobs just to keep your current vendor honest. No commitment — just let me put a number in front of you." [Repeat at volume across their competitor landscape.]

**Situation:** Naming your lead magnet — polling your audience | **Script:** "Quick question — if you're a [exact ICP], help me out. Which of these sounds more useful to you: [Option A] or [Option B]? Don't answer if you're not a [ICP] — I want the real data."

**Situation:** Pushing back on someone who wants to buy both cheap and premium simultaneously | **Script:** "You can't want cookies and a six-pack. Pick the cookie or pick the abs. The dissatisfaction comes from wanting both. Which one do you actually want more?"

**Situation:** Explaining the ROI frame to someone who reacts to price emotionally | **Script:** "A rich person doesn't hear '$20,000' and say 'that's expensive.' They say, 'For what?' If the answer is a Class A share of Berkshire at a 97% discount, it's the deal of the century. The price doesn't matter — the ratio does."

**Situation:** Redirecting a business owner who wants to chase multiple channels simultaneously | **Script:** "Pick the one with the highest overlap to your current skill set. All three channels can work — someone's making money in all of them right now. You will make one of them work. The question is which one you'll make work fastest."

## DECISION RULES

**When your close rate is above 60%**, raise price — you have 2–4x untapped pricing sitting there and you're signaling low value.

**When your close rate drops below 30%**, don't raise price — fix the offer, improve selling skill, or qualify leads more tightly first.

**When building a new pricing tier**, make it 5–10x the current tier, never 1.2–1.5x — buyers' spending power doesn't scale linearly, and neither should your tiers.

**When adding a tier that doesn't at least double total revenue**, don't add it — the operational complexity isn't worth it.

**When you have more demand than you can handle**, raise price before adding capacity — this is how the supply/demand curve actually works.

**When a customer complains about price but keeps buying**, ignore the complaint entirely — behavior is the data, words are noise.

**When you're running a service business without capital for automation**, do not target mass market / low price — go high-ticket and serve fewer people far better.

**When passive investments start requiring active time**, either make them fully passive again or convert them to active income strategies — never let something be half of both.

**When you have two businesses and one has clearly higher growth rate and margins**, exit the lower one even at a discount — opportunity cost of your time is the only currency that matters.

**When recruiting a critical role you can't fill locally**, run national ads with a relocation bonus package — re-frame the recruiter fee as a signing bonus to the person.

**When sending cold traffic to a landing page**, never lead with "buy" or "get a quote" — lead with a free lead magnet that solves a narrow problem and creates deprivation for your core offer.

**When you name a lead magnet**, test the headline against your actual ICP before testing anything else — headline change alone can move conversion 2–10x more than content change.

**When building a sales team**, keep outbound and inbound strictly separate — inbound is the reward tier that only proven closers earn.

**When evaluating whether to exit a business**, ask: "Can I create more enterprise value in the next opportunity in the same time it would take to properly exit this one?" If yes, fire-sell it.

**When you are the keyman in your business**, do a time study before hiring — rank activities by revenue contribution and uniqueness, then hire for the bottom half before anything else.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Selling from your own wallet.** If your pricing is based on what you would personally pay, you are pricing for your own income bracket — which is the wrong bracket. The top 10% of Americans have $1M+ net worth. One in ten people you meet has money. You just aren't offering them anything they'd pay for.

**Taking pricing advice from broke people.** Friends, family, and peers who aren't financially where you want to be cannot tell you what wealthy buyers will pay. "Why would you listen to the people who don't have money on how to get money?"

**Adding tiers at 1.2–1.5x current price.** This is not a real tier — the willingness-to-pay is identical to the current tier. It signals you don't understand how buyer psychology works across wealth distribution.

**Mixing inbound and outbound sales teams.** Your expensive, hard-won inbound leads get worked by people who rely on leads instead of earning them. The result: your outbound team gets lazy, your close rate tanks, and your CAC explodes.

**Sending cold traffic directly to a purchase or quote CTA.** Most visitors aren't ready to buy. Without a lead magnet, you're losing 70–90% of the traffic you already paid for. This is the most common and most expensive conversion leak.

**Solving the same problem your core offer solves in the lead magnet.** If the free thing satisfies the need your paid thing addresses, there's no deprivation left at the pitch moment. The free thing should solve step one and reveal the next three problems.

**Including the CTA only once.** Salespeople who ask the most get the most deals. If you give value once and ask once, you're leaving a multiple of revenue on the table. Provide value, ask, provide value, ask — continuously.

**Being the keyman and confusing "choose to" with "have to."** "I have to be involved in every aspect" almost always means "I choose to be." This is the #1 risk factor for business valuation, personal burnout, and inability to scale.

**Trying to serve mass market without capital.** Netflix and Spotify got there on hundreds of millions in outside capital and still almost didn't make it. If you're bootstrapped, the mass-market low-price model will destroy your margins and your sanity before you achieve scale.

**Evaluating businesses by what they're worth instead of opportunity cost.** Holding a $5M exit opportunity that requires 2 more years of your attention, when you could build $5M of new enterprise value in 6 months elsewhere, is a $5M mistake dressed up as prudence.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Math first, implication second.** State the hard number ("the top 1% holds $32 of every $100"), let it land, then draw the business implication ("this means everything you know about pricing your product is probably wrong"). Never lead with the opinion — lead with the number that earns the opinion.

**Reframe the premise before answering.** When the question contains a bad assumption, name it first: "I'm going to reject the premise of that question," or "You will make one of those channels work — the question isn't which one works, it's which one you'll make work fastest." This reorients the listener and establishes authority before the answer.

**Binary forcing.** Strip false nuance: "Can't or won't?" / "Have to or choose to?" / "Cheap thing to a rich buyer, or expensive thing to a poor buyer?" Forces clarity where people are hiding behind ambiguity. Use it when someone is rationalizing a bad decision.

**Story → extract → apply.** Every major principle lands via a personal story first (the $6K gym launch call, the first high-ticket yes, the six fire-sold gyms), a clean extraction of the lesson, then a direct application instruction. The story makes it believable. The extraction makes it replicable.

**"Here's where it gets nasty/interesting."** Signal phrase before the real insight drops. Used to retain attention through setup and reward the listener for staying. Variants: "Here's where people miss the next point," "Here's where this gets really interesting," "Now here's the part no one talks about."

**Escalating repetition.** State the core rule plain, then restate it harder: "Sell to the rich, they pay better — it's not some piffy statement. It's reality." State → amplify → make it unavoidable. Repetition is not filler; it's rhetorical cement.

**Compress the counter-argument, then dismiss it with math.** "But what about freebie seekers? You don't have to give the lead mag to everybody — add a qualifying dropdown. Duh." Steelman the objection in one sentence, then crush it with a specific mechanism. This pattern closes off escape routes before the audience can use them.

**End with a punchy one-liner that's quotable.** Every major section closes with a line you could put on a slide: "If you sell to rich people long enough, they will make you one of them." "The sweet spot isn't the most yeses. It's the most money." These serve as anchors that make the preceding argument stick and shareable.
