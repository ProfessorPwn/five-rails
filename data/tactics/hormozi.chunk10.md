## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Four Business Model Shapes**
Every business is one of four: e-commerce (stair-step growth gated by capital and distribution), service (slow and steady, people-constrained), education/info (fast spike then plateau), SaaS (slow ramp then exponential). Understanding your shape tells you which hairy problem you're paid to solve and why your current struggle is a feature, not a bug. Apply it by mapping every complaint to its shape's inherent constraint — then put disproportionate resources on that one thing.

**2. The Big Hairy Problem Framework**
Every business model has a core structural problem that creates its shape. E-commerce: cash flow and supply chain. Service: talent acquisition and training. Education: retention in a fundamentally unsticky model. SaaS: surviving pre-PMF and achieving revenue retention. Identify your model's hairy problem, quantify how much enterprise value solving it unlocks (e.g., "if I can onboard 2 people/month, that's $50M EV"), then spend resources proportional to that unlocked value — not proportional to how hard it feels.

**3. The 70/30 Brand-to-Performance Split**
Businesses that persist long-term in e-commerce allocate 70% of ad spend to top-of-funnel brand awareness and association, only 30% to direct-to-purchase. Most companies do 100% direct-to-purchase and wonder why they get commoditized by cheaper competitors. Apply: if you can't articulate your brand associations by name (what you stand for, what you won't associate with, whether you discount), you're building a smash-and-grab, not a company.

**4. Big Head, Long Tail Pricing (Education)**
The one-time, transformational piece of education has enormous upfront value — charge a large lump sum for it. The recurring components (community, creative, updated research, continued credits) have lower per-period value — charge a much lower monthly rate. The error: wrapping education in a payment plan and calling it a subscription. The buyer logic: "I already paid a lot for this — why would I cancel $99/month to keep my skills current?" Apply: always split your offer into big-head (one-time) and long-tail (consumable/recurring) and price each against its actual value.

**5. The Stickiness Stack (Education)**
Education is inherently unsticky — when you do your job, they graduate. To create retention: (1) add consumption-based recurring value (trends, new tools, creative refresh); (2) add community; (3) add collective buying power/discounts that save more than the subscription costs; (4) offer continued education credits/updates. Each layer is independently weak; stacked, they make cancellation irrational. Apply before you launch the initial product, not after churn appears.

**6. The Talent Machine (Service Business)**
In a service business, your recruiting, onboarding, and training pipeline IS the product. The delta between raw-materials-in (underskilled people) and outputs-out (skilled, systematized delivery) is where all profit is created. Most founders treat training as a cost center; the compounders treat it as the core value-creation engine. Apply: build training depth proportional to years it took you to master the skill — not a weekend crash course.

**7. Logo Retention vs. Revenue Retention (SaaS)**
Logo retention = % of customers still paying. Revenue retention = whether those customers are paying you more or less than when they started. Target: >60% logo retention, >100% revenue retention (meaning expansion revenue from remaining customers exceeds churn). A business with great revenue retention is nearly infinitely valuable because the base compounds — you just keep filling the front end.

**8. The Smash-and-Grab vs. Brand Business**
A smash-and-grab business is pure media arbitrage: buy eyeballs at $10, make $11 gross profit. Anyone who sees it can copy it. A brand business has three structural advantages the copier can't replicate: higher click-through (history of good associations), higher repurchase rates, and pricing power. Apply: every discount decision, every influencer partnership, every promotional channel choice is a brand vote. Make it deliberately.

**9. The Overkill Bias (Social Proof in Education)**
Humans apply overkill social proof to everything: if a jacket keeps you warm on Everest, it'll keep you warm from your car to the door. In education, this means people will pay Tony Hawk to teach them an ollie when thousands of skaters could. Real-world proof — exits, revenue, demonstrated results — compounds attention and justifies premium pricing far more than course content quality alone. Apply: build the track record first, then launch the education. Not the other way around.

**10. Systematize-Then-Scale (Service Business)**
You cannot scale a service business that delivers bespoke outcomes to anyone with a pulse. The sequence: (1) narrow to one customer type, (2) collapse your deliverables to 1–3 core offers, (3) systematize delivery of those offers, (4) build training infrastructure around those systems, (5) develop clear career paths so talent stays. Only then does scale become available. Apply: if your team can't deliver your service without you, you haven't systematized yet.

**11. The Viral Loop (SaaS)**
At consumer SaaS scale, paid acquisition cannot be the primary growth engine — unit economics won't close. You need a viral coefficient >1.0, meaning each acquired customer brings in >1 additional customer organically. Design the viral loop into the product before you scale spend. Apply: ChatGPT to 1M users in a week was virality, not ad spend — ask what in your product makes users naturally recruit other users.

**12. The Low-Cost Provider Day-One Decision**
There are two defensible positions in service businesses: premium (high talent, high margin, high reputation) or lowest-cost (offshore, automated, operationally lean, cheap as USP). The fatal mistake is defaulting to cheap after pricing fails. You must choose the low-cost path on day one and orient every decision — hiring, automation, tooling — around operational efficiency from the start. Apply: if you didn't decide on day one, you're on the premium path by default.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Explaining why your business is struggling | **Script:** "There's nothing wrong with my business — this is the core hairy problem that this model was built around. My job is to conquer it, because whoever solves it in my space builds enormous enterprise value."

**Situation:** Deciding whether to invest in solving an operational bottleneck | **Script:** "If I solve this problem, how much enterprise value does that unlock? If the answer is $50M, I should be willing to spend $500K and a year of effort on it. That's the math."

**Situation:** Justifying raising prices in a supply-constrained service business | **Script:** "We do a good job. We have more demand than supply. That's the signal the market gives you when it's time to raise prices and go after better clients who write bigger checks."

**Situation:** Explaining why you're niching down from "anyone with a pulse" | **Script:** "In the beginning, you take anyone. But around $1–3M you have to decide what business you're really in — what customer you actually serve and what 1–3 deliverables you actually do. That's when scale becomes possible."

**Situation:** Pitching an education product with recurring component | **Script:** "There's the one-time education — that's a big number, because knowing how to do this is worth X per year for the rest of your career. And there's the monthly component — that's what keeps the knowledge current, the community active, the tools updated. Very different price points because very different value delivered."

**Situation:** Responding to skepticism about hiring people to deliver your premium service | **Script:** "You can give 10 people a shot glass of the real thing or a full glass of watered-down version. I've seen which one companies choose and what happens to their reputation. I'd rather serve fewer people with the real thing."

**Situation:** Convincing a founder to invest in brand over short-term performance | **Script:** "The companies that are still around in 10 years are running 70% top-of-funnel, 30% direct to purchase. Every company that's complaining about Chinese dupes and ad costs is running 100% direct-to-purchase. The brand is the moat — everything else can be copied."

**Situation:** Explaining why education businesses plateau | **Script:** "When you do your job in education, people graduate. That's the product working. The question is what keeps them in your orbit after they graduate — community, consumables, continued education. That's where the recurring revenue lives."

**Situation:** Making the case for systematizing service delivery before hiring | **Script:** "I can't build training infrastructure around 'whatever you want, we'll figure it out.' I have to pick the 1–3 things we do and make them repeatable. Then I can build training. Then I can hire. In that order."

**Situation:** Justifying patience in SaaS before scaling spend | **Script:** "Product-market fit is like pushing a boulder uphill. Once you have it, it chases you. The mistake is spending on acquisition before you have retention — you're just filling a leaky bucket and calling it growth."

**Situation:** Describing what good software design looks like | **Script:** "You don't make something good. You find the outcome everyone wants and remove everything between where they are and getting it. If you've removed all the friction, you have a good product by definition."

**Situation:** Explaining e-commerce cash flow trap | **Script:** "You can be completely asset-rich and cash-poor. The profit from the first tranche funds the next inventory order. Growth rate is dictated by free cash flow. If you want to grow faster, you need lending partners or you'll hit the ceiling no matter how good demand is."

**Situation:** Advising someone who wants to start cheap in a premium market | **Script:** "If you didn't decide on day one to be the lowest-cost provider — with offshore talent, automation, every decision made around operational efficiency — you're not actually a low-cost provider. You just have low prices. That's a different, worse thing."

**Situation:** Making the case for building real-world proof before education | **Script:** "Nobody paid close attention to my business content until I had a $4.6B exit. You could make a better investing course than Warren Buffett. You just forgot to build Berkshire Hathaway first. That's what you forgot."

**Situation:** Explaining why career paths matter in service businesses | **Script:** "The best people are incredibly hard to get. Once you have them, you cannot afford to lose them to someone who has a clearer path for them. Career paths aren't HR fluff — they're retention tools for your most irreplaceable raw material."

**Situation:** Explaining virality math for SaaS | **Script:** "If I spend to acquire one customer and my viral coefficient gets me two more, my effective acquisition cost just divided by three. Then those two get two more. At that point, paid ads are an accelerant, not the engine."

**Situation:** Justifying the brand investment for e-commerce | **Script:** "Brand delivers three things: higher click-through because people know you, higher repurchase because they trust you, and pricing power because they want you specifically. Lower CAC, higher LTV, higher gross margins. That's the ROI on brand."

**Situation:** Setting expectations for new business owners | **Script:** "It'll take longer than you think and it'll probably suck for way longer than you think it will. And once you're in, you never get out. Just want to make sure you know that going in."

**Situation:** Advising on whether to scale service via cheaper coaches | **Script:** "If you can teach someone in a weekend to do what you do, you're either not selling something that valuable, or you're lying about how good they are. Both kill your reputation. Pick a smaller number of clients you can actually serve well."

**Situation:** On the right metric for service business progress | **Script:** "The one metric that tells me how far along an entrepreneur is in a service business is what they charge. If demand exceeds supply and you're still not raising prices, you're leaving the market's feedback on the table."

## DECISION RULES

1. **When demand exceeds supply in a service business → raise prices, don't hire faster.** More demand than supply means the market is telling you your prices are too low. Raising prices expands gross margin, which funds better talent, which improves reputation, which compounds demand.

2. **When you hit $1–3M in a service business → decide what business you're actually in.** Before that, take anyone and survive. At $1–3M, pick your customer, collapse your deliverables to 1–3, and systematize. Everything after that depends on this decision.

3. **When building e-commerce → allocate 70% to brand, 30% to direct response.** If you can't stomach this split yet, at minimum acknowledge you're building a smash-and-grab with no moat and plan your transition.

4. **When cash flow is tight in a growing e-commerce business → get a lending partner before you need one.** Nothing can be wrong with your business and you still can't grow because cash is the constraint. Solve it before you hit the ceiling.

5. **When considering discounts in e-commerce → ask what brand you're signaling.** Every discount decision is a brand vote. Decide day one whether you're a discount brand or a premium brand and be deliberate — never reactive.

6. **When training people to deliver your service → invest training time proportional to how long it took you to master the skill.** One weekend of training for a decade of expertise produces diluted product and reputation damage.

7. **When building SaaS → measure retention before measuring growth.** A SaaS that isn't retaining looks identical to an info business until you have the data. Get 60%+ logo retention and 100%+ revenue retention before scaling acquisition.

8. **When a business problem feels inherent to your model → recognize it as a feature, not a bug.** Supply chain issues, talent constraints, retention problems, and cash burn are not signals that your business is broken — they are the defining challenge of your model. Solve them; don't escape them.

9. **When allocating capital in a service business → invest first in better talent, second in brand.** Higher-horsepower raw inputs still benefit from your training infrastructure and yield better output. Brand justifies premium prices and attracts premium people simultaneously.

10. **When choosing to be a low-cost provider → decide on day one.** Every system, hire, and decision from the start must orient around operational efficiency. Defaulting to low prices after premium fails is not a strategy — it's a retreat.

11. **When growing an e-commerce brand to scale → plan your retail presence after brand saturation, not before.** Build online, open flagships when brand is proven, then expand physical when brand awareness is so complete that retail removes shipping cost and boosts AOV.

12. **When scaling e-commerce → track repeat purchase rate by cohort.** If 60–70% of customers buy again after the first purchase, you likely have a strong product. Below that, no amount of acquisition fixes the unit economics.

13. **When adding features to a SaaS product → weight the UX cost of complexity against the value of the feature.** Most features add complexity faster than value. Elegance is removing things, not adding them.

14. **When considering which business model to start → match the model to your personality.** Willing to suffer long with detail orientation → SaaS. Promotional, fast-moving, marketing-driven → e-commerce or education. Highly skilled, love teaching → service. People-oriented, love culture-building → service.

15. **When solving the big hairy problem of your model → quantify the EV unlock first.** "If I can onboard 2 people/month, that's $50M in enterprise value. I should therefore spend $500K and a year solving it." Resources should be proportional to the value of the problem, not the size of the headache.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **The Smash-and-Grab E-commerce Play.** Running 100% direct-to-purchase with no brand investment and wondering why Chinese competitors are undercutting you. You built a media arbitrage business with no moat. Anyone who sees you do it can copy it.

2. **Bespoke Service at Scale.** Saying "yes" to whatever any client asks with no systematized delivery. You cannot build training infrastructure around "whatever you want." This makes you irreplaceable in the worst way — trapped by your own expertise.

3. **Weekend Training for Premium Services.** Hiring coaches or delivery people and training them in 5 hours to do something that took you 10 years. The milk is now 90% water. Your reputation will reflect that within 6–12 months.

4. **Education Payment Plans Disguised as Subscriptions.** Charging $2K/month for a $25K course via payment plan and calling it a "community subscription." The buyer correctly perceives that after the education is delivered, they're paying community-level prices for a community-level experience. Churn follows.

5. **Scaling SaaS Acquisition Without Retention.** A SaaS with poor retention looks exactly like an info business from the outside — fast revenue ramp, then churn cliff. Pouring ad spend into a leaky bucket before solving retention is expensive and demoralizing.

6. **Defaulting to Low Prices Without a Low-Cost Strategy.** Charging less because premium didn't work is not the same as being the low-cost provider. The low-cost provider has offshore talent, automation, and efficiency-first decisions from day one. Reactive discounting just destroys margins.

7. **Founder-as-Technician Trap.** Being the best deliverer in your service business means every hard problem comes back to you. This is flattering and fatal. If you haven't systematized your expertise into transferable training, you don't have a business — you have a job that requires you specifically.

8. **Feature Creep in SaaS.** Giving customers everything they ask for. Customers will hang themselves with their own requests. Complexity added = elegance lost. Frankenstein products with every requested feature have terrible retention because nobody can find their way through.

9. **Offer Hopping in Education.** Launching a new topic/product every year because the last one plateaued. This signals you haven't solved stickiness — you've just discovered that new audiences respond to new promises. The compounding brand value never accrues.

10. **Treating Business Model Problems as Business Failures.** Complaining that cash flow is tight in a scaling e-commerce company, or that good people are hard to find in a service business, as though these are signs something is wrong. They are the defining features of those models. Misdiagnosing them costs years.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. Credibility-First, Then Advice.**
Hormozi never gives a tactic without first establishing earned authority: "My credibility with this is..." followed by specific numbers, company names, or years of experience. Apply this pattern before making any strong claim — not bragging, just establishing the right to be believed.

**2. "Now the Dirty" — Labeling Negatives Colloquially.**
He acknowledges downsides with casual, almost conspiratorial framing: "Let me tell you what sucks about this" or "the dirty" or "I'll give you the nasty one." This signals honesty and builds trust before the audience can become skeptical. Never pretend problems don't exist; name them before the listener does.

**3. The Analogy Sandwich.**
Abstract concept → vivid analogy → translation back to business. The milk/shot-glass analogy, the boulder analogy, the Everest jacket analogy. The rule is: if the concept requires more than two sentences, it needs an analogy. The analogy earns the conclusion.

**4. "Right?" as a Live Check-In.**
Frequent "right?" at the end of logical steps — not for affirmation but as a rhythm marker signaling the listener should be tracking along. It mimics the cadence of live teaching and keeps attention tethered. Use sparingly but consistently at the end of each logical beat.

**5. The Inversion Move.**
"I didn't start caring about the first sale — I care about the 10th sale." "You can't actually make something good; you remove what's bad." "You don't win by being great at sales in a service business — getting customers isn't your problem." Hormozi often states the common belief, then inverts it with the more useful frame.

**6. Numbered Structure, Telegraphed in Advance.**
"I'll give you two" or "there are three things you have to solve" before listing them. This creates anticipation and makes content scannable even in speech. The listener stays because they're counting to a known endpoint.

**7. The Self-Aware Confidence Frame.**
He doesn't apologize for strong opinions but frequently acknowledges the other side without dwelling: "I'm not saying I'm better than everyone — I'm just saying there are levels to the game." This prevents arrogance while maintaining authority. Lead with the confident claim; brief acknowledgment of limitations follows.

**8. Time-Horizon Reframing.**
When something sounds hard, he zooms out: "Life is long. You can change your mind later." When something sounds easy, he zooms in: "Fast, easy, cheap to start — and fast, easy, cheap for your competition too." Adjusting time horizon is a core rhetorical tool to recalibrate unrealistic expectations in either direction.
