## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The CLOSER Framework**
Clarify why they're there → Label them with the problem → Overview past pain → Sell the vacation → Explain away concerns → Reinforce the decision. Use this as the sequential spine of every sales call. Never skip C and L before you pitch — without establishing the gap, you have nothing to sell against.

**The Value Equation / Gap Model**
The size of the rubber band between where they are and where they want to be IS your leverage to close. Your job on a call is to expand that gap, not explain your product. The bigger the gap + the more urgent the deprivation, the lower resistance to buying. Apply: use pain-cycle questions to stretch the gap before you ever present price.

**The Pain Cycle**
"What have you done before? How'd that work out for you?" — cycle through every attempt they've made. Associate what worked with your solution; associate what didn't with why you're different. Each loop pops the pain button harder and expands the motivation gap. Never present your offer until you've run at least two full loops.

**The Three-Pillar Pitch**
Pick the three differentiators of your solution. For each, write one analogy or metaphor that maps the unfamiliar concept onto something they already understand. Keep each pillar to two sentences max. Don't monologue — the pitch is a scaffold, not a speech. If it's simple, use an example; if it's complex, use a metaphor.

**The Management Diamond**
Five reasons an employee doesn't perform: (1) didn't know you wanted it, (2) didn't know how, (3) didn't know when, (4) didn't know why, (5) was blocked by an obstacle. Diagnose in that order before assuming motivation failure. Solve the constraint before replacing the person.

**The 3 Ds — Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate**
To transfer any skill: write a step-by-step checklist of what you do (document), run through it in front of them (demonstrate), then watch them run through it using only the checklist (duplicate). If they produce the same output using just the document, the skill has been successfully transferred.

**The Core Four + Four Lead Getters**
Only four advertising actions exist for any individual: warm outreach, cold outreach, content, paid ads. To scale beyond yourself, find people who will do the Core Four on your behalf: customers (referrals/reviews), employees, affiliates, agencies. If you're pre-million, start with warm outreach + content — cheapest and fastest to execute.

**Work = Volume × Leverage**
Output isn't determined by hours — it's volume multiplied by leverage. Leverage compounds with skill: more volume → more skill → more leverage → more output per rep. In the beginning, you have near-zero leverage, so the only lever you control is volume. Do a thousand reps before you optimize.

**The 5/5/5 Pricing Model**
Sell the first five units at your current price. If all five sell with no friction, raise price 20%. Sell the next five. Keep climbing until you hit resistance. Never discount to make a sale — test price by holding it for the committed number, then move. The math: conversion rate × gross profit at each price point tells you the optimal.

**The Infinite Game Mental Model**
Business, health, and marriage are infinite games — the goal is to stay in, not to win. Millionaires who diversify after focusing become less wealthy (Gates), not more. Concentration into one thing until it overflows is the mechanism of compounding. Every pivot resets the compounding clock to zero.

**Common Factors Analysis**
Take any set of outputs (ads, sales calls, content, customers). Identify top 10% and bottom 10%. Find the structural differences. Those deltas are your actual skill gaps — and no course teaches them because they're specific to your market. Apply this to customers (who are your best vs. worst?), content, sales recordings, ad hooks — everything.

**The Three Pillars of Business**
Every business has exactly three functions: Acquisition (marketing + sales), Delivery (fulfillment + customer success), Operations (legal, finance, HR, payroll). Most early entrepreneurs are topheavy in one. A partner must add a leg of the stool you lack — not double-down on a leg you already have.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening a sales call with a warm lead | **Script:** "Hey — before we get into anything, help me understand: what made you actually take the time to respond/show up today? What's going on?"

**Situation:** Labeling the prospect's problem | **Script:** "So let me make sure I have this right — you're here [current state], you want to get to [desired state], and the thing standing in the way is [obstacle]. Does that sound about right?"

**Situation:** Entering the pain cycle | **Script:** "What have you tried before to solve this? Tell me everything. And how'd that go?"

**Situation:** Looping through the pain cycle a second time | **Script:** "Okay, so that didn't fully work. What else have you tried? Walk me through everything you've done on this."

**Situation:** Transitioning from pain to pitch | **Script:** "Based on everything you've told me, I think I might actually have something for you. Would you like to hear about it?"

**Situation:** Three-pillar pitch opener | **Script:** "There are really three things that matter here — [Pillar 1], [Pillar 2], and [Pillar 3]. Let me break each one down for you. [Pillar 1] — it's kind of like [analogy]..."

**Situation:** Soft close after pitch | **Script:** "Does that sound like it makes sense for your situation? Are you ready to get started?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it" | **Script:** "I totally get that — help me understand what specifically you want to think about. Is it [time], [money], [fit], or is there something else going on?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "I can't afford it" | **Script:** "I hear you on budget. Help me understand — is it that this doesn't make financial sense, or is it that you don't have the cash available right now? Those are two different problems."

**Situation:** Prospect says "My spouse needs to approve it" | **Script:** "That makes total sense. When you walk them through it — what do you think their biggest concern is going to be? Let's address that now so you're ready."

**Situation:** Post-close onboarding handoff | **Script:** "Here's exactly what's going to happen next: in the next [X minutes] you'll get an email, you'll click [link], and then [next step happens]. I want to make sure nothing falls through the cracks."

**Situation:** Free offer to warm prospects to build pipeline | **Script:** "Hey — I'm offering to spend [3–5 hours] helping you [specific outcome] completely free. At the end, if it's been valuable, I'll make you an offer about working together. No pressure either way."

**Situation:** Pricing objection on a $10K offer | **Script:** "Look — split over 4 years, that's $200/month. For [result]. Does the math not work, or does the result not feel certain enough?"

**Situation:** Reframing price for a skeptical prospect | **Script:** "The question isn't whether you can afford this — it's whether you can afford not to solve [the problem] for another [timeframe]. What has not fixing this cost you already?"

**Situation:** BAMFAM — booking next meeting before hanging up | **Script:** "Before we wrap — let's put a time on the calendar right now for your onboarding call with [person]. What does your [day/time] look like?"

**Situation:** Employee not performing | **Script:** "Help me understand — did you not know I wanted this done? Did you not know how to do it? Did you not know when I needed it? Or was there something blocking you that I don't know about?"

**Situation:** Warm outreach to announce a new service | **Script:** "Hey [name] — I've been helping [type of person] with [problem] and getting [result]. I've got a few spots open this month. Would you be open to a quick call to see if it makes sense for you?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "I've tried things like this before and they didn't work" | **Script:** "Tell me specifically what happened. [Let them explain.] Okay — what you're describing is [X problem]. What we do differently is [specific contrast]. That's actually why this works when those didn't."

**Situation:** Closing someone who's on the fence about price | **Script:** "Here's the thing — you said [their goal]. You've tried [their past attempts] and they didn't get you there. If nothing changes, where are you in 12 months? Is that okay with you?"

**Situation:** Upsell / ascension after initial purchase | **Script:** "You've [achieved milestone]. The next thing most people in your position do is [next product/offer]. Want me to walk you through what that looks like?"

## DECISION RULES

**When starting a business with no capital:** Sell to the fewest number of people at the highest price first. 100 customers at $10K beats 10,000 at $100 — and finding 100 is easier.

**When choosing a niche:** Pick pain, passion, or profession — but always pick the one where you can provide the most value. Maximum value = maximum price = maximum leverage.

**When picking a market:** Only enter flat or growing markets. Never start a business in a declining market regardless of how good your offer is.

**When you have no leads:** Start with warm outreach first, then content. Only add cold outreach and paid ads after you've exhausted warm. Never start with paid ads before you've closed deals manually.

**When pricing:** Run the 5/5/5 model — raise price 20% every five sales until you hit friction. Never discount to close; test price by holding it for the stated number of attempts.

**When building a team:** Only take a partner who fills a leg of the stool you don't have. Two people with the same skill set is a top-heavy business that will fall over.

**When an employee underperforms:** Run the Management Diamond before assuming attitude. Diagnose in order: know what → know how → know when → know why → blocked by obstacle. Fix the constraint before you fire.

**When a new opportunity looks attractive:** Ask where you are on the compounding curve in your current business. Year 4→5 of a known thing beats Year 0→1 of a new thing every time. The new opportunity is probably the valley of despair in disguise.

**When you feel motivated to quit:** You're almost certainly in the valley of despair — the phase just before informed optimism. The people who win are the ones who just don't stop here.

**When scaling a product:** Evaluate incremental cost per new customer. Lower incremental cost = more scalable. Highly scalable = demand-constrained. Hard to scale = supply-constrained. Match your go-to-market to the constraint type.

**When considering diversification:** Only diversify after the primary business is generating excess cash you cannot productively redeploy into it. Diversification before focus compounds nothing.

**When something breaks in the business:** Check the three pillars — Acquisition, Delivery, Operations. The bottleneck is always in one of them. Fix the constrained pillar before optimizing the others.

**When you don't know what to improve:** Run a Common Factors Analysis. Take your top 10% of outcomes and your bottom 10%. The structural differences are your skill gaps. No course required.

**When learning a new skill:** Volume before optimization. Do a thousand reps first. The feedback data from high volume is what reveals what actually works — you can't analyze what you haven't created yet.

**When you're not sure if someone belongs in your life:** Ask one question — is my goal achievement more or less likely with this person in it? Neutral = they're costing you resources that compound nowhere. Apply the filter ruthlessly.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Pitching before establishing the gap.** If you haven't done C and L — clarified why they're there and labeled them with the problem — you're selling to someone who hasn't felt the problem yet. You will lose this sale and think the prospect wasn't interested.

**Monologuing during the pitch.** Most salespeople lose the sale during the vacation sell because they do a five-minute product dump. The three-pillar pitch is three analogies, not a feature list. Stop talking.

**Discounting to close.** Dropping price to get a "yes" is training your market that your price is fake. Test your price — hold it for the stated number of attempts — then adjust. Never mid-close.

**Chasing multiple income streams early.** "The average millionaire has seven income streams" describes what wealthy people do after they've built the primary asset — it does not describe how they built it. Diversification before focus destroys compounding.

**Confusing sequence.** Tall people play basketball — they didn't grow from playing. Don't model the behavior of successful people without understanding the order it happened in.

**Starting with paid ads before closing manually.** If you can't close a warm lead one-on-one, a paid ad is just burning money to send strangers to a broken sales process.

**Taking a partner with the same skill set.** Two acquisition people, two operators, two product people — the tripod falls over. A partner must bring the leg you're missing.

**Entering the valley of despair and pivoting.** This is the single biggest destroyer of compounding. The valley is the phase all businesses go through. The people who hit informed optimism are the ones who don't mistake temporary hell for permanent failure.

**Delivering without reinforcing the decision.** If you close someone and don't run the R (Reinforce) step with a clear BAMFAM and onboarding sequence, buyer's remorse will cancel you. The post-close 24–48 hours is where you keep or lose the customer.

**Unconscious competence that blocks delegation.** "No one can sell like I can sell" actually means "I haven't documented what I do well enough to teach it." The 3 Ds fix this. Your inability to transfer a skill is a deficit in you, not in them.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**"Here's the math."** Hormozi constantly reduces abstract claims to arithmetic. Establish a concrete number, then reverse-engineer it into weekly/daily actions. "25 clients at $10K over 4 years = $200/month = 2 closes per week = 2 out of 700 contacts." Make it real. Make it small. Make it undeniable.

**Analogy-first explanations.** When explaining anything difficult, he leads with a familiar parallel before naming the concept. "It's kind of like..." Never explain SEO by defining SEO. Explain SEO by explaining why a paycheck is different from a compound investment account. Audiences accept the concept because they already believed the analogy.

**Self-aware qualification then hard pivot.** He addresses the "who are you to say this" objection proactively, delivers credentials in one breath, then immediately returns to the point. Never dwell on credentials — mention them once and move on.

**Constraint naming.** Rather than prescribing best practices, he names the constraint that's stopping you. "You're not failing at sales — you just haven't separated what you do into teachable sub-skills." Every problem has a specific bottleneck. Name the bottleneck before prescribing the fix.

**"I'm not saying X — what I am saying is Y."** A recurring reframe pattern used to steelman a position he's about to challenge. Pre-empts objections, makes him sound fair, then lands the real point harder. Use when making a contrarian claim.

**Volume-to-principle compression.** He builds a long example (gum pricing, Tesla market entry, Bill Gates shares) and then compresses it into a one-line rule. The example earns the principle — the principle alone sounds generic. Always earn your heuristics with a concrete story first.

**Past pain as present leverage.** In sales scripts and in his own storytelling, he constantly returns to the cost of inaction — not the features of the solution. "What has not solving this cost you so far?" Deprivation creates motivation. Your pitch's job is to make the status quo feel more painful than the price.

**The rhetorical concession.** "Now you might be thinking..." followed by the strongest version of the counterargument, then a clean reframe. Never dismiss objections. Repeat them accurately, amplify them slightly, then dissolve them. This pattern appears in both sales scripts and content structure.
