## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. LTGP:CAC Ratio (The Only Business Model That Matters)**
The ratio of Lifetime Gross Profit to Cost of Acquiring a Customer is the engine of every business. If LTGP > CAC by a sufficient margin, you have an infinite marketing budget — you spend every dollar you can. Apply it: calculate last year's total marketing spend ÷ new customers = CAC; calculate (revenue per customer × gross margin) = LTGP. Then ask: is this ratio wide enough to absorb scaling inefficiencies?

**2. The Ratio Thresholds by Automation Level**
The required LTGP:CAC ratio scales with how many business functions (lead gen, conversion, delivery) require human labor. All three automated = 3:1 minimum. Two automated = 6:1. One automated = 9:1. None automated = 12:1+. Apply it: audit which of your three core functions have humans in every step — then set your target ratio accordingly before scaling.

**3. Model vs. Method**
A method is a tactic (hashtag hack, DM script, funnel tweak) that expires when the platform or market shifts. A model is the underlying economics — what you make per customer vs. what you spend to get them. Apply it: when a channel "stops working," diagnose whether it's a method problem (fix the ad creative) or a model problem (raise LTV or cut CAC structurally).

**4. The 30-Day Cash Recovery Rule**
Design your offer so that you recover customer acquisition cost within 30 days. This is the limit of interest-free credit and the cash flow window most bootstrapped businesses can survive. Apply it: map every revenue event (upfront payment, upsell at day 2, prepay at week 3) against your day-1 ad spend and close rate to confirm positive cash position within 30 days.

**5. The 3A Reframe Framework (Acknowledge → Associate → Ask)**
When a prospect says anything but yes, use three moves before attempting a close: (1) Acknowledge — restate what they said back to them, buying time and building rapport; (2) Associate — link their question or objection to the behavior of your most successful customers; (3) Ask — attack the frame of their question with a question of your own. Apply it: never answer a prospect's question directly until you've completed all three steps.

**6. Straw Man / Third-Party Foil**
When you need to deliver a hard truth that would insult the prospect if said directly, route it through a third party: "I had someone earlier today ask the exact same thing — can I share what I told them?" or appeal to a named successful past customer. This lets you be brutal about the belief without the prospect feeling attacked. Apply it: pre-build 3–5 straw man stories for your most common objections.

**7. SPCL Influence Stack (Status → Power → Credibility → Likeness)**
Influence (likelihood of compliance with a request) is built from four stacking elements: Status (you control scarce resources others want), Power (say-do correspondence — you gave directions, they followed, good things happened), Credibility (third-party-validated proof), Likeness (shared values, appearance, or vibe). Apply it: every piece of content should demonstrate at least one of the four; stack all four for maximum effect.

**8. Say-Do Correspondence (The Power Loop)**
Power compounds every time you give someone instructions, they follow them, and a reinforcing event occurs. This is why long-form content converts better than short — more instructions, more compliance cycles, more compounding influence. Apply it: give actionable directions in every video/post, then follow up with proof those directions produced results.

**9. Interest Media vs. Social Media**
The algorithm now targets by topic, not network. The content itself is the ad targeting. Making viral content attracts the wrong audience; making content for a specific avatar attracts that avatar — even if views are low. Apply it: define your exact avatar, make every piece of content for that person's specific problem, and measure success by IRL response quality (texts from ideal clients) not raw view count.

**10. Live Interactive as the Truth Gradient**
The internet moves toward rawer, more unscripted formats over time. Applause at soccer influencer events: A-listers (none) → short-form creators (slight) → long-form (legit) → live streamers (eruption). Live interaction maximizes likeness and reinforcement cycles simultaneously. Apply it: prioritize live formats for audience development; use polished short clips as top-of-funnel to drive live attendance.

**11. The Black Box Presale Model**
Spend on ads → generate leads → close at an offer that returns 5–10× ad spend within 30 days → reinvest proceeds to fund operations, equipment, and growth. No outside capital needed if the black box economics work. Apply it: before signing any lease or major commitment, run a presale that validates the black box returns positive cash in month one.

**12. LTV Optimization Menu**
Seven levers to increase LTGP without acquiring new customers: (1) raise price, (2) decrease delivery cost, (3) add upsells (more/better), (4) add downsells (capture otherwise-lost buyers), (5) add cross-sells (adjacent products), (6) introduce financing (pulls cash forward), (7) change payment terms (collect upfront, deliver later). Apply it: run every business through all seven levers quarterly and implement at least two.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect asks a certification/credential question | **Script:** "So you're curious about our certifications — that's a really rational question, shows you're taking this seriously. Which certifications specifically are you looking for?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it" | **Script:** "Totally understand. What are the main things you're considering? What are the variables going into the decision?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it" (deeper in the sale) | **Script:** "Totally understand. What would make this a no?"

**Situation:** Prospect says timing is off | **Script:** "Totally understand, timing is tough. I think it's actually smart you're already thinking about implementation — our biggest success stories are people thinking exactly like you are right now. So what would make it a good time?"

**Situation:** Prospect says they need to talk to their spouse | **Script:** "Totally understand, that's super fair and really sensible. Just so I understand — what parts do you think they wouldn't like? What do you think they'd disagree with?"

**Situation:** Prospect is stalling and you need to cut to the heart | **Script:** "Dude, what are you afraid of?"

**Situation:** Prospect pushes back on not getting their question answered | **Script:** "It would be like asking the secretary about your heart surgery instead of the surgeon. We'll give you much better answers once we've looked under the hood. And honestly, if anyone gives you an answer before doing that assessment — run, because it means they're just saying whatever it takes to get you to buy."

**Situation:** Prospect says "I hate this feature" | **Script:** "I hate them all too — just kidding. Let me ask you a real question: why?"

**Situation:** You want to introduce a hard truth without losing rapport | **Script:** "It's so funny you ask that — I had someone earlier today with the exact same question. Can I share what I told them?"

**Situation:** Prospect cites a competing credential/certification you don't have | **Script:** "Why those certifications specifically?" [After they answer:] "Those are amazing certifications, I agree. Let me tell you why we chose the ones we did instead."

**Situation:** Referencing a successful past customer to handle an objection | **Script:** "That actually makes you just like Sarah, who had a similar [business type] and absolutely crushed it with us. She asked this exact question too."

**Situation:** Appealing to authority to reframe a doubt | **Script:** "It's so funny you bring that up — Alex just told me something about this. Can I share it with you?"

**Situation:** Prospect has already stated what they want; you want to close | **Script:** "What would it take for you to say yes? What would it take for us to take the next step together?"

**Situation:** Beginner salesperson tempted to ask "Do you have any questions?" | **Script:** [Do not say this — ever. It hands them the wheel and invites objections. Ask a specific forward-moving question instead.]

**Situation:** Opening a video/content piece to establish status immediately | **Script:** "We got [specific metric] this month. Here is how." — Lead with observable proof, not claims.

**Situation:** Prospect appears nearly sold but weakly says "yeah" with no conviction | **Script:** "What are you most afraid of?"

**Situation:** You want to give the prospect a label they'll live up to | **Script:** "That's a really smart question." / "You must be a pretty rational decision-maker — that's exactly what the people who get the best results from us ask."

**Situation:** Bringing a previously given label back at close | **Script:** "You mentioned you're a family man and family's really important to you — I think that's admirable. And that's exactly why this decision makes sense."

**Situation:** Prospect says "I already have [competing solution]" | **Script:** "That's amazing — 90% of the clients who end up working with us already have [competing solution]. It means you already understand how these things work, so you're ahead of the learning curve."

**Situation:** Prospect won't stop asking questions and you can't answer them all credibly | **Script:** "I could give you an answer right now, but it would be like me telling you what's wrong with your car without looking under the hood first. It would be unethical. Let's get inside and I'll give you the real answer."

## DECISION RULES

**When** you're blaming a channel (Facebook, outbound, content) for not working → **do** first check whether you have a model problem, not a method problem. If CAC > LTGP, no channel will save you.

**When** your LTGP:CAC ratio is ≥ 10:1 → **do** spend every dollar you can on customer acquisition with no artificial budget ceiling.

**When** you have humans in all three functions (lead gen, sales, delivery) → **do** target a minimum 12:1 LTGP:CAC ratio before scaling headcount.

**When** CAC is rising → **do** accept it as inevitable (CPMs go up, markets get colder) and focus on raising LTGP rather than cutting CAC.

**When** a prospect asks any question → **do** ask a question about their question before answering. You are never obligated to answer directly.

**When** you feel the urge to answer a prospect's objection head-on → **do** run through Acknowledge → Associate → Ask first, every time, no exceptions.

**When** a prospect makes a statement that sounds like a deal-ender ("I hate this," "I don't have time") → **do** treat it as an invitation to ask more questions, not as a close signal.

**When** you need to deliver a harsh truth to a qualified prospect → **do** use a straw man (past customer, third party, authority figure) to carry the message instead of stating it yourself.

**When** measuring content performance → **do** prioritize IRL response quality (messages from ideal clients) over raw view count.

**When** choosing content format → **do** default to longer and more interactive (live, podcast) over shorter and more polished — reinforcement cycles per hour watched is what builds influence.

**When** your market is narrow (e.g., 9% of population) → **do** recalibrate view expectations accordingly and stop benchmarking against mass-entertainment creators.

**When** a prospect is clearly qualified and hesitating → **do** ask as many times as necessary; the ethical obligation runs toward helping them decide, not toward comfort with resistance.

**When** building a new offer structure → **do** map every dollar collected in the first 30 days against day-one ad spend. If you can't show positive cash in 30 days, redesign before launching.

**When** scaling a team (new sales reps, marketers, account managers) → **do** expect a temporary degradation of all metrics and ensure your LTGP:CAC ratio has enough cushion to survive the ramp period.

**When** tempted to optimize for cheaper leads → **do** instead optimize for higher LTV; the business that makes more per customer always outspends the business that pays less per lead.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Asking "Do you have any questions?"** — This hands control of the conversation to the prospect, invites objections, and signals you don't know what you're doing. Train it out of every rep.

**Answering prospect questions without reframing first** — Jumping directly to an answer makes the prospect judge, jury, and executioner of whether your answer is good. You will fail most of the time.

**Optimizing for cheap leads instead of high LTV** — Cheap leads attract low-quality buyers, tank conversion rates, and erode your competitive moat. It's the wrong variable to minimize.

**Competing on methods instead of model** — If a competitor can copy your tactic (DM script, hashtag, funnel), they will. The model (economics) is the only durable competitive advantage.

**Running a business where LTGP < CAC and blaming the channel** — Facebook, outbound, content — none of them work when the model is broken. Fixing the channel is rearranging deck chairs.

**Measuring content by views instead of avatar match** — A viral video that reaches the wrong audience is noise. A 1,000-view video watched entirely by ideal buyers is a pipeline asset.

**Winning an argument with a prospect** — If you logically defeat a prospect in conversation, you lose the sale. They disengage when they feel beaten, not when they feel wrong.

**Making only short-form content** — Shorts generate insufficient reinforcement cycles to build real influence or move a qualified buyer to a purchase decision. Shorts are a top-of-funnel filter, not a conversion tool.

**Scaling without enough LTGP:CAC cushion** — Growing headcount while at a 3:1 ratio means any staffing inefficiency (new reps ramping, new marketers learning) will push you into unprofitability instantly.

**Trying to act like someone you're not in content** — Zero ROI on performed personas. The audience senses inauthenticity and the likeness element of influence collapses. Lean into what's genuinely you.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Proof-first openings.** Every video, presentation, or sales conversation opens with a specific, observable proof statement before any claim or instruction: "We did $32.7 million views this month. Here is how." Never lead with theory — lead with what you can see with your eyes.

**Operational definitions before emotional language.** Before using a concept, define it in observable terms: "Status is someone who controls reinforcers in a given environment." Avoid vague motivational framing — describe what you would literally see if the thing were present.

**The Rule-of-Three escalation.** Build arguments in tiers: give the principle → give a numbered breakdown → stress-test it with a counterexample or edge case. "Now, many people say 3:1. That's only true under three conditions. Here's what actually happens when one is missing…"

**Metaphor-to-mechanism.** State the vivid metaphor first, then immediately translate it to mechanics: "Be like smoke — you can't catch smoke. What that means in practice is you're always answering their question with a question, always sidestepping, always in motion."

**Validate-then-redirect.** Every prospect response gets acknowledged before being redirected: "Totally understand." / "That makes complete sense." / "That's a really smart question." — then immediately pivot to a question that moves back toward the sale. Never skip the validation step.

**Volume as proof of conviction.** Reference specific large numbers frequently and unpretentiously as anchors of credibility: "35,000 pieces of content." "$250 million in aggregate revenue." "4,000+ sales closes." These numbers are dropped as assumed context, not as brags — they establish the scale at which the principle has been validated.

**Constraint-based logic.** Frame decisions as math problems with real constraints, not as inspiration: "You have to know these two numbers. If number one is not greater than number two, everything else is noise. Here's the third-grade math." This makes complex business strategy feel immediately executable.
