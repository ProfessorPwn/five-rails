## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Iron Man Suit Upgrade**
Your business starts as a suit that enhances you — it still requires you to run it. The goal is to upgrade it so it fights without you. A business that needs you to operate has zero transferable value; a business that operates without you trades at a multiple. Build the suit that runs itself.

*Apply it:* Ask every quarter — "Does this burn down without me?" If yes, you're still wearing the suit. If no, you own an asset.

---

**The Frustrated Fred vs. Wealthy William Framework**
Two businesses, same $2M profit. Fred works 80 hours/week and saves $500K/year. William owns it like a stock — his business trades at 6x profit, making him $12M in net worth. When Fred adds $500K profit he gets $250K after tax. When William adds $500K profit he adds $3M to his net worth. The delta is roughly 20x. Regular income is taxed to oblivion. Enterprise value is not.

*Apply it:* Every operational decision should be evaluated through this lens — does this make the business more ownable and more saleable, or just more profitable today?

---

**Red/Yellow/Green Self-Inventory**
List every single thing you do. Tag each: Green = delegate now to someone on your team. Yellow = build a one-time process, then hand off. Red = you need a person you don't have yet. Solve in green → yellow → red order. Don't try to fix the reds before you've cleared the greens.

*Apply it:* Do a 15-minute time study for one week. Set a timer, log what you're doing every 15 minutes. Run this on any key-man risk — not just yourself.

---

**If-This-Then-That Decision Trees**
Every repeatable decision in your business should be encoded as a rule, not escalated to you. Common inputs (cancel, refund, upgrade, complaint) should have pre-built outputs. The more duplicatable the role, the more standardized the decision tree.

*Apply it:* Set dollar thresholds for autonomous action (e.g., under $500 the team acts; above $500 they escalate). Review monthly financials to catch anomalies — but don't be in the decision loop daily.

---

**The Shadow-Train-Release Model**
Three phases of handing off any task: (1) They watch you do it. (2) You watch them do it. (3) You're available but not involved. Ad-hoc calls taper off as competence builds. Full handoff happens when they own the decision — not just the task.

*Apply it:* Accept 60–80% quality at handoff if you have a clear trajectory of improvement. The employee who learns fastest beats the employee who starts highest.

---

**The One-Time vs. Consumable Split**
Every deliverable you sell falls into one of two columns. Column A: one-time value — once they learn it, you're done delivering value. Column B: consumables — things they must use again and again (fresh ad creatives, curated lists, live community, recurring data). Continuity revenue only comes from Column B. Most education businesses only sell Column A and then wonder why people churn.

*Apply it:* For each product, ask: "If I only sold this ongoing piece as a standalone, what would people pay without the upfront course?" That number is your true continuity price.

---

**The Big Head / Long Tail Pricing Model**
Charge the maximum the market will bear for the one-time transformational value upfront. Charge only what the consumable is worth on its own for the ongoing piece. Most people do the opposite — they spread the upfront cost across 12 months and call it continuity. The result: people exit once the one-time value is extracted because the monthly fee is no longer justified.

*Apply it:* If something is worth $30K once, don't charge $2,500/month for 12 months. Charge $20K upfront, $200/month forever. Price continuity at what it's worth standalone.

---

**The Harvard Standards Model**
Harvard has three properties that most education businesses lack: (1) Not everyone gets in — standards create scarcity and brand. (2) No promises made — no ROI guarantees, just access. (3) Not everyone passes — outcome filtering is a feature, not a bug. The brand is ultimately a curated network of smart people with a logo on top.

*Apply it:* If you accept everyone with a credit card, you can't build a brand around prestige. Selectivity is the product at the high end.

---

**The Permanent Customer Metric**
Stop optimizing for average LTV. Find your permanent CAC — how much does it cost to acquire one customer who never leaves? If 10 people buy and 3 convert to your service and 1 of those 3 never leaves, your real CAC for a permanent customer is 10x your front-end CAC. That ratio tells you whether the business model actually works.

*Apply it:* Run cohort analysis at M12. If your M12 cohort has near-zero churn, you have a business. If they're still bleeding out, you haven't found product-market fit yet.

---

**The Brand Equation Framework (for Ads)**
Great brand advertising works by building a clean association: Brand = X. Then: X = things your avatar already loves. Structure: flood them with those loved things visually, let the brand absorb the positive sentiment. Jeep did it clearly: Jeep = Freedom. Freedom = military, family, open roads, ruggedness. Notice they didn't mention APR.

*Apply it:* Before running any brand ad, write out: "[Brand] = [Core Identity]." Then list 10 specific things your avatar loves that map to that identity. If you can't complete the list, your brand positioning isn't clear enough to advertise.

---

**The Attention → Care → Dream → Proof → Urgency → CTA Scoring Rubric**
Every ad can be scored on six axes: (1) Did it grab attention? (2) Did it make you care? (3) Did it sell the dream outcome? (4) Did it create urgency? (5) Did it show social proof? (6) Did it tell you what to do next? Weak ads usually fail on 3–5. Great ads hit all six and weave in product demonstration throughout — not just at the end.

*Apply it:* Score your current best-performing ad on all six dimensions. If any scores below a 7/10, that's your highest-leverage edit.

---

**The Consumable Business Extraction Play**
If you run an education business, look inside your delivery for anything that requires fresh inputs monthly — curated lists, tested ad creatives, scouted deal flow, live community. That recurring work already exists in your business. Package and sell it separately as the continuity product. The education unlocks the tool; the consumable makes the tool useful month after month.

*Apply it:* Ask: "What do I already do every month that my best customers would pay for on its own?" That's your continuity product hiding in plain sight.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Someone says they can't afford a high-ticket service | **Script:** "You're not buying a course. You're paying today to increase your future earnings. That's not an expense — it's the cheapest way to compress a decade of mistakes into 90 days."

**Situation:** Prospect hesitates on ongoing monthly fee | **Script:** "Forget everything else I've told you. If this one piece — [specific consumable] — was all you got, would it be worth $X a month on its own? If yes, the rest is free."

**Situation:** Owner says "no one can do it like me" | **Script:** "That's not a competitive advantage. That's a prison sentence. You're not building a business — you're building a job you can't quit."

**Situation:** Employee doesn't know how they make the company money | **Script:** "Walk me through this: how does what you do specifically turn into revenue for us? Not the department — you, personally, in your exact role."

**Situation:** Justifying premium pricing for education | **Script:** "Harvard doesn't promise you a job. They don't refund you if you don't get hired. They charge more than anyone and they turn people away. That's the model."

**Situation:** Selling continuity after one-time product delivery | **Script:** "You've got the skill now. What you don't have is what changes every month — [fresh data/curated list/tested creative]. That's what the membership is. Without it, the skill goes stale."

**Situation:** Client churning after learning the one-time material | **Script:** "The education you paid for worked — you learned it. Now the question is: are you going to stay ahead of what changes every month? That's what this next level is for."

**Situation:** Pitching a brand-based ad to skeptical client | **Script:** "You're not selling the product. You're selling the identity. The customer buys the Nike shoe to feel like a winner — the shoe is just the delivery mechanism for that feeling."

**Situation:** Explaining why someone should do a time study | **Script:** "It'll be the most productive week of your life. Set a timer. Every 15 minutes, write one word. That's it. You'll realize what you do and what you should stop doing — simultaneously."

**Situation:** Evaluating a new hire for a leadership role | **Script:** "If I'm not learning from this person in the interview, I'm going to have to teach them on the job. That means my life gets worse before it gets better. Pass."

**Situation:** Convincing a founder to delegate sales | **Script:** "If your face is what gets the customers, you are the key man risk. The moment you get sick, go on vacation, or want to sell — the revenue walks out with you."

**Situation:** Defending a lower continuity price | **Script:** "What would someone pay for just this piece — no course, no coaching, nothing else? That number. That's your continuity price. Everything else is just justifying a payment plan."

**Situation:** HR-style interview for key roles | **Script:** "What's your cost to acquire talent? What's your time to fill? What percentage of 90-day hires get rated 10/10 by both the manager and the employee? If you don't know these numbers, you don't actually manage hiring."

**Situation:** Evaluating ad creative | **Script:** "One test: did I have to rewatch it to understand what they're selling? If yes, it failed. The best ads show the problem, solve the problem with the product, and tell you what to do — in that order."

**Situation:** Debating whether to add more features to stop churn | **Script:** "Every feature in your stack should independently justify three to four times the price. If someone can't say 'it's worth it for this one thing alone,' cut it. Less is usually the fix, not more."

**Situation:** Defending a low-cash-flow software investment | **Script:** "It's not the code that makes software valuable. It's revenue retention, gross margin, and less operational drag at scale. If your customers churn every six months, you have a service — not a software business."

**Situation:** Crowdsourcing ad creative | **Script:** "Run a contest. Let your customers make the ad. They know your product more intimately than any agency. They love it. They'll show it. And it costs you a fraction of what an agency charges for something worse."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether to open a second location | **Script:** "Simple test: leave for three months. Don't answer your phone. Come back. Is it bigger, same, or smaller? If it's smaller, you don't have a business that can scale — you have one location that still needs you."

**Situation:** Explaining enterprise value to a founder | **Script:** "You can take home $500K a year and add that to your net worth. Or you can own a business worth $12 million. One of those gets you a wire transfer for your decade of work. The other just gets taxed."

**Situation:** Someone wants to add software to their education business to make it sellable | **Script:** "I have yet to see that work. Not once. You'd be splitting your attention between two businesses, your lifestyle is already adjusted to the high cash flow, and you'll never want to cut it to fund the other. No half measures — or don't do it."

## DECISION RULES

- When a task is repeatable, **encode it as a decision tree** before hiring for it — the tree is the job description.
- When evaluating two candidates, **pick the faster learner over the more experienced one** — intelligence is rate of learning.
- When your business still needs you to generate customers, **you cannot leave and you cannot sell** — fix acquisition first.
- When adding features to stop churn in an education business, **check whether you have consumables or just one-time value** — more features won't fix a structural pricing mismatch.
- When setting continuity pricing, **price it at what it would sell for standalone** — not as a payment plan for the upfront value.
- When interviewing for a leadership role, **if you aren't learning from them in the interview, pass** — they'll cost you time, not save it.
- When you can't leave your business for 90 days without revenue dropping, **don't open a second location** — you're doubling liability on a broken model.
- When someone asks why customers are churning from an education product, **first ask whether the deliverable is one-time or consumable** — most churn is a product structure problem, not a retention tactics problem.
- When building a brand ad, **write the association equation first** — Brand = X, X = things they love — before writing a single line of copy.
- When launching a new ad, **let the data pick the winner** — run multiple variants and suppress your opinion; the market votes with clicks, not feelings.
- When hiring for any role, **ask how their specific function generates revenue** — if they can't answer, they won't know what winning looks like.
- When building systems to replace yourself, **clear greens before attacking reds** — quick wins build momentum and free up capacity to tackle harder replacements.
- When evaluating business value, **the key question is: does it grow when you're absent?** — flat without you means the value is in you, not the company.
- When someone pitches you a low-grade ad creative, **score it on all six axes** (attention, care, dream, proof, urgency, CTA) — weak ads almost always fail the same two or three every time.
- When adding a software layer to an existing business, **verify it's a core function you're fully committed to** — a distracted half-build destroys both the cash cow and the new thing.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**"No one can do it like me."** This is not a strength — it's a single point of failure. It's also almost never true after sufficient training and reps. You're confusing your head start with a permanent advantage.

**Pricing continuity as a payment plan for the one-time value.** If your $30K course is being sold as $2,500/month for 12 months, you've created the illusion of continuity. People will churn the moment they've extracted the one-time value — usually before month 12.

**Adding features to solve churn.** More calls, more libraries, more Q&As — none of this fixes structural churn if the underlying product is one-time value dressed up as recurring. Solve the model, not the feature set.

**Trying to build software on top of an education business to create enterprise value.** It's a famous last-words move. You end up with two mediocre businesses, a lifestyle adjusted to education cash flow, and no ability to fund the pivot. No half measures or don't do it at all.

**Making your ads about you instead of your customer.** The On/Federer ad was a C because they spent the whole time on their brand name. The GoDaddy ad was a C because the audience didn't know where it was going for 70% of its runtime. No one cares who you are — they care what you solve for them.

**Requiring customers to rewatch an ad to understand it.** If it needs a second watch to be understood, it won't get one. You have half a second — lead with the pain or the dream, not with a clever setup that resolves too late.

**Leading with your brand before establishing relevance.** Booking.com opened with their logo before anyone cared. Establish the problem, establish the person, then reveal the solution and who you are.

**Fear-based messaging without showing the dream outcome.** The Hims & Hers ad showed overweight people but didn't show what they wanted to be. Showing the problem without the aspiration just makes people feel bad — they don't convert, they disengage.

**Measuring employee quality by starting performance instead of improvement rate.** An employee who starts strong and plateaus will be lapped within months by someone who starts slower and improves faster. You want the learning rate, not the first-day ceiling.

**Building a business where the owner is also the marketer, the closer, and the face.** This creates triple key-man risk. The day you stop promoting is the day revenue starts falling. That's not a business — that's personal income with extra steps.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The math setup → reveal → implication loop.** Hormozi frames everything numerically before making a point. He sets up two scenarios ("Frustrated Fred vs. Wealthy William"), runs the math side by side, and then extracts the principle. The math isn't decoration — it's the argument. Use it: "Let's say X and Y. Now here's where it gets interesting."

**"Now here's where it gets [extra / really / super] [X]."** He telegraphs the most important point in a sentence with a marker word — "nasty," "sexy," "crazy." This is a verbal highlighter. It tells you the next sentence is the actual point.

**Repeating the same phrase with escalating specificity.** He'll say the same idea three times: abstract → concrete → example. "You want to replace yourself. You want to get to a point where you can leave. Specifically, take 90 days off and come back to a business that grew without you."

**The "I'll give you an example" pivot.** Every abstract claim gets anchored to a story immediately. The story is short, usually about himself or a specific person he knows. It ends with the principle restated: "And so fundamentally, that's what it taught me."

**Rhetorical questions followed by immediate self-answers.** "Which business do you want? Well, you need more information. Now what if I told you X?" He asks, pauses rhetorically, answers himself. Never waits. This keeps pace and signals confidence.

**Self-interruption to add nuance, then return.** He'll make a claim, interrupt himself ("different than choosing to work, as a side note"), then return to the original thread. This creates conversational density — it sounds like he's thinking in real time and catching himself being imprecise.

**The callback close.** He ends sections by looping back to the original frame ("So this is why frustrated Fred and wealthy William..."). It creates a sense of completeness and makes complex material feel resolved.
