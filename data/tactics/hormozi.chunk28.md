## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Value Equation / Four Value Vectors**
Every product or service improvement maps to four levers: increase the dream outcome, increase perceived likelihood of achievement, decrease time to result, decrease effort and sacrifice. When optimizing anything — a menu, an offer, an onboarding — run through all four. The one that moves the needle depends on the customer's biggest friction point right now.

**Menu Architecture (Profit-Ranked, Anchored, Highlighted)**
Order items within each category from highest gross-profit margin to lowest — that's the first thing customers read. Anchor with your highest price at the top so everything beneath feels reasonable. Add a "can't miss" highlight box at the bottom of each section for first-timers who don't want to make a decision. Apply this to any menu: services, SaaS pricing pages, mechanic shops. The menu is the silent salesperson.

**Nail It Then Scale It**
Solidify unit economics before adding locations or headcount. Know your cost to open, time to profitability, payback period, grand opening playbook, and hiring/culture system before you duplicate. Skipping this saves time on location two but costs you location ten. Every expansion multiplies your current model — good or broken.

**Skit-Based Training (Condition → Behavior Rules)**
Training works when you reduce it to: "When X happens, do Y." Turn each behavior into a skit. Staff run 20 reps per session on one skit before moving to the next. Do it daily in the 20-minute pre-shift window. The script becomes automatic. Works for any customer-facing role: sales, servers, support, SDRs.

**Peak-End Bias**
Customers don't remember the average of an experience — they remember the single highest emotional peak and how it ended. Engineer both deliberately. A surprise free dessert at the end of a first visit costs almost nothing but hijacks memory encoding. Apply to any service: the last touchpoint in an onboarding, the close of a sales call, the delivery of a project.

**Cross-Sell Architecture (The 8 Ways to Increase Customer Value)**
There are eight ways to make a customer more valuable; the cross-sell ("Do you want fries with that?") is one. Structure it as a two-question follow-on after every order: suggest a side, then suggest a drink pairing. Every question asked with one item remaining on the table is a dollar sitting there unclaimed. Train staff to see each follow-on question as direct tip income.

**AOV Breakpoint / Free Gift Threshold**
Set a free-gift threshold just above your current average order value. If average ticket is $60, set the threshold at $75-80. The customer is already most of the way there — the marginal spend to unlock the reward is small. For takeout and e-commerce, this is painless to execute. In dining, use it at checkout or on the order page, not via verbal upsell.

**Affiliate / Influencer Pre-Launch**
An affiliate is a lead-getter: an independent business or person who tells their audience to buy your stuff. For a new location launch, host a VIP influencer night pre-opening. Trade the experience (at cost or free) for content rights and social posts. Run their content as paid ads post-launch — people recognize familiar faces talking about your brand. Prioritize people whose normal business involves foot traffic or an audience of your target customer.

**Running Starts (Loyalty Program Psychology)**
If you run a punch card, pre-stamp 2-3 punches on a 10-punch card. Studies show customers with a running start are 2-3x more likely to complete the card than customers with the same remaining punches on a shorter card. It signals progress already made, activating loss aversion. Always give a running start if you run any completion-based loyalty mechanic.

**Immediate vs. Delayed Incentives**
Incentives only work when delivered immediately at the moment of the desired action. "Leave a review and get a free drink on your next visit" — nobody cares. "Leave a review right now, I'll take the drink off your check before I run it" — that works. The payoff must be at the point of sale. Delayed rewards have near-zero compliance.

**The Manager Card (Personalized vs. Coupon)**
A manager handing someone their business card with "free dessert" written on the back feels like a personalized gift, not a discount. The identical economic offer as a printed coupon feels transactional and cheap. Presentation is part of the product. When you need to give something away to drive a behavior, make it feel personal and scarce, not mass-printed.

**Leaderboard Culture for Upsells**
Post a back-of-house leaderboard showing which server got the most upsells (alcohol, add-ons, etc.) for the week. Give the winner schedule priority or a $50-100 bonus. This creates intrinsic motivation for staff to internalize the training — they have a financial stake in executing the upsell script. Works in any sales-adjacent team.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Server greeting a first-time customer | **Script:** "Is this your first time dining with us? [Yes.] Amazing — we want to make sure you have the best experience. Before we get into the menu, can I tell you about our drink special for the week?"

**Situation:** Introducing the rotating drink special | **Script:** "So every week, the whole team gets together in the back and we taste-test five different cocktails. The one we love the most is what we make for the week — it's freshly made here. This week it's [name], with freshly squeezed [ingredient]. A lot of people love it. Would you like one of those?"

**Situation:** Upselling a side or alcohol after a main order | **Script:** "Awesome choice. A lot of people actually do it with [side/drink] — it pairs really well. Do you want to go ahead and take one of those?"

**Situation:** Asking for a review at the point of sale — version 1 (exchange) | **Script:** "Hey, if you guys are willing to leave us a review, I can take care of one of the drinks. It would mean the world to us — we're a small family-owned business. If you're down, I'll take the check back, pull it off, and bring it right back."

**Situation:** Asking for a review at the point of sale — version 2 (manager ask, no freebie) | **Script:** "Hey, just so you know — your server tonight was [Name], and she gets a small bonus whenever she hits 10 five-star reviews in a month. If she took good care of you tonight, it would mean a lot."

**Situation:** Delivering comeback offer to a first-timer | **Script:** "Since it's your first time, we want to take care of you. [Hands manager card with free dessert written on back.] Next time you come in, just bring this and I'll have another [dessert] waiting for you — on the house."

**Situation:** Handling the review card / digital backup concern | **Script:** "If you're worried about losing it, just snap a picture of the back — if you show it to us on your phone when you come back, we'll honor it."

**Situation:** Table tent descriptor for the weekly drink special | **Script:** "Once a week our entire team tries five different cocktails. The one we agree on is what we make that week — freshly prepared in-house. [Visual image of the drink.] Ask your server about this week's selection."

**Situation:** Pairing suggestion on the menu (written copy) | **Script:** "Pairs well with: [drink name]. Ask your server."

**Situation:** Communicating AOV threshold to takeout customers | **Script:** "Just so you know — for orders over $80, we'll add a complimentary [item] to your order. You're at $62 right now, so you're close if you wanted to add anything."

**Situation:** Pitching priority access as a loyalty program benefit | **Script:** "Members get priority on our weekend waitlist — when you're a member, you skip ahead of the general queue."

**Situation:** Recruiting influencers for a pre-opening VIP night | **Script:** "We'd love to have you come experience the restaurant before we open to the public — this is a private VIP night for people we want to share this with. We'd love it if you felt comfortable sharing what you think. We'll handle the food and experience."

**Situation:** Encouraging multiple reviews from a single table | **Script:** "If each of you is willing to leave a review, I can actually take care of all your drinks. We're trying to build our reviews before the grand opening of our second location — it would mean everything to us."

**Situation:** Staff briefing on the upsell value per question | **Script:** "Every alcoholic beverage you sell, you make about a dollar in tip on top of everything else. Table of five, two drinks each — that's $10 more in your pocket just from two questions per person."

**Situation:** Introducing weekend vs. weekday pricing | **Script:** "Our weekend menu is our full dining experience — it's how we're able to keep the quality where it needs to be on our busiest nights."

## DECISION RULES

- When your product is genuinely excellent, prioritize word-of-mouth mechanics over paid acquisition — marketing accelerates a good product but accelerates the decline of a bad one.
- When considering a pricing change, model it as a percentage of profit, not revenue — a 6% price increase on a 19% margin business is a 30%+ profit lift.
- When choosing between a percentage discount and a free item, always give the free item — discounts train customers to wait for deals; free items create delight without anchoring a lower price.
- When a customer is at point of sale, capture the review exchange then, not later — delayed review requests have near-zero conversion.
- When you have excess demand (weekends booked out), raise prices on those days — surge pricing is standard practice and customers expect it.
- When training staff on a behavior, reduce it to a single "when this, do that" rule, run skits for 20 reps, and add only one new behavior per session.
- When you have a high-margin product underperforming (like alcohol at 1% of sales), treat it as a training and systems problem before assuming demand doesn't exist.
- When designing a loyalty program, lead with operational benefits (priority seating, waitlist skip) over discounts — operational perks cost you nothing and are perceived as high value.
- When launching a new location, host a pre-opening influencer night and secure content rights as part of the deal — you get UGC you can run as paid ads.
- When ordering menu items within a category, rank by gross profit margin from top to bottom — customers read top-first, and the first item anchors consideration.
- When adding a cross-sell, phrase it with social proof and forward momentum: "A lot of people do" and "do you want to go ahead and" both lower resistance without pressure.
- When evaluating whether to scale, ask: do I know my cost to open, time to profitability, payback period, and hiring/culture system? If not, nail the model first.
- When setting an AOV threshold incentive, place it 20-30% above current average ticket — close enough to feel reachable, far enough to pull them up.
- When running a loyalty punch card, pre-stamp 2-3 punches as a running start — completion rates are 2-3x higher than cards with the same remaining punches starting from zero.
- When a first-timer ends their meal, trigger peak-end bias: surprise free dessert + personalized manager card = the experience they'll describe to their friends.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

- **Running percentage-off promotions.** Discounts train customers to become discount-seekers. They select for the worst customers and erode price anchoring. Run free items instead, with a clear path back to full price.
- **Scaling before the unit economics are solid.** Expanding to location two before you know your cost to open, time to profitability, payback period, and culture system means you're duplicating a broken model at speed.
- **Putting incentives on the next visit instead of right now.** "Leave a review and get a free drink next time" has near-zero compliance. If the payoff isn't immediate, the behavior won't happen.
- **Treating alcohol (or any high-margin item) as a passive menu line.** If you're at 1% of sales on your highest-margin product category, it's not a demand problem — it's a systems and training failure.
- **Training by lecture instead of by skit.** Telling staff what to do produces zero retention. Drilling "when X, do Y" behaviors in role-play reps until they're automatic is the only training that transfers.
- **Having no way to identify first-time customers.** If you treat everyone the same, you can't engineer the experience that creates a second visit. Identification is the prerequisite for everything else.
- **Leaving money at a static price when you're turning people away.** If there's a line out the door on weekends and you're not using differential pricing, you're subsidizing peak demand with off-peak pricing.
- **Designing for too many things at once on a table tent or highlight.** Ten featured items is just another menu. One item, one image, one descriptor — that's what moves someone from browsing to deciding.
- **Optimizing for marketing when the product isn't yet excellent.** Marketing a mediocre product accelerates churn and wastes acquisition spend. Fix the product first, then amplify.
- **Ignoring review volume in favor of star rating alone.** A 4.7 with 13,000 reviews dominates a 5.0 with 50. Volume signals legitimacy and destination status. A review-generation system is not optional for local businesses.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**"Simple math, big number."** Hormozi constantly translates small unit-level changes into annualized dollar impact. Pattern: "If every customer paid $2 more, and you have 100,000 customers — that's $200,000 dropped to the bottom line." Forces the listener to feel the stakes of a seemingly trivial decision.

**Lead with the insight, then build the proof.** He states the conclusion first ("This one thing could add 30% to your profit"), then works backward through the math. Listeners don't have to wait to understand why they should care — they're already invested before the explanation starts.

**Constraint-as-opportunity framing.** Every weakness he finds is immediately reframed as an untapped lever: 1% alcohol sales isn't a problem, it's a 5% revenue opportunity waiting to be unlocked. He never dwells on the gap — he immediately pivots to what capturing it looks like in dollar terms.

**Social proof in the upsell phrase.** Every cross-sell script includes "a lot of people do" or "most people like." This removes the customer's sense of being sold to — they're simply being told what the normal behavior is. The ask follows naturally.

**Enumerate then prioritize.** He lists options (3-4 pricing moves, 2 review scripts), gets a reaction, then cuts to the one or two that he actually thinks will move the needle. This respects autonomy while steering toward the high-leverage action.

**Translate staff incentives into personal economics.** He doesn't say "upsell more alcohol to hit our revenue targets." He says "every question you ask is a dollar sitting on that table — five people, two questions each, that's $10 in your pocket tonight." Aligns individual behavior with business outcome through self-interest.

**"I'm going to give you two or three options, you tell me which one feels right."** He consistently offers choices rather than mandates. This reduces resistance, gets buy-in on implementation, and respects that the owner knows their customer better than he does. But the options he offers are all pre-filtered to be good — he's not giving them a bad choice.

**Volume and repetition as proof of quality.** He uses review count (not just star rating), customer count, and years in business as the primary signals that a business has solved its core product problem. Metrics of volume signal legitimacy more reliably than subjective quality claims.
