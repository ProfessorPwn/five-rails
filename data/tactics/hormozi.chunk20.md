## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Three-Part Ad Structure**
Every ad has a Hook (call-out), Meat (value), and CTA. The hook gets the right person to say "this is for me," the meat makes benefits look as big as possible and costs as small as possible, and the CTA drives immediate action. Never skip or conflate these three layers.

**The Reverse Value Equation**
Dream outcome, perceived likelihood of achievement, time to result, and effort/sacrifice — mapped against their inverses (nightmare scenarios). In the ad, you're always either amplifying the good side (fast, easy, risk-free) or shrinking the bad side (slow, hard, risky). Run every claim through these buckets before writing copy.

**Levels of Awareness (Eugene Schwartz)**
Traffic is unaware → problem aware → solution aware → product aware → most aware. Your hook must match where the audience lives on this pyramid. Cold traffic needs curiosity-based hooks with no mention of the solution. Most-aware audiences just need the offer restated clearly. Mismatch here kills conversion.

**Us Versus Them (Underdog Positioning)**
Best used when you are not the market leader. You, the challenger, frame the incumbent as indifferent or predatory ("they hate their customers") and yourself as the antidote. If you *are* the market leader, flip it: you versus the alternative (inaction, the couch, the status quo). Never use this framework to brand yourself alongside something disgusting — associations form whether you want them to or not.

**The Don't Sell**
Beyond plus/minus on your own offer, sell against the cost of *not* buying. "If you don't do this, you'll keep having bad stuff and keep not having good stuff." Run it across all three vectors — risk, speed, ease — in both directions. Four email angles emerge: more good stuff, less bad stuff, keep losing good stuff, keep gaining bad stuff.

**The Dummy Offer / Three-Tier Pricing**
On a three-tier pricing page, the middle option is a positioning lever, not a revenue driver. Move the middle closer to the top to push buyers to the top tier; move it closer to the bottom to push buyers to the middle. Where you place the middle tells a smart observer exactly which tier you're trying to sell.

**Warm Audience Shortcut**
Once an audience already knows and trusts you (brand, followers, retargeting pool), drop the elaborate hook and just make the offer. The effort-to-awareness framework inverts: cold traffic needs curiosity, warm traffic needs clarity. Every influencer with 50K followers has a "most aware" audience that responds to direct offers.

**Same Offer, New Wrapper**
Once you have a winning offer, never retire it — rename it. Holiday Promo, Super Bowl Special, Spring Sale, Back-to-School Deal. The *offer* stays identical; the *reason* changes. The reason creates novelty and urgency without requiring you to rebuild what's already converting.

**UGC as a Creative Machine**
User-generated content is leverage: customers generate 80–90% of creative, you generate 10–20%. The goal is to build the advertising machine *inside* the business so you're not dependent on outbound creative production. UGC also reads as organic, which lowers ad resistance.

**Buy X Get Y Offer Structures**
A "buy more than you pay for" structure outperforms mirror offers. Buy 1 get 2 converts better than buy 3 get 3 because the *perceived discrepancy* between what's paid and what's received is higher. Always test whether you can flip the math in the customer's favor and still hit your economics.

**FAQ as Conversion Stack**
Every FAQ on a sales page represents a segment of customers who have an unresolved concern. Each answered question is worth a few percentage points of conversion. Weave the most common objections into the page body; stack the niche or awkward ones in an FAQ at the bottom so you don't lead with doubt but still resolve it.

**Micro-Step Nurture Sequencing**
In email follow-up, handle one objection per email in order of prevalence — most common concern first. Don't consolidate 13 objections into one email. Instead, get prospects to take micro-steps (check ZIP coverage, enter device info) that advance consideration before purchase.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening an ad to a warm/known audience | **Script:** "Hey, it's [Name], owner of [Brand] — [single-sentence offer]."

**Situation:** Writing an Us vs. Them hook for a challenger brand | **Script:** "At [Brand] we like to do the opposite of what [Big Competitor] does. They charge you a lot. We charge you a little."

**Situation:** Making a price feel trivial | **Script:** "That's two coffees a month. That's $45 until the end of winter — less than a pack of gum a week."

**Situation:** Restating an offer multiple ways in the same ad | **Script:** "It's $15 a month for the first three months — that's $45 total. You could go the rest of the season paying next to nothing without compromising on quality."

**Situation:** Injecting urgency without false scarcity | **Script:** "This deal ends soon. Don't wait — grab it while it's still here." (Keep 'soon' subjective for compliance flexibility.)

**Situation:** Overcoming the quality fear for a budget product | **Script:** "I know what you're thinking — cheap means bad service. [Testimonial or data point disproving it]. Reliable. Fast. No surprises."

**Situation:** Giving a reason-why for a recurring promotion | **Script:** "It's my dog's birthday. It's my 37th week in business. It's back-to-school season. [Ridiculous reason] — so we're running [offer] for the next [X] days."

**Situation:** Writing a hook for cold/unaware traffic | **Script:** "[Location/identity callout] are getting [unexpected result] — here's why." (No product mention. Pure curiosity.)

**Situation:** Writing a UGC-style honest-review hook | **Script:** "Here's my honest review of [Product]. I didn't expect to switch. Here's what changed my mind."

**Situation:** PSA-format hook for organic-native ads | **Script:** "PSA: If you're still paying [X amount] for [thing], you're leaving money on the table. Here's what I switched to."

**Situation:** Resolving the fear of commitment on a sales page | **Script:** "Not ready to commit? Don't worry — once your [trial period] is up, you can renew, upgrade, or walk. No traps."

**Situation:** Showing how to cancel to reduce purchase risk | **Script:** "Canceling is [X steps]. Takes 2 minutes. We'd rather earn your business every month than lock you in."

**Situation:** Email sequence opener for non-converters | **Script:** "You checked us out but didn't pull the trigger. Here's the one thing I think you might have missed: [single objection addressed]."

**Situation:** Selling against inaction ("the don't") | **Script:** "You could keep paying [incumbent price], dealing with [bad thing], and not getting [good thing]. Or you could be using [Product] by tomorrow."

**Situation:** Handling the coverage/quality objection in ad copy | **Script:** "Worried about coverage? Check your ZIP — most customers in [area] get excellent signal. [Show proof or tool]."

## DECISION RULES

- When you are the market leader, use "us vs. the alternative" (inaction, old way), never "us vs. a named competitor."
- When an ad has been running unchanged for 12+ months, treat it as a proven winner and protect it — don't kill it for freshness.
- When targeting retargeting audiences, keep creative constant and just restate the offer — the audience refreshes itself, not the creative.
- When targeting cold/top-of-funnel audiences, rotate creative frequently — everyone sees it, so fatigue hits faster.
- When writing a three-tier pricing page, decide first which tier you want to sell most, then price the middle tier to make that tier look like the obvious choice.
- When an ad uses an analogy or comparison (e.g., chunky milk = bad wireless), ensure the branded product appears *first* in the sequence, not the negative comparator — you brand toward what's shown first.
- When you have one CTA button on a page, add a second. When you have two, consider a third. Stop before the page feels like a used car lot.
- When writing email nurture sequences, address the most prevalent objection first, then descend in order of frequency — never batch objections together.
- When running the same offer under a new promotional name, the reason for the promotion needs only to be *plausible*, not exceptional.
- When looking for which ads to study from a competitor, sort by run length — oldest surviving ads are the highest-converting.
- When making a budget price feel real, translate it into an everyday equivalent the target demographic already spends (coffee, gum, a streaming sub) — avoid ones that feel overused.
- When traffic converts poorly, add a friction step before the sales page to qualify intent; qualified traffic converts at higher rates than raw volume.
- When running email follow-up for unconverted leads, use plain text with one hyperlink for complex/high-ticket products; use image-heavy for physical/e-commerce products.
- When you have a winning offer, never change the core structure — change the wrapper, the seasonal reason, and the creative surface.
- When scarcity isn't real (unlimited supply), lean exclusively on urgency (time) — never fabricate scarcity.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Branding yourself alongside something disgusting.** If your contrast requires showing a negative, you become associated with that negative. Chunky milk = Mint Mobile in the viewer's brain. Contrarian framing is fine; making your negative metaphor the center of the ad is not.

**Removing your biggest asset to build a mascot.** If your brand equity lives in a person (founder, spokesperson, celebrity), replacing them with a character strips the conversion driver. Don't fix what isn't broken.

**Running the same creative hook with a different offer.** The offer is the variable worth testing, not the hook in isolation. Test "buy 1 get 2" vs. "buy 3 get 3" before you test a new hook concept.

**Incongruent ad-to-landing-page experience.** If the ad promises one thing and the page delivers something different (different offer, different tone, different visual language), you are deliberately burning conversion. Congruence between ad and page is non-negotiable.

**Burying your CTA button.** If the button doesn't visually jump off the page, people who want to buy will not find it. An invisible CTA is a self-inflicted wound.

**Batching all objections into one email.** Sending "13 reasons to switch" in a single email is lazy and signals you don't know your customer. One concern per email, in order of prevalence.

**Confusing scarcity with urgency.** Scarcity = limited quantity. Urgency = limited time. Using them interchangeably in copy means you'll often apply the wrong lever and underdeliver on both.

**Ignoring comments as objection data.** The comment section is free market research. Prospects who didn't buy will tell you exactly why. Not reading comments before writing your next ad batch is leaving money on the table.

**Making the fine print do too much work.** If the price in your ad requires seven asterisks to be accurate, your offer has a structural problem — not a disclosure problem. Fix the offer.

**Optimizing for view counts instead of conversion longevity.** A video with 6.5M views does less revenue work than an ad running profitably for 5 years. Don't mistake organic virality for paid performance.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Problem → Contrast → Offer → Humor → Repeat.** Hormozi structures explanations as: here's the thing people do wrong, here's the contrast (what works), here's the specific tactic, add a sideways remark, then move to the next beat. Humor is not ornamental — it's the gear-shift between analytical beats.

**"So if we're thinking about this..."** Used to signal a transition from observation to framework. Forces the listener to adopt the analytical frame before the conclusion lands.

**Short declarative punch followed by elaboration.** "More good stuff. Less bad stuff. That's it." Then he unpacks. The punch lands first; the explanation follows. Never buries the lede.

**Explicit enumeration with embedded stakes.** "There are four questions you can ask... Number one... Number two..." He numbers things not to be academic but to create a sense of completeness — "here's everything you need, nothing left out."

**Self-aware hedging that doubles as authority.** "I'm going to be real with you," "this is just me, Alex's point of view," "I'm assuming" — he pre-empts disagreement by naming his own uncertainty, which paradoxically makes the following claim more credible.

**The rhetorical pivot using "now."** "Now here's the thing..." or "Now what's interesting about this..." signals a non-obvious insight is coming and gets the audience to lean in before the payoff.

**Repetition of the core principle with minor variation.** He restates the same idea (same offer, new wrapper / more good stuff, less bad stuff) three or four times across different contexts in a single piece. This isn't redundancy — it's the teaching technique of cycling back to anchor the model while layering new application.
