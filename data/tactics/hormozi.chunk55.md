## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Magnifying Glass (Focus = Power)**
Your potential, intelligence, and work ethic are the size of the magnifying glass — but it doesn't matter how big it is if it isn't focused on a single point. Spread across five businesses, your energy disperses to nothing. Apply by picking one business, one product, one avatar, one channel until you hit $1M/year. When FOMO hits, ask: "Which one is the 10-year-old?" — pick that one and kill the rest.

**The 20% Guaranteed Decrease Rule**
Every change you make to a business creates an immediate ~20% performance decrease from training lag, muscle memory disruption, and team adjustment — even if the change is superior. Apply by only pulling the trigger on a change when you believe it has at least a 40% upside potential at 50/50 odds (risk-adjusted). That math breaks even. Anything less is burning the house down for a candle.

**The Punch Card (Scarcity of Change Slots)**
You don't have unlimited tests. In people-side operations, you get roughly one meaningful change per quarter per department — four shots per year. Treat each slot like a Warren Buffett stock pick: only make the bet if it's the best thing on your list after weeks of seasoning. Maintain a written "Big List of Crazy Ideas" — write ideas down instead of texting the team, and only pull from it when the idea has survived 30+ days.

**The Management Diamond**
When someone doesn't do what you want, there are exactly five reasons: (1) they didn't know what you wanted — fix with communication; (2) they didn't know how — fix with training; (3) they didn't know by when — assign deadlines; (4) no incentive — fix motivation; (5) something is blocking them — remove the blocker. Walk through this in order before assuming performance failure.

**Bundled vs. Unbundled Language**
Abstract directives ("be more patient," "be more charismatic," "stop being a dick") fail because they're bundled terms the recipient can't operationalize. Break them down to specific observable behaviors: "pause for emphasis, say their name, look them in the eyes, nod when they're talking" = charismatic. Apply this to every training instruction, feedback conversation, and onboarding checklist. If you can't list the behaviors, you can't coach the skill.

**The Value Equation Pricing Trap**
Charging hourly makes you a commodity — people compare your rate to others and you're anchored to time, not outcomes. Charge based on the dream result the client wants. Price the outcome, not the effort. When you reframe from "$250/hour" to "$5,800 to get your IP protection in place so you can sell to a record label," you've tied price to desire — which is always larger than time.

**The AB Offer Anchor**
Whenever you have an offer, create an alternative — not to actually sell it, but to make your primary offer look like a steal. "Option A: $5,800 flat fee + small commission. Option B: $25,000, zero commission." The B offer is an anchor. It re-contextualizes the A offer as obvious. Always present at least two options: "Do you want to start Monday or Tuesday?" Choice removes resistance.

**The Leaky Bucket / Traffic Diagnosis**
Traffic without a clear direction is wasted. Ask: do you know exactly where your best leads come from? Are you actively directing people there? If leads are finding you by accident (e.g., Instagram via YouTube without a CTA), you're leaving 20–50% of revenue on the table. Fix by adding CTAs at 30% of video runtime, in descriptions, pinned comments, and profiles. Then proactively DM new followers to qualify intent immediately.

**Churn Cohort Analysis (Day 90 / Month 6 Rule)**
Churn is not uniform — it's frontloaded. Months 1–3 churn at 20%+. Past day 90, it drops to ~10%. Past month 6, it drops to ~2%. Implication: don't overhaul your business because month-one churn is high — that's structural. Instead, design specific interventions: activation/onboarding for month one, deliver a concrete outcome by day 90, and engineer community connection (member-to-member friendships) by month six.

**Addition vs. Subtraction for Retention**
Most operators try to fix retention by adding more features, content, and bonuses — which makes the problem worse because users feel they're using less of what they're paying for. One community reduced monthly churn from 30% to 5% by *deleting content*. Apply by surveying members: "If I deleted everything, what one thing would you fight for?" Build around that. Remove friction first; add features only if core value is already delivering.

**The ICP Double-Down (Serve the A Client)**
When you have A, B, and C avatars, most operators average their pricing across all three. An A client may be able to spend 10x more than a C client — yet you've priced for the average. There are still plenty of A's. Serve them exclusively. Specialize your product, price to reflect their outcome, and let your pricing filter out B and C. Close rate too high = underpriced for the A client.

**CLOSER Sales Framework**
A structured sales conversation in order: **C**larify where they are, **L**abel them on the problem, **O**verview their past experiences, **S**ell them the vacation (outcome, not features), **E**xplain away concerns, **R**einforce the decision post-close. Pair this with BANT qualification (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing) upfront so you only run CLOSER on buyers who can actually close today.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening a hard performance conversation with an employee | **Script:** "Hey, I wanted you to do X and you haven't done it. I just want to figure out what the reason was so we can solve it and make it less likely to happen in the future. I figured the first possibility is that you didn't know I wanted you to do that — did you know?"

**Situation:** Employee knew what was needed but still didn't do it | **Script:** "Okay, so you knew what I wanted. Did you know *how* to do it?" [If yes:] "Did you know I wanted it by now — was there a deadline associated with this?"

**Situation:** Firing up a new sales or marketing direction in the business | **Script:** "Imagine I came to you and said: I've got this business that everybody wants to buy from me, it has good margins, I sell it like crazy, but I have four other businesses I want to start that don't grow as quickly. What should I do?"

**Situation:** Pricing objection on a service offer | **Script:** "Can you afford $5,800 to get [specific outcome] solved? Is there anyone else involved in this decision? What happens if you don't solve this? And when would you like to start — today, tomorrow, or a few weeks from now?"

**Situation:** Transitioning from hourly billing to value-based | **Script:** "You're not paying for my time. You're paying for [specific outcome they said they want]. Here's what that outcome is worth — and here's what it costs you if it doesn't happen."

**Situation:** Introducing the AB offer | **Script:** "We have two ways to work together. Option A is $5,800 flat fee plus a small commission if we win. Option B is $25,000 with zero commission. Which makes more sense for your situation?"

**Situation:** Filtering new Instagram/DM leads immediately | **Script:** "Hey — are you here for free content, or do you need help with [specific problem]?"

**Situation:** Asking churned/canceling members why they left | **Script:** "Hey, I noticed you canceled. I'm not trying to win you back — I just want to understand what didn't work so I can fix it for the next person. What was the main reason?"

**Situation:** Asking best long-tenured members what keeps them | **Script:** "You've been here longer than almost anyone. What's the number-one reason you've stayed? If you could only keep one thing from what we offer, what would it be?"

**Situation:** Onboarding a new member into a community | **Script:** "This is [new member]. They're great at [X]. [Existing member], you two should talk — you're both dealing with [shared problem]."

**Situation:** Addressing someone on the team acting like a "dick" | **Script:** "I don't care if you hate everyone — I just need you to behave this way specifically. Here are the five things people are describing when they say you're difficult: [list behaviors]. Stop doing those things and they stop describing you that way. That's all this is."

**Situation:** Selling a higher-ticket offer to someone skeptical about price | **Script:** "If you work with me, you get more of [good thing] and less of [bad thing]. If you don't work with me, you get more of [bad thing] and less of [good thing]. Which of those two futures do you want to be in 90 days from now?"

**Situation:** Positioning flat fee + performance as conviction signal | **Script:** "The reason we do flat fee plus a small performance component is we want skin in the game alongside you — we only win when you win."

**Situation:** Saving the business owner from chasing novelty | **Script:** "Every time you want to make a change, add it to your list. If you're still thinking about it in 30 days, it has legs. If you've forgotten about it, the business just saved itself."

**Situation:** Coaching founders who want to help everyone | **Script:** "Do you think you'll be able to help more people if you make money?" [Wait for yes.] "Then stop spreading yourself across people who can't pay and start going all-in on the ones who can."

**Situation:** Diagnosing a broken sales motion | **Script:** "Walk me through exactly how someone goes from finding out you exist to giving you money. Every step." [Then identify where they're leaking.]

**Situation:** VSSL framing — answer objections preemptively | **Script:** "A lot of people who get on a call with us ask [common objection]. Here's how we handle that: [answer]. Another common one is [objection 2]. Here's that answer: [answer]." — repeat for 5–7 objections.

**Situation:** Closing after presenting price | **Script:** "It sounds like that number landed a little heavy — what does fair look like to you?"

**Situation:** Diagnosing if something is actually a logic claim or just noise | **Script:** "Okay — what does that mean to you? How do you know? And so what?" [Logic. Evidence. Utility.]

**Situation:** Explaining focus to an overwhelmed multi-business founder | **Script:** "You have to think about which one is the 10-year-old of your businesses. Pick that one. The others stay on the list, not in your calendar."

## DECISION RULES

**When your close rate is exceptionally high → raise your prices.** High close rate is the market telling you you're underpriced for your avatar. Price friction is a feature, not a bug — it filters out unqualified buyers.

**When a change only improves performance by less than 40% at 50/50 odds → don't make it.** The guaranteed 20% decrease from change lag makes anything under that math a losing bet.

**When you find yourself working on unprofitable side projects → kill them immediately.** Every hour spent on the unprofitable thing is an hour stolen from scaling the cash cow.

**When someone on your team isn't performing → diagnose before you discipline.** Walk the Management Diamond first (know what/how/when/incentive/blockers) before labeling it a performance problem.

**When customers keep paying you for one thing but you're pushing them toward another → stop and serve the thing they're buying.** The market has spoken. The door they knock on is the business you build.

**When you have a retention problem → don't add features.** First ask: what one thing would members fight for if you deleted everything? Strip everything else. Addition increases the feeling of under-utilization.

**When churn is high in months 1–3 → optimize onboarding and the first win, not the overall product.** Early churn is a first-90-day problem, not a product problem. Get people to a win in 24 hours.

**When non-ideal customers say your price is too high → ignore it.** Pricing acting as a filter is correct behavior. If your ideal customer says it, respond with a tier or a payment plan.

**When you have a new idea → write it down and tell no one.** Revisit in 30 days. If it's survived, rank it against everything else on your list by upside × probability. Only pull the trigger if it beats what's already there.

**When you're trying to fix a behavior in someone → describe the behaviors, not the trait.** Don't say "be more professional." List the five things they do that cause people to describe them as unprofessional. Fix those.

**When revenue is flat and you have multiple businesses → cut to one.** The plateau is a direct function of divided focus, not market ceiling.

**When you want to test a change → change one thing at a time.** Multiple simultaneous changes destroy attribution. You can't learn from a test you can't isolate.

**When a customer gives you money without you asking → double down on that exact thing.** The market giving unsolicited buying signals is the most reliable signal you will ever get.

**When someone says price is the cancellation reason → survey to confirm.** If it's your best customers saying it, create a lower tier. If it's fringe customers, it's the filter working. Don't react to a sample of one.

**When you're growing revenue but feel spread thin → you're at the transition point.** Stop adding. Focus everything on the single most profitable thing. The complexity is the reason you're not scaling.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**The "Help Everyone" Trap.** Running charitable or unprofitable products alongside your cash cow because you want to "give back" — while hemorrhaging the time and capital you need to scale the thing that actually works. You can't help anyone if you're broke.

**Abdicating instead of delegating.** Handing something off with "here you go, good luck" and being surprised when it gets worse. If you didn't document what, how, when, why, and remove the blockers — you didn't delegate.

**Changing too many things at once.** No attribution possible. You'll never know what worked or what broke. Four simultaneous process changes means the only variable you can isolate is you.

**Optimizing for the wrong traffic source.** Continuing to create content on the channel you're comfortable with, not the channel where leads are actually converting. If they're finding you by accident on Instagram from YouTube without a CTA, you're leaking leads.

**Adding to fix retention.** Building more features, more content, more bonuses to reduce churn. This is exactly backwards — it inflates the "I'm not using this" feeling that drives cancellation.

**Bundled feedback as training.** Telling someone to "be more patient," "stop being a dick," or "communicate better" without breaking those into specific observable behaviors. Soft skills aren't unteachable — they're just under-unbundled.

**Changing when people are still getting better on their own.** Most teams naturally improve performance over time if left alone. Intervening with a change to get 5% faster will cost you 20% immediately — and they would have gotten there anyway.

**Solving every complaint individually.** Knee-jerk product pivots based on a single customer complaint. Look for patterns across 20+ cancellations before you act on any single cancellation reason.

**Pricing by time or comp-set.** "$250/hour because that's what other attorneys charge" makes you a commodity. The moment you anchor price to time or competitors, you've lost the conversation about value.

**Building for the hobbyist audience when you have a premium product.** Letting non-ideal customers shape your product direction because they're louder. Their price objections, feature requests, and complaints are noise. Serve the A client first.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The "Real Real" Pattern.** Hormozi uses "the real real" to signal he's about to say the thing nobody else will say. It's a frame-setter that elevates what follows: *"But the real real underneath of this is that most entrepreneurs are changing so many things so often they never know what broke besides them."* Use it before uncomfortable truths.

**The Self-Answering Rhetorical.** He poses an objection, then demolishes it in the same breath: *"You're like, 'Holy cow, that's way bigger.' Now remember, he had plenty of sales conversations... and a pretty good close rate. And so if you have a really good close rate, I hear that and think: we're underpriced."* Pattern: raise the obvious objection, pivot immediately to the frame that neutralizes it.

**The Hypothetical Reversal.** "Imagine I came to you and said [obvious scenario]. What would you tell me?" — then he lets the silence confirm the answer. Forces the audience to reach the conclusion themselves. Extremely effective in live coaching or sales conversations.

**Short declarative punches after a long setup.** He builds context for 3–4 sentences, then drops a 5-word verdict: *"...all of that time. 13.5 hours per week."* Or: *"...you pay the price of impact. That's the price."* Use rhythm: long-long-long-short. The short line lands harder after the buildup.

**Numbered frameworks with tactical sub-bullets.** Hormozi almost always structures advice as numbered items with specific, actionable sub-steps (not principles). Not "be better at retention" — "track joins vs. cancels monthly, label the business growing/flat/declining, period." The specificity is the credibility.

**The Anecdotal Proof Loop.** He establishes a claim, gives a personal example, then extracts the universal principle: *"I eat Chipotle twice a day... 500+ transactions... my accountant told me I was crazy... but look at my grocery bills: non-existent."* Pattern: claim → personal story → extracted rule. Makes abstract advice feel tested.

**"You already know what to do" confrontation.** When someone is dancing around an obvious answer, he names it directly: *"It sounds like you know which one you should do and want to do something different."* Or: *"Weird. Sounds so easy when you say it."* Disarms rationalization without being preachy. Use when a prospect or team member is already aware of the answer and needs permission, not advice.
