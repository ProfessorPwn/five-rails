## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Grand Opening / Pre-Sale Strategy**
Start marketing and selling before you can deliver. Collect cash upfront, delay opening as long as possible — that's when costs begin. For brick-and-mortar, run $500–$1,000 in ads across 5–10 markets before signing any lease; pick the market with the lowest lead cost, not your gut feeling. Pre-sale insurance costs $5–10K and saves you from a bad 5-year lease.

**The Value Equation / Guarantee Formula**
Every guarantee must answer the "or what": *If you don't achieve X by Y time, I will Z.* The word "guarantee" alone is weak — the stakes you put on the line are what close the sale. Run two types: a service-based guarantee (keep delivering until they hit the goal) and an unconditional guarantee (if they don't feel they got $X of value at any point, full refund). The unconditional forces your team to bring it every single day.

**Best Case / Worst Case Close**
After the offer: "Best case, you change your life forever. Worst case, you do this basically for free and I give your money back. The only thing more guaranteed than worst case is that if you leave right now, you're definitely not going to get where you want to go." This reframes the decision as zero-risk and isolates inaction as the only real downside.

**Sell the Vacation, Not the Plane Flight**
Open by amplifying the pain of their current state (the rain, the problem, the deprivation). Then paint the destination in vivid sensory detail — what life looks, feels, and sounds like after. Never describe the process of getting there (the TSA lines, the baby on the plane). Them signing up *is* the plane flight — don't sell it.

**Bonus Stack as Objection Arsenal**
For every objection a prospect raises, create a one-time templated bonus that resolves it — then keep it in your back pocket forever. Travel meal plan, budget grocery guide, at-home workout guide. In one-on-one sales, don't reveal the stack upfront; match each objection with its corresponding bonus in real time: "Oh, don't worry — I actually have something for that. Would this help?"

**The Flywheel: Get → Results → Proof**
Step 1: Get customers in. Step 2: Get them results. Step 3: Use those results to get more customers. Engineer "wow moments" into the customer journey — milestone weigh-ins, before/afters, transformation moments — and be present to capture the reaction on video or in writing. Proof collected is useless unless it's *displayed* in the sales environment.

**Internal Plays (Defined-In Promotions)**
Run a themed, scoped promotion to your existing customer base once per quarter. Peel off one specific benefit of your core offer (Big Booty Boot Camp, Abs & Arms, one body part of their business) and sell it as a defined program. Cost of customer acquisition = $0. Gross margins typically 90%+. Four plays a year can double take-home for a small service business.

**Zero-Cost Upsells to Liquidate Ad Spend**
Your ad cost should ideally be recovered on day one through a product upsell attached to the service sale. Identify one or two products your customer already wants (supplements, mats, apparel, tools) where you hold margin. Sell them at point of purchase. If your supplement margin covers your ad bill every month, your entire service revenue becomes gravy — which lets you outspend every competitor.

**TEAM Framework for Partnerships**
A partner is only worth bringing in if they provide at least one of four things you lack: **T**ime, **E**xpertise, **A**ssets (brand, audience, distribution, customer base), or **M**oney. The worst partnership in the world is two people with the same expertise who are friends. Slice equity by risk exposure, not by headcount.

**Profit Share / Operator Model for Brick-and-Mortar Scale**
Every scaled brick-and-mortar business needs a location operator with skin in the game. Set their on-target earnings (OTE) at what a skilled hire would command. Split OTE 50/50: half as base salary, half tied to a percentage of location profit. If the location outperforms, they earn more; if it underperforms, they earn less. This creates a self-managing career path and aligns operator incentives with yours permanently.

**Absolute vs. Relative Return**
Stop optimizing for ROAS or ROI percentage. Optimize for absolute profit dollars. Getting 10:1 on $1,500/month is worse than 4:1 on $15,000/month. If you can get 2:1 on a million dollars, do it — you made a million dollars. A falling ROAS is not a signal to stop; it's often a signal to *increase* spend because you're still profitable per unit.

**Lead Nurture as Primary Throughput Lever**
Closing rate improvement (50% → 80%) is a ~60% lift. Show-rate improvement (10% → 50% of leads) is a 5x lift. The discrepancy in performance between a mediocre lead nurture system and a great one dwarfs the discrepancy between a mediocre closer and a great one. If you call a lead within 60 seconds, you see a ~5x increase in conversion. Hire one dedicated person whose *only job* is to stop everything and call leads the moment they come in.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect asks for a discount | **Script:** "Well, I could actually do it for more." [Pause. Let it land. Many buy immediately.]

**Situation:** Prospect still wants a discount after the above | **Script:** "How about this — if you refer me three friends, I'll give you that discount right now. The terms change, but the deal is there."

**Situation:** Discount as barter opportunity | **Script:** "I'll knock $100 off if you leave me a review on Google, Yelp, and Facebook and let me use your before/after in my ads. Deal?"

**Situation:** Prospect raises a specific objection (travel, budget, time) | **Script:** "Oh, don't worry — I actually made something for exactly that. [Pull out bonus.] Would this take care of it for you?"

**Situation:** Using the Best Case / Worst Case close | **Script:** "Let's zoom out for a second. Best case: you change your life, you finally hit this goal, you find a community, and you stick with it for good. Worst case: you do six weeks basically for free, you tell me I suck, and I give you your money back. You have nothing to lose except the guarantee that if you walk out that door right now, you're definitely not going to get there. So what do you want to do?"

**Situation:** Prospect raises objection about proof / skepticism | **Script:** "How much proof would you need to see to believe? [Start showing.] One? Two? Three? Just tell me when." [Keep going until they say stop.] "Right. So at some point it's probably not about the proof. What are you actually afraid of?"

**Situation:** Identifying the real objection beneath the stated one | **Script:** "It sounds like maybe the evidence isn't really the issue. What's the thing underneath that — what are you actually worried about?"

**Situation:** Agency owner who says he doesn't sell his time | **Script:** "I get it. But it's America — there's a price for everything. Would you do it for a million dollars an hour? [Yes.] Okay, so there is a price. Just give me one you'd actually say yes to."

**Situation:** Prospect is skeptical about whether they can stick to the plan | **Script:** "Let me ask you — out of seven days, how many days did you eat all three meals exactly to plan with nothing added or removed?" [They revise down significantly.] "So not quite 100%. Let's talk about what actually gets in the way."

**Situation:** Setting up a location operator with clear autonomy | **Script:** "Here's the deal — this phone doesn't ring. A pipe breaks mid-session: call the plumber, not me. Someone has a heart attack: call 911. Just think one step further and take that step. You have up to $1,000 to solve any problem without calling me. That's the job."

**Situation:** Someone asks why they should act now (urgency) | **Script:** "The only thing more guaranteed than worst case here is that if you leave, you're definitely not going to get to where you want to go. So what's the move?"

**Situation:** Transitioning a challenge winner to a long-term membership | **Script:** "You've lost 14 pounds — that's amazing. But you told me your goal is 50. This is going to take longer than six weeks, and you know that. So let's just set you up to actually get there for good, right?"

**Situation:** Prospect in a sales office surrounded by social proof | **Script:** [After showing proof]: "How much more would you need to see to feel comfortable? Just tell me when." [Keep showing.] "I can keep going."

**Situation:** Teaching a new employee the guarantee as a tool | **Script:** "Five weeks, six days, and 23 hours from now — if you don't feel we gave you $500 of value, independent of your results, I'll give you your money back. That means you could call me at 11:59 PM on the last night. I'd still give it back."

**Situation:** Introducing a quarterly internal play to existing customers | **Script:** "[Subject line: Big Booty Boot Camp — we're running it again.] If you've ever wanted to really zero in on [X], we're running a six-week intensive — one session a week, starts [date], $300. Spots limited. Reply 'I'm in' to grab one."

**Situation:** Asking agency owner to teach instead of just execute | **Script:** "Here's my intention upfront — I'll sign up with you, but I'm going to do it with the intention of learning everything I can so I can eventually replace you. I want to be on the calls, I want to touch the keyboard, I want you to explain every decision. Is that a problem?"

**Situation:** Negotiating one-on-one learning from an expert | **Script:** "What would it take for you to teach me one-on-one? There's a price for everything — just give me the number."

**Situation:** Probing customer to understand what the product actually does for them | **Script:** "What need does this actually fulfill for you? What problem does it solve? Where does it fit in your life?" [Don't lead them. Just listen.]

**Situation:** Announcing hat change to an employee-friend | **Script:** "Friend hat: [personal conversation]. Okay — boss hat: here's what I need to change and why."

**Situation:** Identifying customer non-adherence accurately | **Script:** "How many days out of seven did you eat all three meals exactly to plan — nothing added, nothing swapped?" [Then:] "And how many days did you have all three meals plus nothing else?" [Specific = accurate.]

## DECISION RULES

- When you have no money, barter. Every line item on your expenses is a potential trade. Offer whatever your service costs you in marginal delivery — not cash, not equity.
- When evaluating a new market or location, spend $500–$1,000 on ads there *before* signing a lease. Lead cost is your market validity signal. Don't roll the dice on a 5-year lease.
- When a prospect asks for a discount on core services, never give it. Either say "I could do it for more" or change the terms (referrals, reviews, advertising rights). The discount must cost them something.
- When a prospect raises an objection, match it with a pre-built bonus that resolves it. Don't discount — solve the stated obstacle and move on.
- When someone has complaints about proof, shift to: "How much would you need? Tell me when." If they can't say when, the real objection is fear — go there instead.
- When your business is full and demand exceeds capacity, raise prices or open a new location. Do *not* change the product or reduce quality to fit more customers.
- When deciding whether to scale to location two, don't move until location one has grown *without you* for six months straight. Not "runs without you training" — runs without you making decisions.
- When hiring a partner, require at least one of: Time, Expertise, Assets, or Money (TEAM). Two people with the same expertise who are friends is the worst possible partnership.
- When setting operator compensation, split their OTE 50/50: half base, half profit-tied percentage. If profit exceeds target, they earn more; below target, they earn less.
- When ROAS drops, don't stop spending — check absolute profit. A 4:1 on $15K/month beats a 10:1 on $1,500/month. Optimize for dollars in the bank, not percentages.
- When you have a service business, identify one product upsell that costs you nothing to add at point-of-sale and earns enough margin to cover your entire ad spend. Once that's in place, advertising becomes self-liquidating.
- When managing employees, pair responsibility with authority always. If you're giving someone a responsibility, give them spending authority up to the dollar amount needed to handle 90% of situations without calling you.
- When teaching a team member a skill, your goal is not to be the best at it — it's to be the best teacher of it. If your closer is closing 50% and you could close 80%, you're losing as a manager.
- When you find that customers love your product and retention is high, do not change the product to fix a business model problem. Fix pricing instead. Customers who love the product is the hardest thing to create.
- When entering any new industry, immediately pay to access people who are already succeeding in it. The cost of the mastermind is almost always recovered in mistakes you don't make in the first conversation.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Posturing when you should be asking for help.** If your Instagram makes you look like you've got it figured out when you don't, you're shutting off the help you most need. People help those who are visibly building, not those performing success. Early-stage is the only time the world gives you free mentorship — don't waste it.

**Selling the plane flight instead of the vacation.** Describing your process, your steps, your methodology to a prospect is the same as listing the TSA wait, the layover, and the crying baby. They don't care how they get to Maui. They care that they get to Maui. Stop talking about your service; start talking about their result.

**Obsessing over ROAS instead of absolute profit.** Getting 13:1 on $1,500/month is not a success if a competitor is getting 4:1 on $15,000/month and netting 3x more than you. Stopping spend because efficiency dropped while leaving money on the table is a very expensive mistake.

**Scaling before you've nailed it.** Opening location two while location one depends on you is just creating two broken businesses. If customers are churning, more marketing only accelerates the leak. Fix retention first; then scale.

**Spending on appearance instead of product.** A $250K buildout does not produce more revenue than a $34K buildout if customers are paying for accountability and community, not granite countertops. Know what job your product is actually doing before you invest in upgrading it.

**Making complexity feel like sophistication.** A 100-hour sales training is not better than a 75-minute one if the 75-minute one produces closers and the 100-hour one doesn't. Every layer of complexity you add to a system reduces the number of people who can execute it reliably.

**Relying on another business to feed yours.** If a single partner, platform, or referral source can shut off your lead flow tomorrow, that's an existential risk. There's a difference between single-channel risk (fixable) and single-business dependency (catastrophic). Never let another entity's decision determine whether your business eats.

**Giving equity too early.** The equity you give away at the start is the most expensive equity you'll ever give. Never default to 50/50 just because there are two people. Slice by risk exposure. Whoever's taking the most risk gets the most pie.

**Solving problems your customers don't have.** Optimizing training periodization when customers are choosing gyms based on music and community is an excellent waste of time. Ask customers what job your product does for them — don't assume it's the same job you think you're doing.

**Changing what works when capacity is the real problem.** If the gym is full and people love it, the answer is expand, not restructure. Churning a third of your customer base because you re-engineered a loved product to fit more people is one of the most expensive mistakes in brick-and-mortar.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Enumerate, then zoom in.** Hormozi states a numbered list upfront ("So it's going to be: how I started, my first $100K, then the lessons") then digs deep into each item with a specific story. The structure signals credibility before the content delivers it.

**Concrete numbers over vague outcomes.** Never "more revenue" — always "$4,972 in cash the first month / rent was $4,972." Never "better close rates" — always "10% of leads vs. 55% of leads, one girl, full-time, no other job." The number is the argument.

**Mechanic + story + application.** Each tactic follows the same rhythm: state the rule → tell the exact story where he learned it → translate it into what you should do. The story isn't decoration — it's proof. The application closes the loop.

**Reframe through contrast.** Sets up the bad version first (spending 1,500/month, excited about 10:1 ROAS), then reveals the better version (spending 15K/month, 4:1 ROAS, 3x more net profit). The contrast does the persuading; he just narrates it.

**Self-deprecation as credibility.** Hormozi regularly leads with his own failure ("that was a crazy mistake," "it cost me $250,000 to learn this lesson," "I didn't understand what I was actually doing"). This disarms resistance and makes the subsequent lesson land harder — he paid for it, so you don't have to.

**"I don't know why it works, I just know it works."** Used deliberately to strip the advice of theoretical baggage and anchor it entirely in observed results. This pattern signals epistemic honesty and makes the tactic feel more battle-tested than anything with a polished explanation.

**Permission-granting before the uncomfortable truth.** Before delivering a hard observation ("a lot of y'all's Instagrams — you know who you are"), he contextualizes it as coming from someone who lived it. Then delivers the truth bluntly, without hedging. The setup buys the directness.
