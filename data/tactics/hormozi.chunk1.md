## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Blame-Power Equation**
Wherever you point your finger of blame is exactly where your power drains. If you say your mom's the reason you can't commit, she now controls your love life. If you say the economy is why your business isn't growing, the economy runs your business. Own everything up to this moment — not because it's fair, but because it's the only way to be in control. Apply it every time you catch yourself explaining why something isn't working.

**2. Earn vs. Learn Seasons**
There are seasons to build skills and seasons to cash them in — and confusing them destroys compounding. If you're under-skilled and you optimize for income, you're selling your future cheap. The 18-year-old who takes $100K at a nobody company instead of learning under a world-class operator is trading a decade of leverage for one year of salary. Use it as a filter: before taking any role or deal, ask "am I in a learn season or an earn season right now?"

**3. Exploration → Exploitation**
In the beginning, say yes broadly to find the thing you have a real edge at. Once you've identified it, cut everything else ruthlessly — because the exploitation phase is where compounding happens. The failure mode is staying in exploration forever (nine simultaneous businesses) or moving to exploitation on the wrong thing. The signal to switch: you have at least one proof-of-result and a clear path to go deeper.

**4. Declarative vs. Procedural Knowledge**
Declarative knowledge is knowing that something exists (private equity deals, cold outbound, Facebook ads). Procedural knowledge is knowing how to execute it. Most people mistake watching ten videos on sales for knowing how to sell. They don't. You need both — declarative first (learn the thing exists and its structure), procedural second (do it until you no longer have to consciously think about it). When buying education, know which one you're paying for.

**5. Experience Points / Failure Resume**
Every failed attempt isn't a loss — it's an XP gain. With enough XP, you level up. This reframe is essential for persistence because it converts failure from a verdict into a resource. Apply it by asking after every failed bet: "What specific XP did I just earn?" The best entrepreneurs have the longest failure resumes.

**6. The Ignorance Debt Framework**
Your biggest monthly expense isn't rent or food — it's the gap between what you're making and what you'd make if you knew what you should know. If you make $50K and could make $1M, you're paying $950K/year in ignorance tax. This reframes education from a cost to the highest-ROI investment available. Every dollar into skill is a dollar that compounds forever; every dollar into stuff depreciates immediately.

**7. Logic / Evidence / Utility (Dr. Cashy's Frame)**
When confronted with a belief, story, or limiting identity, run it through three gates. Logic: define it in terms of observable behavior — what does "I have demons" actually mean as an action? Evidence: how many times did that behavior actually occur, and is the pattern real? Utility: who cares, and does it help you hit your goals? Strip the voodoo, replace mysticism with behavior, and most "blocks" dissolve into addressable skill gaps.

**8. Input/Output Delineation**
Outputs are results — money made, followers gained, deals closed. Inputs are the actions you control — dials made, content posted, pages written. You can only control inputs. When you feel impatient ("why hasn't this worked yet?"), that feeling is about outputs. The correct response is to increase inputs. Make this your anxiety coping mechanism: whenever you want results faster, do more of the input. Track leading metrics, not lagging ones.

**9. The Infinite Game Frame**
Health, marriage, and business aren't finite games with a winning condition. They're infinite — the point is to stay in them. You don't "win" at marriage; you stay married. You don't "win" at business; you stay in business. This reframe eliminates the existential crisis of "why haven't I won yet?" The only loss condition is quitting. If quitting isn't on the menu, you win by default on a long enough timeline.

**10. Reference Group vs. Inner Circle**
The five people you compare yourself to — not the five you spend the most time with — predict your outcomes. Your reference group is whoever's voice you hear when you're about to make a major decision. If those voices don't have what you want, they shouldn't be deciding. Upgrade your reference group before upgrading your income, not after.

**11. 100x Not 2x**
Most people try to optimize existing effort by 10-20%. Real leverage comes from doing 100x more volume in a narrow lane. When growth stalls, don't tweak — look at raw volume. The billionaire friend doing 66 in-person events per quarter isn't smarter; he's doing 10x the reps. Volume creates the feedback loops that create skill. Apply by auditing total output units per week versus whoever's winning in your lane.

**12. The Trade-Off Auction**
Everything in life is a bid, not a transaction. Privacy is the price of fame. Loneliness is the price of ambition. Pleasure is the price of discipline. Most people bid 30 for a $300 pair of Nikes and then complain the store is unfair. They're not willing to pay the real price. The move is to keep bidding — to reset expectations, absorb the new price, and bid again — until you cross the threshold. Apply when rationalizing why results haven't come: ask what you're actually willing to bid, then bid that.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** First cold call from a Facebook lead who doesn't know who you are
**Script:** "John." [pause] "Hey, this is Alex." [pause] "From Facebook — you signed up for our thing. Just calling to make sure you're not a crazy person from the internet, and neither am I. We're actually on the corner of X and Y, right next to the 7-Eleven. Just wanted to see what time works to come by — we've got the sweetest facility in the area. Do you want a picture of me or just the address?" [half-grin tone throughout]

**Situation:** Prospect gives a list of objections at a live event or sales call
**Script:** Listen fully, then say: "You're right. You'll never be able to make this work for your business." Wait. They'll push back with their own reasons why they *can*. Then: "Great — so what's stopping you from starting today?"

**Situation:** Objection — "I need to think about it" / stall after price reveal
**Script:** "It sounds like that number landed wrong — what does fair look like to you?"

**Situation:** Someone asks what's stopping them from taking action on a big decision
**Script:** "You already know what you should do. What you're really asking is whether you have permission. So let me ask you: whose voice do you hear when you imagine *not* doing this? Is that the person you want running your life?"

**Situation:** Sales call energy management — between appointments
**Script:** [Use a physical reset — bounce, change posture, recall a strong close] Then re-enter with: "Okay. Same energy. Same conviction. We're back."

**Situation:** Managing an employee who wants narrow scope / special accommodation
**Script:** "The question isn't 'how does the organization serve your skill set?' The question is: how can your skill set meet the demands of the existing business? Meeting reality where it is."

**Situation:** Explaining why someone hasn't gotten results yet
**Script:** "It's not that ads don't work. It's that you don't yet know how to make them work. You're not making as much money as you want because you're not as good as you think you are. That's fixable."

**Situation:** Someone claims they're "traumatized" or "self-sabotaging" as an explanation for underperformance
**Script:** "Okay — define that in terms of behavior. What actually happens, and how many times has it happened? Now: does it matter to your goals? If yes — what's the skill you need to behave differently under that condition? That's all this is."

**Situation:** Early hiring / referral for a young person
**Script:** "Find the hardest worker in the room and double their input. They knock 100 doors, you knock 200. Don't match — double. Then wait 36 months."

**Situation:** Selling education or program value to someone price-sensitive
**Script:** "Write your income goal on a whiteboard. Now subtract what you make today. That difference is what you pay reality every single year for not knowing how to achieve it. The course costs less than one month of that bill."

**Situation:** Someone says they don't have time
**Script:** "Every person has 5 to 9 a.m. and 5 to 9 p.m. That's eight hours a day outside a full-time job. You have time. You just haven't decided this is worth it yet."

**Situation:** Someone wants to start multiple businesses
**Script:** "Do you remember the guy who got rich dropshipping, day trading, buying crypto, and wholesaling all at the same time? Yeah. Me neither. Pick one."

**Situation:** Responding to "you should have work-life balance"
**Script:** "If you compete against smart people and you work smart but not hard, you lose to the person who works smart *and* hard. I'd rather be that person."

**Situation:** Conversation with a complainer on your team
**Script:** "When you complain, you lose twice — you waste the energy you could have used to fix it, and you lose respect from every person who hears it. They won't remember what you complained about. Only that you complain."

**Situation:** Helping someone reframe failure
**Script:** "Every time after the first time you start from scratch, you start with experience. You never actually start at zero again. The bet always nets positive."

**Situation:** Helping someone identify their actual reference group
**Script:** "When you're about to make a big decision, whose voice do you hear? Not who you spend time with — whose voice. That's your actual reference group. Are those the people who have what you want?"

**Situation:** Someone waiting for the "right moment"
**Script:** "The perfect moment doesn't come. It becomes the perfect moment when you look back on it, because today it just looks like now."

**Situation:** Explaining why to document struggle, not success
**Script:** "The time to document your life is when it sucks, not when it's great. Those are the stories you're going to tell. Those are the proof of who you really are."

**Situation:** Closing the loop on taking action now vs. later
**Script:** "If you genuinely believe you're going to do this sooner or later — why later? You're going to enjoy the benefits either way. Why delay them?"

## DECISION RULES

1. **When you're about to blame something or someone for your results:** Ask "Does placing blame here increase or decrease the likelihood I hit my goal?" If decrease — drop it. Blame transfers power to the thing blamed.

2. **When choosing between two activities:** Run both through: "Does this make it more or less likely I hit my goals?" Drop the one that scores lower. No exceptions for feelings or relationships.

3. **When something is working:** Double down immediately. The moment of momentum is when competitors will come. Go harder, not looser.

4. **When something is hard and not working:** Ask "Is this hard because it's genuinely broken, or hard because I haven't done enough volume yet?" If volume is the answer, do more. If it's structurally broken, do different.

5. **When you're deciding whether to keep a friend in your life:** Ask "Does having this person in my life increase or decrease the likelihood I hit my goals?" If decrease — begin the exit.

6. **When you feel anxious or uncertain about the future:** Use action as your coping mechanism. Don't think your way out. Do your way out.

7. **When you're evaluating a new opportunity:** Map out uninformed optimism → informed pessimism → valley of despair → upside. Decide if you can commit through the valley before entering. If not, don't start.

8. **When you're setting deadlines:** Ask "Can this be done by end of day instead of end of week?" If yes, move it. End of hour instead of end of day? Move it. Decision right now instead of next meeting? Decide now.

9. **When you're tempted to buy status items:** Ask "Does this impress the people above me or below me?" If below — it's ego spending, not leverage spending. Rich people are impressed by your output, not your assets.

10. **When you're evaluating any education or program purchase:** Frame it as: cost of ignorance = (goal income) − (current income). If the program costs less than one year of that delta, it's almost certainly worth it.

11. **When you want to start multiple things at once:** Default to one. If you can't articulate why this specific one above all others, you haven't decided yet — you're still in exploration. Don't spend money in exploration mode.

12. **When someone gives you advice:** First ask: "Do I want their life?" If no — don't take the advice. Only accept input from people whose outcomes you'd trade for.

13. **When something takes longer than expected:** Remind yourself: it happens slower than you expect, then faster than you can imagine. Stay in. The delay is the cost of entry.

14. **When you're building proof vs. building brand:** Build the business first. Proof creates brand; brand without proof is noise. Don't make content until you have something real to document.

15. **When you feel like you need balance:** Ask "Is balance the goal, or is it what mediocre people use to explain average results?" If you want extraordinary outcomes, imbalance in the right direction is required — for a season.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Delaying pain to "tomorrow," "Monday," or "next year."** This is the hallmark of poverty thinking. They flip the equation — pleasure now, pain later — and are then shocked when today is yesterday's deferred pain. Watch for this in yourself and in prospects: if every action is "starting soon," nothing will start.

2. **Spreading across multiple businesses or income streams before any one works.** Seven income streams means you're split seven ways. You can only be CEO of one thing. Every additional lane is a vote against the lane you're actually in.

3. **Building a personal brand before building a track record.** Everyone can copy your promises, your logo, your services. Nobody can copy what you've actually done. Credibility before likability. Proof before pudding.

4. **3-hour morning routines.** Morning routines are what people who got rich do *after* they got rich — then they attribute their success to the routine instead of the decade of relentless execution that preceded it. Replace the routine with three hours of work.

5. **Using "trauma," "demons," or "self-sabotage" as explanations instead of skill gaps.** These are stories, not diagnoses. They cast power to the past, which you can't change. The reframe: "Under these specific conditions, I behave in a way that's not conducive to my goals. What skill do I need?" That's solvable.

6. **Networking instead of working — especially early.** Networking is exploration mode behavior. In exploitation mode, every hour at a lunch is an hour not building. When the work works, every door will open. But if the work never gets done, no network will save it.

7. **Treating understanding as mastery.** Watching ten videos on cold calling is declarative knowledge — you know it exists. You don't know how to do it. People mistake "I get it conceptually" for competence and then wonder why results aren't coming. There is no substitute for the first hundred reps.

8. **Buying status before buying leverage.** Watches, cars, and leased Ferraris impress people below you — the exact people whose opinions cost you nothing. The people ahead of you are only impressed by evidence that you outwork them. Every dollar into status is a dollar taken from compounding.

9. **Quitting at the valley of despair.** The valley is not evidence that it's broken — it's the price of entry. The cycle is: uninformed optimism → informed pessimism → despair → results. Most people exit at despair and restart at optimism with something new, forever cycling without upside.

10. **Confusing intensity for volume.** Working "really hard" for a few weeks isn't volume. Volume is the relentless accumulation of reps across months and years. The top 1% podcaster has 21 episodes minimum. 90% of people never post one. The price of excellence has never been lower — which also means most people aren't paying it.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. The Extreme-Then-Ground Pattern**
State an uncomfortable absolute, then immediately ground it in a specific real story. *"You have to be willing to lose relationships to get the ones you want. I talk to one friend from high school. One."* The absolute grabs; the personal example makes it credible rather than preachy.

**2. The "You're Right. So What?" Pivot**
Acknowledge the person's complaint or objection fully — don't fight it — then strip away its power by asking what it actually produces. *"You're right. You'll never make this work. Feel better? Now what?"* This is the reverse-objection pattern: agree to the conclusion, expose the uselessness of holding it.

**3. Binary-Then-Spectrum Correction**
Start with a sharp binary (rich vs. poor, winner vs. loser) to create a frame, then immediately qualify it with nuance. This keeps things memorable without being cartoonish. *"It's not active vs. passive income — that's a bad frame. It's just: what's my return on time?"*

**4. Layered "And So" Construction**
Chain logic with "and so" to build inevitability. Each sentence creates the premise for the next. *"You learn a skill. You get evidence. People ask you about it. You charge. And so the path reveals itself only as you walk it."* Never jumps. Always earns the next step.

**5. First-Person Failure First**
Open any prescriptive claim with a personal failure or embarrassing story before giving the advice. *"I let a $46M sale almost not happen because I was worried about impressing a single colleague. Real talk."* The vulnerability earns the credibility that makes the prescription land.

**6. The Reframe Ladder**
When a person holds a limiting belief, Hormozi doesn't argue directly. He runs them up a ladder: accept the premise → take it to its logical extreme → show the extreme is unacceptable → let them discover the exit themselves. *"Great — so you're right, your mom ruined your love life. Forever. That's what you want? Okay. Now she controls every relationship you'll ever have."*

**7. Volume-to-Permission Pattern**
End a prescriptive section by giving explicit permission to start now, imperfectly. *"You don't need to figure out the whole path. You need to take the next step. The path reveals as you go."* This disarms the paralysis that kills action. Often followed by a specific low-barrier first action (post the first video, make the first call, set the first timer).

**8. The Scoreboard Check**
When someone has a clever argument for why they can't move, redirect to outcomes only: *"Give me one example — one — where blaming something else made you better. One."* This strips rhetoric and forces honest accounting. Use whenever intellectual justifications are being deployed to avoid action.
