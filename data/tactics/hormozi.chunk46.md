## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The STAR System (Why People Don't Do Things)**
Five sequential failure modes: they didn't know you wanted it done; they didn't know *what* you wanted; they didn't know *how* to do it; they didn't know *when* it was due; or something *blocked* them. Run through all five before concluding someone lacks motivation — motivation is the last variable, not the first. Apply it in every underperformance conversation before delivering judgment.

**2. Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate (D3 Training)**
To transfer any skill: write the exact checklist, do it in front of them, have them do it in front of you. Every training failure is a failure to operationalize — "be kind" isn't trainable; "smile when you answer the phone, repeat back what they said, raise your voice when you enter a room" is trainable. Use this whenever you're tempted to say "they just don't get it."

**3. The Value Equation / LTV–CAC–Payback Period Triangle**
Business health = (Lifetime Gross Profit per Customer) ÷ (Cost to Acquire) within a (Payback Period). Bad customers destroy all three simultaneously: they compress LTV via churn, inflate CAC via reputation damage, and extend payback via low margins. Fix the customer before you fix anything else.

**4. Structural Churn**
Churn that exists independent of your delivery quality — caused by the fundamental instability of your customer base. A CRM selling to gyms will lose 3% monthly because gyms close. No amount of better service fixes structural churn. Diagnosing whether your churn is structural vs. delivery-caused is the first question to ask.

**5. Local Maximum Trap**
You've climbed the visible hill but can't see the taller mountain behind the clouds. Going backwards in revenue is sometimes the only path forward. The ego attachment to your current revenue number is the #1 reason people stay trapped at a local max. Apply whenever a founder is stuck at a ceiling they can't seem to break through.

**6. Supply-Constrained vs. Demand-Constrained Reframe**
Most entrepreneurs are trained to solve demand problems (get more customers). Supply-constrained businesses (not enough practitioners, not enough talent) require a completely different playbook — treat talent acquisition with the same urgency, creativity, and budget as customer acquisition. Calculate gross profit per employee to size the right referral/recruiting bounty.

**7. The Avatar Constraint Rule**
Never switch avatars until you've exhausted the current one. New avatar = new offer + new acquisition + new team + new everything. It's a new business. If you have the itch for new, get a second *channel* first. Changing customers is the riskiest move in business.

**8. Customer Profitability Analysis (Demographics → Psychographics → Done)**
Look at your best historical customers: who they are (demos), what they were doing when they arrived (context/readiness signals), and what they *did* in your program that drove outcomes. The intersection is your true ICP. Rebuild all messaging, offers, pricing, and qualification gates around this person.

**9. Behavioral Specificity in Feedback**
Every abstract trait ("lazy," "not a culture fit," "not a leader") is just a cluster of unspecified behaviors. Your job as a leader is to decompose the label into observable actions — what they should *stop, start, and keep*. You cannot change what you cannot describe. Morphous terminology is a cop-out that protects the leader's ego at the expense of the employee's growth.

**10. The Constraint-First Growth Model**
Before launching anything new, answer: "Why can't I do more of what's already working?" The answer to that question *is* the constraint. Solve the constraint. The common mistake is misattributing the constraint (assuming it's the channel or the model when it's really sales efficiency, ops, or creative quality) and launching something new to avoid solving it.

**11. BAM FAM (Book a Meeting From a Meeting)**
Never end an interaction without securing the next one on the calendar in real time. Close every group session with "let's all pull up our calendars right now." Pair with automated reminders plus a manual outreach from the session host. Use to solve consumption/attendance problems before addressing any deeper retention levers.

**12. Rollup / Synergy vs. Concentrated Focus (PE Strategy)**
Two viable paths at scale: (a) concentrate on 4–6 businesses you understand deeply and inject capital/talent; (b) go vertical with 10+ same-type businesses, centralize costs, distribute best practices. The death zone is the middle — 14 dissimilar companies with no synergy playbook and insufficient holdco bandwidth. Pick the identity, then execute the corresponding path.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Customer/attendee wronged by a mistake you made | **Script:** "I'm sorry this happened. Here's what we're going to do — [overcompensate specifically]. We want to make sure you walk away better off than if this never happened."

**Situation:** Underperforming employee conversation opener | **Script:** "Hey, I haven't felt like I've seen a lot of production from you compared to what I think you're capable of. I'm curious — is it that you don't know what I want done, how to do it, when it's due, or is something blocking you?"

**Situation:** Someone didn't complete a task | **Script:** "Did you not know I wanted you to do it? Did you not know what I wanted? Did you not know how to do it? Did you not know when? Or was there something blocking you?" — run through sequentially before assuming motivation.

**Situation:** Setting a deadline with a team member | **Script:** "How long will the actual work take — not from now until you deliver it, just the work itself? And what do you have going on between now and then? What else is on your plate?"

**Situation:** Giving behavioral feedback instead of trait feedback | **Script:** "Hey [name], I need you to stop [specific behavior]. I need you to start [specific behavior]. Keep doing [specific behavior]."

**Situation:** Someone says "no one can do X like me" | **Script:** "That's not a fact. That's a statement only true in your mind right now. Let me reframe it: 'I don't yet know *how* to find someone who can do X like me.' That's solvable."

**Situation:** Founder wants to launch a new avatar/channel before exhausting current one | **Script:** "What stops you from 10x-ing what you're currently doing? Answer that question first. Because the problems you're running from exist on the new path too."

**Situation:** Closing an AB offer for individual vs. group onboarding | **Script:** "Would you rather start your program one-on-one with [coach name] or jump right into the group? … Great. So this is the investment for the one-on-one track — that's what gives you the best shot at results."

**Situation:** Selling group coaching, reduce churn via onboarding | **Script:** "Before you go into the group, I want to make sure you're set up for success — so we're going to do [4–6] sessions together first. Once you've gone through those, you'll graduate into the group program. Most people find they get way more out of it after the one-on-ones."

**Situation:** Speaking engagement as acquisition — scarcity of events | **Script:** "How many events are you speaking at per year? … One. Okay. And every time you speak you make [X]? Then let's get to 50 events next year and start there."

**Situation:** Referral incentive for supply-constrained talent recruiting | **Script:** "I'll give you [X] for every [practitioner/employee] you refer who stays [Y months]. [X] is calibrated to the $[GP/employee/year] they generate." — size the bounty to the gross profit, not what feels reasonable.

**Situation:** Transitioning off bad customers | **Script:** "Each sales rep gets a quota of [N] legacy-tier customers per month to close cash flow. Everyone else must meet the new ICP criteria. If it doesn't fit, we pass."

**Situation:** Firing an employee you hired by mistake, keeping the relationship | **Script:** "I messed this up — not you. I'm giving you [2–4 weeks] severance because that's on me. I want to leave the door open. When we fix this, I want you to know you'd be the first call I make."

**Situation:** Motivating a disengaged salesperson on a repetitive script | **Script:** "Your ability to maintain focus on the same conversation over and over again is itself a skill that separates good salespeople from great ones. You said you wanted to be world class — boredom is the skill deficiency between where you are and there."

**Situation:** Employee uses vague excuse for non-delivery | **Script:** "Okay — was that obstacle more important than this task? … How hard would it have been to resolve it? … How long did it block you? … Three days? Let's talk about what agency looks like here."

**Situation:** Prospect or client who got a bad experience | **Script:** "The people you wrong and then super-compensate become your biggest ambassadors. Let's not give a refund and call it done — let's figure out what would make this person *more* than whole."

## DECISION RULES

1. **When a founder wants to switch avatars or start something new:** Ask "Why can't you do more of what's already working?" If they can't give a bulletproof answer, don't switch.

2. **When diagnosing a churn or retention problem:** Get attribution data *first*. Don't ideate solutions without data. The data makes the solution obvious.

3. **When someone doesn't do what you asked:** Run STAR before assigning blame — know, what, how, when, blocked — in that order.

4. **When giving feedback on a behavioral problem:** Never use trait labels. Decompose into specific observable behaviors they should stop, start, or keep.

5. **When sizing a referral or recruiting bounty:** Calculate annual gross profit per employee/customer first, then size the bounty as a fraction of Year 1 GP — not as a flat "reasonable" number.

6. **When setting timelines:** Default to end of day. Force the question "How long will the work itself take?" not just "When can you get it to me?"

7. **When you know something needs to happen eventually:** Do it today. Don't wait for the market or the tide to force it.

8. **When your CAC is rising faster than platform CPMs:** You have a reputation problem from bad customers. Fix the avatar, not the ads.

9. **When a customer has a tragic experience:** Don't just refund — over-deliver to convert them from a detractor into an ambassador.

10. **When onboarding new members into a group program:** Run 4–6 one-on-one sessions first to earn their trust and make the group feel like a prize they graduated into.

11. **When you have a supply-constrained business:** Treat recruiting with the same core-four acquisition framework as customer acquisition — warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, organic content.

12. **When a business has multiple acquisition channels all "working somewhat":** Find which one has the highest leverage (lowest time or money per customer) and 10x that one before touching the others.

13. **When training someone on a complex skill:** Chunk it down until you find the *specific sub-skill* they're missing. Train that sub-skill, not the parent label.

14. **When evaluating a PE portfolio:** Do the 80/20 — identify the 20% driving 80% of enterprise value, go passive or divest the rest, double down on the winners.

15. **When someone is stuck mid-market (custom price, commodity customer):** They are structurally unable to scale — either go fully templated/DIY at low price or go custom at high price. The middle is where businesses die.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Solving the wrong constraint.** Building a second channel or new avatar when your real problem is sales efficiency, churn, or ops. The problems you run from follow you to the new path.

2. **Giving morphous feedback.** "Be less creepy." "Stop being lazy." "Be a better leader." No one knows what to do with that. You're protecting your own ego at the expense of their performance.

3. **No data before ideating.** Brainstorming hypothetical solutions to a problem you haven't measured is circular. Nine times out of ten the entrepreneur can't even articulate the data that precedes the problem.

4. **Saying yes to everyone.** The telltale sign of a small business is selling to anyone who shows up. Qualification = respect for your own model. The best agencies in the world can't be hired by a dry cleaner.

5. **Chasing B2B to escape B2C problems.** B2B Enterprise sales adds multi-stakeholder complexity, longer sales cycles, and lower deal velocity. The problems you have in B2C exist in B2B with more surface area.

6. **Revenue ego attachment.** Refusing to take a temporary revenue step down to fix the foundation. A local maximum is not the summit. The fastest path to $10M is sometimes to temporarily leave $1M.

7. **Overpromising to close bad-fit customers.** Once you promise salvation to someone who needs a savior, you've set an expectation no delivery can meet. Every sale like this degrades your reputation.

8. **Treating everything as motivation-first.** Jumping to "they're not motivated" before ruling out: they didn't know, didn't understand, weren't trained, didn't have a deadline, or were actually blocked. Motivation is the *last* variable, not the first.

9. **Verbal-only commitments.** "We talked about it" is not accountability. If it isn't written, it never happened. No timestamp = no proof = no standard to hold anyone to.

10. **Building headcount on a bad customer base.** You hire to serve volume you created by selling the wrong people. Now you can't stop selling the wrong people without laying people off. Don't scale a broken foundation.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**1. The reframe-then-rename move.**
Name the real problem the person is presenting, then rename it to reveal the underlying one. *"She was presenting with a low consumption issue — which translated into low renewal — which translated into high churn. The issue that precedes that is low consumption."* Always trace symptoms back to root cause through a chain of logic.

**2. The math-close.**
Convert abstract incentive into specific dollar numbers to make the decision feel obvious. *"How much do you make in gross profit off a physical therapist per year? $350K. So if I told you I could add $350,000 to your business, how much would you pay for that? … So giving someone $500 for a referral — you see why that doesn't move people."*

**3. Hypothetical-future-state compression.**
Fast-forward to the logical end of the current trajectory to make inaction feel untenable. *"If you choose to play like everyone else, you will lose. Period."* Then offer the alternative path.

**4. Inoculate the objection by naming it first.**
State the objection the listener is forming before they voice it, then defuse it. *"You might hear this and be like, 'That sounds like such a pain.' But it takes two or three extra minutes and saves two or three hours. Clarity is high-leverage work."*

**5. Analogize to something visceral and simple.**
Use universally understood analogies to collapse complex business concepts. Eggs/omelette for resource blockers. Potato telephone for organizational communication decay. Hill-climbing for local maxima. Baby learning to walk for "everything is trainable."

**6. Triple-hit structure.**
State the point → prove it with a data point or story → restate in action terms. Rarely makes a claim without immediately grounding it in an observable fact or a concrete consequence. *"Most business owners are broke. Average income ~$50K. If that's true, then the vast majority are wildly underperforming their aspirations. And a big part of that is because they model people like them."*

**7. Permission-then-punch.**
Soften a hard truth with an acknowledgment of the person's situation, then deliver the diagnosis without hedging. *"I don't say this from a pulpit — I've been through this myself and I know these pains personally. … Don't do this."* Creates trust before delivering the confronting insight.

**8. Convert "no one can" statements into "I don't yet know how to."**
Every time a founder makes a limiting absolute statement, reframe it as an open question. *"'No one can sell like me' is a statement you can't do anything with. 'I don't know how to find someone who can sell like me' — that's solvable."*
