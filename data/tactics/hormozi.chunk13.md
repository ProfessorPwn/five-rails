## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Offer Wrapper / Seasonal Skin**
Every offer needs a reason to exist right now — not a new business, just a new wrapper. Slap a seasonal or event-based frame around your existing offer: anniversary, holiday, milestone. Doesn't matter if the reason is thin; the wrapper creates legitimate urgency. Apply this any time you need a promotional hook without reinventing the product.

**The Give-to-Ask Ratio**
The amount of value you give before the ask is almost directly proportional to the revenue you generate when you ask. Butcher Box ran free, no-CTA cooking classes for weeks before ever linking to purchase. Calculate your ratio: if you're at 70% asks / 30% give (old New Balance model), flip it — and hold for 18+ months before judging results.

**Hyper Buying Cycles**
Buyers enter a purchase window — a moment of intent — and will buy multiple adjacent things if you keep them in the flow. Someone decides to run a 5K: they want shoes, shorts, and an armband in one session. If you break that flow (redirect, friction, extra pages), they re-enter the cycle at a competitor. Your job is to identify the window and eliminate all exits from it.

**Frictionless Checkout / Meet Them Where They Are**
Every step between desire and purchase costs you a compounding percentage of conversions. QVC built concurrent video shopping — you watch, you click a thumbnail, you're checked out without leaving the video. Audit your funnel: every redirect, every form field, every page load is a leak. Take one step toward the customer — it produces an order-of-magnitude improvement.

**The Pre-Test / Data Flywheel**
Early market presence isn't just about early sales — it's about generating data before your competition has any. Gymshark's 12-week popup let them know their top holiday sellers in October. Fly By Jing's advent calendar tested 2025 SKUs with 2024 buyers. Every campaign should double as a data-collection mechanism; label what you're learning, not just what you're selling.

**Gamification as Data Capture**
Quizzes, challenges, and interactive unlocks aren't gimmicks — they're high-conversion data-collection events. BarkBox ran a pet quiz to unlock a discount; they left with pet name, breed, age, and buyer profile for precision remarketing. Rule: if you're going to give a discount anyway, make the customer earn it with information. You get data; they feel agency.

**Collaborate Before You Compete**
When facing a larger incumbent, partnership often beats head-to-head competition. BK Beauty partnered with Maybelline and L'Oreal rather than competing for their TikTok audience. The small player brings speed and innovation; the large player brings budget and distribution. Ask: is there a Goliath whose goal I can attach my growth to?

**Own the Media, Own the Ad Spend**
When you acquire or build media, you're buying your competitor's ad inventory at a one-time price. Butcher Box acquired Truffle Shuffle (cooking classes) and got a perpetual content channel their competitors have to pay to access. Every piece of owned media is amortized ad spend with compounding brand equity.

**The Niche TAM Expansion Model**
Stop calculating TAM as "share of existing buyers." Start calculating TAM as "everyone I could convert from a worse alternative." Little Sleepies doesn't compete for existing pajama buyers — it competes against sleeping naked, old t-shirts, and Mumus. Define your real competition as the behavioral alternative, then expand toward it.

**Content Volume as Signal Filter**
Post volume is not vanity — it's a signal-extraction mechanism. Running 450 short-form pieces a week to identify 1-2 long-form winners is a valid production strategy. You're not making 450 pieces of content; you're running 450 experiments to find the ones that earn the long-form slot. Only make long-form from confirmed winners.

**The Education = Behavior Change Model**
Marketing's job is to change behavior under the same market conditions. If someone comes in as a commodity buyer and leaves as a brand loyalist, you educated them. Structure every content piece, every email, every video around one behavioral shift you want the viewer to make — not one product you want them to buy.

**Channel Agnosticism / Default Global**
There is no "online" or "offline." There is no "TikTok brand" or "TV brand." There is only: where does the customer want to buy, and can I be there? Gymshark is an online brand crushing it in physical retail. BK Beauty is a TikTok brand also on The View. Default global on day one — Canada is three hours away with 40M people and the same household income.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Launching a promotion without a real holiday | Script: "I'm celebrating [anniversary / milestone / birthday] — and because of that, everything [relevant category] is [offer]. Good through [specific date]."

**Situation:** Prospect objects to price, pauses more than 3 seconds | Script: "It sounds like that number landed wrong — what does fair look like to you?"

**Situation:** Creating a first-purchase offer for new buyers | Script: "Get your first [product] for $[anchor-breaking low price] — we'll take the risk because we know once you try it, you'll stay."

**Situation:** Running a discount without eroding brand perception | Script: Use a checkout code rather than showing a struck-through price. The retail value stays intact on the page; the savings materialize at the moment of commitment.

**Situation:** Directing site visitors during a promotional period | Script: Reduce navigation to 3-4 items max. Remove everything that isn't the sale. Every remaining link goes to a sale category. Leave breadcrumbs, not a maze.

**Situation:** Educating a cold lead without making a hard pitch | Script: "Here's everything you need to know about [specific topic they care about] — [60-second or short video]. No pitch, just the information." Run this weekly on every relevant platform.

**Situation:** Someone dismisses your niche as too small | Script: "You're calculating TAM as a share of existing buyers. I'm calculating TAM as everyone I could convert from doing nothing, or from a worse alternative. Those are different numbers by an order of magnitude."

**Situation:** Justifying a long brand-building runway with no immediate ROI | Script: "New Balance ran 70% brand / 30% direct for 18 months with zero payoff. Month 19 it went vertical. The question isn't whether it works — it's whether you can hold long enough to find out."

**Situation:** Competitor has more budget than you | Script: "They compete with capital. We compete with creativity and things that don't scale. A handwritten note from the CEO sells 10 machines and costs 8 minutes. They can't do that."

**Situation:** Explaining why you're giving away free content | Script: "Marketing is education. Every piece of content I put out is changing one behavior. When they're ready to buy, they already know who they trust."

**Situation:** Making the case for a quiz or challenge before discount | Script: "Instead of giving you the discount outright, we want to make sure we're actually recommending the right thing for you. Takes 60 seconds — and if you finish, you unlock [offer]."

**Situation:** Advising on site structure during a high-intent buying period | Script: "If your site looks the same on Black Friday as it does in March, you're leaving money on the table. The site should scream: this is for right now, it ends tonight."

**Situation:** Advising someone to stop trying to compete with everyone | Script: "You don't have to beat them everywhere. Be the mayor of one small niche. Own it completely. Then grow the pie outward from there."

**Situation:** Pitching a collaboration to a larger brand | Script: "We know how to do [TikTok / content / community] better than you do right now. Instead of competing for the same audience, why don't we do a collaboration — you get distribution on our platform, we get your credibility."

**Situation:** Entrepreneur doubting legitimacy of their business | Script: "You have a product. You sell it. Customers are happy. Money is exchanged. You own a business. The only thing that can invalidate that is quitting."

**Situation:** Pushing for urgency without gimmicks | Script: "Authorized for today. The banner should say exactly what day it is and why that matters. Make it so obvious they know they're in the right place at the right time."

**Situation:** Explaining the value of a loss-leader first offer | Script: "We know our LTV. We can afford to sell the first box at $7 because we know what the customer is worth over 12 months. You're not losing money — you're buying a relationship."

**Situation:** Defending content-first strategy to a skeptic | Script: "If you're an accountant and the tax code just changed, a 60-second breakdown of the new section owns that search. PWC can't put that out in under 6 weeks — you can put it out today."

**Situation:** Introducing scarcity around an unscalable offer | Script: "We made [X quantity]. That's it. It's not coming back. If you're a [hardcore fan / early adopter / serious buyer], this is the moment."

**Situation:** Pitching the advent calendar / drip product model | Script: "They buy once. Then they get something [daily / weekly]. There's an element of surprise, mystery, discovery — and you get the data on what they actually want to see from you next year."

## DECISION RULES

**When you're running a promotion, narrow the navigation** — reduce site links to 3-4 maximum, all pointing to the offer. Paradox of choice kills conversion; breadcrumbs close sales.

**When deciding how to spend your first promotional budget**, allocate proportional to the revenue opportunity. If Black Friday can equal a month of revenue in 3 days, treat it like an additional month of the year in preparation resources.

**When a competitor has more capital**, compete with things that don't scale: handwritten notes, founder video, personalized outreach. These compound into word-of-mouth that their ad budget cannot replicate.

**When calculating your market size**, define your competition as the behavioral alternative (the couch, sleep, doing nothing), not other businesses in your category.

**When giving a discount**, make the customer earn it — quiz, challenge, or quiz completion — so you capture data that funds future remarketing. Never discount for free.

**When determining what content to make long-form**, only promote to long-form after short-form has confirmed it as a winner. Volume creates signal; signal informs investment.

**When you're early on a platform that's pushing a new feature** (TikTok Shop, YouTube native, Spotify podcast), go all-in immediately. Platform-native integrations outperform off-platform links by an order of magnitude while the platform is still subsidizing growth.

**When your conversion window opens** (buyer enters hyper-buying mode), do not redirect, add friction, or interrupt the session. Extend the buying window; do not break it.

**When an incumbent won't do something** (move fast, test publicly, write personal notes, make a 60-second video), do it. Their committee-review cycle is your moat.

**When launching a new product or SKU**, put it inside an existing campaign (advent calendar, bundle, challenge reward) before going standalone. You get real buyer signal from your warmest audience before full commitment.

**When you want platform adoption of an app or offer**, pair the incentive with a fun, cheeky, shareable mechanic — not just a discount. The mechanic creates earned media; the discount just creates margin compression.

**When someone says your niche is too small**, your response: stop calculating share of existing pie, start calculating how much pie you can create by converting non-buyers.

**When deciding give vs. ask ratio in content**, default to majority give until the audience is primed. 18 months of brand building before the flywheel kicks is normal for cold audiences. Don't measure ROI until month 6 minimum.

**When your site is not optimized for a specific date or event**, add a seasonal skin. You don't need a new offer — just a wrapper that makes it timely. The reason justifies the promotion.

**When you feel outgunned by a larger competitor**, look for a collaboration angle before a competition angle. Their scale + your speed = a deal that neither could execute alone.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Discounting without a reason** — every promotion needs a frame (holiday, milestone, launch, event). A discount without a story is just margin erosion; a discount with a story is a buying event.

**Showing all your products during a high-intent moment** — running 24 jars of jam gets more browsers, fewer buyers. During peak buying periods, narrow to 3-4 hero SKUs. More choice = fewer decisions.

**Measuring brand spend at month 3** — the New Balance model didn't pay off until month 19. Measuring brand investment on a direct-response timeline kills legitimate brand strategy before it can compound.

**Redirecting buyers out of the buying experience** — making someone leave a video to go to a checkout page, adding form fields mid-session, requiring account creation before purchase. Every exit is a conversion killer.

**Competing head-to-head with incumbents on their terms** — they have the budget, the distribution, the brand equity. Going head-to-head on paid spend is a losing bet unless you have equivalent capital. Find the collaboration angle or the uncontested niche.

**Calculating TAM as "share of existing buyers"** — this caps your thinking at the current pie size. Real TAM includes everyone doing the inferior alternative. Model against the behavioral competition, not the product competition.

**Building for hypothetical customers instead of testing with real ones** — Fly By Jing put experimental sauces in an advent calendar before committing to a full SKU launch. Test with your warmest audience before scaling production.

**Treating your promotional period the same as your evergreen period** — if your Black Friday site looks like your March site, you're not signaling urgency. Buyers need to feel they're in the right place at the right time or they'll wait.

**Asking before giving** — launching a hard CTA before the audience has received disproportionate value. Butcher Box ran full cooking classes with zero CTAs. The ask lands harder when it follows genuine generosity.

**Assuming small market = small business** — niche-first thinking isn't a ceiling; it's a launchpad. Little Sleepies sells pajamas. Lola Blankets sells blankets. Specificity creates trust, which creates conversion, which creates scale.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The triple-beat declarative**: "No regressions. No missing imports. No broken routes." — Hormozi uses staccato three-part lists to land a rule with finality. Structure: [no X. no Y. no Z.] or [do X. do Y. do Z.]

**The ratio reframe**: "If you're spending 70% on asks and 30% on give — flip that." He constantly converts abstract strategic advice into specific ratios, percentages, or time windows. Makes the abstract executable.

**The "what that actually means is" pivot**: Translates a case study into a principle in one sentence. "What that really means is: when the consumer is ready to buy, you must not make them leave." Always moves from the specific to the transferable.

**The double-dip / triple-dip frame**: "How can I get a double-dip or triple-dip on one move?" He evaluates every tactic by how many simultaneous wins it produces — sales + data + PR + brand equity in a single campaign.

**The couch competitor reframe**: Whenever someone names a business competitor, he redirects to the behavioral alternative. "Your biggest competition is the couch. Netflix's biggest competition is sleep." Expands the frame of the market immediately.

**The math-in-your-head gut check**: "$3.4 million a minute. 28,000 orders per minute. While we've been on this show, another $50 million in sales happened." He grounds abstract scale claims in specific numbers the listener can run in their head — creates visceral believability.

**Give first, ask once, mean it**: Every story he tells about brand strategy follows the same arc — extended period of generosity, minimal asks, then one clear call when the audience is primed. He tells it through case studies (Butcher Box, Gymshark, New Balance), not as abstract advice, which makes it feel like observed truth rather than opinion.
