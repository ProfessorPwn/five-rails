## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**Build to Learn vs. Build to Earn**
Product managers and designers exist to figure out *which* solution is worth building — that's build to learn (product discovery). Engineers exist to build something customers can depend on at scale — that's build to earn. You must complete build-to-learn before committing engineering effort; shipping first and learning after is customer abuse.

**The Three Models of Product Development**
Every company runs one of three models: (1) the Agile product-owner/backlog-management model — dying fast; (2) the project model — stakeholders drive roadmaps of features, PM gathers requirements, ~15% success rate; (3) the product model — teams own outcomes, not outputs. Know which model you're in before taking any career or coaching advice, because the job description is entirely different in each.

**The Product Trio**
Product manager, designer, and engineering tech lead form the core discovery unit. PM owns value (will customers buy/use it?) and viability (does it work for the business — legal, finance, marketing, sales). Designer owns usability. Engineer owns feasibility. No one role can credibly cover all three judgment areas at scale; triple threats exist but aren't replicable at organizational scale.

**The Four Dimensions of a Great Solution (Value / Usability / Feasibility / Viability)**
A solution must clear all four bars to ship. Value: does the customer choose this over alternatives? Usability: can they actually use it? Feasibility: can engineering build and maintain it? Viability: does it work for the business across legal, compliance, marketing, revenue, and ops? The product manager is personally accountable for value and viability — those are the hardest and most differentiating dimensions.

**Product Strategy → Discovery → Delivery (The Three Big Challenges)**
Strategy = deciding the most important problems to solve (product leader's job). Discovery = finding a solution that solves those problems better than competitors (product team's job). Delivery = building a production-quality solution (engineering's job, now dramatically faster with AI). AI has commoditized delivery; the real moat is now strategy and discovery quality.

**The Triple Threat**
A person who has developed genuine judgment — product sense, design sense, and engineering architecture sense — across all three disciplines. Not someone who can operate tools (anyone can learn that), but someone whose brain holds the learned expertise of all three. Rare, valuable, not scalable — celebrate them as outliers, don't plan your org around them.

**The Product Model's OKR Connection**
OKRs were literally created for the product model. The Objective = the problem to solve. The Key Results = the measurable outcome that defines success. A product team's job is to discover and ship a solution that moves those key results. If you're using OKRs but still handing teams feature roadmaps, you've broken the loop.

**Day One vs. Day Two Companies**
A Day One company uses process as a tool; a Day Two company has been taken over by process as a substitute for thinking. Every time you follow a framework without thinking about whether it fits, you're behaving like a Day Two company. The PM's irreplaceable contribution is thinking — judgment that can't be prompted away.

**Responsible Experimentation**
Big experiments are necessary for real breakthroughs — not trivial A/B color changes. But established companies must run experiments in ways that protect customers, protect revenue, and don't pull the rug out from under customer success. Exposing paying customers to constant unpredictable change is customer abuse. The principle: be bold in hypothesis, disciplined in execution.

**Premortem as a Discovery Technique**
Before committing to a solution, explicitly walk through every way this could fail. Not a pessimism exercise — a structured forcing function to surface assumptions you haven't stress-tested. Combine with LLM tools to generate failure modes you'd otherwise miss. This is the kind of thinking no tool can do for you.

**AI as Personal Product Coach**
Configure a language model explicitly to operate in the product model (tell it which model you're working in). Use it to develop product sense — deep knowledge of customers, data, stakeholder constraints, competitive landscape — not to generate PRDs or prompts. 24/7 access to product coaching with no additional cost; game-changing for people without access to great senior PMs.

**Mission vs. Vision vs. Strategy**
Mission: the problem you exist to solve (many competitors can share the same mission). Vision: how you will solve it over the next 5–10 years — the product-level approach that distinguishes you. Strategy: specifically which problems you'll solve this quarter. PMs must understand all three but should primarily own the strategy-to-discovery execution layer, not confuse the levels.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Executive asks why delivery speed hasn't moved the needle | **Script:** "We're building faster than ever, which is great. But the bottleneck was never engineering. The bottleneck is whether we're working on the right problems and whether our solutions are better than the alternatives. Speed of delivery just makes that gap more visible."

**Situation:** Leadership says "you have AI tools now, you don't need a designer/engineer on your team" | **Script:** "The tools lower the cost of operating — anyone can learn to use them. What they can't provide is the judgment. Product sense, design sense, architecture sense — those come from years of learning what good actually looks like. If we remove that expertise, we'll ship faster and fail faster."

**Situation:** Interviewing for a product role, given a problem to solve | **Script:** "Here's the prototype I'd build first and why — it tests the riskiest assumption. I'd run [qualitative/quantitative] testing because we need to learn [specific thing]. Our success metric is [outcome], so we know we're done when [result]."

**Situation:** PM complains their calendar is wall-to-wall with meetings | **Script:** "That's a signal you're playing project manager, not product manager. The real job — building to learn — looks more like an engineer or designer: head down, thinking, prototyping. Offload the coordination work. Your value to the team is not running standups."

**Situation:** Asked to defend why you don't just generate your narrative with AI | **Script:** "Writing the narrative is the thinking. If I prompt an LLM to write it, I'm outsourcing the judgment, not amplifying it. I can use AI to pressure-test the narrative, surface blind spots, sharpen the language — but the synthesis has to be mine."

**Situation:** Stakeholder pushes a feature onto the roadmap | **Script:** "I want to understand the outcome you're trying to achieve with this. If we can agree on the result we need, my team can discover the best solution — which might be this feature, or might be something faster or cheaper that delivers the same outcome."

**Situation:** Team is debating which prototype to build first | **Script:** "What's the riskiest assumption in this solution? That's what we prototype first. We're not trying to build the best demo — we're trying to kill the idea fastest if it's wrong."

**Situation:** PM says "we're following the agile process" as justification | **Script:** "Process is a tool, not a defense. What problem are we trying to solve? What outcome are we chasing? What's our evidence that this approach gets us there? The process doesn't answer those — we have to."

**Situation:** Aspiring PM asks how to break in | **Script:** "Find someone who's built in the product model and ask them to mentor you. Within six months you'll understand things about product that years of the project model won't teach you. If you can't find that person, use an AI coach configured for the product model — and read Inspired."

**Situation:** PM asks how to handle a solution that only partially solves the customer problem | **Script:** "Not enough. Your customers have more choices than ever. It's not sufficient to do the job — you have to do it dramatically better than everything else out there, including the option of doing nothing."

**Situation:** CEO wants to apply AI to speed up the roadmap | **Script:** "AI is making delivery faster — that's real. But the 15% success rate on roadmap features isn't an engineering problem. We're building the wrong things faster. The investment needs to go into strategy and discovery, not just delivery velocity."

**Situation:** Evaluating whether a PM is actually doing product or project management | **Script:** "Are you being given problems to solve and outcomes to hit? Or are you being given features to ship? If it's features, you're a project manager with a product manager title. Those are completely different jobs."

**Situation:** Team debates whether to run a big bold experiment on a live product | **Script:** "We need to do this in a way that protects our paying customers and doesn't pull the rug out from the customer success team. Big hypothesis, but disciplined execution. What's the minimum exposure we need to get a real signal?"

**Situation:** PM is overwhelmed and behind on learning new tools | **Script:** "The 60-hour weeks usually mean someone is trying to do both product and project management. Offload the project management work — scheduling, status, coordination. The head-down discovery work is the actual job and it's much more sustainable."

**Situation:** PM asks whether to invest time in AI prototyping tools | **Script:** "Absolutely, and non-negotiably. Anyone not learning these tools is going to be obsolete quickly if they aren't already. But using the tools is table stakes. What makes you valuable is the product sense you bring to deciding what to build and why."

## DECISION RULES

**When you're given a roadmap of features instead of outcomes to achieve** → You're in the project model. The decisions have been made by someone else. You are a project manager with a product manager title. Decide whether to change companies or change how your organization operates.

**When delivery is getting faster but business results aren't improving** → The bottleneck is strategy or discovery, not engineering. Stop optimizing delivery. Invest in finding the right problems (strategy) and validating solutions (discovery).

**When a company asks you to collapse the product trio into one role** → Test whether the person truly has developed judgment across all three disciplines — not just tool access. If they haven't, the quality of solutions will drop and failures will be harder to diagnose.

**When deciding what to prototype first** → Identify the riskiest assumption. Prototype to kill the idea quickly if it's wrong, not to make it look impressive.

**When an executive escalates a feature request** → Translate the feature into the outcome they want. Get alignment on the outcome. Let the team discover the solution.

**When evaluating whether your experiments are responsible** → Ask: does this protect paying customers? Does it protect our revenue? Does it avoid pulling the rug out from under customer success? Big hypotheses are fine; undisciplined execution is customer abuse.

**When a PM can't make decisions without escalating everything** → They lack product sense. They don't have sufficient knowledge of customers, data, business constraints, competitive landscape. That's the gap to close — not process, not tools.

**When product sense is weak and no senior PM coach is available** → Configure an LLM explicitly in the product model and use it as a product coaching engine. Not to generate outputs — to develop judgment through dialogue.

**When a PM's day is wall-to-wall meetings** → They're playing project manager. Real discovery work — thinking, prototyping, testing — doesn't live in meetings. Restructure their week or they won't be doing the actual job.

**When evaluating whether a solution is good enough** → Apply all four bars: Is it valuable enough that customers choose it over alternatives? Is it usable? Is it feasible for engineering? Is it viable for the business? All four must pass. Value is the battlefield.

**When AI speeds up delivery on a weak strategy** → That's garbage in, garbage out faster. Accelerating a feature factory doesn't fix the strategy problem. It makes it more visible.

**When choosing between breadth of tools and depth of product sense** → Depth wins. Tools are learnable in days. Product sense — deep customer knowledge, business constraints, competitive landscape — takes years and is your actual contribution to the team.

**When a team produces a written narrative using AI** → Verify the PM can defend every claim and judgment in it without the AI present. If they can't, the thinking was abdicated, not amplified.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**The Feature Factory Trap** — Using AI to ship more features faster without changing the strategy or discovery quality. The 15% success rate of roadmap features isn't an engineering problem; accelerating it just produces failures faster and makes the real problem impossible to ignore.

**Process as a Substitute for Thinking** — "We follow the agile process" as a justification for a decision. Day Two companies run on process. Day One companies use process as a tool in service of thinking. If someone can't explain the reasoning behind a decision without pointing to a framework, the thinking isn't there.

**Abdicating Thinking to Tools** — Prompting an LLM to write the narrative, generate the PRD, or produce the strategy and calling it done. The written narrative *is* the thinking. Outsourcing it means you have no idea whether you're right, and you can't defend any of it.

**Automating Your Own Replacement** — PMs using AI to automate their project management tasks (scheduling, backlog grooming, status updates) without recognizing that those tasks are the only thing their role currently does. They're demonstrating the role has no floor.

**Conflating Mission with Vision with Strategy** — Treating "be the most personalized learning platform" as a strategy. It's a mission. Strategy is which specific problems you're solving this quarter. Conflating levels means no one is actually making the hard prioritization calls.

**Customer Abuse via Unconstrained Experimentation** — Running continuous large changes on paying customers without protecting their ability to depend on the product. Especially lethal once you have real enterprise customers. Big experiments are necessary; responsible execution of them is non-negotiable.

**Hiring Triple Threats as an Org Design Strategy** — Triple threats exist and are exceptional. They're not scalable. Planning your team topology around unicorns means your execution degrades the moment any one of them leaves.

**Teaching the Project Model as Product Management** — Most online courses, bootcamps, and university programs teach roadmap management, PRD writing, and feature prioritization. That's the project model. Learning it fluently and calling it product management is a category error that compounds over an entire career.

**Wall-to-Wall Calendars for PMs** — If a PM's day is entirely meetings, they are performing project coordination, not product discovery. Discovery is head-down work — thinking, prototyping, testing, learning. A meeting-filled schedule is a structural signal, not a workload signal.

**Escalation Dependency** — A PM who cannot make value and viability decisions without pushing them to leadership. Lack of product sense forces escalation. Escalation removes the team's ability to move fast and signals the PM isn't actually contributing to the outcome.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The "Two Things" Structure** — Cagan consistently separates complex topics into exactly two components before elaborating: "There are two things. First... Second..." — or "The tools are easy. The hard part is..." This creates immediate clarity and signals depth without overwhelming. Use it when distinguishing table-stakes from differentiating skills.

**The Direct Reframe** — When someone uses the wrong framing ("that's the product vision"), Cagan corrects it cleanly and immediately with one sentence, then explains why the distinction matters: "What you described is actually the mission. And it's worth pointing out lots of companies could have that exact mission." No hedging; the correction is the teaching.

**The Percentage/Stakes Anchor** — Grounds abstract claims in specific numbers to create credibility: "15% of what you have on your roadmap actually delivers a business result." "20 to 50 prototypes per week." "Team sizes going from eight people to one or two engineers." Use a real number before the insight, not after.

**The "To Be Clear" Bridge** — Used to anticipate the obvious objection or the charitable interpretation before dismissing or qualifying it: "To be clear, this model has been around forever. It's just not well distributed." Preempts defensiveness by showing you've already thought through the counter.

**The Escalation from Principle to Specific** — Cagan states a principle, then immediately gives a concrete operational manifestation: "You have to experiment — there's no way around it. The only question is how fast." Then immediately grounds it: "With Gen AI tools we can do not only better prototypes, but there's always been four kinds..." The pattern is: claim → why it's true → what it looks like in practice.

**The "Long Way of Saying" Pivot** — After a long explanation, Cagan uses "which is a long way of saying..." to deliver the single-sentence conclusion. Signals self-awareness, keeps listeners oriented, and ensures the bottom line lands: "Which is a long way of saying the third model, where the product manager is actually responsible for outcomes."

**The Sympathy + Brutal Clarity Pattern** — Leads with genuine empathy ("I have so much sympathy for aspiring product people"), then delivers an unvarnished diagnosis ("those jobs are in serious trouble," "that is not product management, it's project management"). The sympathy earns permission for the directness; the directness is the actual value.
