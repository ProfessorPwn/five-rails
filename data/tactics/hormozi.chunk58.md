## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Fallacy of the Perfect Pick**
Most people stall because they're hunting for the perfect strategy instead of executing the good-enough one they already have. The mountain has multiple paths up — the path matters far less than your willingness to walk it. Apply it: when you catch yourself researching a fifth option, stop. Pick the most sensible path in front of you and commit to it for a minimum of 12 months before evaluating.

**Culture = Rules of Reinforcement**
Culture isn't values on a wall — it's what actually gets rewarded and punished inside your business. Every ignored lateness, every uncelebrated win, every unkept standard teaches your team what's real. Apply it: audit your last 30 days of management behavior. List every moment you let bad behavior slide. Those silences are your actual culture.

**Start / Stop / Keep**
The only feedback framework you need for managing people. What do I want you to START doing that you're not doing? What do I want you to STOP doing that you are doing? What do I want you to KEEP doing that I'll keep reinforcing? Apply it: replace every "you need to be more accountable" conversation with a specific start/stop/keep list of observable behaviors.

**Translate Abstractions Into Behaviors**
No one knows what "accountability" means. You have to convert big words into small actions. "Accountability" becomes: text your team every morning, do end-of-week one-on-ones, give public kudos to top performers, privately drill anyone who missed their number. Apply it: take every value or competency in your business and ask "how would I know this happened?" until you have a physical behavior you can observe.

**The Four Components of Mental Toughness**
Mental toughness is not binary. It has four independent, measurable components: (1) **Tolerance** — how much it takes to knock you off your normal behavior; (2) **Fortitude** — how far you fall when you do get knocked off; (3) **Resilience** — how fast you return to baseline; (4) **Adaptability** — whether your new baseline is higher or lower than before. Apply it: rate yourself 1–10 on each. Your lowest score is the specific skill to train — not "toughness" in general.

**The Dice Game (Infinite Game Mental Model)**
Every entrepreneur is given a many-sided die. One side is green, the rest red. You don't know how many sides you have. The only two guarantees: the more you roll, the more greens you accumulate; if you quit, you lose. Apply it: when you hit a losing streak, reframe it as clearing red sides off the die. Comparison to other players is meaningless — you don't know if it's their hundredth or hundred-thousandth roll.

**Spell Out The Trade**
Every hard decision feels hard because you haven't clearly named what you're actually trading. Lay out Option A and Option B explicitly: what you gain, what you give up, what the delayed cost of staying put is. Apply it: when stuck on a decision, write "If I do nothing, I guarantee ___. If I make the trade, the immediate cost is ___ and the 12-month upside is ___." Then decide.

**New Money Behavior**
There are four ways to buy things: past money (savings), income money (monthly cash flow), debt money (future earnings), and new money (generating a specific new income stream to fund a specific purchase). The wealthiest people use new money for non-essential purchases. Apply it: before buying anything grandiose, ask "what underutilized asset or capacity do I have that I can deploy to generate exactly this amount?"

**Capacity Before Opportunity**
Opportunities visit everyone. Only people with capacity can recognize and capitalize on them. Build capacity — physical, financial, skill, network, attention — before you know exactly what you'll use it for. Apply it: if you don't know what to do next, default to: sleep on time, save money, add a skill, build an audience, expand your luck surface area.

**Stack Skills Like Compound Interest**
Skills multiply. Math alone = low value. Math + bookkeeping + accounting + taxes + M&A = extremely high value. Each new skill makes all previous skills worth more. Apply it: map your current skill stack and identify the adjacent skill that would most multiply the value of everything you already know.

**Commitment + Low Expectations = Sustainable Satisfaction**
Dissatisfaction is structurally caused by wanting the benefits of multiple mutually exclusive paths simultaneously. The fix is high commitment (eliminate alternatives) plus low expectations (reality will likely exceed them). Apply it: when you feel a creep of dissatisfaction, ask: "What expectation did I set arbitrarily that the world failed to meet?" Then drop the expectation or change reality — those are the only two levers.

**The Talent Arbitrage**
The 50th-percentile hire for most roles costs ~$75K/year. The 90th-percentile hire costs ~$115K. The 90th-percentile person is often 5x more effective for 30% more cost. That's the best ROI in business. Apply it: stop optimizing for payroll cost. Optimize for output per dollar. The lower the bar you set for who works for you, the lower the ceiling on your business.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Interviewing a candidate for a skilled role | **Script:** "What are you obsessed with? What do you do in your off time that has nothing to do with work?" — If their answer isn't the same domain you're hiring for, they're moonlighting to their passion on your clock.

**Situation:** Someone is not performing a stated value or principle | **Script:** "Show me what [accountability / excellence / ownership] looks like as a behavior. How would I know it happened? What would I physically observe?"

**Situation:** Business owner spread across too many initiatives | **Script:** "How much were you making when you had all of these? How much would you make if you cut everything except the best one and went all in for 12 months? Now, which number is bigger?"

**Situation:** Helping someone stuck on a hard business decision | **Script:** "Spell out the trade. If you do nothing, what does your life look like in 12 months? If you make the trade, what does it look like? Which version of yourself do you want to be?"

**Situation:** Owner afraid to raise prices | **Script:** "You're supply-constrained. That means you have more demand than you can handle. The fix is raise price — demand drops to a manageable level, margin goes up, you use that margin to hire talent, now you can scale. There are two or three order consequences here. Walk them out."

**Situation:** Owner won't increase ad spend past arbitrary ceiling | **Script:** "If I offered you: give me $10K, I'll give you $100K — or — give me $1M, I'll give you $2M, which do you take? You take the million. The relative return is lower. The absolute return is higher. That's the whole game."

**Situation:** Founder switching strategies every 90 days | **Script:** "You've been going down and restarting the path. Every time you switch, you lose all the altitude you gained. The path isn't the problem. The problem is talent, culture, or training. Which one is it?"

**Situation:** Building a high-performing team culture | **Script:** "Who you allow to stay in your business tells your team more about what you find acceptable than anything you ever say. Every behavior you don't correct is a behavior you just approved."

**Situation:** Someone wants to buy something expensive without a clear plan | **Script:** "You can buy it. But the deal is: you pay it off in a year. Can you do that? Great. Then don't worry about it — go make the money."

**Situation:** Employee not changing behavior after feedback | **Script:** "I don't want 'accountability' — I want: text your team every morning, do end-of-week reviews with every direct report, give public kudos to top performers by name. Can you do those four things?"

**Situation:** Founder feeling behind competitors | **Script:** "You don't know if that's their hundredth roll or their hundred-thousandth. The game's been going on for 5,000 years. The board gets wiped every 100 years. Your job is to stay in the game and keep rolling."

**Situation:** Someone grieving or dealing with hardship | **Script:** "Does the amount of time it takes you to recover have any bearing on how much you love that person? If not, would any reasonable person want the people they love to suffer? You suffer as long as you decide is adequate."

**Situation:** Making a painful business pivot | **Script:** "How can I make today feel worse so tomorrow feels better? Investments require your bank account to go down before it comes up. Muscles don't get bigger before they get smaller first. There's always a step back before the outsized return."

**Situation:** Someone wants to buy a luxury item they can't justify | **Script:** "What underutilized asset or time do you already have? Write yourself a swimming pool. Paul McCartney didn't use his income. He wrote a song. What's your version of that?"

**Situation:** Founder who can't grow past a plateau | **Script:** "It's been a year and nothing changed because you changed nothing. You behaved the same and got the same outputs. The change is painful — but staying is also a choice with a cost. What's the cost of staying?"

**Situation:** Business owner trying to attract better customers | **Script:** "Your messaging is attracting who it's attracting. If you say 'tired of your job,' you get consumers. If you want business owners, talk to business owners. Change the language, change the lead magnet, change the price point to make the economics work."

**Situation:** Someone demoralized by repeated failure | **Script:** "You're just clearing red sides off the die. You don't know how many you have left. The only guarantee is: keep rolling and eventually the green streak comes. Quit and you lose for certain."

**Situation:** Manager frustrated that team isn't following through | **Script:** "Your plan isn't working because no one's doing the plan. Why aren't they doing it? It's one of three things: culture — the rules of reinforcement are wrong. Talent — you have the wrong people. Training — they don't know what the behavior actually looks like."

**Situation:** Addressing a person low on resilience | **Script:** "As soon as you hit rock bottom on this, just reverse. It only controls you as long as you let it. Break the direct link between how you feel and how you act. Return to baseline as fast as possible — not because your feelings don't matter, but because your behavior doesn't have to mirror them."

**Situation:** Someone paralyzed choosing between two paths | **Script:** "You're standing at the door waiting for more information on an apparent contradiction. There is no more information. You have to make the choice. Every day you don't choose, you lose the benefits of both."

## DECISION RULES

**When you feel the urge to switch strategies,** diagnose first: is the issue talent, culture, or training? Fix the root cause before abandoning the path.

**When you're hiring,** ask what they obsess over in their off time. If it's not the skill you need them to dominate, pass — they'll always be moonlighting to their real passion.

**When you're supply-constrained,** raise prices. More margin → better talent → more capacity → scale.

**When you hit a plateau,** assume you haven't changed, not that your strategy is wrong. Same inputs = same outputs. What behavior needs to change first?

**When you're making a big purchase,** don't touch savings, income flow, or take on debt without a one-year payoff plan. Generate new money instead from underutilized assets.

**When you want to build a culture,** stop with the values posters. Ask: what specific behaviors do I want to see, and am I actually rewarding and punishing them consistently?

**When you're stuck on a hard decision,** spell out both sides explicitly: cost of staying, cost of the trade, delayed upside of the trade. Then decide. Hard decisions are only hard because the easy ones are gone.

**When you can't figure out what to do,** build capacity. Sleep. Save money. Add a skill. Build an audience. Expand your luck surface area. Then wait for the fat pitch.

**When an employee isn't performing,** don't fire abstractly. List 2–3 specific behaviors that aren't happening. Make it observable. "You're not being accountable" is useless. "You didn't send the morning check-in for 3 weeks" is actionable.

**When you feel dissatisfied,** ask: "What arbitrary expectation did I set that the world failed to meet?" Remove the expectation — it's as good as having the thing you want.

**When someone says they want to grow fast,** tell them they're asking for a sustained, intense period of pain and inadequacy. Make sure they want what they're actually asking for.

**When you're spread across multiple businesses,** prune the garden. Consolidation typically 10x's output vs. the spread-thin version — even if you're a highly capable operator.

**When training doesn't stick,** the abstraction level is too high. Translate every principle into the smallest observable unit of behavior. Test that the team can recite exactly what it looks like in action.

**When ad spend ROI appears to drop,** check whether absolute return is still going up. Most owners cap spend at break-even on margin, leaving all the profit on the table above that threshold.

**When you're new and feeling behind,** recognize you're early in the game, not late. You're clearing red sides off the die. The more you roll, the more greens accumulate. Stay in the game.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Strategy-switching as a coping mechanism.** Changing paths every 90 days isn't strategic iteration — it's avoiding the hard work of figuring out one path. Every switch resets you to zero altitude.

**Hiring for potential without obsession.** Someone who's smart and hardworking but not obsessed with the domain they're hired for will always underperform someone who thinks about it 24/7.

**Translating nothing into behavior.** Holding an off-site to define six "pillars" of leadership and then sending people back to do the same things they did before is not training. It's theater.

**Capping ad spend at break-even on acquisition.** This is leaving all your profit on the table. Break-even on the marginal spend is where the profit starts — not where it ends.

**Keeping bad hires to avoid conflict.** Who you allow to stay signals to your entire team what's acceptable. Tolerating under-performance is a culture-destroying action, not a neutral one.

**Wanting the benefits of all paths simultaneously.** You will build in dissatisfaction by design. Every path has costs. Name the costs of the path you're on and commit to them, or pick a different path — but you cannot have both.

**Comparing your roll count to others'.** You have no idea if someone is on roll 100 or roll 100,000. Comparison without context produces nothing except demoralization and quitting.

**Using savings or income for non-essential luxury purchases.** Draining existing resources for consumption items builds no capacity and creates scarcity anxiety. Generate new money from underutilized assets instead.

**Treating "just work harder" as the diagnosis.** Most business owners who are stuck aren't stuck because they lack hustle. They're stuck because they don't know what to do next — and that's a different problem with a different solution.

**Suffering proportional to love.** Lingering in low-resilience behavior does not prove caring or depth of feeling. You can choose how long the recovery takes; behavior does not have to mirror emotional state.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Restate the obvious, then undercut it.** Hormozi names what everyone already knows ("you should eat less, move more"), then immediately pivots to why knowing it is irrelevant — only doing it is. Pattern: "You already know this. The issue isn't knowledge. The issue is execution."

**The conditional stack.** He builds logic in layered if-then chains: "If you're supply-constrained → raise price → margin goes up → hire talent → no longer supply-constrained." Each step is stated flatly, then linked forward. No hedging.

**The personal confession pivot.** He discredits himself mid-argument to increase credibility: "I had nine businesses and wasn't making money. Looking back it's obvious. At the time, I thought I needed to change my strategy." Self-deprecation is used surgically to validate the audience's current struggle.

**The visceral analogy, then the walk-back.** He uses aggressive/dark analogies ("skin the cat," "beat the strength out of you"), then pauses to mock the imagery. This creates a rhythmic break that marks his transitions and humanizes otherwise harsh content.

**The flat declarative close.** After a long build, he lands with a short, blunt summary statement: "If you want everything from life, you will get nothing." No softening. No hedging. The contrast with the preceding explanation gives it weight.

**Repetition with escalation.** Key phrases repeat with slight variation each time, building to a crescendo: "Roll again. Roll again. Keep rolling. The more you roll, the more greens you get." The repetition is intentional — it's the mechanism, not filler.

**The anti-should stance.** He explicitly opts out of prescribing universal paths: "I'm not the should guy. Do whatever the hell you want. I'm just showing you the trade." This disarms defensiveness and allows him to deliver the same hard message without sounding moralistic.
