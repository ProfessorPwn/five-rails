## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Value Equation**
Four levers that determine perceived value: Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice). To increase value, increase the top two and crush the bottom two. Apply to every offer audit: if conversion is weak, diagnose which lever is suppressed before changing anything else.

**Supply vs. Demand Constraint Triage + The Constraint Accordion**
Every business is either supply-constrained (capacity, delivery, talent) or demand-constrained (leads, awareness) — never both simultaneously. Ask: "If I doubled ad budget tomorrow, would I double sales or create a mess I can't handle?" Constraints also alternate — solve demand well enough and supply becomes the bottleneck. Audit which constraint is active *now*, not which hurt last year. Never prescribe a growth tactic without running this diagnostic first.

**The Big Hairy Problem (BHP) / Enterprise Value Unlock**
Every business has one dominant constraint that, if solved, unlocks disproportionate enterprise value. Quantify the bounty: estimate current value vs. post-BHP value, then ask "Would I solve this if someone paid me that delta?" The gap converts emotional resistance to doing hard work into a rational calculation. Use it to override avoidance.

**Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate (3D Training Method)**
The only reliable skill-transfer system: (1) Document — checklist of every action the top performer takes; (2) Demonstrate — execute the checklist in front of the trainee; (3) Duplicate — trainee executes in front of you. Delegation isn't complete until performance stays flat or improves. If it dropped, you failed to teach — repeat the cycle. Adjust checklist granularity to the trainee's starting skill level.

**The Four Business Risks Framework**
Every sub-$10M business has four hidden risks: keyman risk (marketing, sales, delivery, ops), single-channel risk (>50% of leads from one source), key-customer risk (>20% of revenue from one buyer), key-vendor risk (one supplier controls a core function). Each risk caps value and killability. Audit quarterly. Fix in order of highest impact first.

**Complaint → Question Reframe**
Any unsolvable complaint becomes solvable the moment you convert it to "I don't know how." "No one can sell like me" → "I don't know how to find and train people who sell like me." Complaints externalize power and kill momentum. Questions direct attention toward problems with knowable answers. Always reframe before acting.

**LTV:CAC as the Base Unit of Economic Arbitrage**
If it costs $10 to acquire a customer worth $1,000, you have a machine. The only rational move when LTV:CAC exceeds 10:1 is to feed the machine without limit. ROIC scales this to the full capital deployment question. If you can't articulate this ratio, you don't understand your business.

**Grand Slam Offer / Front-End / Back-End Architecture**
A normal offer gives them the thing. A Grand Slam Offer makes it easy, fast, low-risk, tied to a dream outcome, and stacks bonuses that eliminate every remaining objection. The front-end (intro/promo price) is a lead magnet, not a profit center — profit lives in the upsell. Measure Step 1 → Step 2 conversion rate, not front-end close rate.

**Default Close (Opt-Out Framing)**
Countries with opt-out organ donation get 80%+ participation vs. 10% for opt-in. Structure every upsell so the desired outcome appears to be what they're already doing. For repeat customers: "You've already been paying for this — you just haven't been getting the bonuses. We just need to formalize it."

**The Rule of 100 + Rule of 300**
Rule of 100: pick one acquisition channel and apply 100 reps daily — 100 outreaches, 100 cold contacts, 100 minutes of content, or $100/day minimum on ads. Rule of 300: post one piece of content → make 100 thoughtful comments in communities where buyers congregate → DM everyone who liked, commented on, or replied to your post. These have self-selected as warm — open every DM referencing the specific action they took.

**Sell at Point of Greatest Pain**
Buying intent is highest at maximum pain, not after relief. Present the offer before delivering the first dose of value. Sequence: assessment → sale → first treatment. If you adjust the back and then ask for the 12-week plan, you've already neutralized the urgency. Reverse it.

**Starving Crowd Market Selection (Four Filters)**
Before building anything: (1) Are they in pain? (2) Do they have purchasing power? (3) Can you find them easily? (4) Is the market growing? Fail any one and no offer saves you. Run every new opportunity through all four before spending a dollar on product or ads.

**Whisper → Tease → Shout Launch Sequence**
Whisper: create mystery, show behind-the-scenes, maximize curiosity. Tease: fuel anticipation, let people speculate. Shout: hard CTA, it's live, go buy. Never pitch immediately. Make people want to want it before revealing what it is. Every day of the tease phase is demand built without ad spend.

**97/3 Brand-vs-Direct-Response Rule**
Only 3% of your market is ready to buy today. Direct response fights over that 3%. Brand advertising moves the 97% along the awareness continuum, so your ready-to-buy pool is 5x–15x larger than competitors' on a one-year horizon. If your only campaigns are conversion campaigns, you've capped your ceiling artificially.

**Nail It Then Scale It (Operational Readiness Test)**
The infrastructure required to run one great location is the same infrastructure needed to scale to 50. Ask before opening the next location: "Can this run without me present at 80%+ capacity?" If not, fix the model. Shortcuts that let you open faster destroy the foundation needed to scale later.

**ICE Prioritization Framework**
Impact × Confidence × Ease. Score every initiative and execute highest-ICE items first. Maps to the Value Equation: Impact = Dream Outcome, Confidence = Perceived Likelihood, Ease = Time + Effort. When you have 10 possible improvements, rank on all three dimensions and attack the top three ruthlessly before touching the rest.

**Good Friction vs. Bad Friction**
Good friction filters bad leads without deterring good ones — income qualification, start-date questions, budget ranges. Bad friction repels everyone — slow page loads, photo uploads, multi-step forms. Design intake to increase lead quality. Only add friction that bad leads are unwilling to pass.

**Drop Low / Go High (SMB Pricing Positioning)**
For SMB-facing businesses there are exactly two viable models: ultra-cheap with near-zero delivery cost and high volume, or premium with a higher-sophistication buyer who knows their metrics. The $1,500–$3,000/month mid-market price point produces predictable 4–6 month average stick, compressing margins until the business feels like a nonprofit. Pick a direction or accept the slow bleed.

**Affiliate Compounding Node**
Affiliates are not referrals. Referrals are one-time. Affiliates are recurring traffic nodes — each one sends 1–2 customers per month indefinitely. One-time outreach compounds forever. Identify audience-aligned businesses (coaches, attorneys, trainers) and treat each as a channel acquisition, not a relationship favor. Activation requires ongoing cadence — quarterly events, shared wins — or they go cold within 90 days.

**Owned vs. Rented Audience**
Social platforms are rented land — the algorithm can cut reach overnight. Email and podcast subscribers are owned. Build rented audiences for growth (discoverability, virality), funnel them into owned audiences for monetization. If you're heavy on rented and light on owned, you're one algorithm update from a revenue crater.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Rebook before customer leaves building | **Script:** "Your [appointment] is going to be ready in about 3 weeks — I'm getting booked up pretty quick so let's just get that on the calendar right now while you're here. Does the [date] work, or the one after that?"

**Situation:** Membership upsell to repeat customer using opt-out framing | **Script:** "Hey, I was looking at your history — you've been in six times in the last six months. I realized you haven't been getting [bonus benefits]. All you have to do is sign up as a member. You pay nothing more than you're currently paying, but you get all of these things you should already have been getting."

**Situation:** Prepayment offer after membership yes | **Script:** "Want to save some money? If you prepay today, you can save [dollar amount]. Would you prefer twelve months or six?"

**Situation:** Explaining why not to raise in your first business | **Script:** "You have ignorance debt — the cost of not knowing what you're doing. Pay that down on your own money. The last thing you want is to pay it down on your friends' and family's money."

**Situation:** Justifying unlimited ad spend into a working channel | **Script:** "You have a machine where you put $1 in and get $16 back in 30 days. There is no rational limit on what you should be feeding that machine."

**Situation:** Affiliate cold outreach | **Script:** "We offer a complimentary [service] to your new clients, at no cost to you. We've found it helps them [specific outcome] — which makes your results look better too. Worth a quick call?"

**Situation:** Finding pricing ceiling | **Script:** "I just keep edging up the price until people stop saying yes. Then I back it down one notch. That's my number."

**Situation:** Complaint → question reframe live | **Script:** "That's a complaint. Let me turn it into a question. You said 'I can't find good plumbers.' What if the real sentence is 'I don't know how to recruit, hire, and train good plumbers'? Because now we can answer that."

**Situation:** BHP with enterprise value anchor | **Script:** "If someone handed you a check for $57 million to solve this one problem, would you figure it out? Yeah? Then you'd probably solve it for a million. So you've got a $56 million surplus between what you'd accept and what it's actually worth."

**Situation:** Supply constraint diagnosis before recommending any growth tactic | **Script:** "If you doubled your ad spend tomorrow, would you double sales or create a mess? That answer tells you everything. One answer means fix demand. The other means touch marketing and you'll make it worse."

**Situation:** In-person upsell for home services / in-person delivery | **Script:** "Once you finish the primary job, ask them: 'While I'm here, would it make sense to take a look at [X]?' Two lines. Role-play it every morning. Track what percentage say yes."

**Situation:** Review exchange at point of sale (in-restaurant) | **Script:** "Hey, if you guys are willing to leave us a review, I can take care of one of the drinks. It would mean the world to us — we're a small family-owned business. If you're down, I'll take the check back, pull it off, and bring it right back."

**Situation:** Lead magnet CTA for cold traffic | **Script:** "If you're a [Avatar] and you're not getting [outcome], schedule one of our [freebie sessions] where we'll personally go through [specific deliverable] and show you exactly what to do with what you already have."

**Situation:** Pitching career path to new hire | **Script:** "Put your two years in, you'll continue to make more and get fully trained up. Once I have a new location to open, the person who's performed best here gets first shot at running it — 10% profit share year one, 20% year two."

**Situation:** Partnership dissolution via shotgun offer | **Script:** "I'll buy you out at [X] on these terms — but I'll also accept that same offer if you want to buy me out. Either way I want to make sure this is fair for both of us."

**Situation:** Diagnosing keyman risk to a business owner | **Script:** "If you walked away tomorrow, what happens to the revenue? If it drops, that's what a buyer sees — and they're right. You don't have an asset. You have a high-paying job."

**Situation:** Server greeting a first-time customer | **Script:** "Is this your first time dining with us? [Yes.] Amazing — we want to make sure you have the best experience. Before we get into the menu, can I tell you about our drink special for the week?"

**Situation:** Asking for referrals after delivering results | **Script:** "I've got your before-and-afters now. I need a few more case studies — do you know anyone who's seen your results and might want the same thing?"

**Situation:** Reframing "unscalable service" | **Script:** "We're building cities in the middle of the ocean. We're sending rockets to space. And you think you can't scale a service business? That's not a fact about your business. That's a fact about your current skill level. Which means it's solvable."

**Situation:** Activating a dormant email list | **Script:** "You have 10,000 people who've already raised their hand for you and you're spending money on cold traffic. Email returns 36-to-42 dollars per dollar spent. That list is the most profitable thing you own and you're not touching it."

**Situation:** Selling at point of greatest pain — intake | **Script:** "Before we go over your results, I want to make sure we have a plan ready for you. Based on what I'm seeing, here's what I'd recommend…" — present the package before first treatment, not after.

**Situation:** Explaining 97/3 brand vs. direct response | **Script:** "On a 30-day window, direct response beats branding. On a one-year window, branding smashes it. And then everyone else is still fighting over the 3% who were already going to buy, and you've got this massive pie that you grew that nobody else can touch."

**Situation:** Redirecting supply-constrained founder asking for more marketing | **Script:** "Hold on — do you have more leads than you can handle right now, or fewer leads than you need? Because those are completely different problems and they have completely different solutions."

**Situation:** Opening a DM to someone who liked or commented on your content | **Script:** "Hey, saw you liked my post — what about it drew you in? Are you looking to [specific outcome your content covered]?"

**Situation:** SMB agency stuck at mid-tier pricing | **Script:** "You're right in the sweet spot of churn. Average stick at that price with an SMB is 4 to 6 months. Your only moves are go way down in price with near-zero delivery, or go way up-market to a buyer who actually knows their metrics."

**Situation:** Timid price increase | **Script:** "Add $3 and see what happens. Most people are about to add 25 cents. The market is telling you what they're willing to pay. You're the one getting in the way."

**Situation:** Never discount — full price or free | **Script:** "It's either full price or it's free. If I'm going to do it for less, I'll make a strategic business decision to lower the price for everyone — not make a one-off deal that tells you and everyone you tell that my prices aren't real."

**Situation:** Founder stops selling to "figure things out" | **Script:** "Nothing will grow the business if the new sales number is zero. I can promise you that. Start selling again first — figure everything else out while the business is moving."

**Situation:** High-stakes decision with emotional clouding (Solomon Frame) | **Script:** "Write the situation as a DM to your 85-year-old self. You have all the context, no confidentiality problem, and you can't jade the data to get the answer you want."

**Situation:** Founder tempted to start new business to escape hard problem | **Script:** "Here's what'll happen. You'll build this new thing in a third of the time. You'll think 'this was the right call.' And then you'll hit the same level — the one you couldn't pass before — because you never learned it. The woman in the red dress looks better because you don't know her problems yet."

## DECISION RULES

- **When supply-constrained** → raise prices first (fastest fix, zero operational change), then improve service ratio, then hire. Never jump to hiring to solve a pricing problem.
- **When demand-constrained** → fix advertising and sales process before adding headcount. Doubling a broken funnel doubles the cost, not the revenue.
- **When a channel has >10:1 LTV:CAC** → max it out before diversifying. Putting a dollar cap on a 13:1 return machine is irrational.
- **When >50% of customers come from one source** → start building a second channel immediately. Invest before you need it, not reactively.
- **When a single customer represents >20% of revenue** → treat as existential risk, not success. Install one of four whale-risk remedies: add smaller customers to dilute %, lock whale into long-term contract, get more whales, or let the whale swim.
- **When adding a new acquisition channel** → pair it with a known operator, not a new one. Never run two unknowns simultaneously — you pay to learn nothing.
- **When delegating a function** → success is not the handoff. Success is performance holding flat or improving after handoff. If it dropped, the delegation failed — repeat 3D cycle.
- **When you have a list you haven't emailed** → email them with an offer immediately. The acquisition cost is already sunk; revenue from that list is near-pure profit.
- **When close rate exceeds 60–70%** → raise prices. Healthy close rate for optimal price/volume balance is 30–40%. Above that, you're leaving money on the table.
- **When underlying assumptions still hold but results haven't come yet** → push. Iterate on execution, not strategy. Quitting an ad channel after one month is paying the ignorance tax twice.
- **When data disproves a core assumption** → pivot immediately. Don't push through bad hard.
- **When evaluating a business to acquire** → check keyman risk and customer concentration first. Either alone is a yellow flag; both together is a no.
- **When ranking tactics** → apply ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease) and start with triple-high items first. Don't start with exciting — start with certain and high-leverage.
- **When someone says yes to any recurring service** → book the next appointment before they leave the building, ideally before they receive the current service.
- **When structuring a membership offer** → add free bonuses instead of discounts. A 10% discount costs real margin. A free add-on costs almost nothing and is worth far more in perceived value.
- **When someone agrees to monthly membership** → immediately offer 6- or 12-month prepay. This is a separate sale; don't conflate it with the membership close.
- **When training a skill into someone else** → document the checklist first, demonstrate second, have them duplicate third. Never skip the checklist step.
- **When building a recurring business** → have the next offer ready before the current engagement ends. Don't close the 12-week program and then scramble.
- **When a constraint shifts after you've solved it** → switch what you're working on immediately. Continuing to solve the old constraint after it's cleared creates a new crisis.
- **When in doubt on email format** → make it look like a personal email: plain text, 1–2 links, no money language, short paragraphs.
- **When you can't increase prices** → increase average cart value per transaction. One upsell script deployed consistently outperforms most marketing investments.
- **When picking a market** → run it through four filters first: pain, purchasing power, findability, growth trajectory. Fail any one and no offer saves you.
- **When someone objects to a required process step** → respond with "that's just how we've always done it." Over-explaining creates negotiation. Policy closes the loop.
- **When content goes viral but doesn't convert** → audit content-to-offer alignment. Viral ≠ relevant. Relevant-to-your-buyer is the only metric that drives revenue.
- **When a business is stuck at the same revenue for 2+ years** → assume the constraint has shifted and the founder didn't. Diagnose which new constraint emerged before prescribing anything.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Taking equity partners to solve a manpower or capital constraint.** You're paying permanent, compounding, relationship-laden equity for what should have been a hired role or a loan. The role will eventually need backfilling at market rate anyway — now you've also got a co-owner who may not share your vision.

2. **Raising external capital in your first business.** You have ignorance debt — the cost of not knowing what you're doing. Pay that down on your own money, not your investors'. Fix fundamentals first; you can always raise later.

3. **Selling out of your own wallet.** Pricing to what you'd personally pay rather than what the market will bear. High-end clients don't buy on your budget — they buy on theirs.

4. **Scaling before the unit economics are solid.** Expanding to location two before you know your cost to open, time to profitability, payback period, and culture system means duplicating a broken model at speed.

5. **Running discount-only memberships with no added value.** Discounts attract price-sensitive customers, train them to think about price, and churn the moment a competitor goes lower. Layer in media access, personalized recommendations, delayed bonuses, and community to make leaving irrational.

6. **No attribution tracking while running ads.** You're generating results by accident. You cannot make intelligent scaling decisions without this data, and it takes almost no effort to capture.

7. **Leaving partnership disagreements in the gray.** Every month a difficult conversation is avoided, the stakes get higher and the resolution gets uglier. The problem doesn't disappear — it compounds.

8. **Treating rebooking as an administrative conversation.** The scheduling conversation is a sales conversation with the stakes of your entire retention rate. If you treat it like paperwork, you get paperwork-level results.

9. **Solving the problem you enjoy, not the one the business requires.** Founders keep throwing effort at what they're good at while the actual constraint — monetization, talent, distribution — goes untouched. Enjoyment is not a signal of priority.

10. **Starting a second business to escape a hard problem.** The constraint follows you. New business, same plateau — but now you're behind on the new one too. Serial horizontal pivoting is always rationalized but almost always avoidance.

11. **Delegating by announcement.** Handing something off and calling it delegation without a feedback loop is abdication. Real delegation ends when performance confirms the handoff worked.

12. **Single-channel dependency (>50% of leads from one source).** A single platform change, ban, or algorithm shift can crater the business overnight. Build the second rod before the first one breaks.

13. **Key customer concentration (>20% of revenue from one buyer).** That whale is not an asset — it's a liability dressed up as a business relationship.

14. **Confusing keyman retention with business-building.** If someone is so critical that you're afraid of their departure, that fear is the problem. Systematize the role or you're sitting on a time bomb.

15. **Marketing when supply-constrained.** Running more ads into a business that can't serve existing customers creates chaos, delays, bad reviews, and rep damage. You are literally making it worse.

16. **Rushing to open new locations before nailing the model.** The fastest way to five locations is not the fastest way to fifty. Rushing creates hodgepodge deals, inconsistent execution, and a brand that can't scale because the foundation was never built.

17. **Discounting to close.** Every discount communicates that your stated price was fake. Every person that buyer tells now knows your prices are negotiable. You've trained the entire market to haggle you. Full price or free — never in between.

18. **Solving for AI disruption when business metrics aren't actually off.** If churn isn't meaningfully up and revenue is growing, you may be solving a problem that doesn't exist. The person already all-in on AI wasn't your customer to begin with.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

1. **The Rhetorical Setup-and-Flip.** State the naive assumption first, then detonate it with evidence or math. Forces the listener to hold the wrong belief just long enough to feel the correction. Never just state the correct frame alone — the contrast is the mechanism.

2. **Compressed Cause-and-Effect Chains.** Link 4–5 logical steps without pause: "Niche down → solve more precisely → charge more → acquire cheaper → every metric improves." No filler. Each step validates the next. Avoids subordinate clauses; uses sequential declarative sentences.

3. **Math on the Spot, Narrated Aloud.** Walk the arithmetic live in plain language. "She's spending $12,000 on a $2.3M business. If she spent 1% of revenue that'd be $23,000. So she's at half a percent." Always bridge from revenue impact to margin impact. Numbers terminate emotional objection and reframe the question as arithmetic, not opinion.

4. **Complaint → Question Flip (Live, Out Loud).** Take a static belief and convert it to "I don't know how" in real time. "Salespeople suck. What if you said: I don't know how to find good salespeople." The flip models the cognitive move the listener needs to make themselves.

5. **Personal Story as Proof, Not Inspiration.** Stories aren't told to be relatable — they're told to demonstrate a specific mechanism. The lesson is the last sentence, not the anecdote. "I outsourced food prep to reclaim selling hours, and one 30-minute sale was worth more than a week of cooking." The anecdote earns the mechanism; don't reverse the order.

6. **"Play It Out" Logic Chains.** Walk through the exact scenario to its conclusion rather than asserting a point. "Let's say 30% of clients leave after you double prices. Do the math. You make more money while working with fewer people." The chain does the persuasion; he just walks you through it.

7. **The "And So" Chain.** Arguments are rarely more than two steps: premise → implication → action. "Referrals are free. And so if you're poor, great place to start. And so reach out to everyone in your contacts before you spend a dollar on ads." Short causal chains keep logic auditable.

8. **Stakes Escalation via Inversion.** Don't just sell the benefit of acting — describe the cost of not acting. "If she doesn't deal with this thing, it's just going to get bigger and nastier." The cost of inaction is almost always more visceral than the benefit of action.

9. **Three-Option Framing for Hard Decisions.** "Path A. Path B. Path C." Present all exit options with neutral labels. Give the actual recommendation *after* the options are laid out. The options make the recommendation feel like a selection, not a command.

10. **Quantified Specificity Over Vague Claims.** Never round when you have the real number. Not "about 90 seconds" — "91 seconds." Not "almost 50%" — "48%." Precision signals you actually looked at the data. Every assertion gets a number from a named source.

11. **Self-Deprecating Admission Before Teaching.** Lead with your own failure or delay on the exact topic being taught before prescribing the fix. "I didn't email for the vast majority of my life because I hate money apparently." Disarms skepticism and signals the lesson was paid for with real failures, not borrowed from a book.

12. **Constraint-First Business Analysis.** Always ask for the bottleneck explicitly before making any recommendation. Sequence improvements by: (1) low effort / high impact first, (2) high effort / high impact second, (3) nothing structural until people and process are validated. Never recommend a growth tactic before confirming operations can execute it.
