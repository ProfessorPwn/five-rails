## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. The Value Equation**
Four levers determine how much someone will pay: Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement ÷ Time Delay ÷ Effort & Sacrifice. Maximize the top, minimize the bottom. Apply it by asking: "How do I make it faster, easier, risk-free, and more aligned with exactly what they want?" Every upsell, every offer, every repositioning decision runs through these four variables.

**2. Customer-Financed Acquisition (CFA)**
Structure your economics so the first customer pays for themselves AND the next customer within 30 days. The formula: 2× CAC + COGS must equal ≤ 30-day cash collected. Apply it by engineering upfront cash collection — prepay incentives, bundled packages, immediate upsells — so capital is never the growth constraint.

**3. Wealth Alchemy (Enterprise Value Arbitrage)**
The real game isn't revenue — it's the gap between permanent CAC and enterprise value multiple. $900 to acquire a permanent customer generating $1,200/year at a 10× SaaS multiple = $12,000 in enterprise value, tax-deferred. Apply it by obsessing over churn, retention, and choosing high-multiple business models, not just cash flow.

**4. Permanent Customer vs. Churn Factory**
Two businesses that sell 300 customers per year are not equal. Company B keeps every customer; Company A churns and re-acquires. With zero increase in marketing spend, Company B compounds. Apply it by calculating: what % of my customers stay 2+ years? That cohort is your real business. Everything else is sifting for them.

**5. The "What Would It Take to Be Number One?" Framework**
Start from the end state — assume you are #1 in the market — then reverse-engineer what must be true. This bypasses incremental 10% thinking and forces resource reallocation toward the actual constraint. Apply it by asking the follow-up: "Do we already have the resources to do that? If yes, why aren't we doing it?"

**6. Need-to-Believes Minimization**
Every belief a customer must hold before buying is friction. Two required beliefs = hard sale. One = manageable. Zero = Amazon/Uber scale. Apply it by mapping your funnel: write down every assumption a cold prospect must accept. Repackage the offer until you're down to one belief — or none.

**7. Pre > Post Production**
The concept of a video (or ad) has more leverage than any post-production polish. A better idea, poorly executed, outperforms a mediocre idea with great production. Apply it by spending the majority of creative time on hooks, structure, and examples before hitting record — then make post-production purely about comprehension, not entertainment.

**8. The Hyper-Buying Cycle**
When someone makes a new identity decision (I'm a runner, I'm a gym owner, I'm starting a business), they enter a buying window. Every problem solved reveals the next problem. Apply it by mapping the downstream purchase sequence and making the next offer immediately after the first one is fulfilled — don't wait.

**9. Damaging Admission Formula**
Bad, Bad, Bad — BUT — Good. Lead with honest negatives (no big parking lot, not the biggest gym) and then pivot to your real strength. The admission kills skepticism and amplifies the positive claim. Apply it: identify the most obvious objection your prospect has, claim it first, then flip it with "but."

**10. The Affiliate Model That Actually Works**
Stop offering 20% commissions. Instead, peel a component off your offer and give the affiliate 100% of revenue from that piece. Your cost becomes the hard cost of delivery (~$90 for 3 massages); their upside is unlimited. Apply it: find one standalone deliverable, price the minimum to filter quality buyers, let the affiliate set their price above floor and keep everything.

**11. Sell in a Vacuum**
Compete either on *where* (fish in a pond with no other fishermen) or *what* (sell something no one else can). Both eliminate price competition. Apply it by picking one: find an underserved channel/segment, or build a capability moat so buyers say "it's you or nothing."

**12. Split Testing Priority Hierarchy**
Rank tests by leverage: (1) Offer, (2) Headline/packaging, (3) Images, (4) Third-party integrations. Most people never test #4, which has produced the largest single-test gains — switching form tools or CRM integrations can produce explosive conversion lifts with no creative change. Apply it: before assuming you need a new offer, audit your tech stack conversion rates.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect balks at high price | **Script:** "If you could put in a dollar and get five back, would you do it? The only question is: how likely is that, how long does it take, and how hard is it? Let me walk you through each of those."

**Situation:** Explaining price relative to competition | **Script:** "Everyone else in this space charges about a tenth of what we do. The reason we can command more is that our clients make back multiples of what they invest. We price off the value we create, not the cost it took us to build it."

**Situation:** Client says "your leads cost too much" | **Script:** "Leads costing too much is rarely the real issue. The question is: how much are you making per customer? Because the business that can spend the most to acquire a customer wins the market — always."

**Situation:** Pitching a higher-tier package | **Script:** "You're already spending the money on marketing. You're already paying for sales. The only variable is the price you're commanding per customer. A 10x price increase on a well-positioned offer can produce a 31x increase in profit."

**Situation:** Asking for upfront payment | **Script:** "We actually build quarterly — and with that we include [X onboarding benefit] at no extra charge. The 5% discount we build in basically pays for itself. Want to lock that in?"

**Situation:** Customer says no to main offer | **Script:** "Totally fair. Hey — can I just help you with [adjacent problem]? We have a [seminar / resource / session] on that. No charge. Come check it out." [Then sell them something different that solves the same underlying problem.]

**Situation:** Pitching an affiliate deal | **Script:** "I don't want 20% of your referrals. Here's what I'd rather do: sell my three-session package to your customers for whatever price you want — keep every dollar. I just need the price to be at least $300 so we're attracting the right people."

**Situation:** Addressing an obvious weakness in your offer | **Script:** "We're not the biggest [gym/firm/agency] in the area. We don't have [X]. But when you walk in, you're going to forget about all of that — because [specific compelling experience]. That's the trade-off, and most of our clients say it's not even close."

**Situation:** Opening a value conversation with a skeptical buyer | **Script:** "Let me ask you something. If this took about 10 hours a week to implement, got you results in about a year, and the average return is five to one — is that a bet you'd take? Because that's exactly what we're describing."

**Situation:** Justifying a premium price to a cost-focused buyer | **Script:** "You're thinking about what it costs you. I want you to think about what it's worth to you. Those are different numbers — and the gap between them is how much you're leaving on the table by not doing this."

**Situation:** Introducing an upsell immediately post-purchase | **Script:** "Now that you've got [X], the next thing most people in your position realize they need is [Y]. We have something built specifically for that. Want me to walk you through it real quick?"

**Situation:** Competitor undercuts on price | **Script:** "There's a reason the biggest players in any market aren't the cheapest. They charge more because they've figured out how to deliver more value. We're building toward that. If you want to win on price, there's only ever one winner — and it costs everything."

**Situation:** Prospect asks why they should pay so much upfront | **Script:** "Think about the capital stack. If it's up to you, you might as well choose to get paid sooner. We structured it this way so we can put more resources toward your result from day one — not at the end."

**Situation:** Building truth into advertising | **Script:** "I'm not going to tell you we're the fastest or the cheapest, because we're not. What I will tell you is [specific, verifiable, true strength]. And if that's what matters to you, then we're the obvious choice."

**Situation:** Explaining why giving away premium content makes sense | **Script:** "Step one: look at what other people charge for. Step two: figure out what it costs you to deliver it. Step three: give it away free. Step four: count the leads. Step five: count the conversions. That's the whole strategy."

## DECISION RULES

**When** you're setting a price, **do** price off the value you create for the customer, not the cost it took to build — then work backward through the Value Equation to justify and de-risk it.

**When** your cash conversion cycle exceeds 30 days, **do** redesign the offer to pull cash forward — prepay incentives, bundled packages, or upfront onboarding fees — until you break even or profit within the billing cycle.

**When** a customer says no to your primary offer, **do** make them a completely different offer that solves the same underlying problem before ending the conversation.

**When** evaluating marketing hires, **do** keep the team small and pay for exceptional talent — one A-player marketer can produce 100× the output of a mediocre team of five.

**When** your business is growing, **do** ask "what would it take to be number one?" before planning the next quarter — then check if you already have the resources and reallocate accordingly.

**When** building an affiliate program, **do** give affiliates 100% of a peeled-off component rather than 20% of your main offer — enough to change their behavior, not just acknowledge the relationship.

**When** designing any new offer, **do** reduce the number of "need-to-believes" to one or zero — the fewer beliefs required, the faster and broader the sales cycle.

**When** your CAC feels high, **do** check LTV first — leads are almost never the problem; margin per customer almost always is.

**When** split testing, **do** prioritize in order: offer → headline → images → third-party integrations. Most businesses never test #4, which produces the largest absolute gains.

**When** a customer stays with you 2+ years, **do** study them obsessively — their acquisition source, their profile, their behavior — then rebuild your targeting around attracting only that type.

**When** producing content or ads, **do** invest the majority of time in pre-production (concept, hook, structure) and minimize post-production to comprehension only.

**When** you're competing in a crowded market, **do** choose to either sell *where* no one else is (channel/segment) or sell *what* no one else can — never try to win on price.

**When** you spot an obvious weakness in your offer, **do** lead with it in your marketing using the Bad-Bad-Bad-But-Good formula before the prospect can use it against you.

**When** thinking about taxes and wealth building, **do** focus on enterprise value growth (tax-deferred until sale) over optimizing near-term income tax — compounding unrealized gains beats loophole chasing.

**When** your business growth stalls, **do** identify whether the constraint is capital, people, or systems — CFA solves only the capital constraint; don't mistake one for another.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Pricing off your cost, not the customer's value.** If your margins feel impossible, you're probably pricing like a plastic table when you're delivering a hand-carved wood table. The fix isn't cutting costs — it's repositioning.

**Competing on price.** 85% of companies try it. 1% win. You will almost certainly be the loser. There is no second-place prize for cheapest.

**Waiting 60–90 days to collect cash.** "Industry standard" net-60 terms are how you let competitors with better cash cycles outspend and outgrow you. Collecting later is a choice, not a requirement.

**Chasing cheaper leads instead of increasing LTV.** Every business owner who's been burned by a good campaign tries to recreate it forever. That first campaign always performs best. Stop chasing that high — build LTV instead.

**Building a marketing department with bodies instead of talent.** More people in marketing almost always means lower output. Going from 17 to 5 people tripled output. Fewer, better, incentivized.

**Getting too far from your highest-leverage skill.** If your best asset is marketing or sales, every layer of management between you and the market dilutes it. Stay close to the work that moves the needle.

**Offering 10–20% affiliate commissions.** It doesn't change behavior. Affiliates will nod, agree, and do nothing. If it doesn't hurt a little to give, you haven't given enough.

**Too many need-to-believes.** If a prospect has to be convinced of two separate things before buying, you've already lost most of your market. Repackage until you're down to one.

**Skipping the follow-on offer after a "no."** A "no" to the main offer is not a "no" to solving their problem. They still have the problem. Make a different offer.

**Focusing on new customer acquisition while ignoring churn.** A churn factory that doubles acquisition every year still has the same number of customers as a retention-focused business that markets at half the pace — and is worth a fraction of it.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The "let me walk you through this" setup.** Hormozi rarely asserts — he leads. He frames insights as walkthroughs: "Let me walk you through an example," "Let's work this out," "I'll show you how this works." This disarms resistance before the argument lands.

**Math as storytelling.** He does arithmetic out loud and in real time, often with round numbers: "You spend $9 million, you get back $12 million in revenue, which is worth $120 million, on which you pay essentially 1.5% in taxes." Numbers aren't decoration — they're the argument.

**Contrast structures.** Nearly every concept is anchored in a before/after or Company A vs. Company B comparison. Two businesses, same customers acquired, radically different outcomes. The contrast does the persuasion; the explanation just confirms it.

**Rhetorical questions that stack.** He doesn't ask one question — he asks three in a row, each building on the last: "If it were guaranteed. If it were immediate. If you had to do zero work. Would you pay more for that? Of course you would." The stacking creates momentum toward the obvious conclusion.

**The "crazy part" reveal.** Hormozi consistently signals the most important insight with a phrase like "here's where it gets even wilder," "here's the crazy part," or "this is where it gets nasty." It's a pattern interrupt that signals: pay attention, the real point is coming.

**Concession before assertion.** He names the obvious objection himself before the listener can raise it: "Now you might be wondering why doesn't anyone do this — well it's usually an entirely different skill set and there's usually fear associated with it." Preempting objections builds credibility and controls the frame.

**Compounding logic chains.** Arguments build on themselves with explicit connective tissue: "So that means... which means... and that's why... so the only question is..." Each step locks the listener in before the next one is added. By the end, the conclusion feels inevitable rather than sold.
