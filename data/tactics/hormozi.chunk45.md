## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. Organic + CTA (The Free Testbed Ad Method)**
Make organic content relevant to what you sell. Let the algorithm surface winners — content that already gets shares, clicks, and watch-through. Then take those winners, attach a direct call to action, and pay to amplify. You already know it works because you tested it for free. Apply this before ever building ads from scratch; your organic feed is a no-cost split-test machine.

**2. Hook + Meat + CTA (The Direct Ad Framework)**
When you don't have organic to pull from, build ads in three parts: Hook (steals attention — borrow across industries, have 50+ variants minimum), Meat (the four-step persuasion argument), CTA (specific, visible, congruent with what the next page delivers). Use this as a production checklist. Hooks are interchangeable across niches; meat is audience-specific.

**3. Four-Step Persuasion**
Every piece of copy, pitch, or ad hits four angles on a single action: (1) You get MORE GOOD STUFF if you act. (2) You get LESS BAD STUFF if you act. (3) You get MORE BAD STUFF if you don't act. (4) You get LESS GOOD STUFF if you don't act. Cover all four quadrants in every offer, ad, and VSL. Miss one and you leave a segment of buyers unconvinced.

**4. The Problem-Solution Cycle (Lead Magnet Positioning)**
Every large customer goal contains dozens of small problem-solution loops. Your lead magnet must solve exactly ONE of those tiny loops — completely and credibly. The goal is not to solve everything; it's to prove you can solve this one thing so well that the prospect believes you can solve the rest. Position your lead magnet as a single step in a multi-step journey, not as the entire solution.

**5. The VSSL (Video Sales Letter) — Two Structures**
For complex or unfamiliar offers: **5 Ps** — Promise, Pain, Proof, Plan, Picture (visual of the process), then FAQs (in the prospect's exact language), then testimonial proof stack, then CTA. For well-understood services: **5 Ws** — Who, What, Where, When, Why, then FAQs, proof stack, CTA. The goal of both: answer every question a prospect would reasonably ask before buying so your sales call is a decision call, not an information call.

**6. BAND Qualification**
Before any sales call, filter for: **Budget** (do they have the money?), **Authority** (can they decide alone?), **Need** (do they actually need this?), **Timing** (do they want to move now?). Only take calls with people who confirm all four — in writing, via your application. If your sales team is underutilized, loosen filters; if overloaded, tighten them.

**7. The Runway Model (Information Needed to Close)**
Every prospect needs a certain amount of information before they can buy — it's a fixed quantity. If you need three calls to close, the prospect needs ~3 hours of content. Build your funnel to deliver that content in days, not months. Stack: ad (hook + credibility) → lead magnet (30 min) → VSSL (45–90 min) → sales call (conversion, not education). This is the plane and runway: you have to give the runway enough length for the plane to lift.

**8. Four Competitive Vectors (Speed / Risk / Price / Ease)**
Every business must win on at least one of: faster delivery, more reliable outcome, lower cost, or less friction. Pick the vector your customer values most. You cannot effectively market all four — customers can't process multiple benefits. Winning one vector creates sustainable competitive advantage. Winning three or four is domination. Tech enables all four; labor enables three at best.

**9. The Value Equation Mapping**
Speed = time delay compressed. Risk = likelihood of achievement. Ease = effort and sacrifice removed. Price = dream outcome cost. These four are the levers of perceived value. Every tactic in marketing and fulfillment maps to one of them. When you're stuck, ask: which lever am I currently ignoring?

**10. The Microwave vs. Slow-Cook Model**
Cold traffic needs to be "nuked" — compressed exposure to everything a loyal follower would consume over a year, delivered in a 2–3 day funnel window. Stop trying to make ads close without warming. Build the infrastructure that heats the prospect fast. Everyone who doesn't buy gets dumped into long-term nurture (community, email) and re-microwaved later.

**11. The Brand Pyramid**
The brand is the base of a pyramid. The top slice are buyers. The middle is warm audience. The base is everyone who's ever gotten value from you. Growing revenue means growing the base, not just converting the top. Short-form content grows the base; the funnel converts a slice of it. Without a growing base, every funnel caps.

**12. The Ease Inversion**
You don't make a product easier by adding convenience features. You make it easier by identifying everything that's hard — then removing it one item at a time until only the value remains. The customer will tell you what's hard. Collect their friction points as a list. Cross them off one by one. Ease is not a feature. It's the absence of obstacles.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Writing ad copy that says a benefit without stating it | **Script:** "Don't say 'you'll feel better.' Say 'You'll bend down, tie both shoes standing up — no more holding your gut in to lean over.' Describe the moment, not the label."

**Situation:** Choosing hooks for paid ads | **Script:** "Pull hooks from outside your industry. Finance hooks work in hotels. The hook draws attention — it doesn't need to be niche-specific. Have at least 50 variants. You're not making too many ads."

**Situation:** Structuring the CTA in an ad | **Script:** "Tell them exactly what to do AND show them exactly what will happen next. Say 'click the button below' — and make sure a button is visibly below. Congruence between the promise and the page kills bounce rate."

**Situation:** Qualifying leads on a call intake form | **Script:** "Ask: Do you have [budget range] to invest in this? Are you the decision-maker, or do others need to be involved? What's the primary problem you're dealing with? Are you ready to move forward in the next 30 days? Only book calls where all four are yes."

**Situation:** Handoff between setter and closer | **Script:** "Start the second call with: 'I spoke with [setter name]. They told me your revenue is [X], you're in [industry], and the biggest issue you're facing is [Y]. Does that sound right?' They feel heard. The close opens warm."

**Situation:** Pre-handling price objection in VSSL | **Script:** "Show the investment in the video. Say 'You're probably wondering what this costs. Here's exactly what it is and what you get.' People find out eventually — better they find out pre-call so only qualified prospects book."

**Situation:** Handling timing objection — FAQ in VSSL | **Script:** "Use their exact words. If they say 'I'm really busy right now' — address 'I'm really busy right now.' If they say 'I've got a lot on my plate' — address that separately. Same objection class, different emotional triggers, different overcomes."

**Situation:** Opening a lead magnet | **Script:** "Solve the tiniest specific problem completely. 'How to know if a girl wants you to kiss her' — then deliver a one-step answer that actually works. Don't give a course. Give one loop of the problem-solution cycle, solved completely."

**Situation:** Describing what happens after the opt-in | **Script:** "Frame the VSSL as: 'Watch this short video — I'll show you exactly how [outcome], why most people fail at [problem], and what to do instead.' Set the expectation of what they'll watch before they watch it."

**Situation:** Positioning speed as your competitive vector | **Script:** "We get you [outcome] in [X time]. Most [category] take [longer time]. Our entire operation is built around this — because we know your time is the constraint, not the budget."

**Situation:** Selling reliability when you can't compete on price | **Script:** "Look, she's been with me for 10 years and never missed a day. You could pay 20% less — but is that risk worth it? Consistency is the most underpriced thing in service businesses."

**Situation:** Nurturing leads who don't buy immediately | **Script:** "Drop non-buyers into a community or email sequence. Keep answering their FAQs. They're not saying no — they're saying 'not yet, I need more information.' Give them that information over time. Then bring them back through the funnel."

**Situation:** Explaining why ads aren't working | **Script:** "It's not that ads don't work for you. It's that you haven't put enough friction in your process. You're trying to close from cold. Add the lead magnet. Add the VSSL. Give them the runway."

**Situation:** Making the case for more availability | **Script:** "What's your booking window look like? If someone calls on Saturday and the earliest slot is Thursday, you just lost them. Staffing the hours better will drive 20–30–40% more revenue through the same operation."

**Situation:** Positioning your offer against a free competitor | **Script:** "Free is irrelevant when the experience is miserable. LimeWire was free — Spotify won. DMV is free — she charged $50 and had a line out the door. Fast beats free. Make the alternative so slow and painful that your price is the obvious value."

**Situation:** Writing ad meat using time dimension | **Script:** "Play it out today AND tomorrow. What does their life look like at this moment if they act? What does it look like two years from now if they don't? The future-pacing of both sides doubles the emotional stakes."

**Situation:** Defending premium pricing against a cheaper competitor | **Script:** "We're not competing on price. We compete on [speed/reliability/ease]. If your primary concern is getting it done right the first time and not having to worry about it again, you're in the right place."

**Situation:** Describing what a brand actually is | **Script:** "A brand is just someone who's gotten tons of information from you over a long period of time about who you are, what you're about, and your way of doing things. The funnel converts them. The brand creates the volume to convert."

**Situation:** Deciding whether to include price in the VSSL | **Script:** "If you have more demand than supply, show the price. It filters. If you're underbooked, hide it and qualify on the call. The longer I do this, the more I just put everything upfront — they're going to find out eventually."

**Situation:** Getting a prospect to trust your next prediction | **Script:** "Say exactly what will happen next, then make sure it happens. 'Click below and you'll see a short video, then a form to book a time with my team.' When that's what they find — they believe the next thing you say. You're planting seeds for the sale."

## DECISION RULES

**When your ads aren't converting:** Before changing the offer, diagnose the stage — is it the hook (low CTR), the lead magnet (low opt-in), the VSSL (low booking rate), or the call (low close rate)? Fix the broken stage, not the whole funnel.

**When building a new ad:** Write 50+ hooks before you film anything. Hooks are 80% of performance and require the least production effort. Spend your research time here.

**When deciding your competitive vector:** Start with the customer. What does their worst current experience look like — wasted time, unpredictable outcomes, high cost, or painful process? Pick the vector that eliminates their biggest complaint.

**When pricing your offer:** If you're good, compete on performance. Offer skin in the game — performance-based, conditional, or implied guarantees signal confidence. Guarantees matter most when you have no reputation yet; once you have reputation, the anti-guarantee (everyone knows you deliver) is more powerful.

**When your close rate requires 3+ calls:** That means prospects need 3 hours of information to buy. Compress it into the funnel: ad + VSSL + application. The 3rd call should be the 1st decision call, not the 3rd information call.

**When picking a niche for speed advantage:** Narrow avatar = fewer variations in the work = more templates = faster delivery = competitive advantage. Every generalist decision sacrifices speed.

**When leads don't buy immediately:** Never discard them. Put them in community or email nurture. They're high-information buyers who need time. Bring them back through the front of the funnel when they're warm.

**When your cost to acquire customers is rising:** Before blaming CPMs, ask: has your word-of-mouth quality declined? Negative word of mouth is 10x as viral as positive. A reputation problem raises CAC invisibly.

**When building for the cheapest vector:** Build AI, automation, and offshoring into the backend from day one. You don't have to tell customers. Just charge fair rates with a cost structure your competitors can't match.

**When designing your product experience:** Ask what's hard about this — then remove it one item at a time. Don't add convenience features. Remove obstacles. Ease is the absence of friction, not the presence of amenities.

**When writing copy:** Break down the benefit into the moment it occurs. Not "you'll feel better" — but "you'll bend down to tie your shoe and realize you're not holding your breath." Specificity creates believability.

**When choosing how much information to give upfront:** Default to more. Most prospects are high-information buyers. The 3% who buy cold are great. But if you want to convert the other 97%, give them the runway they need.

**When onboarding a new customer:** The quality of your sales process is their first data point about the quality of your service. If you call in 30 seconds, they assume you fulfill in 30 seconds. Every touchpoint is a proof point.

**When you have only one competitive vector to market:** Lead with it in every ad, every headline, every sales call open. Customers can hold one clear reason to choose you. Give them exactly one.

**When competing against free alternatives:** Price is irrelevant if the free option is painful, slow, or risky. Find where the free option fails the customer and make your paid version eliminate that failure completely.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Generic copy that labels benefits instead of describing moments.** "You'll feel better" is dead copy. "You'll pick your kid up off the floor without your knees buckling" is alive. If your copy doesn't create a scene in the prospect's head, rewrite it.

**One or two ad variants running.** If you have fewer than 50 hooks in rotation and you're spending real money on ads, you're starving the algorithm of choices. The algorithm finds winners — but only if you give it enough to test.

**Trying to close cold traffic directly from an ad.** Sending ad traffic straight to a sales page without a lead magnet and VSSL is like trying to take off with a runway that's too short. You'll kill your conversion rate and blame the ads.

**Multi-call sales processes where the closer re-qualifies from scratch.** If your closer is re-asking questions the setter already captured, your team looks incompetent. Notes must transfer. The second call should open with a summary, not a questionnaire.

**Lead magnets that try to solve the whole problem.** A full course on "everything you need to know" isn't a lead magnet — it's a commitment. Solve one micro-loop. Prove competence on one small thing. The follow-up sale covers the rest.

**Competing on multiple vectors in your marketing messaging.** "We're the fastest, cheapest, and most reliable" is three claims that cancel each other out in the buyer's mind. Pick one. Make it undeniable. The other vectors can exist in the product; only one should be in the pitch.

**Ignoring the long-term nurture bucket.** Non-buyers aren't lost leads — they're slow buyers. If you don't have an email sequence or community capturing these people, you're paying acquisition cost twice when they eventually buy.

**Adding "convenience" features to improve ease.** Ease doesn't come from adding things. It comes from removing friction. Adding another dashboard, another option, another path through the product makes it harder, not easier.

**Guarantees without stakes.** A guarantee that costs you nothing if you fail is not a guarantee — it's theater. Guarantees only work if there's real downside for you. Put actual skin in the game: money-back, pay-for-performance, a tangible commitment.

**Treating word-of-mouth as a passive variable.** Negative word of mouth is 10x more viral than positive. People who would have bought are choosing not to because of something they read or heard. Your reputation is an active lever on your CAC — not a footnote.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The rhetorical permission ask.** Hormozi gets agreement before making the point: "Can we agree on that?" / "Now, I'm going to just use this as our placeholder." He pre-commits the audience before delivering the framework. Use this before every model you introduce.

**The anti-complexity pattern.** Hormozi aggressively deflates complexity: "People over-complicate this." / "I'll just tell you — there is no big box of magic." / "This is very straightforward." Reassure the listener that the answer is simpler than they feared, then deliver it in plain language.

**The mirror inversion.** He inverts the obvious framing to reveal a deeper truth: "Easy is not the outcome. It's removing all hard." / "You don't make something convenient because you want to add a feature — it's the opposite." State the conventional belief, then flip it.

**The math anchoring move.** He grounds assertions in specific numbers before drawing the conclusion: "78% of customers had consumed at least two long-form pieces." / "Average piece was 17–20 minutes... so basically an hour." He builds the quantitative case, then delivers the so-what. Never assert without the math.

**The explicit stakes escalation.** Hormozi paints the cost of inaction by pushing time forward: "You're 50 lbs overweight now — what are you going to feel like at 70 lbs overweight?" He always pairs current bad state with the worse future state. Use the time-push on both sides: great future if you act, worse future if you don't.

**The redundant restatement for emphasis.** He restates the rule three times in slightly different forms before moving on: "No untested code. No regressions. No broken features." / "Speed, risk, price, ease — if you win one, you can have an incredibly successful business. If you can do multiple vectors, you'll crush everyone." Triplets or paired restatements signal that a rule is non-negotiable.

**The earned analogy.** He only introduces analogies after establishing the mechanical principle: the math on the prospect information comes first, the "runway and plane" visual comes after. Analogy is used to cement understanding, not substitute for it. Build the logic, then give the image.
