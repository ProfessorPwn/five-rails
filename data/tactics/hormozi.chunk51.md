## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. Supply vs. Demand Constrained Diagnosis**
Before touching anything in a business, ask: can you handle more customers right now? If yes, it's demand-constrained — fix lead flow and creative. If no, it's supply-constrained — fix operations and delivery. This is the first fork in the road; everything downstream depends on answering it correctly. Apply it the moment someone says "we're stuck" before you touch a single tactic.

**2. Workflow-Based Thinking (vs. Role-Based)**
Every job title is just a bundle of 5–10 discrete tasks. Stop thinking "I need a hire" and start asking "which of these specific tasks could live inside a workflow?" List exactly what the person does with their hands — respond to emails, cut clips, write copy, QA ads — then automate each task individually. Apply when considering any new hire or when AI-adoption feels overwhelming.

**3. The UGC Loop**
The highest-leverage content machine for visual businesses: incentivize customers to post about their results (60-second video, montage of the trip, transformation before/after), give them something gated in exchange, collect permission, blast the best performers as organic then paid ads. It decentralizes content creation so you get 20–30 new test creatives per week without a production team. Apply to any business where customers have visual outcomes.

**4. Kaleidoscope Creative Testing**
When you find a winning ad, don't search for a new one — extract every possible variation of it. Black-and-white filter, Ghibli-ified, 3-second AI video of a static, cartoonized, remade with a different avatar, different hook same body. Copy and messaging: leave it alone — some campaigns run winning copy for 3–6 months unchanged. Creative format: swap constantly. Apply the moment a creative starts scaling.

**5. Lead Scoring for Outbound Prioritization**
Identify the 1–2 factors most predictive of a closed sale, then embed them into opt-in forms so your dialer team can triage. Two qualifying factors = call first. One = call second. Zero = call if you have nothing else. Running a power/parallel dialer without lead scoring is wasted talk time. Apply any time outbound volume exceeds your team's capacity.

**6. Damaging Admissions Front-Load**
Before you pitch the upside, list every legitimate reason this won't work for them — the constraints, the trade-offs, the inconveniences. Say "let me tell you the bad first." This accomplishes two things: (1) prospects self-qualify out if the constraints are dealbreakers, (2) everything positive you say afterward is now believed. Especially critical when the offer sounds too good to be true or is associated with a stigmatized category.

**7. The Rule of 100**
Deliberate practice means doing the core repetition 100 times before you perform it live. Not 100 per total — 100 per day for outbound contacts, 90 full run-throughs of a presentation before launch. Research on deliberate practice confirms it is not enjoyable — that's the point. Apply when launching anything high-stakes: sales call script, pitch deck, content format.

**8. The Fixed Cost of Suffering**
Suffering is not a signal that you're on the wrong path — it's the toll on every path. The amount of suffering on the path to your big goal, your moderate goal, and your small goal is approximately equal. The regret is different; the difficulty is different. But the cost is fixed. Use this frame when someone is tempted to pivot because it feels hard — pivot = same cost, smaller reward.

**9. The Martyrdom Effect**
Pain tolerance roughly triples when you are suffering for someone else rather than yourself. If you're struggling to stay motivated, it's because the goal is self-referential. Attach the mission to a person or group outside yourself. Apply when building a content mission, a sales narrative, or coaching someone through a hard stretch.

**10. Passion = Worth Suffering For (Latin Root Reframe)**
Passio = suffering. The Passion of Christ = crucifixion. "Follow your passion" was always "find something worth suffering for," not "find something that feels fun." Apply this reframe whenever you or a prospect uses the word passion — it immediately clarifies whether their commitment is real or emotional.

**11. The Barbell Strategy (AI Adoption)**
On one end: go fully AI-first — hard conversations, automation, raised bar for all roles. On the other end: identify the things that will not change regardless of AI (human bodies, food, entertainment, leisure) and build there. Avoid the mushy middle of "kinda AI" and "kinda traditional." Apply when setting business strategy or investment priorities in an AI-disrupted market.

**12. The Circle of Competence Build-Out**
Don't start with passion — start with what you're already above average at. Natural advantages compound faster than built-from-scratch skills. List what you were doing early, what got you compliments before you tried for them, what you were recruited for. Build commercial relevance on top of that base rather than starting from zero because it "sounds exciting." Apply when advising someone on what business to start.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Opening a business diagnostic session | **Script:** "First question — is this a supply-constrained or demand-constrained business? Can you handle more customers right now if they showed up?"

**Situation:** Prospect thinks their testimonials look fake or AI-generated | **Script:** "Listen, before I tell you about the good, let me tell you about the bad. If you want to go on a specific day every year, this won't work for you. If top-shelf is non-negotiable, let me stop you there. The trade is flexibility for savings — if that's a dealbreaker, this isn't for you."

**Situation:** Someone says AI can't do what humans can | **Script:** "Humans learn by reinforcement — do something, get an outcome, do more of what worked. AI does the exact same thing, except the feedback cycle that takes a person 18 months takes AI 100 minutes."

**Situation:** Someone tries AI once, gets slop, gives up | **Script:** "If you hired a brand new employee and they gave you bad work on day one, would you fire them immediately? You'd train them. You didn't fail at AI — you failed to train it."

**Situation:** Writing AI prompts and getting generic output | **Script:** "Give it 12 rules it can never break, 16 writing samples of your own work, and tell it to write only in that style. You'll get something five times better before you've even started iterating."

**Situation:** Stuck on what to automate first | **Script:** "Write down everything you do every day at the most granular level. Not 'I run ads' — 'I make campaigns, set budgets, analyze results, write copy, test headlines.' Then take the first task and put it into AI: 'Help me automate this, what steps would you take?' Then do the first thing on that list."

**Situation:** Stuck in AI implementation | **Script:** "Screenshot your screen, paste it in, and say 'What do I do now?' Then screenshot the next screen. Repeat."

**Situation:** Running outbound with a small team and low pickup rates | **Script:** "You need a parallel dialer — it calls 10 numbers at once per rep. At 5% pickup, two people answer. It auto-connects. Maximize talk time, not dial time."

**Situation:** Business hitting a creative ceiling on paid ads | **Script:** "The creative unlock is usually why someone gets limited. We scaled to $10k a day — that's all we could spend based on how good this creative is. The algorithm exhausts the most-interested buyers first, then your CPA climbs because new creative isn't good enough for the next ring."

**Situation:** Client wants to diversify channels before fixing existing ones | **Script:** "More and better first — before new channel. The nature of what you sell can probably scale to $100k/day in spend on the existing platform. You haven't unlocked it yet — you've hit a creative ceiling, not a channel ceiling."

**Situation:** UGC incentive to build a content flywheel | **Script:** "Post a 60-second montage of your trip. In exchange you unlock [gated bonus content]. Here's exactly how to put the video together." They get something. You get permission and a new ad creative.

**Situation:** Someone blames their industry for low margins | **Script:** "People are used to paying $2,000/month for something. Embedded in that price is the cost of labor. If you can still charge $2,000 and it now costs you $50 instead of $500, that's not a margin improvement — that's a business model transformation."

**Situation:** Someone wants to quit because entrepreneurship isn't fun | **Script:** "You're using the excuse of a lack of passion to disguise your inability to handle difficulty — to repeatedly do things you don't enjoy to have something meaningful happen."

**Situation:** Younger entrepreneur confused about what to pursue | **Script:** "No matter what path you choose, it will be hard. So pick one that pays better if that's what you think is worth it. Suffering is a fixed cost — change the reward, not the path."

**Situation:** Pitching travel savings but facing credibility skepticism | **Script:** "Most people place their vacation chip on roulette before the marble drops. The J-Go way is placing the chip after the marble drops — you find the good deals first, then plan the vacation around them."

**Situation:** Sales call where prospects say "it sounds too good to be true" | **Script:** "These are real. I'm 53. I don't even know how to work AI." [Then front-load every reason it won't work so the believable part lands clean.]

**Situation:** Advising someone about hiring vs. automation | **Script:** "For every hire you're considering, write down the four to ten things that person actually does with their hands and eyes. Then ask whether each of those activities could live inside a workflow instead of headcount."

**Situation:** Making the case for big goals vs. safe ones | **Script:** "What if I told you the suffering is equal across all three paths — the thing you hate, the moderate goal, and the swing-for-the-fences goal? You suffer the same. You just change the reward. So aim big."

**Situation:** Someone asks how to find their passion | **Script:** "Don't follow your passion — develop it. Start with curiosity, not passion. Find a feedback loop. Get someone to tell you that sucked and here's what to fix. Competence creates passion, not the other way around."

**Situation:** Advisor or investor asks why you're still working when you don't need money | **Script:** "The self-servingness cannot be the goal. You will satisfy your own needs relatively quickly if you get good at anything. The why has to be bigger than you, or you'll only overcome obstacles smaller than you."

## DECISION RULES

**When demand-constrained:** fix creative and conversion before adding new acquisition channels. New channels are a distraction; the existing channel has ceiling room you haven't unlocked yet.

**When a creative is winning:** do not change the copy. Run the same headline and body for months. Kaleidoscope the format and visuals; leave the message alone.

**When outbound volume exceeds team capacity:** implement lead scoring immediately. Score on the 1–2 factors most predictive of close. Dial twos before ones; ones before zeros.

**When an AI output is bad:** don't abandon the tool — diagnose the prompt. Add rules, add examples, define what good looks like in observable terms. Bad output = undertrained, not broken.

**When considering a new hire:** list their 5–10 actual tasks first. If those tasks can live in workflows, don't hire — automate. Raise the bar; the people who meet it stay.

**When someone says they lack passion:** reframe — passion means worth suffering for. Ask what they'd be willing to suffer for, not what they enjoy. Enjoyment follows competence.

**When creative hits a spending ceiling:** the fix is almost always creative quality, not budget cap or audience size. The algorithm exhausts warm buyers first; better creative reaches colder rings.

**When building a product that's highly visual:** prioritize video UGC over produced creative. Good video beats images; bad video loses to images. Most businesses think images work better because their video is bad.

**When the offer sounds too good to be true:** front-load damaging admissions before the pitch. List every legitimate reason it won't work. Credibility on the constraints transfers to believability on the benefits.

**When scaling a business, identify the constraint type first:** supply-constrained (can't handle more customers) → fix ops. Demand-constrained (can handle more but don't have enough) → fix marketing, creative, and sales.

**When someone is about to quit because it's hard:** don't validate the pain as a signal they're on the wrong path. Every path has equal suffering. Pivoting restarts the clock with the same fixed cost.

**When motivating yourself or a team:** connect the work to someone outside yourself. Pain tolerance triples in service of others. "I'm doing this for X" is not soft — it's the highest-leverage motivational reframe available.

**When testing content organic vs. paid:** post it organic first with no spend. When it wins, add a 5-second CTA at the end and run it as an ad. Double dip — no wasted budget on losers.

**When an employee role gets automated:** don't find them something else to do. Raise the bar for the whole company. The people who meet the new bar stay; the ones who don't, don't. Sentiment is not a strategy.

**When building anything for the long term:** use the Bezos frame — bet on things that won't change. Human bodies, food, entertainment, leisure. Everything built on stable demand is defensible even through tech disruption.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**"Follow your passion" without suffering context.** Passion in Latin means suffering. If someone follows their passion and expects it to feel good, they've misread the map. Passion = what you're willing to suffer for. Enjoyment is the occasional byproduct, not the signal you're on the right track.

**Adding a second ad channel before fixing creative on the first.** This is the most common scaling mistake. You hit a ceiling, blame the channel, open a new one, and hit the same ceiling faster. Fix the creative — that's what broke.

**Changing winning copy because it "feels stale."** After 5 years and hundreds of thousands per day in ad spend, the best companies run three hooks. When something works, the instinct to change it is emotional, not strategic. Resist it.

**Using AI once, getting mediocre output, concluding "AI doesn't work."** This is training failure, not tool failure. You wouldn't fire a new employee on day one for bad output. Define what good looks like in observable, specific terms — rules + examples — before you iterate.

**Role-based organizational thinking.** "We need a video editor" is legacy thinking. "What are the five tasks an editor does, and which can live in a workflow?" is the new operating model. Org charts organize human communication — they don't optimize outputs.

**Passion as a prerequisite for starting.** Waiting to feel passionate before starting means never starting. The feedback loop that creates passion only begins after you start doing the thing. Curiosity is sufficient fuel to begin.

**Chunked-up task analysis.** "I run ads" is not a task. It's a bucket. The granularity you need is: make campaigns, set budgets, analyze results, write copy, test headlines, QA landing pages. Without that granularity, you can't automate, delegate, or improve.

**Treating hard times as a signal to pivot.** Every path has equal suffering. The discomfort you're experiencing right now is not evidence you chose the wrong thing — it's the fixed cost of choosing anything. Pivoting restarts the cost without changing it.

**Believing humans will outlast machines at any specific skill.** "Machines will never beat us at chess." "Machines will never beat us at Go." They always do. The winning frame is humans + superior tools vs. humans with inferior tools — not humans vs. machines.

**Self-referential "why."** If your goal is to serve yourself, you'll hit the ceiling of your own needs and stall. The why must be bigger than you — or the obstacles you face will also be bigger than you, and you'll quit.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The diagnostic funnel.** Hormozi always establishes constraint type before offering tactics. The pattern: "First question — [binary frame]." Then he builds the decision tree from the answer. Never tactic-first, always diagnosis-first. Steal this structure: name the fork, ask which side they're on, then solve for that side only.

**Concrete specificity over abstraction.** He doesn't say "practice a lot" — he says "90 full run-throughs of a 90-minute presentation, three per day, four and a half hours of deliberate practice, for 30 days." Every principle gets a number, a frequency, and a behavior. When making a claim, ask: what does this look like in hours, reps, or dollars?

**The "real quick" pivot.** He interrupts his own argument at peak tension to offer a separate resource ("Real quick, I'm going to show you the exact 10-stage roadmap…"). In conversation, this is the pattern of inserting a credibility anchor mid-explanation before closing the loop. It signals: I've done this, here's the proof, now back to the point.

**Rhetorical inversion.** "Everyone says follow your passion. Here's what that actually means." Hormozi takes a widely-held belief, etymologically or empirically inverts it, and rebuilds it on his terms. The structure: [popular claim] → [why it's wrong or imprecise] → [correct reframe] → [practical implication]. Use this on every received wisdom your audience believes.

**Self-referential examples as proof.** He rarely cites research alone — he anchors it with "I did this: slept on the gym floor for six months, opened the gym at 4:15 AM, caught athlete's foot that I still have." The specificity of personal cost (not just personal success) builds credibility. Don't just say what worked — say what it cost.

**"And so" as a logical bridge.** Hormozi uses "and so" not as filler but as a causal connector — every "and so" signals a conclusion being drawn from evidence just presented. His arguments don't jump; they chain. Model this by never making a point without the "and so that means…" close.

**The triple-suffering structure.** When confronting hard truths, he stacks three versions: "Growing a business is hard. A plateaued business is hard. A decaying business is hard." Then: "Being broke is hard. Being rich is hard." The triple stack normalizes the pain, removes the illusion of a pain-free alternative, and forces the listener to select the suffering that pays best. Use it whenever someone is looking for the easy path.
