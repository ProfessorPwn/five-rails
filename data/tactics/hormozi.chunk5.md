## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The CLOSER Framework**
C = Clarify why they're here. L = Label them with the problem. O = Overview past pain. S = Sell the vacation (outcome, not process). E = Explain away concerns. R = Reinforce the decision. Use for transactional 1–2 call closes. Run it in sequence; back-load closes for after the first "no."

**The Three-Pillar Pitch**
Every solution has three core pillars that prospects need to believe in to buy. Find yours, name them, and build metaphors around each. When prospects describe past failures, map which pillar was missing — then position your offer as the missing leg. A stool with two legs falls; your product adds the third.

**The Pain Cycle**
Deprivation creates motivation. Expand their felt deprivation before asking for money: what have they tried, how much have they spent, what has the failure cost them daily in status/money/time? The deeper you anchor the pain, the more urgency and motivation to act. "The pain is the pitch."

**The 100 Golden BBs**
Closing is not one big lever — it's 100 small things each incrementally increasing purchase likelihood. Frame your entire sales process this way: every detail (speed of response, show-up reminders, agenda-setting) adds a BB. Manage the whole system, not just the close.

**Sales-as-Teaching**
"Same conditions, new behavior." Your job is to identify what information the prospect is missing, then deliver it efficiently. If a perfectly informed prospect wouldn't buy, you shouldn't sell them. If they would, find the gap and fill it.

**The Runway / Plane Model**
Low-ticket = small plane, short runway. High-ticket = large plane, long runway. If you're consistently taking 3–5 calls to close, your marketing isn't doing enough pre-education. The close is a 5-yard-line play, not a Hail Mary.

**BANT Qualification**
Budget, Authority, Need, Timing. Collect all four before asking for money — through the application, the setting call, and the pain cycle questions. Anything you ask about after money is on the table feels extractive. Asked before, it's helpful fact-finding.

**The Continuum Model (Marketing + Sales)**
Marketing and sales are one revenue continuum, not separate departments. Marketing covers informational gaps 1–3; sales covers 4–6, personalized to each prospect. Put them under one roof with one revenue leader. Internal conflict disappears.

**Feed the Killers**
Best leads go to best closers. Worst leads go to worst closers. This minimizes net waste, maximizes output, and creates a training gauntlet where only real closers survive. The top rep becomes a magnet for talent who'll work cheap for the shot.

**The AAA Method (Acknowledge → Associate → Ask)**
When any objection or question lands: (1) Repeat it back to buy time and show you listened. (2) Associate it with something positive — "totally understandable" or tie it to a success story / authority figure. (3) Ask your next question. Never rebut directly. Stay in the sale without breaking rapport.

**The Handshake (not Handoff)**
After the close, don't toss the customer to CS — do a warm handshake. Edify CS to the buyer. Brief CS with goals, fears, pains, exact language used. BAMFAM: Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Every interaction ends with who they see next and when.

**Proof-Promise-Plan Opener**
Open every sales call the same way you'd open a video: proof (we've done this for people like you), promise (here's what we're going to accomplish today), plan (here's how this call goes, and at the end we'll decide together if it makes sense). Takes 15–20 seconds. Sets authority, agenda, and safety.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it." | **Script:** "Cool — what's your main concern?"

**Situation:** Prospect still stalling after first redirect. | **Script:** "What are you afraid of having happen?"

**Situation:** Isolating the real close condition. | **Script:** "What would make this a yes? … Can I share what I usually hear when I ask that?"

**Situation:** All-purpose close when stalled on any objection. | **Script:** "Do you think the reason you haven't succeeded so far is because [repeat their exact objection back as the cause]?"

**Situation:** 1-to-10 temperature check. | **Script:** "On a scale from 1 to 10, where are we? … What would make it a 10? … And why not a 1?"

**Situation:** Prospect brings up cheap competitor. | **Script:** "If ours was the same price as theirs, which would you choose? … Why? … Right — that's why we're more."

**Situation:** Prospect asks about certifications, specs, or details you don't know the answer to. | **Script:** "Which certifications were you looking for specifically? … It sounds like you just want to make sure you're in good hands — here's what I can tell you."

**Situation:** Prospect says "I have to talk to my spouse/partner." | **Script:** "What would happen if they said no?" [If they say 'I'd do it anyway': "So what are we waiting for?"] [If not: "What do you think their main concern would be?"]

**Situation:** Breaking the decision-maker foil. | **Script:** "I think you're asking for permission when you should be looking for support. If things don't change, who gets the blame in this partnership — and is that fair to either of you?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "can you do it for less?" | **Script:** "We could do it for more." [Full stop. Let it land.]

**Situation:** Prospect says "I'll go through your free content first." | **Script:** "Absolutely — you should. And one of two things will happen: you'll succeed, and run into the next problem I can help you solve — or you won't, and I can help you solve that. Either way, let's get you started."

**Situation:** Prospect gives detail-trap question mid-close. | **Script:** "That's a fair question — I'd be like asking a mechanic what's wrong with your car before bringing it in. We have to look under the hood first. Anyone who gives you a firm answer before that is just trying to sell you."

**Situation:** Opening a local opt-in lead call. | **Script:** "John—" [pause, tone implying he should know you] "It's Alex." [pause] "I'm calling because you opted in on Facebook for [specific promotion] — wanted to make sure you weren't a crazy person on the internet [laugh] and see if there's a time that works for you to come in. I've got 2:00 and 4:00 today — which works better?"

**Situation:** Cold outbound voicemail to business owners. | **Script:** "Hey [Business Name], I'm calling about [Competitor]. Give me a call back."

**Situation:** Prospect says they're too busy. | **Script:** "Do you think you'll ever not be busy again? … So if you can't make this work when you're busy, you'll fall off when you're busy again. If you want this to stick, we have to figure out how to make it work in all seasons — does that make sense?"

**Situation:** Pre-call reminder the night before (personalized). | **Script:** "Hey [Name], really looking forward to our call tomorrow — I already pulled three things from your site I think we can immediately help with. Very excited to share them. See you at 4pm, hit me up if anything comes up."

**Situation:** Zooming out when lost in details. | **Script:** "Okay, let's zoom out — big picture: best case, we get you [outcome]. Worst case, you get [guarantee/refund]. Both options are risk-free. One guarantees you stay exactly where you are. The other gives you a shot. Which do you want?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "give me a couple quarters." | **Script:** "Awesome — so what are you going to be doing instead? … Do you think that's going to get you better returns than what we're talking about?"

**Situation:** When you don't know what to say after an objection. | **Script:** [Slowly repeat their exact words back]: "So you want to know [X]…" [pause, buy time, then ask a question about the question.]

## DECISION RULES

1. **When a lead opts in → contact them within 60 seconds.** After 5 minutes, close rate drops 80%. Speed of response is your single highest-leverage variable.

2. **When scheduling → use 15-minute increment slots, 7 days a week.** More scheduling precision = higher show rates. Blocking weekends costs you 29%+ in annual revenue and kills same-day show rates.

3. **When you have an off-the-call block → call existing booked leads and try to pull them up to same-day.** Same-day appointments convert far higher than future ones.

4. **When a prospect says anything you're not prepared for → you are underprepared.** Drill until nothing surprises you. Prep = smooth.

5. **When a prospect says yes → shut up and close.** Don't add details. Don't keep selling. Ask for payment information immediately.

6. **When doing role play → give feedback on one thing at a time, in real time, repeatedly until drilled.** Batch end-of-session feedback doesn't work. Speed of feedback loop determines training speed.

7. **When the team is in a slump → read testimonials before drilling technique.** Conviction produces correct tonality. Technique without belief is a sociopath's game.

8. **When closing → ask questions; never answer questions you don't already know the answer to.** Question the question first. Get them to tell you the right answer, then confirm you have it.

9. **When a detail question comes up → ask why they want to know before answering.** Chunk up to the reason behind the reason, then close the reason — not the detail.

10. **When a prospect says something like "wow, that's expensive" → don't immediately pivot to price overcome.** It may just be a reaction. Only overcome if they say they won't buy.

11. **When "zombies" are predictable (spouse, money, timing) → kill them early in the pain cycle, before asking for money.** Asking post-price is adversarial. Asking pre-price is collaborative.

12. **When you're hot (on a closing streak) → record everything and analyze obsessively.** Great closers systematize their "hot" state rather than hoping it returns.

13. **When hiring salespeople → use group interviews, not resume screening.** Real closers assert themselves. The best ones also encourage nervous peers — flag both.

14. **When you have a guarantee → make it the last line of the best-case/worst-case close.** It collapses the perceived risk to near-zero and forces a simple yes/no decision.

15. **When prospects say no → follow up every 3–6 months with low-friction touchpoints (memes, quick check-ins).** No for now ≠ no forever. The pipeline compounds if you don't abandon it.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

1. **Round-robining leads to all reps equally.** Giving your best leads to your worst closers is throwing money down the toilet. Feed the killers.

2. **Pretending automation is a real person.** Everyone knows. It looks bad. Layer manual outreach on top of automation — don't replace the difference.

3. **Changing price to close a deal.** Once you do it, every price you quote is negotiable forever. Change terms or components if you must — never discount unprompted.

4. **Talking more than the prospect (past ⅓ of call talk time).** If your talk-time is above 33%, you're answering their questions instead of asking yours. You've lost control of the sale.

5. **Giving batch feedback after role play.** Delayed feedback requires more repetitions to create behavior change. One thing, in real time, repeatedly — or don't bother.

6. **Making your best closer your sales manager.** Closers want to hunt. Managing requires patience, coaching, and ego suppression — often the opposite skill set. Phil Jackson wasn't Michael Jordan.

7. **Getting lost in detail questions without chunking up.** In details, they set the frame — you don't. You can't win there. Zoom out every time.

8. **Starting training at the top of the script.** Train back-to-front. If they can't collect payment and ask for the sale, everything upstream is irrelevant.

9. **Creating internal sales vs. marketing conflict.** Separate departments with separate leaders create blame games. One revenue roof, one leader — conflict disappears.

10. **Having no off-the-call SOP.** Salespeople with empty calendar blocks who have no protocol are wasting the highest-leverage downtime in the business. Give them a step-by-step.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

1. **Decelerate into the ask, accelerate into the pitch.** Speed up as you build momentum and excitement ("and this is the part that—"); slow way down when asking for money or delivering a hard truth. The drop in pace signals gravity.

2. **Tonality as punctuation.** Rising tone at end of sentence = question, invites response. Flat/falling tone = statement, keep moving. Consciously choose which you're doing on every sentence, especially the close.

3. **Chunking in threes.** Every argument, every pitch, every list lands in groups of three. "Nutrition. Fitness. Accountability." "Best case. Worst case. Both risk-free." Threes create completeness in the listener's mind.

4. **The rule-then-story-then-implication structure.** State the principle. Prove it with a specific story (name, company, number). Draw the implication for the listener. E.g.: "Feed the killers. [Story of timeshare rep.] So stop round-robining your leads."

5. **Acknowledge → Associate → Ask, every time.** Never rebut directly. Always soften, connect to something familiar or someone relatable, then redirect with a question. This structure is the engine of the entire close.

6. **"It sounds like…" as a bridge.** Use to reframe whatever the prospect said into your frame without disagreeing. "It sounds like you're not saying no — you're just nervous about making the decision. Which actually makes you exactly like the people who get the best results."

7. **"Does that sound fair?" / "Does that make sense?" as micro-closes.** Drop these after every logical step. They're not closing statements — they're agreement checkpoints that stack small yeses and keep the prospect nodding through to the final ask.

8. **"Big picture…" as a reset.** When the conversation drifts into weeds, use "big picture" or "let's zoom out" as an instant frame-reset. It's non-combative, signals authority, and immediately re-anchors the conversation to outcome versus process.
