## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**1. LTGP:CAC Ratio + Automation Thresholds**
The ratio of Lifetime Gross Profit to Cost of Acquiring a Customer is the engine of every business. Required minimum ratio scales with how many core functions (lead gen, conversion, delivery) require human labor: all three automated = 3:1; two = 6:1; one = 9:1; none = 12:1+. Calculate: total marketing spend ÷ new customers = CAC; revenue per customer × gross margin = LTGP. At ≥10:1, spend every dollar you can with no artificial ceiling.

**2. The 3A Reframe (Acknowledge → Associate → Ask)**
When a prospect says anything but yes: (1) Acknowledge — restate what they said back; (2) Associate — link their question to your most successful customers' behavior; (3) Ask — attack the frame of their question with your own question. Never answer a prospect's question directly until all three steps are complete.

**3. The CLOSER Sales Framework**
Clarify they're qualified → Label their problem out loud → Overview their past experience → Sell the vacation (outcome, not features) → Explain away concerns → Reinforce the decision post-close. Pair with BANT qualification upfront so you only run CLOSER on buyers who can close today.

**4. More / Better / New (MBN)**
Three ways to grow — in strict order: do More of what works (highest risk-adjusted return; compounds a known winner), do it Better, or try New. Exhaust More before touching Better. Never touch New until both are maxed.

**5. The Four Components of Mental Toughness**
Mental toughness has four independent, measurable stats: Tolerance (how much hardship before behavior changes), Fortitude (how far you drop when you snap), Resilience (how fast you return to baseline), Adaptability (whether new baseline is higher or lower). Diagnose which is low; drill that specific component. "Toughen up" helps no one.

**6. The Theory of Constraints**
Every business has exactly one bottleneck limiting all growth right now. Solve it and everything else gets easier or becomes irrelevant. Once removed, immediately reassess — the next constraint will differ from what you expected.

**7. Workflow-Based Thinking (vs. Role-Based)**
Every job title is a bundle of 5–10 discrete tasks. List exactly what the person does with their hands — write copy, QA ads, cut clips, respond to emails — then automate each task individually. Ask "which of these specific tasks could live inside a workflow?" before signing any headcount.

**8. Puddle → Pond → Lake → Ocean + Five Expansion Directions**
Niche down early to be the biggest fish in the smallest viable water body; dominate before expanding. When growth stalls, run five moves: (1) up-market, (2) down-market, (3) adjacent, (4) broader, (5) narrower. Pick whichever leverages existing assets hardest — don't pivot the whole business, add a lane.

**9. The 20% Cost of Change Rule + ICE**
Every change to a working system costs ~20% performance before upside materializes. Demand at least 20%+ realistic upside (score by Impact × Confidence × Ease) before absorbing the guaranteed dip. Most "optimizations" fail this bar. Reserve change for bets where ICE clearly justifies the cost.

**10. Start Expensive, Work Down (Tesla Model)**
Sell to a select few at high price to generate proof and cash flow, then serve the masses. Unscalable one-on-one has 100% margin, teaches faster, attracts better clients, and anchors everything downstream as more valuable. Build the 10x expensive tier even if few buy it.

**11. Supply/Demand Lever + The Value Equation**
When you have demand, cut supply — this forces price up. Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice). Speed (reducing time delay) is the single highest-leverage lever for premium buyers — faster delivery + "always first in line" justifies the price increase.

**12. Damaging Admissions Front-Load**
Before pitching upside, list every legitimate reason this won't work for them. Saying "let me tell you the bad first" causes prospects to self-qualify out if the constraints are dealbreakers, and makes everything positive you say afterward credible. Critical when the offer sounds too good to be true.

**13. The Management Diamond**
When someone doesn't do what you want, walk five causes in order: (1) didn't know what you wanted — fix with communication; (2) didn't know how — fix with training; (3) didn't know by when — assign deadline; (4) no incentive — fix motivation; (5) something blocking them — remove it. Never assume performance failure before exhausting all five.

**14. Bundled vs. Unbundled Language**
Abstract directives ("be more accountable," "stop being a dick") fail because recipients can't operationalize them. Break every trait into specific observable behaviors: "text team every morning, do end-of-week reviews, give public kudos by name, privately drill missed numbers" = accountable. If you can't list the behaviors, you can't coach the skill.

**15. Retention Cliff Map**
Churn is frontloaded: months 1–3 at 20%+, month 3 at ~10%, past month 6 at ~2%. The goal is getting customers to month six. Design specific interventions: activation win for month one, concrete deliverable by day 90, member-to-member community connection by month six. Don't overhaul your business because month-one churn is high — that's structural.

**16. LTV Optimization Menu (7 Levers)**
Increase LTGP without new customers: (1) raise price, (2) decrease delivery cost, (3) add upsells, (4) add downsells, (5) add cross-sells, (6) introduce financing, (7) change payment terms to collect upfront. Run every business through all seven levers quarterly; implement at least two.

**17. The Fallacy of the Perfect Pick**
Most people stall hunting for the perfect strategy instead of executing the good-enough one they have. The mountain has multiple paths — path matters far less than willingness to walk it. When you catch yourself researching a fifth option, stop. Pick the most sensible path and commit for 12 months minimum.

**18. The Dice Game (Infinite Game Model)**
Every entrepreneur has a many-sided die — one green side, rest red. Guarantees: (1) the more you roll, the more greens accumulate; (2) if you quit, you lose. When you hit a losing streak, reframe as clearing red sides. Comparison to other players is meaningless — you don't know if it's their hundredth or hundred-thousandth roll.

**19. The One Priority Rule + Punch Card**
You can only have one priority — the word is singular by definition. If you have four, you have none. One meaningful change per quarter per department — four shots per year. Maintain a written "Big List of Crazy Ideas," write ideas down instead of texting the team, pull only when the idea has survived 30+ days and beats everything else on the list.

**20. Cringe Reframe + Status Arithmetic**
Cringe is a defensive status play by people who've never committed to anything. When someone calls your effort cringe, translate: "You're threatening my relative status — you're on the right path." Ask: whose rule says this is embarrassing, did you agree to that rule, what outcome does it optimize for? Being scared of looking cringe is the most cringe thing of all.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Prospect says "I need to think about it" | **Script:** "Totally understand. What would make this a no?"

**Situation:** Prospect stalls deeper in the sale | **Script:** "Dude, what are you afraid of?"

**Situation:** After asking for the close, prospect goes silent | **Script:** [Say nothing. Wait up to 8 seconds. Do not fill the space. Waiting up to 8 seconds after asking closes 30% more sales.]

**Situation:** Prospect raises any non-logistical objection | **Script:** "I think [the reason they gave] is actually the very reason you should do this. The problem you're giving me as a reason not to buy is the exact problem this solves."

**Situation:** Prospect says timing is off | **Script:** "Totally understand, timing is tough. Our biggest success stories are people thinking exactly like you are right now. What would make it a good time?"

**Situation:** Prospect says they need to talk to their spouse | **Script:** "That's super fair. Just so I understand — what parts do you think they wouldn't like?"

**Situation:** Opening a business diagnostic | **Script:** "First question — is this supply-constrained or demand-constrained? Can you handle more customers right now if they showed up?"

**Situation:** Chunking discovery into a diagnosed problem | **Script:** "Just to make sure I have this right — you're not getting leads consistently. So it sounds like you have a marketing issue. Is that fair? Great. What else do you think is holding back your growth?"

**Situation:** Transitioning from discovery to pitch | **Script:** "It sounds like we've got a marketing issue and a pricing issue. Is that fair? If we solve both of those, do you think it would help grow the business? Okay — can I tell you about our thing?"

**Situation:** Matching solution to each diagnosed problem | **Script:** "For the marketing issue — do you think it would help if you sat down with someone who's done $106 million in sales and only works with businesses like yours? Great. Problem one solved. Second issue: we can't really do marketing without fixing gross margin first, because you need margin to fuel the machine. Is that right? So if we brought in our pricing expert — do you think they could help? Then I think you'd be a perfect fit. Ready to rock and roll?"

**Situation:** Presenting a high-ticket one-on-one offer | **Script:** "Would you like to work with me directly? It's [price]. I think it's the highest-likelihood path to getting you [specific outcome]." Then stop talking. Say nothing until they respond.

**Situation:** Prospect balks at premium price | **Script:** "Let me pull out a couple of the components and give you something with 90% of what's in this first option, but structured more scalably. How does that work?"

**Situation:** Introducing the AB offer anchor | **Script:** "We have two ways to work together. Option A is [price] flat fee plus a small commission. Option B is [10x price] with zero commission. Which makes more sense for your situation?"

**Situation:** You need to deliver a hard truth without losing rapport | **Script:** "It's so funny you ask that — I had someone earlier today with the exact same question. Can I share what I told them?"

**Situation:** Prospect says "I already have [competing solution]" | **Script:** "That's amazing — 90% of the clients who end up working with us already have [competing solution]. It means you already understand how these things work, so you're ahead of the learning curve."

**Situation:** Someone says their market is too saturated to grow | **Script:** "You haven't saturated your market. You haven't even saturated one platform on one medium. Are you on every platform? Every ad format? Are you doing outbound AND content AND paid? You're not out of leads. You're out of channels and output."

**Situation:** Someone is about to pivot after 2–3 years | **Script:** "Walk me through the math. Year one of the new thing looks better than year three of this. But now it's year five and you're still catching up — and the bigger you are, the easier growth gets. You're buying imaginary upside and selling real compounding."

**Situation:** Founder wants national scale before dominating local | **Script:** "You haven't conquered a city yet. You want to conquer the world. In order to conquer the world, you have to conquer a country. Before a country, a city. Right now you're at 10 sales a month. Let's talk about 100."

**Situation:** Opening a hard performance conversation | **Script:** "I wanted you to do X and you haven't done it. I just want to figure out the reason so we can solve it and make it less likely to happen again. First possibility: you didn't know I wanted that. Did you know?"

**Situation:** Employee knew but still didn't do it | **Script:** "Okay, you knew what I wanted. Did you know *how* to do it?" [If yes:] "Did you know I wanted it by now — was there a deadline?"

**Situation:** Team member resists dropping current work for #1 priority | **Script:** "I know you want to do a good job. But if we don't fix this one thing, none of the other stuff matters. [Name], if CS complains, tell them I said it's fine — I pay everyone's paychecks and I'm telling you: let it burn."

**Situation:** Assigning tasks and verifying comprehension | **Script:** "Tell me back what you're going to do next — right now." [If wrong:] "You're going to do X, then Y, then Z. Say it back to me. Now write it down."

**Situation:** Prospect objects on price in a service context | **Script:** "This isn't a cost line — it's an investment. Based on [specific data], you're looking at [X return] within [timeframe]. We're really just moving money from one pocket to another, and you keep the asset."

**Situation:** Prospect says "no one in my market can afford that price" | **Script:** "That's just not true. You've seen 200 people this year. There are 2,000 more out there. 9% of Americans have a million-dollar net worth — they just look older than you think. Run the ads. Let's find them."

**Situation:** Someone stuck in overwhelm | **Script:** "You're in the vague. Fear lives in the vague, not in the specific. Tell me exactly what you're afraid happens. Then what happens after that? Now how bearable is that specific outcome compared to the shapeless dread you were carrying a minute ago?"

**Situation:** Cringe/effort mockery from someone less successful | **Script:** "Yeah, it shows, right?" — then say nothing. Let the silence work.

**Situation:** Selling before the product exists | **Script:** "I know I can deliver value and help you make more money. I won't leave until you're satisfied. Sign me up and we'll figure out the details together."

**Situation:** Documenting a setback for future content | **Script:** "I will never let this happen again — and this is the beginning of my comeback story." Record it. Date it. Keep it.

**Situation:** Coaching someone who wants to quit because it's not fun | **Script:** "You're using the excuse of a lack of passion to disguise your inability to handle difficulty — to repeatedly do things you don't enjoy to have something meaningful happen."

**Situation:** Framing a crushing setback for someone | **Script:** "If you were the main character in a movie with a happy ending — and the protagonist just got passed up, lost the deal, got broken up with — what would the character do right now to move the plot toward the happy ending? Do that."

## DECISION RULES

**When a channel stops working** → diagnose model problem before method problem. If CAC > LTGP, no channel saves you.

**When you ask for the close** → shut up for at least 8 seconds. First person to talk after the ask loses the leverage.

**When a prospect gives any non-logistical objection** → reframe and ask again. Never try to win the argument. Every "overcome" is just an excuse to ask one more time.

**When you have more than one priority** → force rank until only one remains. Everything else waits.

**When your close rate is exceptionally high** → raise prices. High close rate means you're underpriced for your avatar.

**When you have something that works** → do more before you change it. A guaranteed 20% loss for a possible 5% gain is a bet you should never take.

**When evaluating any change** → demand at least 20% potential upside at 50/50 odds before absorbing the guaranteed 20% performance dip.

**When you're supply-constrained** → raise prices. Margin goes up → hire better talent → no longer supply-constrained.

**When a competitor undercuts you** → don't react. Focus on the customer. The price-cutter kills their own margins.

**When churn is high** → fix the avatar before fixing ops. You're either selling to the wrong person or failing to activate the right one. Segment first.

**When measuring content performance** → prioritize IRL response quality (messages from ideal clients) over raw view count.

**When someone says their market is saturated** → list every channel they're NOT using before accepting the claim.

**When considering a pivot** → calculate the break-even year. If the new thing takes more than 2–3 years to exceed the current trajectory, stay.

**When evaluating a new hire** → list their 5–10 actual tasks first. If those can live in workflows, don't hire — automate.

**When you want national scale** → ask if you've demonstrably dominated local first.

**When a customer is new** → obsess over days 1–30. Month-one churn is 20%+; that's the single highest-leverage intervention in any subscription business.

**When you have a retention problem** → don't add features. Ask: what one thing would members fight for if you deleted everything? Strip everything else.

**When a new idea arrives** → write it down, tell no one, revisit in 30 days. If it survived, rank it against everything else on your list. Only pull the trigger if it beats what's already there.

**When you feel the urge to switch strategies** → diagnose first: is the issue talent, culture, or training? Fix the root cause before abandoning the path.

**When something feels urgent** → ask literally: does this change what I eat, where I sleep, who I'm with? If no, it's voluntary suffering.

**When you're under 30 with no major obligations** → maximize hours worked. You will never have fewer responsibilities than right now.

**When tracking any skill** → if you're not measuring it, you don't care about it. Install a metric before spending another hour practicing.

**When building a business with weak moats** → invest in brand before anything else. Brand is the fastest way to de-commoditize any product or service.

**When comparing absolute vs. relative returns** → always choose absolute. $1M net on 2:1 ROAS beats $900 on 10:1 every time.

**When faced with a hard decision** → spell out the trade explicitly: "I am trading X for Y." If you can't name Y specifically, don't make the trade.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**"My market is saturated."** Almost always false. Almost always means: I've exhausted one format on one platform and haven't touched ads, outreach, or any other channel. This is ego protection, not market reality.

**Asking "Do you have any questions?"** Hands control to the prospect, invites objections, signals you don't know what you're doing. Train it out of every rep.

**Winning an argument with a prospect.** If you logically defeat a prospect, you lose the sale. They disengage when they feel beaten. Reframe; never rebut directly.

**Changing winning copy because it "feels stale."** When something works, the instinct to change it is emotional, not strategic. Resist it until you have a 20%+ upside thesis.

**Optimizing for cheap leads instead of high LTV.** Cheap leads attract low-quality buyers, tank conversion rates, and erode competitive moat. Wrong variable to minimize.

**Cutting prices to fight a competitor.** Churn does not change at or above a threshold — revenue just drops permanently. The customer's first comment will be "I can't believe I was paying more."

**Trying to go national before dominating local.** An army overexpanding before its base is fortified collapses. You haven't conquered a city; stop aiming at the world.

**Adding features to fix retention.** Addition inflates the "I'm not using this" feeling that drives cancellation. Strip first; add only if core value is already delivering.

**Multiple "priorities."** The word has no useful plural. If you say priorities, you have none.

**Pivoting after 2–3 years because "the new thing looks better."** You're selling compounding momentum for a shiny object. The math almost never closes in your favor within a reasonable horizon.

**Reading without changing behavior.** Entertainment disguised as education. Learning only exists if behavior changes in the same condition. No changed behavior = no learning.

**Abdicating instead of delegating.** Handing something off with "here you go, good luck" and being surprised when it gets worse. You didn't delegate — you abandoned.

**Building a business with no retention mechanism.** Zero churn compounding beats 3x marketing spend every time. If your model requires constantly replacing every customer you lose, you're on a treadmill.

**Self-referential "why."** If the goal is to serve yourself, you'll hit the ceiling of your own needs and stall. The mission must be bigger than you, or the obstacles you face will also be bigger.

**Treating cringe-fear as wisdom.** Being scared of looking cringe is the most cringe thing of all. It's just high school logic wearing adult clothes.

**Not documenting the struggle.** Shame about where you are destroys the artifact. The bottom is the opening scene of the comeback story.

**Answering prospect questions without reframing first.** Jumping directly to an answer makes the prospect judge, jury, and executioner of whether your answer is good. You will fail most of the time.

**Filling the silence after the close.** The moment you talk after the ask, you've signaled anxiety, removed pressure, and lowered the likelihood of a yes.

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**Proof-first openings.** Every video, presentation, or sales conversation opens with a specific, observable proof statement before any claim or instruction: "We got $32.7M views this month. Here is how." Never lead with theory — lead with what you can see with your eyes.

**Validate-then-redirect.** Every prospect response gets acknowledged before being redirected: "Totally understand." / "That makes complete sense." / "That's a really smart question." — then immediately pivot to a question that moves back toward the sale. Never skip the validation step.

**The Three-Part Scaffold.** Nearly every argument: (1) here's what most people do wrong, (2) here's the underlying mechanic of why it's wrong, (3) here's the specific action that fixes it. Each part is short. No meandering.

**Short declarative stacks at the point of instruction.** When it's time to give the actual rule, sentences compress to their minimum: "Ask for the close. Shut up. That's it." No hedging, no qualifiers. Shortness signals conviction and makes instruction quotable.

**The math pivot.** State the problem, immediately convert to numbers, let the numbers reach the conclusion: "If I'd prep a week for 5,000 people, that's 100 weeks for 500,000. That's just math." Removes argument from the room.

**Rhetorical inversion.** Take a widely-held belief, etymologically or empirically invert it, rebuild it on your terms: [popular claim] → [why it's wrong or imprecise] → [correct reframe] → [practical implication]. Steal the frame before the audience can retreat into it.

**Volume as proof of conviction.** Specific large numbers dropped as assumed context, not as brags: "35,000 pieces of content." "$250 million in aggregate revenue." These establish the scale at which the principle has been validated.

**The personal confession pivot.** Narrate your own failures in detail before making the prescriptive claim: "I cut my top line 20% to fight a competitor. Churn changed zero. I just made less money." Earn the right to the lesson by showing you paid the price.

**The triple-suffering structure.** When confronting hard truths, stack three versions: "Growing a business is hard. A plateaued business is hard. A decaying business is hard." Normalizes the pain, removes the illusion of a pain-free alternative, forces the listener to select the suffering that pays best.

**The "and so" logical bridge.** Every "and so" signals a conclusion being drawn from evidence just presented. Arguments don't jump; they chain. Never make a point without the "and so that means…" close.

**The self-answering rhetorical.** Poses an objection, demolishes it in the same breath: "You're like, 'Holy cow, that's way bigger.' Now remember, he had a pretty good close rate. And if you have a really good close rate, I hear that and think: we're underpriced." Pattern: raise the obvious objection, pivot immediately to the neutralizing frame.

**Concrete specificity over abstraction.** Never say "practice a lot" — say "90 full run-throughs of the presentation, three per day, for 30 days." Every principle gets a number, a frequency, and a behavior. Replace every vague claim with the most specific version you can back up — it's not bragging, it's proof.
