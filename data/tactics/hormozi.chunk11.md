## FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**The Six Business Horsemen**
Six root causes that kill most businesses: serving too many avatars, underpricing, overcompensating labor, overextension, and no data. When a business is stuck, one of these six is almost always the culprit — usually more than one at once. Run every struggling business through this diagnostic before recommending any solution.

**The Friction Removal Framework (Value Equation Applied)**
A product's value is the promise minus everything that makes it suck. The five friction categories are: delay (how long until they get results), effort (new work they have to do), sacrifice (things they have to give up), risk (new exposure they incur), and inconsistency (it works sometimes, not others). Your job is to systematically remove these — not add features. "If you said I can get you this outcome, all the things that make it suck are the delay, the effort, the sacrifice, the risk, and the inconsistency."

**The Woman in the Red Dress**
Any opportunity outside your core business looks beautiful because you don't know its backstory. You have uninformed optimism about new things and informed pessimism about your current thing. The matrix test: if it's not core to your business, it was sent to destroy you. Every time you're tempted by a new product line, location, or offer — ask if this is a missed opportunity or an unsolved problem in the current business.

**Click-to-Close + Close-to-Resell Pipelines**
Every business has two funnels that must be tracked separately. Pipeline 1 (click → close): CTR → opt-in rate → schedule rate → show rate → offer rate → close rate → cash collected. Pipeline 2 (close → resell): activation rate → time to value → churn → ascension rates by cohort → gross profit per product line. If you have problems, start with Pipeline 1. If that's clean, fix Pipeline 2.

**Activation Points Framework**
Find the single early behavior that has the highest correlation with long-term retention. Look at your best customers, identify what they did in the first 30–90 days, then reverse-engineer that behavior into the onboarding for every customer. "Orange Theory found that customers who did five workouts in the first month were dramatically more likely to stay for the next eight months — so all their onboarding drove toward that one activation point."

**The Open Loop / Ascension Timing Model**
The moment of purchase opens a psychological loop. That loop closes as the customer approaches completion. Sell ascensions while the loop is maximally open — 24 hours post-purchase, at a first win, at the halfway point, and at a major milestone. Never wait for the end. "You want to keep the party going rather than wait until they're done and then ask them to make another purchasing decision."

**The Pricing Math Problem**
Stop optimizing for close rate. Optimize for revenue = (price × close rate). If you double price and close rate falls from 30% to 20%, you win. Make this a math problem and remove your ego from the rejection. "You get more nos, but you also make more money — and that's a great deal every single time."

**Measurement as Intervention**
Tracking something is itself an improvement mechanism — before you change a single process. Studies on weight loss show that telling people to weigh themselves daily produces weight loss without any other intervention. The same applies to close rates, profit, churn. "If you track your profit every month, your profit will improve. If you track your close rate, it will improve — because you'll be able to use common sense."

**Build Back to Front**
Most founders pour money into acquisition before fixing retention. The right order: fix delivery quality and back-end economics first, fix onboarding and churn second, scale front-end acquisition last. "I build businesses back to front. I try and solve how do we make this thing so unbelievably profitable that I can then outspend everybody else on acquisition."

**The Avatar Narrowing Model**
Narrow your front-end messaging to the most profitable single avatar. Narrower messaging produces higher response rates from the target segment — LTV:CAC ratio expands, not contracts. "If you say 'attention salon owners,' you get salon owners. When someone sees your ad and thinks 'this is just for me,' that's what you want — not 'I might find some value here.'"

**The "Who" Framework for Expansion**
Overextension happens when you decide to expand without a qualified owner for either the new thing or the current thing. The solve is always finding the right who before pulling the trigger. "Overextension occurs when you don't have a good enough who and then you decide to do it anyway — and that's because of greed."

**The Diluted Water Hiring Model**
When you need to overhaul a team without disrupting operations, bring in high-quality hires at better comp alongside the existing team. Good hires make the bad ones visibly obsolete — many self-select out. You're concentrating the solution into a diluted problem rather than doing a disruptive hard reset. Plan to temporarily fund two teams; the cost is worth it.

## TACTICAL SCRIPTS

**Situation:** Raising price for new customers | **Script:** "When you get to the point in the script where you'd normally say $1,000, just say $1,500 or $2,000. That's it. Let it be a math problem."

**Situation:** Price increase email to existing customers — opening | **Script:** "Big news coming tomorrow — just letting you know. Brace yourself."

**Situation:** Price increase email — body, step 1 | **Script:** "I want to be straight with you: we're raising prices. Not because I'm a greedy bastard — but because I see that we're no longer fulfilling the promises I originally made you, and in order to reinvest in the business and deliver [A, B, C benefit], this change is necessary."

**Situation:** Price increase email — reminding them of past value | **Script:** "You've been with us for [X period]. We've made you [this much money / saved you this much time / delivered this many results]. I want you to know I haven't forgotten that."

**Situation:** Price increase email — softening with loyalty discount | **Script:** "I've already raised prices on new customers. But because you've been loyal, I'm going to give you a discount equal to your current payment for the next six months. After that, you'll move to the new rate. That's my thank-you for sticking with me."

**Situation:** Price increase email — PS human touch | **Script:** "PS — if this price change is going to materially affect your business, please just reach out. I'd love to get on the phone and figure something out that works for you."

**Situation:** Posting a price increase in a community | **Script:** "We want to keep this conversation IRL — please reach out directly with any questions. This is not a comment section discussion."

**Situation:** Cutting compensation for overcompensated employees — opener | **Script:** "I've got something serious I want to talk to you about. We've worked together for [X time] and I've valued everything you've built here. But I have to be honest: this business isn't profitable, and I can't give you the career growth I originally promised — because we're never going to be able to expand. And unfortunately, I can't get fired because I'm the owner, so I have to fix this."

**Situation:** Offering an employee a path forward during comp restructure | **Script:** "Here's what I want you to know: there's a path where you still make what you're making today. It requires [three specific things]. I can outline that if you want. Or if you need time to explore other options, I'm giving you three to six months — whatever you need."

**Situation:** Testing delegation readiness with a manager | **Script:** "Here's the deal: you get the profit share now and equity over five years. In exchange, this phone doesn't ring. If a pipe bursts — what do you do? If your first inclination is to call me, that's not the deal."

**Situation:** Qualifying a customer for ascension via CSM handoff | **Script:** "Hey, I think you should talk to Shawn. You've hit [milestone X] and Shawn can definitely set you up with what you need from here."

**Situation:** Driving a renewal in the context of goals | **Script:** "You told me your goal was [X]. Renewing is the path that gets you there. That's why we built it this way."

**Situation:** Running a product simplification survey | **Script:** "If I got rid of everything except one thing — everything — which one thing would you want me to keep?"

**Situation:** Selling a 6-week challenge with upsells | **Script:** Make three upsell offers: at 24 hours post-purchase, at the halfway mark, and at completion. "You're not hard closing — you're just making offers aligned to their goal. If it's aligned, they'll take it."

**Situation:** Finding activation points by analyzing retention data | **Script:** "Look at your best customers — the ones who stayed the longest and paid the most. What did they do in the first 30 days? Do more of that on purpose for everyone."

**Situation:** Onboarding handoff from sales to customer success | **Script:** "Restate their goals multiple times so they know you know what their goals are. Then tie those goals directly to the actions you want them to take — which should be the same actions that drive your time to value."

**Situation:** Diagnosing a stuck business with no data | **Script:** "You don't know what to do because you don't have data. The first step isn't another marketing campaign. It's tracking. Everything else follows from that."

**Situation:** Framing a price cut to a team as a math problem | **Script:** "This isn't personal. The business has to be profitable for me to be able to keep any promise I ever made you. I made a mistake on comp structure. Let me walk you through three paths forward."

**Situation:** Scaling decision test for brick-and-mortar | **Script:** "The litmus test: the business has to go for six months without you and maintain or grow. If it craters when you leave, you don't have a business — you have a job you own."

## DECISION RULES

**When you're deciding who to serve:** Pick the avatar with the highest LTV:CAC ratio, cut front-end messaging to just that person, and eliminate or bridge the others. Never serve multiple avatars simultaneously just to make payroll.

**When you raise prices:** Test on new customers first. If close rate drops but revenue goes up, keep the new price. Only then bring it to existing customers with the loyalty discount framework.

**When you want to expand (new location, product, service line):** Ask: do I have a who to own the new thing while I run the current thing, or a who to run the current thing while I own the new thing? No who = no expansion. Period.

**When you don't know what to do in the business:** Start collecting data. Not tomorrow. Today. "Data first" always beats "I need to go make money right now."

**When you want to improve any metric:** Measure it publicly and track it consistently. Measurement alone will produce improvement before any process change.

**When you're doing a team overhaul:** Test new comp and talent standards on new hires first. If you can attract good talent at new terms, you have proof before you restructure the existing team.

**When your product feels too complex:** Ask customers "if you could only keep one thing, what would it be?" Then consider removing the lowest-ranked feature and seeing who complains.

**When churn is high:** Fix onboarding before scaling acquisition. Spending more on ads with a leaky bucket only compounds a negative reputation.

**When you want to make a sales ascension:** Offer it at 24 hours post-purchase, at first win, at halfway, and at a defined milestone — never wait for the end of the contract.

**When setting compensation:** Never give revenue share on top-line revenue to someone not accountable for overhead, acquisition costs, or customer retention. That structure is a time bomb.

**When a business seems unprofitable:** Check the back end first — renewals, ascensions, and churn — before touching ad spend. Profit is disproportionately made on the back end.

**When you have a problem in a department:** Go find out where the bodies are buried yourself. If the department head can only tell you things are "going good," you're too far away.

**When deciding whether to add a feature:** Ask if you're solving a real problem or chasing an opportunity. "Are these missed opportunities or problems I promised to solve but haven't?" If it's the former, it's a distraction.

**When hiring a CS leader:** Reject anyone who consistently blames customers or their team for missed outcomes. A real CS leader is accountable, customer-obsessed, and willing to roll up their sleeves and talk to customers directly.

**When evaluating a hire:** Treat it like a $100K/year software contract. Would you sign that contract after one lunch meeting? Then don't hire that way either.

## RED FLAGS / ANTI-PATTERNS

**Serving anyone with a credit card.** If your qualifying criteria is "has money," you'll have diluted marketing, overwhelmed sales, and inconsistent delivery. You are not a real business yet — you're a freelancer with employees.

**Pricing by averaging the market.** Everyone in your market is broke and underpaid. Copying the average is opting into the average result. "The average business owner makes very little money and takes on a tremendous amount of risk."

**Revenue share on top-line with employees not accountable for costs.** This structure makes it impossible to fix the business model. Every improvement you make becomes a free raise for someone who didn't earn it. Avoid at all cost when starting out.

**Expanding before the model is proven.** Opening location two before location one can survive six months without you means you're building a bigger version of a broken thing. "If you go make the front end better and all the holes aren't plugged, you just compounded a negative reputation."

**Trying to ascend customers at the very end of their journey.** The loop is almost closed. They've already mentally moved on. Most businesses get 100% of their ascensions from the worst possible moment — when the customer is done. Move them upstream.

**Adding bodies to solve output problems.** Headcount multiplies without adding value. Delegating downward through layers produces the most important work being executed by the least competent person in the chain. "You want to make sure that your organization doesn't just get dumber and dumber over time."

**Building without data.** You cannot improve what you don't track. A $10M business with no data on close rates, churn, activation, and ascensions is guessing at scale — and scale makes the guesses more expensive.

**Adding features to solve retention problems.** The number one reason customers cancel across industries is overwhelm. "As humans we often want to over-deliver in value, so we think we need to add more stuff — but the number one reason people cancel is there's too much stuff to do."

**Treating overextension as ambition.** Stressing your team about missed opportunities rather than unsolved problems is a sign you're chasing the woman in the red dress. "You will grow bigger by getting better. Choosing to get bigger just makes you bloated."

**Pitting teams against founders or creating cultural toxicity at the top.** A leader who undermines the founder is an existential risk. Remove fast. "The best day to get rid of cancer is today."

## CADENCE / VOICE PATTERNS

**The Math Strip.** When people resist a decision emotionally, Hormozi converts it to pure arithmetic and removes the emotion. "Let it be a math problem and solve for it." Structure: state the numbers → show the outcome → declare the obvious move. E.g., "If you double price and close rate drops from 30% to 20%, that's still a great deal. Here's why: [math]. What are you waiting for?"

**The Short Declaration + Silence.** He makes a bold claim in one sentence, then lets it breathe. "You can't out-sell bad churn." Full stop. No explanation first. The claim lands before the rationale. Use this for counterintuitive truths.

**Analogy → Reveal → Lesson.** Introduce a vivid analogy (the Matrix, the woman in the red dress, the iPhone), let it run, then tie it back to the business reality with a hard landing. "The woman in the red dress looks attractive. She distracts you from the main thing. But if it's not your core business, it was sent to destroy you."

**The "This is embarrassing" confession.** He regularly admits to personal failure before delivering the lesson — it disarms the listener and makes the advice feel hard-won, not theoretical. "I've been so deceived by this girl so many times. That's why I'm making this video." Use this when teaching from past mistakes.

**Step-by-step with numbered anchoring.** For process-heavy advice, he names the number before the step: "Number one is... Number two is... And this is the brilliant one..." This creates expectation and makes complex sequences feel executable.

**"Think about it like this" pivot.** Used to shift from abstract to concrete, or from macro to tactical. Signals an example or analogy is coming. Gives the listener a moment to reset before the illustration. Common before vivid hypotheticals.

**The Contrast Close.** Sets up two options (Business A vs. Business B, employee path 1 vs. path 3) and makes the better path obvious through comparison rather than prescription. "Both businesses have 300 customers after three years. Which would you rather have? I'll tell you which one I want." The listener concludes themselves.

**Brutal plain-speak after extended setup.** Long analytical wind-up → one-sentence truth bomb. "So why do they keep doing it? Greed. There's really no other one." No softening. No caveat. Signal that the diagnosis is final.
